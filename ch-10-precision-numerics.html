Numerics is the one design axis where a 10T-parameter run cannot simply copy a published recipe and move on. Every team that reached frontier scale in low precision invented at least one non-obvious workaround for a specific piece of silicon, and the workarounds do not transfer cleanly across Hopper, Blackwell, and the FP4 frontier beyond it. So this chapter works through the evidence in order: DeepSeek-V3’s blockwise FP8, still the reference point; NVIDIA’s four-recipe Transformer Engine zoo, and which recipe NVIDIA’s own flagship actually shipped with; MXFP8, the Blackwell-native successor with one real 256-GPU corroboration; and NVFP4, a 12B/10T-token existence proof that is nowhere near frontier scale. Then the hardware ground truth — a reconciled GB200 peak-FLOPS table that later chapters lean on — and a verdict: what a $2B run in July 2026 should bet on, and what it should only pilot.

The reference point: DeepSeek V3’s blockwise FP8 recipe

DeepSeek-V3’s mixed-precision framework (technical report, Dec 2024) is the most load-bearing precision recipe in this literature, because it is the only one validated at genuinely frontier parameter count with a clean ablation against a BF16 baseline. All three GEMMs inside every Linear operator — Fprop, Dgrad, Wgrad — run in FP8; the embedding, output head, MoE gating module, all RMSNorm operators, the attention softmax itself, master weights, weight gradients, and optimizer states stay in BF16/FP32. The granularity is fine-grained and asymmetric: activations use 1×128 tile quantization (per-token, per-128-channel group), weights use 128×128 block quantization, both computed online per iteration rather than from a delayed/historical amax buffer. The format choice is uniform E4M3 for every GEMM operand — a deliberate break from the “E4M3 forward / E5M2 backward” convention that dominated earlier FP8 practice, made viable specifically because fine-grained per-block scaling already compensates for E4M3’s narrower dynamic range (Ch4 has the full DeepSeek stack context).

The single most consequential engineering detail is the accumulation fix. DeepSeek found that H800’s Tensor Core accumulates FP8 partial sums natively at roughly 14 bits — not full FP32 — which produced up to ~2% relative error on a K=4096 GEMM without correction. Their fix promotes partial WGMMA results to CUDA-core FP32 registers every Nc=128 accumulated elements (four WGMMAs), overlapping the promotion of one interval with the MMA of the next so the extra CUDA-core work is largely hidden. Colfax Research’s independent analysis (DeepSeek-R1 and FP8 Mixed-Precision Training, 2025) confirms the same ~14-bit accumulation limitation on Hopper tensor cores and notes that the CUDA-core promotion pattern is now standard practice in CUTLASS ≥3.2. Net result, validated at ~1T-token comparison scale before being trusted for the full V3 run: relative loss error below 0.25% versus a BF16-trained reference — “well within the range of training randomness,” in the paper’s own framing.

Two numeric details rarely reproduced elsewhere matter for anyone reimplementing this (report §3.3.3). Inputs to the post-attention Linear are stored in a custom E5M6 format with power-of-2 scale factors, re-tiled from 1×128 to 128×1 for the backward pass, because that specific activation proved sensitive to naive FP8 casting. And MoE dispatch-direction activations are FP8 while the reverse combine direction stays BF16, to protect accuracy on the accumulation-heavy reduction. This is the level of granularity a 10T/200B recipe needs to match or exceed. The paper’s own Appendix B.2 documents that blockwise scaling on activations alone, without the matching weight block scheme, produced training instability — a finding Colfax independently corroborates.

What’s instructive is which recipe NVIDIA’s own flagship chose. Nemotron-H-56B-Base — “the first Nemotron model fully pretrained with an FP8-based recipe” (Nemotron-H technical report, Apr 2025) — used the simplest option in the zoo: per-tensor current scaling, with a hybrid format (E4M3 for weights/activations, E5M2 for gradients — the more conservative choice DeepSeek explicitly avoided), and the first and last 4 GEMMs of the network kept in BF16. In 8B-scale ablations run to 15T tokens, this per-tensor recipe matched or beat BF16 on downstream accuracy. NVIDIA’s stated reason for current scaling over delayed scaling: instantaneous adaptation to real-time distribution shifts, at the cost of a live max-reduction per tensor per step — a bet that per-step overhead is cheaper than the risk of a stale delayed-scaling factor clipping an outlier. Production adoption of DeepSeek’s exact blockwise recipe has since spread: TE ships it as the standard recommendation for Hopper-generation MoE training, validated at DeepSeek-V3 scale plus MiniMax-M2 and Ant Ling-2.0 (TE docs, “FP8 Blockwise Scaling”, 2026).

Why naive FP8 breaks, and where FP8 attention stands

The literature past the headline “FP8 matches BF16” results is more instructive for a one-shot 10T-parameter run than any single success story. To FP8 and Back Again (May 2024) found that removing even a single exponent bit from a float format prevents training entirely — mantissa bits are cheap to lose, dynamic-range bits are not — and identified the LM head and SwiGLU activation outputs as specific divergence sources under naive quantization. The same paper found that ~10% of nanoGPT BF16 baseline runs (18 of 188) diverged more than 5% into training purely from random seed variation — a useful caution against attributing every reported FP8 instability to the format itself rather than to baseline variance.

The FOG paper (Towards Fully FP8 GEMM LLM Training at Scale, May 2025) pushed FP8 into the attention operator itself — Q·Kᵀ and P·V in FP8, TE delayed scaling with history length 1024, E4M3 forward / E5M2 backward — and found that architecture, not just numerics, is the lever that makes this stable: pre-normalization must become post-normalization/LayerScale, trainable QK-RMSNorm gains must be frozen (they explode under low precision), the softmax scale must move from 1/√d to γ₀²/√d, and gated SwiGLU activations should be swapped for non-gated ones. With those changes, “FOG-flash” (8B params, 20B tokens) hit 12,764 tokens/sec/GPU versus 9,105 for BF16 — a 35–43% gain, rising to +43.5% at 8K context. FlashAttention-3 (Jul 2024) adds the kernel-side evidence: its FP8 forward path (block quantization plus a Hadamard-style “incoherent processing” step) reaches ~1.2 PFLOP/s on H100 with 2.6× lower numerical error than naive per-tensor FP8 attention — but Tri Dao’s own writeup explicitly flags that its effect on large-scale training dynamics, as opposed to inference, was not validated. Net for a frontier run: FP8 attention forward is workable with FA3/FA4-class kernels (Ch17 has the kernel-level detail — FA4 CuTe remains forward-only for FP8 on SM100 as of July 2026, with no MXFP8/NVFP4 attention path at all), but a fully FP8 attention training path proven at scale requires FOG-style architectural intervention, not a kernel swap.

One counter-example belongs here because it directly contradicts the “FP8 pretraining is settled” narrative that dominates 2025–2026 commentary. Kimi K2 — a 1T-parameter MoE — explicitly declined to use FP8 in pretraining compute after a “preliminary study” showed risk of degradation, training in higher precision and only casting to blockwise FP8 post-hoc for inference weights (K2 technical report, Jul 2025; a Hugging Face discussion adds secondhand detail). This does not refute DeepSeek-V3’s result — V3 is direct counter-counter-evidence at comparable scale — but it is a named, citable data point that FP8 pretraining at trillion-parameter MoE scale is not universally considered safe by every frontier lab, and the reasons for Kimi’s choice (routing topology, MuonClip/QK-Clip interactions, or something else) were never published in enough detail to generalize.

MXFP8: the Blackwell-native successor

NVIDIA’s Recipes for Pre-training LLMs with MXFP8 (Jun 2025, v2 Aug 2025) is the primary MXFP8 evidence base. Its core technical insight: quantization must run along both the row and column axis separately, because a weight or activation tensor is consumed in both its natural orientation (fprop) and its transposed orientation (dgrad/wgrad), and MX block scales are axis-local — a single quantization pass does not suffice the way it can for a coarser per-tensor scheme. The format is E4M3 uniformly, including for activation gradients — an explicit departure from Nemotron-H’s E5M2-for-gradients choice, justified by an 843M-model ablation showing E5M2 gradients caused “substantial degradation” at 8B scale. At a 32-element block granularity, the per-block dynamic range is already covered by roughly 17.8 binades, so E4M3’s extra mantissa bit is worth more than E5M2’s wider exponent range once per-block scales absorb the range problem. Scale factors use UE8M0 (power-of-two only) and are computed by rounding up (ceiling), deliberately deviating from the OCP MXFP v1.0 spec’s round-down convention, because round-down can let a post-scale value exceed the destination format’s representable maximum and silently overflow.

Because MX block granularity is fine enough, MXFP8 quantizes essentially all linear/GEMM layers — softmax, activation functions, and residual-add stay high precision, but there is no first/last-N-layers BF16 carve-out the way per-tensor FP8 recipes need. Validated to 8B params / 15T tokens, MXFP8 sustains a <0.5% perplexity gap versus BF16 through the entire run, with MMLU/ARC/RACE/PIQA/Winogrande/HellaSwag/OpenBookQA/SocialIQA/CommonsenseQA all matching. The paper claims up to 22% training-time reduction, 14% lower peak memory, and 19% higher throughput versus BF16 — but the large ablation runs were executed on 3072 Hopper GPUs via emulation/kernel support, not native Blackwell silicon, so the loss-curve evidence and the hardware-throughput evidence come from different generations of hardware.

The real-world corroboration is more useful than the paper’s own numbers for run planning. PyTorch’s TorchTitan team combined MXFP8-quantized grouped GEMMs (via TorchAO’s _to_mxfp8_then_scaled_grouped_mm) with DeepEP-overlapped expert-parallel communication on a DeepSeek-V3-671B-architecture run — 256×B200 GPUs, TP2/PP2/EP32, sequence length 8192, local batch 64 (TorchTitan blog, Mar 2026, updated May 2026). DeepEP overlap alone gave +32.0% throughput (651→859 tok/s); MXFP8 plus DeepEP together gave +41.0% (651→918 tok/s), against a BF16 + standard-EP baseline. The disclosed caveat matters as much as the headline: MXFP8 could not be applied to the dense (non-expert) linear layers in the 671B config, because of a torch.compile + tensor-parallel incompatibility — the 41% figure is grouped-GEMM (expert) MXFP8 only, not full-model MXFP8. A companion 16B-scale, 1500-step run showed MXFP8 converging equivalently to BF16 with no meaningful divergence. Nebius then reproduced the same DeepSeek-V3-671B architecture on a 256×B200 TorchTitan cluster with MXFP8 replacing the original 1×128/128×128 blockwise recipe, and reported the same ~41% figure (Nebius ml-cookbook, 2026). A second organization landing on the same number for the same architecture is genuine corroboration by the standards of this literature — with one caveat worth stating plainly: Nebius ran its reproduction on TorchTitan itself, so it likely shares TorchTitan’s MXFP8-grouped-GEMM-plus-DeepEP code path rather than being an independently implemented measurement. Read it as a second team confirming the technique reproduces on their hardware and cluster config, not as two fully independent implementations converging on the same figure by coincidence.

NVFP4 and the FP4 pretraining frontier

NVIDIA’s flagship FP4 result (Pretraining Large Language Models with NVFP4, Sept 2025) is the largest published FP4 pretraining run as of mid-2026: a 12B hybrid Mamba-Transformer (62 blocks: 6 self-attention, 28 FFN, 28 Mamba-2), 10T tokens, NVFP4 for the majority of linear layers. The recipe’s own ablation shows all four of the following techniques are necessary — removing any one measurably degrades convergence:

  1. Two-level block scaling: 16-element blocks (versus MXFP4’s coarser 32) with an E4M3 block scale, plus one additional FP32 per-tensor scale that first remaps the whole tensor into the FP4×FP8-representable range before the per-block E4M3 scale does fine adjustment.
  2. Random Hadamard transforms (16×16), applied only to the inputs of the weight-gradient (Wgrad) GEMM, using a fixed random sign vector held constant across the entire training run.
  3. 2D scaling for weights (16×16 blocks) versus 1D scaling for activations/gradients (1×16) — asymmetric specifically to preserve forward/backward chain-rule consistency, since a weight tensor is read in two different contractions.
  4. Stochastic rounding restricted to gradient tensors during the FP4 cast; weights and activations use plain round-to-nearest-even.

Plus a structural carve-out: 16% of linear layers stay BF16 — the first 2 blocks and final 8 of 62, a front/back sandwich rather than a uniform sprinkling. Results: relative loss error versus an FP8 baseline stays under 1% through the stable-LR phase, widening to ~1.5% during LR decay — the decay phase is where 4-bit’s information loss bites hardest, consistent with the general finding that late-training fine adjustments are the most precision-sensitive part of a run. A late-training precision upgrade — switching to BF16 for the final 18% of tokens, from the 8.2T/10T mark — recovers most of the gap, down to 0.5% relative error, while keeping ~94% of total FLOPs in FP4. MMLU-Pro comes out at 62.58% (NVFP4) versus 62.62% (FP8) — a wash. A smaller 8B/1T-token NVFP4-versus-MXFP4 head-to-head in the same paper found NVFP4’s relative error (~1.5%) beating MXFP4’s (~2.5%), with MXFP4 needing 36% more tokens (1.36T vs 1T) to match NVFP4’s loss — the paper’s own accounting of NVFP4’s format advantage.

The late-decay sensitivity deserves its own paragraph, because it recurs in three separate places in this chapter and predicts where every future format will break. (This is our own synthesis; no single paper states it end to end.) As the LR decays, per-step weight updates shrink until they approach the weight format’s local quantization step. At that point, round-to-nearest silently swallows a growing fraction of every update — the model keeps computing gradients it can no longer apply, and loss flattens onto a noise floor set by the format, not the data. This is why sensitivity peaks late: early updates are large relative to the step size. It is why stochastic rounding is most valuable exactly where it is most contested — SR keeps the swallowed updates unbiased in expectation, at the price of added variance precisely when the signal is smallest, which is the real substance of the SR-versus-RTN dispute. It is why the late-BF16 switch recovers most of the gap: it shrinks the step size back under the update size for the fine-adjustment endgame. It is why “Characterization and Mitigation” (below) finds Adam’s accumulators compounding rather than averaging the error. And it predicts that a continuously maintained weight EMA — the DeepSeek-V3 CPU-EMA practice from Ch9 — partially offsets the floor, since averaging across steps recovers signal the individual rounded weights lost. That last interaction is cheap and worth testing in any FP4 pilot.

The academic literature has moved fast in the roughly ten months since, and it has not converged. Quartet (May 2025) argues native all-MXFP4 training can be compute-optimal on Blackwell once real kernel speedups are accounted for — but it uses deterministic round-to-nearest for the backward pass rather than stochastic rounding, a direct disagreement with NVFP4’s design choice, and it is validated only to 430M params (7B as a spot check), far below the scale needed to trust the “compute optimal” framing. Quartet II (Jan 2026) revisits unbiased gradient estimation specifically for NVFP4-style two-level scaling — a hint the field is converging toward that variant over plain MXFP4. TetraJet-v2 (Oct 2025, ICML 2026 Spotlight) frames the two dominant FP4-training failure modes as weight oscillation and activation outliers, proposing “OsciReset” and “OutControl” for fully-NVFP4 linear layers including gradients. Four Over Six (Dec 2025) is a sharp, under-circulated fix: NVFP4’s E4M3 block scale, set to a block’s max value, leaves a dead zone where no FP4 code point can represent 66.6–100% of the block’s own max. The fix quantizes each block twice (scale=6 and scale=4) and keeps whichever has lower MSE — closing 22.3% of the NVFP4-to-BF16 loss gap on a Nemotron-3-Nano architecture for under 15% compute overhead. Elucidating the Design Space of FP4 Training (Sept 2025) systematically swept technique combinations and concluded Hadamard + tensor scaling + stochastic rounding is the best accuracy/overhead trade-off — with a more hedged verdict than NVIDIA’s or Quartet’s papers: FP4 training is achievable with careful configuration, not a drop-in replacement. Cross-vendor validation exists but only at small scale: MXFP4 on AMD MI355X native FP4 hardware is validated to just 1B params / ~10B tokens (Pretraining LLMs with MXFP4 on Native FP4 Hardware, May 2026). And Characterization and Mitigation of Training Instabilities in Microscaling Formats (Jun 2025) documents why naive MX-format training diverges — block-exponent overflow, quantization-noise floor effects late in training, and, critically, Adam’s momentum and second-moment accumulators compounding quantization error over iterations rather than averaging it out. That last paper is the citation for why the field converged on elaborate multi-technique recipes rather than “just quantize and go.”

The bottom line for a 10T-parameter design: NVFP4’s best public evidence is two orders of magnitude below frontier scale, the technique stack is intricate enough that a single missed component measurably degrades convergence per the paper’s own ablations, and the field disagrees — as of early-to-mid 2026 — on whether stochastic rounding or deterministic RTN is correct for the backward pass. This is not a settled question dressed up as one.

DeepSeek-V4’s FP4: a memory play, not a compute play — and a live gap

DeepSeek-V4 — two variants, V4-Pro at 1.6T total/49B active/33T tokens and V4-Flash at 284B total/13B active/32T tokens — is the first frontier model to publicly commit to FP4 for MoE expert weights. But the report’s own text undercuts the headline reading (V4 technical report, Jun 2026). It states explicitly that “peak FLOPs for FP4×FP8 operations are currently the same as FP8×FP8 on existing hardware” — the FP4 choice for experts is a memory-footprint optimization (roughly halving expert-weight storage versus FP8), not a compute-throughput win, on the hardware the report describes. KV cache uses a mixed format — BF16 for the RoPE dimensions specifically, FP8 for the rest — cutting KV cache size roughly 2× versus pure BF16. Attention inside the “lightning indexer” (the sparse-attention selection mechanism; Ch4 covers the CSA/HCA architecture) runs in FP4 explicitly to accelerate the indexer at million-token context.

The load-bearing caveat: the “FP4 Quantization-Aware Training” applied to MoE expert weights and the indexer’s QK path is described in Section 5.2.1, framed as part of the post-training stage — the report does not disclose the numeric recipe used for the primary 32–33T-token pretraining run itself. Anyone repeating “DeepSeek does full FP4 pretraining” should be corrected to: DeepSeek stores, serves, and QAT-refines expert weights in FP4; the base pretraining precision is undisclosed as of the June 2026 report. The serving-side FP4 lineage now runs unbroken across two labs and three generations — K2 Thinking’s INT4 QAT (Nov 2025) → K2.5’s native INT4 (Jan 2026) → V4’s FP4 expert QAT (Jun 2026) → the NVFP4-RL rollout/serving recipe (Jul 10, 2026, below) → and, three days past the July 13 snapshot, Kimi K3 launching at 2.8T with MXFP4 weights and MXFP8 activations in production serving (launch-coverage numbers until the K3 report lands). Four-bit weights at inference/rollout time is now simply how frontier-scale MoEs ship; the open question the verdict below turns on remains exclusively the pretraining-time backward path. Separately, the report states Muon’s Newton-Schulz orthogonalization iterations “remain stable when computed with BF16 matrix multiplications” — a specific, checkable numerics claim relevant to Ch9’s optimizer discussion — and MoE gradient synchronization across ranks uses stochastic rounding when casting down to BF16 for the all-reduce/all-gather. SR, in other words, is applied not just at FP4/FP8 quantization boundaries but at the BF16 communication-compression boundary too.

Where FP32 islands persist, and why the router is non-negotiable

Every source surveyed here converges on the same short list of precision-protected components, and the reasoning is consistent enough to treat as settled practice rather than caution:

  • Router/gating logits: FP32 is the default in Megatron-Core’s MoE implementation specifically because “router logits computation should use FP32 instead of BF16 to enhance numerical stability,” with softmax/sigmoid over router logits forced to FP32 even when the rest of the model runs BF16/FP8 — a convention consistent across Megatron-LM and DeepSpeed GitHub threads from 2025–2026. Direct empirical support comes from an FP8-RL ablation (paper, Jan 2026; lmsys.org writeup): FP8 routers produce materially higher KL-divergence mismatch in expert-selection distributions than BF16 routers, while BF16→FP32 gives only marginal further improvement — implying BF16 is the real numerical floor for routing and FP32 buys little extra beyond it. A closed GitHub issue is the concrete cautionary tale: applying a naive --fp8-format hybrid recipe to Mixtral-8x7B without protecting the router landed at val loss 7.71 (PPL 2222) versus BF16’s 7.25 (PPL 1410) — a ~57% perplexity blowup from an under-specified MoE FP8 config (Megatron-LM issue #1152). This is the single clearest MoE-specific numerics failure mode in this whole literature, and it is exactly the trap a 10T/200B-active design must not walk into.
  • Attention probabilities / softmax: kept in high precision (BF16 minimum, often FP32 internally) uniformly across DeepSeek V3, MXFP8, and Nemotron-H — none of the production recipes surveyed quantize the softmax operator itself, only its Q/K/V GEMM inputs.
  • Residual/accumulation stream: “To FP8 and Back Again” explicitly keeps residual connections in FP32; the general pattern is that the additive backbone of a transformer is treated as more precision-sensitive than the multiplicative GEMM operators feeding into it.
  • Master weights and optimizer states: universally BF16/FP32 across every recipe surveyed — DeepSeek V3, Nemotron-H, the NVFP4 paper, the MXFP8 paper. Nobody quantizes optimizer state below BF16 for pretraining.

Stochastic rounding follows one consistent pattern across every recipe that uses it: restricted to gradient tensors only, never applied to weights or activations in the forward pass. This matches “To FP8 and Back Again”’s finding that SR helps unbiased gradient estimation for backprop but actively hurts forward-pass convergence, where round-to-nearest has lower quantization error.

Stochastic rounding hardware, and the determinism tax

Stochastic rounding is now a real hardware primitive on Blackwell, not a software convention. PTX cvt.rs on B200/B300 (sm_100a/sm_103a) performs hardware RNG-driven stochastic rounding for FP32→{FP16, BF16, FP8-E5M2/E4M3, FP6, FP4-E2M1} conversions, including a 4-wide cvt.rs.satfinite.e2m1x4.f32 variant for packed NVFP4 output, consuming 13 bits of random source for FP16 and 16 bits for BF16 and the 8-bit formats (Limited-Precision Stochastic Rounding, Mar 2026, paper with code). Because the random source itself is limited-precision, realized rounding probabilities diverge slightly from mathematically exact SR — a second-order bias effect the same paper characterizes formally. No source we found confirms or denies an equivalent hardware SR primitive on TPU or Trainium; treat that as an open question specific to NVIDIA silicon.

Determinism is a distinct, less-quantified cost. NCCL 2.27 computes AllReduce/AllGather/ReduceScatter reductions with FP32 accumulators (FP16 specifically for FP8 tensors over NVLink Switch/NVLS) to improve determinism (NCCL 2.27 blog, 2026) — but true bitwise reproducibility across runs still requires pinning NCCL_ALGO/NCCL_PROTO, because floating-point sum is non-associative and algorithm choice changes reduction order, plus a verified fixed collective ordering. DeepSeek-V4 separately claims “deterministic kernel implementations with bitwise reproducibility across training/inference” without detailing the throughput cost — the report describes deterministic attention backward (per-SM accumulation buffers replacing atomic-add), deterministic MoE backward (per-rank token-order preprocessing), and a deterministic mHC matmul reduction, all opt-in against a fast-math-disabled-by-default, IEEE-754-compliant baseline (V4 report §Training Infra, Jun 2026). One misattribution to avoid: Thinking Machines Lab’s widely discussed “Defeating Nondeterminism in LLM Inference” work is scoped to inference batch-invariance, not training, and is frequently over-cited in training-determinism discussions (Simon Willison’s summary, Sept 2025). The actual training-time determinism tax remains a cost most labs pay only for targeted debugging replays, not production pretraining, given known interactions between fixed collective algorithms and communication-performance tuning.

Precision-aware scaling laws: directional, not load-bearing

Kumar et al.’s Scaling Laws for Precision (Nov 2024, ICLR 2025) is the most-cited general theory here. It proposes that training in lower precision effectively shrinks a model’s usable “effective parameter count,” and separately that post-training-quantization degradation increases with the amount of pretraining data seen — beyond some critical data budget, additional pretraining tokens make a model destined for later quantization actively worse off. The fitted law suggests a 7–8 bit compute-optimal precision band for pretraining. The load-bearing caveat: the fit covers ≤1.7B parameters and ≤26B tokens across 465 runs — nobody has published a re-derivation of its coefficients within two orders of magnitude of a 10T-parameter, tens-of-trillions-of-token run. Every large-scale empirical result surveyed above (DeepSeek V3’s 671B/~1T-comparison-token FP8 run, NVIDIA’s 12B/10T-token NVFP4 run, MXFP8’s 8B/15T-token run) reports near-zero loss gaps at 8-bit and even 4-bit — broadly consistent with Kumar’s claim that 7-8 bits sits comfortably above the compute-optimal-precision floor. But none of these are true post-hoc-quantization setups (they are precision-matched throughout training), so none test Kumar’s more provocative claim directly. Treat the qualitative direction — more data and lower target precision genuinely interact — as trustworthy, and the specific numeric coefficients as not predictive at 10T-parameter scale.

Hardware ground truth: GB200 NVL72, reconciled

Every FLOPS-denominated number here and in the chapters that lean on this one (Ch17, Ch18, Ch23) rests on getting one distinction right, because public sources routinely blur it: the liquid-cooled, 1,200W GB200 NVL72 SKU and the air-cooled, 1,000W HGX B200 SKU are the same reticle-limited Blackwell die (208B transistors, TSMC 4NP) at different power envelopes and cooling — and their published peak numbers differ (NVIDIA “Blackwell” datasheet #3384703, Dec 2024). NVIDIA’s own side-by-side table carries the critical footnote “all Tensor Core numbers except FP64 with sparsity” — every Tensor Core row is a 2:4-structured-sparse figure, and dense is exactly half:

Three independent primary or near-primary sources cross-validate this table exactly: NVIDIA’s datasheet PDF, live-fetched HTML from NVIDIA’s GB200 NVL72 and DGX B200 product pages, and Glenn K. Lockwood’s independently compiled technical wiki (Apr 2026). The correction pattern is worth noticing: a clean, uniform +11.1% on every tensor-core dense precision (5000/4500 = 2500/2250 = 10000/9000 = 10/9 exactly), but only +6.7% on the non-tensor-core FP32 vector path and +8.1% on FP64 — meaning the two SKUs’ published tensor-core numbers are independently chosen round marketing figures rather than a single clock multiplier applied uniformly, and NVIDIA does not publish an official clock speed for either SKU.

The conflation is easy to fall into because good sources fall into it. The JAX Scaling Book (cited in Ch18) uses the HGX B200 dense figures — bf16=2.3e15, fp8=4.5e15, fp4=9.0e15 FLOPs/s/chip — under an undifferentiated “B200” label, while its own Quiz Question 6 separately reasons from the GB200-class 80 TFLOPS FP32 figure without flagging the SKU switch. Published MFU claims inherit the error too: a Megatron-Core MoE technical report (paper, Mar 2026) reports 1,048 TFLOPS/GPU sustained for DeepSeek-V3-685B-scale training on GB200, which against the wrong (HGX B200) 2,250-TFLOPS BF16 denominator reads as 46.6% MFU. The SKU correction alone brings that to 41.9% — and the report’s own Table 11 then resolves the remaining ambiguity by marking the measurement as MXFP8, making the honest figure 21.0% against the 5,000-TFLOPS FP8/MXFP8 dense peak. Ch17 carries the corrected 21.0%. The general rule: check any MFU number quoted against a “B200” or “GB200” denominator against this table before trusting it.

The achievable-fraction reality: marketing peaks versus MoE training shapes

The more consequential finding for a 200B-active MoE design is not which SKU’s peak is correct, but how far actual training GEMMs fall short of any peak. Independent microbenchmarking of a standalone B200 at a realistic GEMM tile shape (m64n8k16) measured 3,850.6 TFLOPS FP8 — 96.3% of the paper’s clock-derived ~4.0-PFLOPS baseline, which itself sits below either SKU’s datasheet number (against HGX B200’s 4,500 spec it is ~86%) — with FP6 at 96.0%, FP4 at 96.2%, BF16 at 96.3%, and INT8 at 98.2% (Microbenchmarking NVIDIA’s Blackwell Architecture, Dec 2025). The same paper found B200 delivers a consistent ~1.27× speedup over H200 across precisions at the raw tensor-core level. In other words, most of the “Blackwell is 2-4× faster” headline comes from the new narrower dtypes (FP4/FP6 have no fast path on Hopper) and from memory bandwidth and capacity gains — not from Blackwell tensor cores being dramatically faster than Hopper’s at a fixed dtype.

That 96%+ efficiency is for isolated, well-shaped GEMM tiles. MoE training GEMM shapes are nowhere close. DeepGEMM reports up to 1,550 TFLOPS on H800 for well-shaped problems, but a vLLM benchmark at a realistic per-expert training shape — M=128, N=4096, K=7168, the small-M grouped-GEMM slice typical of a single expert’s token batch — measured only 190.49 TFLOPS for CUTLASS on H800 (vLLM PR #13917, 2025). This is the crux of why sparse-TFLOPS marketing numbers are close to irrelevant for MoE pretraining planning: a 10T-total/200B-active run’s realized TFLOPS/GPU will be dominated by per-expert GEMM shape efficiency — which scales with expert capacity and batch size, not peak tensor-core throughput — far more than by which precision format’s peak appears on a spec sheet. Ch17’s kernel-level work on B200 NVFP4 grouped GEMM corroborates this directly: a naive implementation runs at 238µs geomean across representative expert shapes, a hand-optimized persistent-kernel version reaches 23.8µs (~10×), and even that optimized kernel only achieves ~50% average tensor-core utilization and 12.4% SM occupancy, because TMEM (the accumulator plus scale-factor tensor memory) fits only one CTA per SM at small M — a hardware constraint precision format choice does not fix.

Field MFU data teaches the same lesson from a different angle: on H100, FP8 MFU runs consistently lower than BF16 MFU as a percentage of each format’s own peak — 39.5% versus 54% on GPT-3-175B across 128×H100, 35.5–38.1% versus 53.7–54.5% on Llama3-70B, ~43% versus ~54% on Llama3-405B (per SemiAnalysis’s “H100 vs GB200 NVL72 Training Benchmarks”, 2025, partially paywalled). This does not contradict FP8’s value — it still wins on absolute tokens/s and $/token because its peak is roughly 2× higher — but it directly rebuts any planning heuristic that assumes FP8 achieves the same utilization fraction as BF16. The same source notes GB200-generation FP8 MFU improved from 29.5% to 39.5% “through software gains alone” over an unspecified maturation window — roughly a third of Blackwell’s realized FP8 throughput gain over its early software state came from kernel and scheduling maturity, not hardware, a caution against reading any July-2026 MFU snapshot as a hardware ceiling. One figure to quarantine: the specific “38-46% sustained FP8 training MFU on NVL72” number sometimes attributed to NVIDIA could not be traced to any retrievable primary source — treat it as unconfirmed pending a located citation, not an official NVIDIA number.

The verdict: what to bet the run on in July 2026

For a 10T-total/200B-active MoE on ~100,000 GB200 GPUs:

Bet the run on:

  • FP8, DeepSeek-V3-style blockwise (1×128 activation / 128×128 weight, uniform E4M3) or NVIDIA’s TE blockwise recipe, as the default GEMM format across attention QKV/output projections and MoE expert FFNs — the only recipe with published evidence at genuinely frontier scale, corroborated independently at 8B/15T tokens by MXFP8 and Nemotron-H, landing consistently under 0.5-1% loss gap across every reproduction.
  • On GB200/Blackwell specifically, prefer MXFP8 over legacy per-tensor/delayed FP8 where the integration gap allows: it needs no first/last-N-layers BF16 carve-out, gives a real (not just theoretical) throughput win corroborated by both TorchTitan and Nebius at 256×B200, and is architecturally the native Blackwell path. Budget explicitly for the torch.compile+TP integration gap both groups hit on dense layers — this needs custom kernel work, not a drop-in flag, to reach 100% of layers rather than expert GEMMs only.
  • Router/gate logits and softmax in FP32, non-negotiable. Every piece of evidence — Megatron’s own defaults, the closed GitHub issue’s 57%-perplexity-blowup case, the FP8-RL routing-KL ablation — says this is cheap insurance against a catastrophic, hard-to-diagnose failure mode, and router GEMMs are tiny relative to expert FFNs at MoE scale, so the cost is negligible.
  • Master weights, optimizer states, residual stream, attention softmax in BF16/FP32, following universal practice across every recipe surveyed.
  • Stochastic rounding restricted to gradient tensors at FP8/FP4 quantization boundaries and to the BF16-compression step in gradient all-reduce, using Blackwell’s native cvt.rs where the format pair is supported — this is now near-free hardware, not a software tax.
  • A late-training precision upgrade as scheduled insurance: switching some fraction of the final 15-20% of tokens to BF16 recovers most of the accumulated low-precision loss gap for a small FLOP cost, and every paper surveyed here finds precision-sensitivity peaks during LR decay.

Only pilot, not bet the full run on:

  • NVFP4 (or any FP4 variant) for the dense/attention path of the pretraining GEMMs. The best public evidence is 12B/10T tokens — two orders of magnitude below a 10T-parameter model — and the field has not converged on stochastic-rounding versus deterministic-RTN for the backward pass, an active disagreement between concurrently publishing groups as of mid-2026. One important boundary on this verdict, dated three days before the evidence window closed: NVFP4 is no longer monolithically experimental. On July 10, 2026, humans& (with NVIDIA and RadixArk) published the first open, stable, hardware-native NVFP4 RL recipe (humansand.ai/blog/nvfp4-rl): per-token FP32 activation scales (following Cursor’s Composer 2 report), a “dequantized backward” that keeps backward GEMMs in BF16 but computes them on the exact dequantized forward operands to close the chain-rule mismatch, four-over-six adaptive block scaling applied to weights as well as activations, selective BF16 for final layers and shared experts, and a bit-exact trainer/sampler contract shipped in TransformerEngine and FlashInfer — achieving reward curves indistinguishable from BF16 at Qwen3-30B-A3B scale, plus production NVFP4 online serving in SGLang. Note carefully what that does and does not change: the backward GEMMs still run in BF16, so this validates NVFP4 in the rollout/serving/forward loop, not FP4 gradient compute at pretraining scale — but the per-token-scale, 4/6, and bit-exact-contract machinery is exactly what a 10T/200B program’s precision stack would inherit, and the forward-quantized/backward-dequantized asymmetry is a named template the MXFP8 pilot path above should borrow.
  • FP4 for MoE expert weights specifically, following DeepSeek-V4’s disclosed direction, but note it is a memory play, not a compute-speed play, on current hardware, and DeepSeek applies it as post-training QAT rather than as the pretraining-time format. A reasonable pilot: FP4 for expert-weight storage/QAT refinement after an FP8-pretrained base, not as the from-scratch pretraining format for a first frontier run.
  • FP8 inside the attention softmax/probability computation itself. FA3/FA4’s FP8 forward paths are validated for inference-grade accuracy, not large-scale training dynamics, and FOG’s fully-FP8-attention training result required architectural changes beyond a kernel swap. Pilot with FOG-style mitigations built in from the start.
  • Treat Kimi K2’s counter-example as a genuine, unresolved data point rather than noise — a team training a genuine trillion-parameter-class MoE explicitly declined FP8 pretraining compute after a stability study, and the specifics of why were never published.

Implications for the 10T/200B run

The precision decision that most directly threatens a $2B run is not the FP8-versus-MXFP8-versus-NVFP4 choice — it is getting the MoE-specific numerics wrong. An under-specified router precision, or a naive recipe applied uniformly across dense and expert layers without DeepSeek-V3’s or Nemotron-H’s carve-outs, is the documented path to a 57%-perplexity-class blowup, not a subtle degradation. The router-FP32 rule, the BF16 combine-direction convention, and the first/last-N-layers BF16 sandwich are all cheap, well-evidenced, and should be treated as fixed requirements rather than tunable hyperparameters. Second, the GB200 ceiling used throughout (Ch18, Ch23) must be the reconciled dense figures in the table above — 5,000 TFLOPS/GPU dense FP8, 2,500 dense BF16, 10,000 dense NVFP4 — not the HGX B200 numbers that have already contaminated the JAX Scaling Book’s tables and the Megatron-Core MFU arithmetic. Third, and most consequential for cost modeling: the precision format chosen matters less to realized $/token than the small-M MoE grouped-GEMM shape-efficiency problem documented here and in Ch17 — a 200B-active design with narrow per-expert batches will leave far more throughput on the table from kernel and scheduling immaturity than from choosing FP8 over MXFP8, or MXFP8 over NVFP4. Read Ch18’s parallelism math and Ch23’s MFU and cost derivations with this chapter’s ceiling numbers and its shape-efficiency caveat both in hand.
