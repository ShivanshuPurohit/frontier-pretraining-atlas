Every system examined in this book has been validated somewhere between one and three orders of magnitude below the scale this chapter assumes. DeepSeek-V3 proved the FP8-blockwise-plus-DualPipe recipe at 2,048 GPUs; Kimi K2 proved MuonClip at 1.04T total parameters; Meta proved 90%+ goodput at 16,384 GPUs; OpenAI’s MRC has a stated theoretical target of 131,072 GPUs but production evidence at 75,000. None of that is a validated recipe for 10T total / 200B active on 100,000 GB200s — it is the nearest available evidence, and the distance between “nearest available evidence” and “the plan” is this chapter. What follows is organized by the seven systems a team actually has to stand up, in each case naming the gap, why it binds, the evidence pointer, whether to build or adopt, a rough effort class, and — the operative question for a team that has to start now — how many months of lead time it needs before the first training step.

The Training Codebase

Megatron-Core is the only framework with a primary-source MoE-at-scale paper current as of this writing — Scalable Training of Mixture-of-Experts Models with Megatron Core [PAPER, 03/2026] reports 1,233 TFLOPS/GPU (GB300) and 1,048 TFLOPS/GPU (GB200) for a DeepSeek-V3-685B-class model, and 974/919 TFLOPS/GPU for Qwen3-235B, both under force-balanced routing — and Ch17’s framework survey concludes it should be the base for a build at this scale. The base is not the gap. The gap is the modification list that stands between “Megatron-Core runs DeepSeek-V3-685B at reasonable MFU” and “Megatron-Core runs a 10T/200B model at 100,000 GPUs without EP dispatch eating the run.”

The single largest binding item is EP all-to-all overlap correctness and efficiency. Unoptimized EP all-to-all consumes 20–60% of total training time depending on whether the expert-parallel group stays inside an NVLink domain (~20%, DeepSeek-V3-shaped EP64 on GB200 NVL72) or crosses nodes (40–60%, the same config on H100 across 8 nodes) Scalable Training of Mixture-of-Experts Models with Megatron Core [PAPER, 03/2026]. At 100,000 GPUs a 10T/200B model’s EP group will by construction span far more than one NVL72 domain (Ch16 derives the parallelism layout in full), which puts the run in the expensive regime by default unless the dispatch kernel is genuinely tuned for the topology. Megatron-Core now ships two production dispatcher backends — DeepEP (Hopper-native, cross-node, MIT-licensed) and HybridEP (NVIDIA’s own kernel, tuned for GB200/GB300 Multi-Node NVLink, claimed comparable-or-higher bandwidth at lower SM usage) — confirmed directly in fused_a2a.py in the Megatron-LM repository [CODE, github.com/NVIDIA/Megatron-LM, 2026]. Adopt both and validate the crossover point for the reader’s specific expert width and EP degree; do not assume either backend’s published numbers, which were measured at DeepSeek-V3/Qwen3 scale, transfer linearly to a 50x-sparser, 200B-active model.

MoE Parallel Folding — the architectural fix that decouples attention parallelism (TP×CP×DP×PP) from expert parallelism (ETP×EP×EDP×PP), breaking the old EP ≤ DP constraint — is already merged into megatron/core’s process-group management MoE Parallel Folding [PAPER, 04/2025]; this is load-bearing infrastructure, not a research proposal, and should be adopted rather than rebuilt. Two more recent, less-publicized production fixes close the remaining gap to full CUDA-Graph coverage for dropless MoE: ECHO (Elastic Cloning for Hot Experts, dynamically clones overloaded experts to spare slots via bin-packing, merged February 2026) and Paged Stashing (replaces per-layer worst-case activation buffers with one shared buffer plus paged storage, cutting CUDA-Graph memory from O(layers×worst-case) to O(worst-case+actual-total)) [CODE, github.com/NVIDIA/Megatron-LM, PR #2368/#2690]. Both are already merged into dev — adopt rather than rebuild — but the framework’s own Q2-2026 roadmap still lists “sync-free, full-iteration CUDA-Graph MoE training” under long-term priorities [ROADMAP, github.com/NVIDIA/Megatron-LM/issues/4815], meaning full end-to-end integration is not yet default/stable as of this writing; budget validation time before treating either as a turnkey flag.

Precision is a build item, not an adopt item, if the goal is MXFP8 across the full model. TorchTitan’s own 671B-class, 256×B200 reproduction got +41.0% throughput (651→918 tok/s) combining MXFP8 grouped-GEMM with DeepEP overlap — but explicitly could not apply MXFP8 to the dense (non-expert) linear layers because of a torch.compile+tensor-parallel incompatibility, so the 41% figure is expert-GEMM-only pytorch.org/blog, “Enabling up to 41% faster pre-training… MXFP8 and DeepEP” [OFFICIAL-BLOG, 03/2026, updated 05/2026]. Getting MXFP8 across 100% of layers — the version of the recipe Ch8 recommends betting the run on for GB200 — requires closing that torch.compile+TP gap, which is genuine kernel-integration work, not a config flag. Router and gate logits stay FP32; this is the one item on the list that costs nothing and is unambiguous — a naive --fp8-format hybrid MoE config with an unprotected router landed at val loss 7.71 (PPL 2222) vs a BF16 baseline’s 7.25 (PPL 1410) on Mixtral-8x7B, a 57% perplexity blowup traced directly to router precision [CODE, github.com/NVIDIA/Megatron-LM/issues/1152]. Full precision recipe and the FP8/MXFP8/NVFP4 verdict are Ch8’s territory; here the point is narrower: the recipe Ch8 recommends is not yet a drop-in flag in the framework the team will actually run.

The optimizer is the largest open validation gap in the entire codebase list, not primarily an engineering one. Distributed Newton-Schulz for Muon is now solved-but-nontrivial engineering — NVIDIA’s Megatron-Core “Emerging-Optimizers” library ships three sharding modes (duplicated/distributed/blockwise NS) and measures Muon within 1–3% of AdamW throughput on GB300 NVL72 for Kimi-K2-shaped and Qwen3-30B-A3B-shaped configs [developer.nvidia.com, Megatron Core Emerging-Optimizers] [OFFICIAL-BLOG, 2026] — but no optimizer, Muon or AdamW, has been publicly validated on a >100B-active-parameter MoE. DeepSeek-V4-Pro (49B active), Kimi K2 (32.6B active), and GLM-4.6 (32B active) are the largest confirmed data points; Llama 4 Behemoth targeted 288B active and never shipped. A 200B-active design is genuinely unprecedented optimizer territory, and this gap does not close by adopting a library — it closes by running the de-risking ladder Ch7 specifies and the next section of this chapter restates.

Checkpointing at this parameter count is a systems-build item with no off-the-shelf equivalent inside Megatron-Core today. NVIDIA’s own resiliency extension (nvrx) provides fault-tolerance and async/local checkpointing primitives but is, in its own documentation’s words, “infrastructure plumbing more than a benchmarked research contribution” [CODE, github.com/NVIDIA/nvidia-resiliency-ext] — it is not, out of the box, ByteRobust-grade in-memory peer replication with the measured 0.01–0.04s blocking stall ByteDance reports at 9,600–16,384-GPU scale ByteRobust [PAPER, 09/2025]. This item is covered in full under the Reliability Layer below, since it is fundamentally a checkpointing/fault-tolerance build, but it belongs on the codebase gap list too because it has to be built into the training loop, not bolted on afterward.

Finally, the framework’s own open issue tracker is a pre-flight test list, not background noise. As of this research, NCCL collective-order deadlocks in EP all-to-all overlap on uneven pipeline layouts are open (#5749, #1810), HybridEP RDMA queue-pair depth overflow at large batch×seqlen was found and fixed (#3999), and fault-tolerance/local-checkpoint corruption causing infinite restart loops under ft_launcher is open (#5281) [CODE, github.com/NVIDIA/Megatron-LM, verified 07/2026]. These are the concrete failure modes a 100,000-GPU shakedown run is most likely to hit first, and each is a targeted regression test to write before committing the schedule, not a surprise to discover during it.

The Scaling-Laws Program

Ch6 and Ch7 lay out the ladder recipe and its cost; the gap this section names is organizational, not technical — the ladder has to be budgeted and run before the architecture, optimizer, and precision recipe are frozen, and every source in this corpus that discloses numbers treats it as a large, non-optional line item rather than an afterthought. HuggingFace’s SmolLM3 ablations cost 161,280 GPU-hours against a final run of roughly 276,000 GPU-hours — about 58% [SmolLM3 ablations, HuggingFace] [CREDIBLE-HEARSAY, 2025]. Budgeting 30–60% of final-run compute for law-fitting and ablation is the empirical norm at labs that publish, not an edge case.

Three specific technical gaps sit inside that budget. First, muP/muTransfer is solid for width transfer and unreliable alone for depth, batch size, and training-horizon transfer, and is genuinely ambiguous for MoE fan-in (total vs. active parameters) — a live disagreement, with a Qwen-affiliated paper arguing muTransfer underperforms a directly-fitted LR(N,D) law once QK-Norm is present [arXiv:2601.05049] [PAPER, 01/2026] against Essential AI’s claim that muP transfer held for both LR and weight decay at 3.7B [arXiv:2505.02222] [PAPER]. A team building a 10T/200B host needs its own resolution of this disagreement at its own architecture before committing, not an import of either paper’s conclusion. Second, critical batch size is not well-predicted by the classical gradient-noise-scale estimator at LLM scale — Ai2’s “Critical Batch Size Revisited” found the noise-scale proxy off by orders of magnitude for OLMo 7B, and CBS instead has to be measured directly via branched short runs [arXiv:2505.23971] [PAPER, 05/2025]; skipping this and trusting a formula is a specific, named way to mis-budget the batch-size schedule. Third, MoE-specific scaling is now its own joint law rather than a dense law with a sparsity correction bolted on: the Ling team’s Efficiency Leverage law (300+ models to 28B) and the “comprehensive MoE law” (446 experiments) both find granularity and shared-expert ratio have a non-monotonic optimum that is roughly scale-independent, while activation ratio and efficiency leverage follow clean power laws that get sparser as N grows arXiv:2507.17702 [PAPER, 07/2025]; arXiv:2509.23678 [PAPER]. Ch5’s strawman spec already builds on this; the gap is running the granularity/shared-expert/activation-ratio sweep at the reader’s own d_model and expert width rather than importing Ant Group’s fitted optimum wholesale, since both source papers stop several orders of magnitude of compute short of a 10T/200B run (Ch5, Open Questions).

Two further items are cautions rather than build items, but belong on the checklist because they are easy to get wrong under schedule pressure. Precision-aware scaling laws disagree with each other on the numeric optimum — Kumar et al. put compute-optimal precision at roughly 7–8 bits, a competing Chinese-lab law (“Capybara”) puts the cost-optimal range at 4–8 bits with a critical-data-size effect beyond which more tokens actively hurt a low-precision model [arXiv:2411.04330] [PAPER, 11/2024]; [arXiv:2501.02423] [PAPER] — and both are fit at ≤1.7B params / ≤26B tokens, two-plus orders of magnitude below the target run; treat the qualitative direction as trustworthy and the specific bit-width coefficients as not (Ch8 reaches the same conclusion independently). Downstream-task scaling laws are unreliable far more often than pretraining-loss laws — an EMNLP-2025 meta-analysis found only 39% of published downstream scaling-law fits extrapolate reliably, and the Gemstones suite (4,000+ checkpoints, 10T+ cumulative tokens) shows scaling-law prescriptions are highly sensitive to which checkpoints and LR schedule they’re fit on [arXiv:2507.00885] [PAPER]; [arXiv:2502.06857] [PAPER]. A law fit once on pretraining loss and trusted for downstream capability commitments is a specific, documented failure mode, not a hypothetical one.

The sequencing implication is unambiguous: this program gates the architecture spec (Ch5), the optimizer choice (Ch7), and the precision recipe (Ch8), all of which need to be frozen well before the main run’s procurement and scheduling commitments are made. It has to start first, not run in parallel with codebase or cluster work.

The Data Factory

Ch10, Ch11, and Ch12 own the deep evidence on the crawl/curation/annotation/mixture stack; the gap-list question here is narrower — what has to be built, not what the corpus already establishes about the state of the art. Three items recur as genuine build gaps rather than adopt-and-configure items. First, the annotation/taxonomy layer that makes runtime curriculum control possible: Essential-Web v1.0 demonstrates the pattern (24T tokens, every document annotated with a 12-category taxonomy via a distilled 0.5B annotator model claimed within 3% agreement of a 32B-instruct judge) Essential-Web v1.0 [PAPER, 2025], but this is a pattern to reproduce against the reader’s own corpus and taxonomy, not a shared artifact to import — the annotator model itself has to be trained and validated on the specific document mix the run will draw from. Second, and this is the sharpest genuinely-unfilled gap found anywhere in the input-adjacent literature: no mainstream OSS dataloader — not Megatron-Core’s BlendedDataset, not Megatron-Energon, not MosaicML Streaming, not Grain — exposes a native filter/reweight-by-per-example-metadata API at load time. Every system applies the taxonomy filter once, upstream, producing separate per-category shard files, then blends at the source level (Ch11, Findings §17). For a 10T-run wanting genuinely adaptive, fine-grained-taxonomy-conditioned curricula rather than coarse pre-materialized-category blending, this is a build-it-yourself system, and the closest published architecture — ByteDance’s MegaScale-Data Planner — auto-scales mixing ratios at source granularity, not per-example predicates MegaScale-Data [PAPER, EuroSys 2026], so even the best available reference implementation would need extending, not just adopting. Third, the midtraining/anneal-phase mixture staging Ch12 specifies as a curriculum needs its filtered, weighted shard sets materialized and validated well ahead of the phase transition — this is a data-factory deliverable with a hard dependency on the curriculum plan being frozen, which in turn depends on the scaling-laws program above.

The remaining data-factory items — own-crawler infrastructure, CC-decline mitigation, licensing economics, dedup/classifier pipelines — are Ch10/Ch11’s core content and are adopt-grade rather than novel-build items once a curation stack is chosen; the binding constraint the reader should track from this chapter’s vantage point is lead time. Building or substantially extending a crawler, plus the legal/compliance review that accompanies it, is a 12-month-plus item independent of any GPU procurement timeline, and it has to start in parallel with the scaling-laws program, not after architecture is frozen, because the token-budget math (Ch10’s stock-vs-30-50T-budget accounting) constrains what architecture and training-token count are even feasible.

The Input Pipeline

Ch13 owns the full loader-requirements matrix; the headline finding worth restating here is that raw token ingestion is nowhere near the binding constraint. The compute ceiling for a 200B-active MoE caps aggregate ingestion at roughly 75–76M tokens/s cluster-wide, which at 4 bytes/token (int32, any modern >64K-vocab tokenizer) is only ~300MB/s aggregate — three to four orders of magnitude below what a single NVMe drive delivers (Ch11, Hard Numbers). The gap list here is therefore not “make the dataloader faster”; it is a short set of specific, evidenced failure modes that a naive deployment will hit.

Sequence packing is a solved problem and should simply be adopted: Best-fit Packing achieves 0.00063–0.0028% additional sequences versus naive concatenation at 2k–8k context — functionally zero waste — and OLMo-core’s Optimized-BFD gets packing time to O(N log L) with a 60% runtime reduction versus naive BFD at 1B-document scale Best-fit Packing [PAPER, ACL 2024]; [olmo-core.readthedocs.io] [OFFICIAL-DOCS]. Intra-document masking via cu_seqlens/varlen is likewise a first-class, adopt-grade code path: Megatron-Core’s GPTDatasetConfig.inter_document_masking emits cu_seqlens directly from the dataloader, and FlashAttention-4’s Blackwell-native backend is co-designed with exactly this load pattern in mind (Ch11 Findings §14; Ch15 covers the kernel side in full).

The genuine gap is multi-source workload imbalance, and it is quantified, not speculative: ByteDance’s MegaScale-Data measured per-microbatch FLOP imbalance up to 3.2× (image) and 6.9× (full sequence) across 8 ranks purely from non-uniform per-rank sample-length distribution in a multi-source job, invisible to a naive samples/sec metric MegaScale-Data [PAPER]. A 10T-run with a heterogeneous source mix (web, code, math, multilingual, long-context) and expert-parallel sharding is exactly the configuration this failure mode targets — an unlucky microbatch composition can stall an entire pipeline stage while other ranks idle, and no mainstream OSS loader (Megatron-Core, Streaming, Grain) implements the actor-model redesign MegaScale-Data uses to fix it. This is a build gap inherited from the data-factory section above, since fixing it requires either adopting and extending MegaScale-Data’s unreleased architecture or building an equivalent source-loader/data-constructor disaggregation.

Two smaller, cheap-to-fix items round out the list. Dataloader cold start can genuinely stall a 100,000-GPU job: Megatron-Core’s own documentation flags that index-map construction for large blends “can take several minutes and leave all GPUs idle,” mitigated by tools/prepare_cache.py and the --dataloader-fast-cache-load/--dataloader-defer-npy-index-mmap flags (Ch11 Findings §1) — cheap, adopt-grade, but easy to miss if nobody runs the offline cache build ahead of a billing-metered job start. And object-storage support is already native: Megatron-Core’s object_storage_utils.py supports direct S3/msc:// streaming for .bin/.idx shards, relevant for a 10T-run’s cold/archival tier (Ch11 Findings §1) — adopt, do not rebuild.

Cluster and Network

Ch14 and Ch18 carry the fabric and topology evidence in full; the gap this chapter names is that GB200 NVL72’s reliability and diagnostic maturity are, as of the most recent primary field data available in this corpus, not yet where a synchronous mega-training run needs them to be. SemiAnalysis’s field report found GB200 NVL72’s mean-time-between-interruption running 1,000–3,000 GPU-days versus H100’s 2,000–5,000 — GB200 racks fail roughly twice as often per GPU-day as H100 servers, even at the most-advanced-operator end — and the failure blast radius is the entire 72-GPU rack for a switch-tray or backplane-cartridge swap, versus 8 GPUs for an HGX server, because the NVLink domain is shared across the whole rack (Ch14; SemiAnalysis, “H100 vs GB200 NVL72 Training Benchmarks” [CREDIBLE-HEARSAY, 08/2025]). Diagnostic tooling — Nvloom, multi-node dcgmi diag, ubergemm — is explicitly described by operators as “behind and sub-optimal” for backplane-specific fault localization, and the Paladin HD copper connectors are rated for at most 200 mating cycles before requiring discard, a hard physical limit on how many times a given pairing can be serviced (same source). As of that report, no large-scale synchronous training run had completed on GB200 NVL72 at any frontier lab or CSP.

The direct consequence for a 100,000-GB200 procurement is that stricter-than-vendor-default ODM/OEM acceptance testing on every rack before it enters the fleet is not optional hygiene — it is SemiAnalysis’s own top recommendation, and it is the single highest-leverage gap-closing action available before the run starts, because every other reliability number in this section assumes the fleet has already survived burn-in. Budget for a first-3-to-6-month “tribal knowledge” tax on backplane/switch-tray fault diagnosis and staff a dedicated NVL72 hardware-diagnostics team ahead of the run, not reactively during it (Ch15’s Layer 0 makes the same recommendation from the reliability side).

Transport and topology, by contrast, are closer to adopt-grade than the hardware layer. MRC — the OpenAI/Microsoft/AMD/Broadcom/NVIDIA joint spec extending RoCEv2 with per-packet entropy-value spraying over static SRv6 source routing — is already in production on GB200 clusters including OCI Abilene and Microsoft’s Fairwater supercomputers, measured at 42,000-GPU NCCL scale (92GB/s send/recv, ~770Gb/s peak link bandwidth, 96% of theoretical) with a stated theoretical target of 131,072 GPUs Resilient AI Supercomputer Networking using MRC and SRv6 [PAPER, 05/2026]. NVIDIA’s own framing positions MRC as an alternative alongside Spectrum-X’s native adaptive RDMA on the same ConnectX SuperNIC/Spectrum-X silicon — both are adopt-grade choices for a fleet this size, and the decision is a procurement/vendor-fit question more than an engineering-risk one (Ch18 covers the transport comparison in full). NVL72-aware scheduling is likewise adopt-grade but non-optional: NVIDIA’s own public guidance states outright that “operating GB200 NVL72 clusters at scale requires new workload scheduling algorithms to treat NVLink domains as hard boundaries for jobs,” and the SLURM topology/block plugin co-developed with SchedMD (SLURM 23.11+) is the shipping answer [developer.nvidia.com] [OFFICIAL-BLOG, 2025-2026] — a job that fragments across a partial NVL72 rack is a self-inflicted reliability and performance problem, and the fix is a scheduler configuration, not a research question.

Two structural topology choices carry direct evidence and should be treated as design requirements rather than options. Alibaba’s HPN 7.0 paper found single-ToR-switch failure to be the single largest network-reliability risk to an LLM training job, motivating a non-stacked dual-ToR design that cut single-link-failure degradation from a full job halt to 6.25% throughput loss [SIGCOMM’24 Qian et al.] [PAPER, 08/2024] — a finding independently consistent with MRC’s own multi-plane Clos design philosophy. And because backplane/switch-tray maintenance drains an entire 72-GPU NVL72 rack rather than a single server, the in-rack 8-of-72 hot-spare provisioning NVIDIA’s own rack design bakes in is necessary but not sufficient — operators need whole spare racks on top of it (Ch14; Ch15 §3). Finally, in-network reduction correctness needs independent verification rather than trust in standard health checks: a documented case had DCGMI diagnostics reporting healthy while an NVSwitch ALU silently corrupted AllReduce results (Ch14 §8, citing SemiAnalysis’s multi-datacenter-training analysis) — SHARP and other in-network compute paths should be canary-checked, not assumed correct because the surrounding hardware passed a standard diagnostic.

The Reliability Layer

Ch19 develops the full failure-rate tables, checkpoint math, and layered spec; this section names the specific build items a 10T/200B/100k-GB200 run cannot get by adopting off-the-shelf tooling alone. The scale of the problem is set by two numbers. First, the expected interruption rate: triangulating Llama 3’s own observed rate linearly scaled to 100,000 GPUs (~2/hour), SemiAnalysis’s field-derived MTBI ranges for H100 and GB200, and a 2026 production paper on 100K-GPU HSDP training that reports a failure every 18 minutes directly, the best single planning number is 1.5–4 unplanned interruptions per hour, 35–100 per day (Ch19 §4). Second, the checkpoint size: Adam-style state for 10T parameters is 140TB (20TB BF16 weights + 40TB FP32 master + 80TB Adam m/v), and a naive <60-second synchronous drain of that state implies roughly 2.3TB/s sustained aggregate write bandwidth — comparable in scale to Meta’s entire 7,500-server Tectonic fleet, built for a far smaller checkpoint (Ch11 §9; Ch19 §6).

No production system in this corpus actually tries to hit that 2.3TB/s number, and that convergence is itself the build target. VAST Data’s survey of 40 real production runs (85,000+ checkpoints, 18 clusters, 45B–1T+ parameter models) found actual global checkpoint-storage bandwidth demand is only 50–200GB/s, “well below 1TB/s even for trillion-parameter models,” because production systems write to local/peer NVMe first and drain to durable storage asynchronously over minutes rather than seconds [blog.vastdata.com] [OFFICIAL-BLOG, 2026]. ByteRobust achieves 0.01–0.04s per-step checkpoint blocking time via in-memory peer replication with backups placed outside a rank’s own parallel group, sustaining 99.11–99.71% MFU versus 39.84–43.07% for synchronous-remote-filesystem checkpointing [arXiv:2509.16293] [PAPER, 09/2025]. AWS’s December 2025 “checkpointless training” launch for SageMaker HyperPod goes further, recovering directly from peer GPU memory over EFA with no filesystem round-trip in the common case, cutting recovery from 15–30 minutes to under 2 minutes and reportedly used to train Amazon Nova [aws.amazon.com/blogs/aws] [OFFICIAL-BLOG, 12/2025]. None of this exists natively, at this level of sophistication, inside the framework the reader will actually run (the Training Codebase section above makes the same point from the other direction) — building a ByteRobust/AWS-checkpointless-grade peer-replication layer, with the local-first-then-async-drain architecture and cross-parallel-group replica placement both systems converge on independently, is the largest single build item in this entire chapter.

Silent data corruption is the second major gap, and it is unvalidated at this scale in a specific, documented way: every quantitative SDC study in this corpus (LLM-PRISM, “Understanding Silent Data Corruption in LLM Training,” the OCP whitepaper) is calibrated on ≤670B-parameter, ≤16K-GPU regimes, and the core finding — that loss/NaN monitoring alone is insufficient because a consistently-corrupting node can produce loss curves indistinguishable from healthy nodes while gradients diverge by several percent — was established at that smaller scale Understanding Silent Data Corruption in LLM Training [PAPER, 02/2025]. The only production-viable mitigation demonstrated is a shadow-replica gradient comparison during all-reduce, at roughly 2x compute cost for the shadow — a real, unavoidable trade-off decision the reader has to make without a directly-applicable precedent at 10T scale (Ch19 §2, §10). One genuinely useful, non-obvious mitigation costs nothing extra: FP8 training doubles as SDC insurance, because FP8’s narrow dynamic range forces faults to either saturate harmlessly or trigger a detectable NaN, essentially binarizing corruption outcomes, whereas BF16/FP16 permit large-but-finite silent corruption that NaN-checking never catches — a controlled fault-injection study found perplexity deviations up to 400–600% under BF16/FP16 that never triggered a crash [LLM-PRISM] [PAPER, 2026]. This is one more reason, independent of the throughput case in Ch8, to commit to FP8-or-lower as the default GEMM precision.

Finally, no validated elastic-pretraining path exists at frontier scale, and this constrains the fault-tolerance architecture the reader can actually build. Every production system surveyed — Llama 3, MegaScale/ByteRobust, FT-HSDP, Gemini — runs a fixed logical world size with hot-spare/warm-pool replacement, not elastic membership; torchft/DiLoCo and its 2026 successor ElasWave both work, but the largest publicly documented deployment is 300 GPUs (torchft) or 96 NPUs (ElasWave) — three to four orders of magnitude below the target scale [github.com/meta-pytorch/torchft] [CODE]; [arXiv:2510.00606] [PAPER, 10/2025]. The practical implication is that the reader should plan a fixed-world-size, warm-standby architecture — the FT-HSDP paper’s own demonstrated numbers (failure every 18 minutes at 100K GPUs, a naive synchronous restart leaving only 44% effective training time, its own scheme cutting the stall to ~3 minutes and reaching 80% effective training time with 11-of-12 healthy replicas) are the best available target to engineer against, not an elastic-membership system that has never run at more than a few hundred devices [arXiv:2602.00277] [PAPER, 02/2026].

Operations and Organization

Ch20 develops the incident taxonomy, debugging doctrine, pre-flight gauntlets, and war-room/on-call design in full; this chapter’s role is to state the organizational consequence of everything above. Three items above are, in practice, org decisions before they are engineering ones. The scaling-laws program’s 30–60%-of-final-run compute budget has to be approved as an org commitment 12-plus months out, not discovered as a cost overrun once ablations are already running. The dedicated NVL72 hardware-diagnostics team named under Cluster and Network is a staffing decision with the same lead time as the hardware procurement itself — a team assembled after racks arrive is already behind the “couple of hours to localize a fault, 8–24 hours to swap a backplane” timeline SemiAnalysis documents (Ch14; Ch15 §10). And the SDC shadow-replica decision under the Reliability Layer is a standing capacity commitment (roughly 2x compute for the shadow, if deployed continuously), which means it has to be sized into the cluster’s total GPU-hour budget rather than treated as a free software add-on.

The concrete pre-flight test list this chapter can add to Ch20’s gauntlet is the set of already-observed, named failure modes surfaced across the notes above: the open Megatron-LM EP-overlap deadlock and checkpoint-corruption issues (#5749, #1810, #5281), the documented NVSwitch-ALU silent-corruption case despite healthy DCGMI diagnostics, and the ByteDance straggler-accumulation case where progressive slow-chip contamination over roughly 11,500 steps dropped MFU from 40% to 30% — a 25% relative loss — entirely undetected by link-failure tooling (Ch14 §8, citing SemiAnalysis). Each of these is a specific, reproducible scenario a shakedown run should deliberately inject and verify recovery against before the real run’s clock starts, rather than a category of risk to acknowledge in the abstract.

The Master Checklist

The items above resolve into five lead-time bands. This is the version to pin to the wall.

T-14 to T-12 months — must start now, gates everything downstream:

  • Approve and staff the scaling-laws ladder as an org-level compute commitment (30–60% of final-run budget).
  • Begin or substantially extend own-crawler infrastructure and its legal/compliance review.
  • Assemble the dedicated NVL72 hardware-diagnostics team ahead of rack delivery.
  • Begin the distributed-Muon->100B-active de-risking ladder (Ch7); this and the scaling-laws program share critical path.
  • Begin design and staffing for the hierarchical peer-replication checkpoint stack (largest single build item in the chapter).

T-12 to T-9 months — architecture- and framework-dependent, needs the ladder’s early results:

  • Run muP-transfer completeness ablations (depth, batch, MoE fan-in) at target architecture.
  • Run direct critical-batch-size measurement via branched runs.
  • Sweep granularity/shared-expert-ratio/activation-ratio at target d_model and expert width (feeds Ch5 spec freeze).
  • Build the full-model MXFP8 kernel integration (torch.compile+TP gap).
  • Build the metadata-conditional load-time sampling layer, if adaptive curriculum is in scope.
  • Stand up strict rack-level acceptance testing as a standing process, not a one-time gate.
  • Decide and size the SDC shadow-replica detection scope.
  • Engineer the fixed-world-size warm-standby fault-tolerance architecture to the FT-HSDP demonstrated bar.

T-9 to T-6 months — integration and topology:

  • Tune EP dispatch kernels (DeepEP/HybridEP) for the target EP degree and expert width.
  • Build multi-source workload-imbalance mitigation for the dataloader.
  • Finalize transport choice (MRC vs. Spectrum-X adaptive RDMA) and dual-ToR/multi-plane topology.
  • Provision whole-rack spare capacity on top of in-rack hot spares.
  • Materialize midtraining/anneal mixture shard sets against the frozen curriculum plan.
  • Run the largest feasible scaling-law confirmation run.

T-6 to T-3 months — configuration and pre-flight:

  • Enable NVL72-domain-aware scheduling (SLURM topology plugin).
  • Adopt sequence packing and intra-document masking (cheap, should already be done, verify it is).
  • Build SHARP/in-network-reduction canary verification.
  • Inject and verify recovery against the named failure-mode scenarios (EP-overlap deadlocks, checkpoint-corruption loops, silent NVSwitch corruption, straggler accumulation).
  • Configure dataloader cold-start mitigations and object-storage native shard streaming.

Ongoing / day-0 and beyond:

  • Router/gate-logit FP32 discipline — non-negotiable, zero cost.
  • FP8-as-default-GEMM-precision, both for throughput and as SDC mitigation.
  • Continuous straggler detection and root-cause automation.
  • Target 85–93% goodput as the standing operational bar (Ch19 §10); anything below that on a sustained basis is a signal to re-open one of the gaps above, not to accept as the cost of scale.
