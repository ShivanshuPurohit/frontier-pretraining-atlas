Chapter 08 · Attention is no longer matmul-bound

Attention and kernels in 2026: FlashAttention-4, MLA, native sparse attention, and linear/hybrid attention

At 10T-total / 200B-active on 100,000 GB200 GPUs, the routed-expert GEMMs swamp attention's FLOPs — but attention is the term that decides whether your model is servable at 1M context, and the one whose Blackwell kernel co-design is most fragile. Blackwell scaled asymmetrically: the matmul got ~2× cheaper, softmax exp did not. Everything below follows from that inversion.

~28 min read6 figuresgrounded in MaxText attention_mla.py / attention_op.py

By mid-2026 attention is no longer the FLOP-dominant term in a frontier MoE forward pass — at 200B active parameters the routed-expert GEMMs swamp it — but it is the term that decides whether your 10T/200B model is servable at long context, and it is the term whose kernel co-design with Blackwell is most fragile. The central fact this chapter is built around is that GB200 scaled asymmetrically: BF16 tensor-core throughput roughly doubled to ~2.25 PFLOP/s dense BF16 per B200 die, while the transcendental/SFU path (MUFU.EX2) and shared-memory bandwidth did not move proportionally (Colfax FA4 deep-dive). Softmax exp is now the attention bottleneck on Blackwell, not the matmul. That single inversion reorders every design decision below it: it makes fewer attended tokens (sparsity) and smaller per-token cache (compression) pay off more than raw kernel TFLOP/s, and it explains why the entire 2025–26 frontier — DeepSeek DSA/V4, Kimi Linear, Qwen3-Next, MiniMax, Nemotron-H — converged on cheaper attention rather than faster dense attention. We ground the discussion throughout in the user's actual codebase (MaxText), whose attention_mla.py, attention_op.py, and attention_compressed.py are a production JAX/Pallas re-implementation of exactly this frontier.

A note on the FLOP envelope before we begin: a 100,000-GPU GB200 fleet is roughly 0.45 EFLOP/s dense FP8 peak (100k × ~4.5 PFLOP/s dense FP8 per B200), or ~0.23 EFLOP/s dense BF16. The widely-quoted "720 PFLOP/s FP8 / 360 BF16 per NVL72 rack" and the resulting "~1.0 EFLOP/s cluster" are NVIDIA's 2:4-sparse marketing figures — the GB200 NVL72 page footnotes "Dense is one-half sparse spec shown" (NVIDIA GB200 NVL72). Dense per-rack is 360 PFLOP/s FP8 / 180 BF16. Quote dense; attention training FLOPs are dense. Against that envelope, realistic large-MoE FP8 MFU sits ~35–40%.

The one inversion to remember

On Blackwell the matmul got ~2× cheaper while MUFU.EX2 (softmax exp) did not. Attention is now exp-bound, not matmul-bound — so the winning move is to make the kernel do less work (sparsity, compression), not run faster. Every recommendation in this chapter is downstream of that fact.

01FlashAttention-4: co-designing for Blackwell's asymmetric scaling

FlashAttention-4 (arXiv 2603.05451, Zadouri, Hoehnerbach, Shah, Liu, Thakkar, Dao; March 2026) is the reference training-attention kernel for any GB200 plan, and it is best understood not as "FA3 but faster" but as a kernel re-architected around the exp bottleneck. On Hopper, FA3 hid softmax exp under the matmul via warp-specialized ping-pong; that worked because the MMA side and the MUFU.EX2 side were roughly balanced. On Blackwell the matmul got ~2× cheaper while MUFU.EX2 did not, so the naive FA3 schedule leaves the tensor cores starved waiting on the exp units. FA4's three load-bearing answers:

1. Fully async UMMA into Tensor Memory. FA4 issues tcgen05.mma (5th-gen tensor-core) instructions that accumulate in Tensor Memory (TMEM, 256 KB/SM) rather than registers, decoupling the matmul pipeline from the register file that softmax needs. The single-CTA UMMA atom is 128×256×16, roughly 2× the Hopper WGMMA atom; the 2-CTA MMA mode fuses a UMMA across a CTA pair to 256×256×16, which halves operand-B SMEM traffic. In the backward pass the 2-CTA mode also cuts global atomic reductions ~50% by exchanging the dQ partial reduction over DSMEM (distributed shared memory across the CTA pair) instead of going to global memory (Colfax).

2. Software-emulated exp2 on the FMA units, running alongside hardware MUFU.EX2. This is the headline trick. Rather than route all exp through the saturated MUFU.EX2 path, FA4 also computes 2x on the otherwise-idle FMA pipes: Cody–Waite range reduction of 2x into 2n · 2f (integer n, fractional f ∈ [0,1)), then a degree-3 Horner polynomial with Sollya-fit coefficients p0 = 1.0, p1 ≈ 0.6951, p2 ≈ 0.2276, p3 ≈ 0.0771, fit for minimum relative error over [0,1). Splitting the exp workload across two physical pipelines is what unblocks the matmul throughput (Colfax).

3. Conditional / two-stage softmax rescaling. A dedicated correction warpgroup handles the online-softmax running-max rescale, but skips the rescale when the new block's max jump is below a threshold τ — pulling the rescale off the critical path whenever the running max is stable (the common case deep into a sequence). The forward schedule is a ping-pong over two Q tiles (QH, QL, each 128 query tokens) driven by two softmax warpgroups plus one correction warpgroup, deliberately staggered to avoid MUFU contention.

cute-dsl — FA4's software exp2 on the FMA pipe (degree-3 Horner, illustrative)
# Cody-Waite split: 2^x = 2^n * 2^f, n integer, f in [0,1)
n = floor(x)
f = x - n                                  # fractional part, hits the polynomial
# Sollya-fit coeffs, minimized for RELATIVE error on [0,1):
p0, p1, p2, p3 = 1.0, 0.6951, 0.2276, 0.0771
poly = p0 + f * (p1 + f * (p2 + f * p3))   # degree-3 Horner, runs on FMA units
exp2 = ldexp(poly, n)                       # 2^f * 2^n  -- no MUFU.EX2 traffic
# scheduled ALONGSIDE hardware MUFU.EX2 so neither exp pipe starves the MMA

The reported result: ~1605–1613 TFLOP/s BF16 on B200 = 71% of the 2.25 PFLOP/s dense peak, 1.3× over cuDNN 9.13 and 2.7× over Triton, with deterministic backward at ~85–90% of nondeterministic throughput (arXiv 2603.05451). FA4 ships entirely in CuTe-DSL (CUTLASS's Python kernel DSL) at Dao-AILab/flash-attention/.../cute, claims 20–30× faster compile than C++ templates, and is wired as a FlexAttention backend so masking variants compose without hand-writing each kernel.

The engineering implications for the 10T/200B target are sharp:

  • 71% is your attention-FLOP ceiling on GB200, and you will rarely hit it. Design batch/seqlen/headdim around the exp bottleneck, not the matmul. Larger head_dim amortizes more matmul per exp and is favorable on Blackwell — one reason MLA's 192-element QK head dim (128 nope + 64 rope) and Step-3's 256-dim heads are not the liability they'd be on memory-bound hardware.
  • Attention is no longer matmul-bound on GB200. This is the thesis that justifies the rest of the chapter: if you can't make the kernel much faster, make it do less work. Sparsity and compression beat kernel micro-optimization.
  • FlexAttention is the masking escape hatch. Chunked/sliding/compressed/document masks at 10T scale will proliferate; a FlexAttention backend over FA4 avoids a combinatorial kernel-zoo.

02MLA: the KV-cache compression that anchors the dense layers

For the dense-attention layers of a frontier MoE, Multi-head Latent Attention (MLA) is the default, and the exact cache arithmetic governs your memory plan. In the user's actual codebase, attention_mla.py caches only the compressed latent: kv_lora_rank = 512 (the kv_norm-ed low_rank_main) plus a single shared decoupled-RoPE key of qk_rope_head_dim = 64 — 576 elements/token/layer. Compare to GQA-8 with 128-dim heads, which caches ~2 × 8 × 128 = 2,048 elements/token/layer for separate K and V. MLA is a ~3.5× cache reduction over GQA-8 before quantization, and it is reconstructive: MlaKVCache stores key_latent (512) and key_rope (64) and rebuilds per-head K and V on read via the wkv_b up-projection (mla_get_key_value).

The crucial systems knob is absorb-vs-materialize, exposed as mla_naive_kvcache. With the absorbed form (default), you cache the 576-element latent and fold wkv_b into the query-side projection so you never materialize full per-head K/V — minimal cache, maximal arithmetic intensity on the QK product (good for Blackwell, where you want AI). With mla_naive_kvcache=True, MLA materializes full K/V into the cache — simpler kernels, higher bandwidth, larger cache. The roofline tradeoff is quantified in the hardware-centric MLA analysis (arXiv 2506.02523): on GB200's memory hierarchy, the absorbed form wins for decode (cache-bound) while materialize can win for very short prefill (compute-bound, where the extra cache is irrelevant).

Recommendation: absorbed MLA, FP8 cache

For the 10T/200B target: absorbed MLA on the dense layers, KV cache in FP8 (quantize_kvcache, kv_quant_axis), giving ~576 elements × 1 byte = 576 B/token/layer. Absorbed keeps arithmetic intensity high on the QK product, which is exactly what Blackwell rewards now that exp — not the matmul — is the bottleneck.

Two more MLA details from the codebase that matter at scale:

  • YaRN mscale absorption. When max_position_embeddings > original, MLA folds the YaRN attention-temperature into softmax_scale: mscale = 0.1 · mscale · ln(rope_factor) + 1.0, then softmax_scale *= mscale². The DeepSeek-V3 config (deepseek3-671b.yml) uses rope_factor = 40 for 163,840-token context — confirmed in the user's actual codebase alongside q_lora_rank = 1536, kv_lora_rank = 512, qk_nope_head_dim = 128, qk_rope_head_dim = 64, v_head_dim = 128, 128 query heads.
  • The [nope, rope] split and interleaved RoPE layout. MLA splits each query/key head as [nope, rope] with interleave=True. This is the opposite of the DSA indexer's [rope, nope] + concatenated layout (below) — a subtlety MaxText calls out in comments and a real footgun if you port between them.
  • QK-clip support. MLA supports use_qk_clip (Kimi/Muon-style max-logit clipping via record_max_logits), gated on MODEL_MODE_TRAIN. MLA at scale is prone to attention-logit blow-up under Muon, so this hook exists specifically to bound it — but recall it is not yet available on GPU flash, so the clip path runs on dot_product on GB200.

03NSA → DSA → V4 hybrid CSA/HCA: the learned-sparse-attention trajectory

The most consequential attention story of 2025–26 is DeepSeek's three-step march from natively trainable block sparsity (NSA) to a shipped lightning-indexer top-k (DSA, V3.2) to a layer-interleaved hybrid of two compressed-attention variants (V4). This is the path you should plan to walk for the 200B model's long-context capability.

NSA: the natively-trainable ancestor

NSA (arXiv 2502.11089, DeepSeek+PKU+UW, Feb 2025) runs three parallel branches fused by a learned gate: a coarse compression branch (mean/learned-pool every block into one entry), a fine-grained selection branch (top-n blocks), and a sliding window for locality. The hardware co-design is the lesson: selection is block-sparse by construction and GQA/MQA-aligned — all heads in a group share the selected KV blocks, so a single block load amortizes across the group, keeping arithmetic intensity high. The reference kernels are Triton, arithmetic-intensity-balanced so no branch starves. NSA proves you can train sparsity from scratch with full-attention parity. But it never became the shipped DeepSeek attention; DSA did.

DSA: what shipped, and what MaxText implements

DSA (DeepSeek V3.2-Exp, arXiv 2512.02556, Dec 2025) replaces NSA's three-branch structure with a single lightning indexer that scores every (query, key) pair and selects the top-k = 2048 KV positions per query, dropping core attention from O(L²) to O(Lk). It is implemented in the user's actual codebase as the Indexer class in attention_mla.py, and that implementation is, frankly, clearer than the V3.2 paper's prose. The mechanism, verbatim from the code path:

  1. logits = ReLU(einsum("bthd,bsd->btsh", q, k)) — a ReLU similarity, not softmax; MQA-style broadcasting so each key is shared across the indexer heads.
  2. weights = weights_proj(inputs_q) · n_heads-0.5 · softmax_scale — head-importance weights from a separate FP32, unquantized projection.
  3. indexer_score = einsum("btsh,bth->bts", logits, weights) — head-summed relevance.
  4. _, topk_indices = jax.lax.top_k(indexer_score, k=indexer_topk) — top-k selection; tokens outside top-k get DEFAULT_MASK_VALUE.

Three implementation facts in MaxText are non-obvious and load-bearing:

1A mathematical wall. inputs_q, low_rank_q, inputs_kv are all stop_gradient-ed: the LM loss cannot flow into the indexer and the indexer loss cannot perturb the main model. This bidirectional barrier is what makes joint training stable.

2ReLU similarity. Not a softmax score — MQA-style broadcasting shares each key across the indexer heads, keeping the indexer cheap (only a few heads, FP8-able).

3FP32, unquantized. The code comment is explicit: "deepseek3.2 enforces FP32 and does not quantize, for precision and stability." The indexer body can be FP8, but weights_proj cannot — violate this and top-k selection goes unstable.

4O(Lk), not O(L²). Top-k=2048 KV positions per query; everything outside gets DEFAULT_MASK_VALUE. indexer_sparse_training toggles whether the KL target is the dense or already-sparse attention.

  • weights_proj is forced FP32 and unquantized. The code comment is explicit: "deepseek3.2 enforces FP32 and does not quantize, for precision and stability." The rest of the indexer can run FP8 (only a few heads), but this head-weighting projection cannot. Violate this and the indexer's top-k selection becomes unstable.
  • Gradient isolation in both directions. inputs_q, low_rank_q, and inputs_kv are all jax.lax.stop_gradient-ed before the indexer touches them. The comment calls this "a mathematical wall": the LM loss cannot flow into the indexer, and the indexer loss cannot perturb the main model. This bidirectional barrier is what makes joint training stable.
  • The indexer is trained by KL divergence to the main attention. calculate_indexer_loss computes KL(attention_probs ‖ indexer_probs) against the main attention's head-summed, L1-normalized distribution (both softmaxed in float32 for stability), scaled by indexer_loss_scaling_factor. indexer_sparse_training toggles whether the KL target is the dense or the already-sparse attention.
  • RoPE layout differs from MLA. The indexer splits [rope, nope] (MLA is [nope, rope]) and uses concatenated, non-interleaved RoPE (apply_partial_rope with interleave=False); YaRN is applied with the concatenate layout.
Two code-review invariants you must enforce from the codebase

The indexer weights_proj stays FP32 + unquantized, and the bidirectional stop_gradient barrier between indexer and main model is mandatory. Both are load-bearing for training stability, and both are trivially easy to break in a refactor (e.g. someone adds blanket FP8 casting, or removes a stop_gradient "for cleanliness"). Pin tests on both.

The continued-training recipe from V3.1-Terminus is the cheapest known way to retrofit sparse attention onto a dense checkpoint, and you should budget for exactly this: a dense warm-up (1,000 steps / 2.1B tokens, LR 1e-3, KL loss only — the indexer learns to mimic the still-dense main attention) followed by a sparse stage (15,000 steps / 943.7B tokens, LR 7.3e-6, top-k active) (arXiv 2512.02556). Reported ~3–6× long-context cost reduction at 128K with quality parity. One scaling caveat worth flagging: DSA's jax.lax.top_k over the full sequence has a memory footprint that grows with L; the 2026 follow-on StreamIndex (arXiv 2605.02568) makes the top-k memory-bounded and streaming, which is the right primitive at 1M context where a dense score matrix is itself the problem.

DeepSeek V4: hybrid CSA/HCA, mHC, and FP4-in-the-loop

DeepSeek V4 ("Towards Highly Efficient Million-Token Context Intelligence," report ~April 2026) generalizes DSA into a layer-wise interleave of two compressed-attention variants. Treat V4 specifics as partially unverified — the arXiv HTML at 2606.19348 is the primary anchor but active-param counts and layer ratios vary across blog summaries, so the secondary derivations below (prathamp blog) carry a rumor tag.

Two more V4 systems bets transfer directly to a GB200 plan, both medium/rumor confidence:

  • mHC (manifold-constrained hyper-connections) rumor replaces residual connections with a ~4× wider inter-layer pathway whose channel-mixing matrix is constrained to the Birkhoff polytope (doubly-stochastic) via Sinkhorn–Knopp (t_max=20), with overhead held to ~6.7% of the 1F1B pipeline stage. This is a deep-stack-stability mechanism for the very deep, very wide layouts a 10T model implies, and it is trained with Muon.
  • FP4 QAT in the loop. V4 applies FP4×FP8 matmuls to routed-expert weights and the indexer QK path — the first trillion-scale model reported to train with FP4 in the loop rather than post-hoc, which directly validates GB200's native NVFP4 microscaling (E2M1 values with E4M3 block scales) as a training format, not just an inference trick. MaxText's quantizations.py already carries the AQT intmp mixed-precision and nanoo_fp8/Qwix paths to mirror this.
The FP4 caveat you must not gloss over stated in V4

V4 explicitly notes that FP4×FP8 currently executes at FP8×FP8 speed on existing silicon and only realizes the ~1/3 efficiency win on future hardware (DeepSeek-V4, by extension of the same FP4 reasoning). On GB200 today, FP4 on the expert/indexer path is a memory-bandwidth and footprint win (half the weight bytes), not yet a compute win. Budget accordingly.

04Linear-attention and SSM hybrids: crushing the decode KV wall

At 10T/200B on 100k GB200, long-context inference is dominated by the KV-cache wall, and 2025–26 saw linear/SSM hybrids cross decisively from research into production. The transferable pattern across every one of them is a linear:full ratio of 3:1 to 7:1, plus periodic full/MLA layers for global recall — the linear layers give constant per-token state and O(L) compute, the sparse full layers preserve needle-in-haystack retrieval and long-chain-of-thought.

The ratio is the load-bearing choice, not the mechanism

Across MiniMax, Qwen3-Next, Kimi Linear and Nemotron-H, the durable lesson is that the linear:full ratio (3:1 to 7:1) matters far more than which exact linear primitive (lightning attention vs Gated DeltaNet vs KDA vs Mamba-2) you pick. Choose the ratio first; the mechanism is a second-order, swappable decision.

Recommendation: a hybrid backbone, not pure-anything

The convergent evidence points to a hybrid backbone: MLA (absorbed, 576-elt latent, FP8 cache) on the dense layers, a DSA-style lightning indexer (top-k ≈ 2048, FP8, FP32 weights_proj, gradient-isolated, KL-trained) for long-context sparse selection, and a 3:1 linear:full interleave (Gated-DeltaNet or KDA) to crush the decode KV wall — keeping ~25% of layers as sparse-full/MLA for global recall. This composes cleanly: the linear layers carry constant state, the MLA layers carry the 576-elt latent, and the DSA indexer prunes the attended set on the layers that need 1M reach. Given MiniMax's M2 reversal, I would not push past 3:1 (i.e., not toward 7:1) for a first frontier run — the marginal KV savings are not worth the retrieval-quality risk at 200B active. The V4 CSA/HCA interleave is the more aggressive alternative and is the right target if you can validate FP4-QAT-in-the-loop and mHC stability at your scale; otherwise the linear-hybrid + DSA path is lower-variance.

CuTe-DSL is now the production path for the most-optimized NVIDIA kernels — FA4 is the proof, and the 20–30× compile-time win over C++ templates is what makes iterating on a 100k-GPU kernel tractable. Triton remains the rapid-prototyping and portability layer and is NSA's reference implementation, but at ~0.37× FA4 it is not your training kernel. ThunderKittens 2.0 (HazyResearch; released Jan 11 2026) is the serious tile-DSL alternative: full Blackwell support (5th-gen tensor cores, TMEM, CTA pairs), MXFP8/NVFP4, attention fwd/bwd at near-cuDNN on B200 and up to 2× FA3 on H100, and in production at Together/Cursor/Jump (ThunderKittens). 06Synthesis: the attention stack for a 10T/200B/100k-GB200 run

The 2026 frontier has decisively answered "how do you attend at 1M context on a 200B-active model": not with a faster dense kernel, but with less attention. FA4 sets the matmul envelope (71% of dense peak, exp-bound), and that ceiling is precisely why sparsity and compression — which reduce the number of attended tokens and the per-token cache — pay off more than chasing kernel TFLOP/s. The recommended stack, grounded in what the user's codebase already implements:

  1. Dense layers: absorbed MLA, 576-elt latent (kv_lora_rank=512 + qk_rope_head_dim=64), FP8 KV cache. ~3.5× cache reduction over GQA-8, high arithmetic intensity (Blackwell-favorable). Keep use_qk_clip available on a dot_product path for logit-blowup defense under Muon.
  2. Long-context: a DSA lightning indexer, top-k ≈ 2048, FP8 body, FP32 + unquantized weights_proj, bidirectional stop_gradient isolation, KL-trained to the main attention. Bootstrap via the V3.2 two-stage recipe (1k-step dense warm-up → 15k-step sparse). Watch top-k memory at 1M — adopt StreamIndex-style streaming top-k.
  3. Decode-wall mitigation: a 3:1 linear:full interleave (Gated-DeltaNet/KDA), tempered by MiniMax's M2 reversal — do not exceed 3:1 on a first run.
  4. Kernels: FA4 (CuTe-DSL) + FlexAttention for training/masking on GB200, cuDNN/TE as the supported fallback, ThunderKittens/HipKittens as the portability and AMD hedge, and the MaxText Pallas/Splash/Tokamax path as the TPU substrate.
  5. The two non-obvious gotchas to enforce in code review, both from the codebase: indexer weights_proj stays FP32 + unquantized, and the stop_gradient barrier between indexer and main model is mandatory — both are load-bearing for training stability, and both are easy to break in a refactor.
The lower-variance frontier choice

The aggressive frontier — DeepSeek V4's CSA/HCA + mHC + FP4-in-the-loop, claiming 10% of V3.2's KV at 1M — is the right next target once FP4 QAT and doubly-stochastic mHC residuals are validated at your scale, but it carries more unverified risk. The MLA + DSA + 3:1-linear-hybrid stack is the lower-variance, frontier-competitive choice for a first 10T/200B run.
