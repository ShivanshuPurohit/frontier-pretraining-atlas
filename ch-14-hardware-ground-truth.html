Every FLOPs-per-dollar argument anywhere in this book rests on a single contested number: what one GB200 GPU can actually do, sustained, on a sparse-MoE workload. That number turns out to be surprisingly hard to pin down even from primary sources — NVIDIA’s own reference documents, and at least two notes in the research corpus behind this book, silently substitute the wrong SKU’s peak FLOPs into a GB200-labeled calculation. This chapter reconciles that conflict, walks the only rigorous public before/after benchmark of GB200 against H100, and then asks the question the rest of this book has so far assumed away: whether 100,000 GB200-class GPUs is even the right unit of account for a run that will not start moving tokens until well into 2026 or 2027.

The Reconciled Spec Table: What “GB200 NVL72” Actually Means

GB200 NVL72 and HGX B200 are the same reticle-limited Blackwell die — 208B transistors, TSMC 4NP — configured at different power envelopes and cooling classes. NVIDIA’s own side-by-side datasheet (document #3384703, Dec 2024) puts them in one table, with a single footnote that resolves most of the confusion in downstream literature: every Tensor Core figure in the table except FP64 is a 2:4 structured-sparse number, and the dense figure relevant to ordinary training rooflines is exactly half. NVIDIA “Blackwell” datasheet #3384703 [OFFICIAL-DOCS] Dec 2024

NVIDIA “Blackwell” datasheet #3384703 [OFFICIAL-DOCS] Dec 2024; cross-validated against NVIDIA GB200 NVL72 product page and NVIDIA DGX B200 product page [OFFICIAL], fetched 2026-07; independently reconstructed by Glenn K. Lockwood, “NVIDIA B200” [CREDIBLE-HEARSAY] Apr 2026.

The correction across every tensor-core precision is a clean, uniform +11.1% (5,000/4,500 = 2,500/2,250 = 10,000/9,000 = 10/9 exactly), but the vector-ALU path (FP32/FP64) only gains 6.7–8.1%. Back-calculating an implied clock from the vector path (≈1.98 GHz HGX vs ≈2.1 GHz NVL72 GB200) predicts only a ~4,770 TFLOPS dense-FP8 GB200 figure if the same ~6% gap applied uniformly to tensor cores — not the actual 5,000. NVIDIA publishes no official clock speed for either SKU, and the gap between the tensor-core ratio and the vector-ALU ratio is unexplained in any source located for this corpus; the likely mechanism is a more aggressive sustained-clock floor specifically under Tensor-Core-saturated load at 1,200W, but this is inference, not a cited fact. [reconciliation, this corpus] 2026-07

This distinction matters practically because two notes in this book’s own research corpus got it wrong in exactly the way a careless reader would: one imported the JAX Scaling Book’s undifferentiated “B200” FLOPs table — unambiguously the HGX dense figure (2.3e15 BF16, 4.5e15 FP8, 9.0e15 FP4, matching HGX within rounding) — into a GB200 NVL72-specific cluster analysis (see Ch16’s critical-batch and EP-viability-threshold derivations, corrected there); the second computed a GB200 MFU of 46.6% for DeepSeek-V3-scale training using the HGX 2,250-TFLOPS BF16 denominator, when the correct GB200 denominator of 2,500 gives 41.9% (see Ch15/Ch16 for the corrected kernel-level MFU discussion). The scaling book’s own text is internally inconsistent on this point — its main FLOPs table uses HGX-class numbers while a worked example three sections later reasons from an 80-TFLOPS FP32 figure that is unambiguously the GB200 number, without ever flagging the SKU switch. Any GB200 FLOPs figure in this book uses the corrected 5,000/2,500/10,000 TFLOPS dense FP8/BF16/FP4 figures above; any number tracing to an un-disambiguated “B200” citation should be treated as suspect. jax-ml/scaling-book, gpus.md [PRIMARY]; reconciliation per this corpus, 2026-07

A third SKU, B100 (700W, a drop-in HGX H100-baseboard-compatible part), exists specifically as a compatibility bridge and is generally excluded from benchmarking literature because of reported yield/throttling problems that kept it from hitting claimed specs — worth knowing only so a stray “B100 TFLOPS” search result is not conflated with either number above. glennklockwood.com [CREDIBLE-HEARSAY] Apr 2026

MFU Over Software Releases: The SemiAnalysis H100-vs-GB200 Benchmark

The single most load-bearing empirical document for this chapter is SemiAnalysis’s “H100 vs GB200 NVL72 Training Benchmarks — Power, TCO, and Reliability Analysis, Software Improvement Over Time” (Dylan Patel & Daniel Nishball, Aug 20, 2025), built on NVIDIA’s own DGXC benchmarking-team scripts run on NVIDIA’s internal H100 EOS reference cluster, using NeMo Megatron-LM — explicitly not native PyTorch/FSDP2, a methodological gap the authors themselves flag since many end users do not run NeMo Megatron-LM in production. SemiAnalysis, “H100 vs GB200 NVL72 Training Benchmarks” [SA-PAID] p.2-3, Aug 2025

The document contains zero theoretical-peak TFLOPS figures for either chip anywhere in its 27 pages — its entire analysis runs in measured tokens/s/GPU, %MFU (without disclosing the FLOPs-peak denominator), $/M-tokens, and J/token. Anyone pairing this article’s MFU% with a peak-FLOPs figure from elsewhere must import that denominator independently; this book uses the corrected 5,000/2,500 TFLOPS GB200 dense FP8/BF16 figures established above wherever one is needed alongside this source’s numbers. [SemiAnalysis, “H100 vs GB200 NVL72”] [SA-PAID] Aug 2025

Capex/TCO baseline. H100 server ≈$190k (all-in ≈$250k); GB200 NVL72 rack ≈$3.1M (all-in ≈$3.9M). Across buyer tiers, GB200’s all-in capex-per-GPU runs 1.6–1.7x H100’s, and combined TCO is ~1.6x — meaning GB200 needs ≥1.6x the effective throughput just to break even on perf/TCO, before reliability is factored in at all. [SemiAnalysis, “H100 vs GB200 NVL72”] [SA-PAID] p.3-4, Aug 2025

Software-only MFU gains on fixed hardware are large and fast. On a static 128×H100 cluster training GPT-3 175B (TP=4, seq 2,048, global batch 500k tokens), BF16 MFU rose 34%→54% and FP8 MFU rose 29.5%→39.5% between Jan and Dec 2024, from CUDA-stack/NCCL software improvements alone — no hardware change. This translated to FP8 pretraining cost falling from 72¢ to 54.2¢ per million tokens ($218k→$162k to replicate GPT-3’s original run) and energy from 3.63 to 2.46 J/token. [SemiAnalysis, “H100 vs GB200 NVL72”] [SA-PAID] p.9-13, Aug 2025

Dense-model weak scaling is close to flat, but MoE is where GB200 actually earns its premium. Llama3-405B FP8 MFU held ~43% and BF16 ~54% across 576–2,304 H100 GPUs; Llama3-70B FP8 MFU instead degraded with scale (38.1%→35.5% from 64 to 2,048 GPUs) while BF16 stayed comparatively flat (54.5%→53.7%) — a reproducible signal that small-scale FP8 MFU should not be extrapolated naively to 100k-GPU regimes. DeepSeek-670B (MoE) landed at just 16.6% BF16 MFU on H100, versus 54.5% for dense Llama3-405B, attributed to massive dispatch/combine communication for token routing (comm volume scales with top-k — routing to 4 experts instead of 1 roughly quadruples traffic). On GB200, DeepSeek-670B throughput went from a barely-compelling 1.3x H100’s tokens/s/GPU in May 2025 to 2.5x by July 2025 (a 1.5x perf/TCO advantage), with NVIDIA’s Dec-2025 roadmap projecting 4.7x. The mechanism is structural: GB200’s 72-GPU NVLink domain makes 64-GPU all-to-all collectives — the exact primitive MoE dispatch/combine needs — run 18x faster than on H100. Llama4 Maverick 400B MoE (17B active) showed an even sharper GB200 advantage in FP8: 2.4x H100’s throughput, a 50% perf/TCO win ($0.061 vs $0.093/M tokens). [SemiAnalysis, “H100 vs GB200 NVL72”] [SA-PAID] p.15-20, Aug 2025

This is the chapter’s central mechanistic finding for a 10T/200B MoE program: GB200’s economic case over H100 is overwhelmingly a sparse-MoE, all-to-all-bound story, not a generic compute-density story. A dense-model buyer gets a much weaker case for GB200 over plain HGX B200 — SemiAnalysis’s own closing verdict on the dense case is blunt: “It appears the vanilla B200 is better performance TCO than GB200 NVL72 for pretraining normal sized models. As model sizes scale or reinforcement learning comes in, GB200 NVL72 will take a lead again.” [SemiAnalysis, “H100 vs GB200 NVL72”] [SA-PAID] p.24-26, Aug 2025

Independently, NVIDIA’s own published Megatron-Bridge performance tables (dense-FP8-peak-normalized, using the corrected 5,000-TFLOPS GB200 denominator) show the same dense-vs-MoE split from a different angle: dense Llama models land in a tight 32–39% MFU band across H100/B200/GB200, while MoE models on the identical hardware land at 9–18%.

NVIDIA, Megatron-Bridge Performance Summary [OFFICIAL-DOCS], accessed Jul 2026, TFLOP/GPU tables normalized against corrected dense-FP8 peaks

The GB200 36.8% Llama3.1-405B figure cross-validates against NVIDIA’s own separately-stated claim of “sustained FP8 training MFU on NVL72: 38–46% of peak” — though this specific range could not be traced to any retrievable primary source (not the SemiAnalysis PDF, not NVIDIA’s own GB200 developer blog) in this corpus’s reconciliation pass, and should be treated as directionally consistent rather than independently confirmed. [OFFICIAL-BLOG claim, unverified] n.d.; see NVIDIA GB200 NVL72 spec reconciliation [reconciliation, this corpus] 2026-07

A separate, from-first-principles reconstruction of DeepSeek-V3’s actual delivered MFU — using the paper’s own 2.664M H800-GPU-hour pretraining figure, 14.8T tokens, 37B active parameters, and the standard 6·N·D FLOPs approximation against H800-SXM’s 1,979-TFLOPS FP8 peak — gives ~17.3% MFU, consistent with the NVIDIA-table pattern above. A widely-circulating “58% MFU for DeepSeek-V3” figure does not survive this reconstruction and appears to be a misreading; it should not be treated as fact in any downstream synthesis. DeepSeek-AI, arXiv:2412.19437 [PAPER] Dec 2024, MFU reconstruction per this corpus

Reliability: NVL72 in the Field

GB200 NVL72’s mean time between interruption (MTBI) is measurably worse than H100’s — 1,000–3,000 GPU-days versus 2,000–5,000 GPU-days — compounded by a much larger blast radius per event: a switch-tray or backplane fault takes down the entire 72-GPU rack, not an 8-GPU node. The mechanical root cause is concrete: each rack’s four copper backplane cartridges connect via Paladin HD connectors rated for only 200 mating cycles before the gold plating must be discarded, and root-causing a backplane signal-integrity fault (“XID 149”) can take hours because the ambiguity spans three possible connector interfaces. The repair-time ladder is steep: compute-tray swap 1–4 hours (job continues on other trays); switch-tray swap 1–4 hours but requires a full-rack drain; backplane cartridge swap 8–24 hours. Operators run 64-of-72 GPUs live with 8 held as hot spares, but the Slurm --segment flag that pins 64-of-72 provides no protection against switch-tray or backplane events, which drain the whole rack regardless — genuine resilience requires provisioning entire spare racks, not just spare GPUs within a rack. [SemiAnalysis, “H100 vs GB200 NVL72”] [SA-PAID] p.20-23, Aug 2025

Pricing in this reliability tax materially changes the GB200 economic case: Llama4 Maverick’s headline 50%-cheaper-per-token GB200 advantage falls to 20% cheaper once the 8-of-72 hot-spare GPUs are charged as opportunity cost, or 30% cheaper if only fully-idle spare racks are charged. [SemiAnalysis, “H100 vs GB200 NVL72”] [SA-PAID] p.24-25, Aug 2025 A separately-documented firmware saga corroborates the operational maturity gap: GB200 NVL72’s early NVLink backplane cabling/sync issues, which affected AWS’s, GCP’s, and Meta’s initial NVL36x2 deployments broadly, were traced to a firmware bug (not hardware) but took 6–7 months to resolve — engineering-sample units shipped November 2024, with a stable firmware v1.3 not released until roughly spring/summer 2025. Top providers (CoreWeave, Oracle) have since moved to rack-level SLAs at 99% uptime rather than component/node-level SLAs, which effectively requires provisioning hot-spare racks as standard practice. SemiAnalysis, “ClusterMAX 2.0” [SA-PAID/ANALYSIS] Nov 2025

For calibration on what “good” reliability looks like at scale even on mature Hopper-class hardware: Meta’s 54-day, 16–24k-GPU Llama3-405B run recorded 466 job interruptions (419 hardware failures), an implied MTBF of ~2,111 H100-days — over 5x better than the sub-400-GPU-day MTBF “gold/silver”-tier neoclouds reportedly deliver on smaller clusters. [SemiAnalysis, “ClusterMAX 2.0”] [SA-PAID] Nov 2025 As of the Aug 2025 benchmark’s own writing, no large-scale frontier training run had yet completed on GB200 NVL72 at all — a dated claim this corpus could not confirm or refute for mid-2026, but worth carrying forward as a live open question: as of late 2025 the largest publicly-verified GB-generation MLPerf submission was ~5,120 GPUs (v5.1, Nov 2025); by MLPerf v6.0 (June 2026, discussed below) that ceiling moved to 8,192 GPUs, but even the newer figure is a ten-minute benchmark, not a months-long pretrain — a materially lower reliability bar than sustaining a 10T-parameter run across 100,000 GPUs for months. [SemiAnalysis, “H100 vs GB200 NVL72”] [SA-PAID] p.20-23, Aug 2025; MLPerf figures per this corpus’s MLPerf-benchmarks note

Power, Cooling, and the Rack Envelope

NVIDIA’s own GB200 NVL72 SuperPOD reference architecture defines one Scalable Unit as 8 racks / 576 GPUs at a stated 1.2 MW TDP — implying ≈150 kW/rack on average, though the document never states this per-rack figure directly, only the SU-level total. NVIDIA GB200 NVL72 SuperPOD RA [DOC], updated Nov 2025 Real deployed racks diverge from this reference by a wide margin: Meta’s Catalina platform, its OCP contribution built on the same NVL72 GPU domain, draws only 93.5 kW/rack at its highest disclosed power tier — genuinely lower than NVIDIA’s own implied figure, the opposite of the “hyperscalers push power density higher” intuition, reflecting Meta’s different rack composition (OCP-form-factor NICs instead of PCIe CEM cards, a doubled 1:1 Grace-CPU-to-GPU ratio that forces the 72-GPU domain across two physical racks rather than one). Hot Chips 2025, Arnold & Bowman, “Meta’s Catalina Platform (NVL72)” [TALK] Aug 24, 2025 Cooling on the GB200 RA is stated only as “hybrid direct-liquid and air cooling” without a quantified split; Meta discloses more granularity — purpose-built Facility Liquid Cooling for liquid-native datacenters plus an “Air Assisted Liquid Cooling” retrofit path for air-cooled facilities, governed by a per-rack Rack Management Controller with a four-tier leak-detection response. [Hot Chips 2025] [TALK] Aug 2025

A genuine planning trap sits in NVIDIA’s own storage-bandwidth guidance: the top-tier (“Enhanced”) per-GPU storage-bandwidth figure in the GB200 RA is ≈217 GBps read per 1,000 GPUs, roughly half the H100 RA’s top-tier (“Best”) ≈488 GBps per 1,000 GPUs — NVIDIA’s own reference sizing dropped materially between generations even though GB200-class checkpoints and datasets are unambiguously larger. This should be read as a signal that NVIDIA’s published sizing assumes dense/lower-active-parameter H100-and-GB200-era workloads, not validated for a 200B-active/10T-total design point whose checkpoint payload scales with total, not active, parameter count (see Ch19 for the full checkpoint-math treatment). [docs.nvidia.com H100 and GB200 SuperPOD RAs, Table 6] [DOC], both updated Nov 2025; derived calculation per this corpus

Finally, NVIDIA’s own compute-fabric reference tables have a hard, disclosed ceiling: the GB200 RA fully tables a non-blocking two-tier InfiniBand fabric only to 16 Scalable Units / 9,216 GPUs, beyond which the document adds an undertabled third “core” tier and directs the reader to “contact your NVIDIA representative.” Every hyperscaler’s 100k+-GPU fabric — OCI’s 131,072-GPU Supercluster, Meta’s DSF, Azure’s MRC, AWS’s EFA “10P10U,” Google’s Jupiter — is proprietary in-house engineering built past that point, not an extension of a published NVIDIA reference design (see Ch18 for the full fabric-by-fabric treatment). No single vendor-published reference architecture covers the full 100,000-GPU scale this book’s hypothetical run targets. docs.nvidia.com GB200 SuperPOD RA, Table 3 [DOC], updated Nov 2025

Rubin Timing: Build Now or Wait

The build-now-vs-wait-for-Rubin question turns on one architectural fact that is easy to miss in FLOPs-headline comparisons: NVLink6 doubles link count relative to NVLink5, not just per-lane rate.

ml-engineering repo, network/README.md [CODE] 07/2026

Because per-lane rate stays fixed at 25 GBps/lane between NVLink5 and NVLink6, the entire generational jump comes from doubling link count — and because PCIe stays at generation 6 across both Blackwell and Rubin, the NVLink-over-PCIe advantage roughly doubles from ~7x (Hopper, Blackwell) to ~15x (Rubin). For an EP-heavy 10T/200B MoE design, this is directly relevant, not a generic bandwidth footnote: NVLink-domain all-to-all bandwidth is the dominant lever on MoE dispatch/combine MFU (per the DeepSeek-670B 18x-faster-collective finding above), and Rubin doubles the raw headroom on exactly that primitive relative to Blackwell. [ml-engineering repo, network/README.md] [CODE] 07/2026

But Rubin’s own spec sheet is still moving as of its most recent public reveal, in directions that complicate a simple “wait for the bigger number” argument. Rubin GPU: 35 PFLOPS dense FP4 (3.5x GB200), but BF16/TF32 tensor-core width only grows 1.6x over Blackwell — a signal that NVIDIA expects training and inference to keep migrating to FP8/FP4, not that BF16 workloads get a proportionate speedup. HBM4 target is 22 TB/s at 10.8 GT/s pin speed, but memory suppliers are reportedly struggling to hit that above-JEDEC-spec ask — Samsung’s own ISSCC 2026 disclosure shows only ~50% front-end yield on the 1c DRAM node through 2025, and the informed prior is initial shipments land closer to ~20 TB/s. TDP was raised late in the design cycle from 1,800W to 2,300W (Max-P), and VR NVL72 rack TDP reaches 180–220 kW versus GB200/GB300’s 120–140 kW, with NVIDIA’s roadmap explicitly targeting 1 MW/rack within a couple of years. SemiAnalysis, “Vera Rubin – Extreme Co-Design” [SA-PAID] Feb 2026; [SemiAnalysis, “ISSCC 2026 Roundup”] [SA-PAID] Apr 2026

NVIDIA’s own GTC 2026 recap resolves a real ambiguity about whether Rubin forces a co-packaged-optics transition on the reader’s cluster design: it does not, at least not yet. Rubin NVL72 and Rubin Ultra NVL72/144/288 stay entirely copper for scale-up; only Rubin Ultra NVL576 (8 racks bridged) and the following Feynman NVL1152 generation introduce CPO, and only rack-to-rack, not within a rack — copper remains NVIDIA’s medium of choice as long as physically possible, because pushing NVLink SerDes rates further is itself described as “a challenging feat” requiring unproven bidirectional echo cancellation. SemiAnalysis, “Nvidia GTC 2026 recap” [SA-PAID] Mar 2026

The broader supply-side signal cuts against an aggressive wait-for-Rubin strategy: AI workloads (accelerator + host CPU + networking) are modeled to consume just under 60% of TSMC N3 wafer output in 2026, rising to 86% in 2027, with effective N3 utilization projected to exceed 100% of nameplate capacity in H2 2026 — every major accelerator family converges onto the same constrained N3 process, and HBM (already ~3x the wafer-per-bit cost of commodity DRAM, rising to 4x with HBM4) competes directly with server DRAM for the same fab capacity. SemiAnalysis, “The Great AI Silicon Shortage” [SA-PAID] Mar 2026 Read together: GB200 is field-validated, if imperfectly, and its dense-FP8/BF16 peak, HBM capacity, and rack power envelope are settled numbers; Rubin’s headline FP4/HBM4/NVLink6 numbers are directionally compelling — especially for a genuinely EP-heavy MoE, where the NVLink6 link-count doubling matters more than any other single Rubin spec — but its TDP, realized HBM4 bandwidth, and CPO scope were still moving as of its most recent disclosure, and it sits behind the same N3/HBM4 supply constraint squeezing every other 2026–2027 accelerator family. A program with a 2026 procurement deadline is choosing GB200/GB300 not because it is unambiguously superior on paper, but because it is the only option with settled specs and a maturing reliability track record; a program with genuine flexibility to slip into 2027 has a real, quantified reason — not just a hype-cycle reason — to wait for Rubin specifically because of the EP all-to-all doubling. See Ch16 for how this delta would reshape the parallelism math if the build timeline allows it.

The Alternative Silicon Verdicts

AWS Trainium2/3 (Project Rainier). Anthropic’s disclosed Trainium2 chip count roughly doubled in six months — from “nearly half a million” (Oct 2025) to a present-tense “currently over one million Trainium2 chips” (Apr 20, 2026) — with the same announcement confirming an internal Trainium4 designation already inside the AWS-Anthropic contract. Anthropic/Amazon official announcement [OFFICIAL-BLOG] Apr 20, 2026 Trainium2 is 96 GiB HBM3 @ 2.9 TB/s, 1,299 FP8/667 BF16 TFLOPS/chip — a 3.85x raw-FLOPs gap versus GB200 per chip, with SemiAnalysis’s blunt framing that “400k Trainium2 is less raw FLOPs than a 100k GB200 cluster.” The 64-chip Trn2 UltraServer’s 3D torus is asymmetric — the Z-axis carries only 64 GB/s versus 128 GB/s on X/Y, a topological wart invisible in spec-sheet comparisons that a TPU’s symmetric ICI mesh does not share. SemiAnalysis, “Amazon’s AI Self Sufficiency” [ANALYSIS] ~2025 Software maturity is the real open question: NKI only reached Beta→Stable in April 2026 — roughly 16 months after Trainium2’s late-2024 GA — with AWS’s own launch blog admitting hand-tuned kernel development requires expertise “few teams can afford”; PyTorch/XLA is being fully retired the same window for a ground-up native-PyTorch/TorchNeuron rewrite; and the NeuronX Collective Communication Library’s all-to-all gap — the exact primitive MoE dispatch/combine needs — has reportedly been outstanding since roughly 2022. Vendor-disclosed MFU brackets the gap directly: Trainium3 Day-0 PyTorch-native/torch.compile hits only 20–30% BF16 MFU on MoE workloads, rising to ~40% (DeepSeek-670B-class) with hand-written NKI kernels. SemiAnalysis, “AWS Trainium3 Deep Dive” [ANALYSIS] Dec 2025 No public Trainium2/3 MFU number exists for a DeepSeek/Kimi-scale MoE pretraining run — the same disclosure gap this book finds on every non-Nvidia platform.

Google TPU v7 (Ironwood) / TPU 8t. Sundar Pichai stated on the record that Google trains and serves its own frontier models on Ironwood, but no Google source names a specific Gemini generation with citable specificity — even SemiAnalysis’s own deep-dive, while confirming “Gemini 3 was trained entirely on TPUs,” does not specify which generation. Sundar Pichai, X [OFFICIAL X POST] Nov 6, 2025 Google bifurcated training and inference silicon outright five months later: TPU 8t (training, 9,600 chips/pod, 3x Ironwood’s processing power) versus TPU 8i (inference, 1,152-chip pods, 3x on-chip SRAM), announced at Cloud Next, April 22, 2026 — a fast reversal of the “one chip trains and serves” framing Ironwood used at GA five months earlier. Google Cloud Next [OFFICIAL-BLOG/TALK] Apr 22, 2026 SemiAnalysis’s MFU/TCO breakeven math is the sharpest quantified case for TPU economics available: at Nvidia’s ~30% assumed training MFU baseline, Google needs only ~15% MFU (internal) or ~19% (Anthropic’s GCP-priced floor) to match GB300’s cost-per-effective-FLOP, and Anthropic’s realized ~40% MFU implies ~52% lower TCO per effective PFLOP once GCP’s external margin is included. SemiAnalysis, “TPUv7: Google Takes a Swing at the King” [ANALYSIS] Nov 2025 Apple pretrains its cloud foundation model on the latest-generation TPU while serving its largest cloud model on Nvidia GPUs hosted inside Google Cloud — a clean illustration that training-chip and inference-chip choices diverge even within one model family. Apple Machine Learning Research [OFFICIAL-BLOG] Jun 2026 TPU is the most credible non-Nvidia software-maturity story here (MaxText/Pathways, a decade of Google-internal hardening), but it shares the same MLPerf-absence gap as every other non-Nvidia platform (below).

Huawei Ascend / CloudMatrix 384. Ascend 910C is a dual-die package, 128 GB HBM2e @ 6.4 TB/s, built on SMIC 7nm(N+2) — but the binding constraint is national-level HBM supply, not chip design: CXMT’s domestic HBM output caps China at roughly 250,000–300,000 Ascend-910C-equivalent chips/year, well short of Huawei’s own 600,000-unit 2026 target, as the TSMC-fabricated die bank that has cushioned production runs dry within the 2026 window. SemiAnalysis, “Huawei Ascend Production Ramp” [ANALYSIS/CREDIBLE-HEARSAY] 2025-2026 The single most concrete named-lab data point on Ascend’s frontier-pretraining readiness remains DeepSeek’s reported R2 experience: urged to shift training to Ascend, the attempt reportedly hit unstable performance, slow interconnect, and immature CANN tooling, and DeepSeek reverted to Nvidia for training while keeping Ascend for inference only. Tom’s Hardware/TrendForce [REPORTED] Aug 2025 The counter-datapoint is Huawei’s own team: PanGu Ultra MoE, 718B parameters trained on 6,000 Ascend NPUs, reached 30.0% MFU after optimization (up from 18.9% at baseline), via simulation-driven architecture co-design tuned specifically to Ascend’s NPU characteristics. arXiv:2505.04519 [PAPER] May 2025 A June 2026 update shows a Huawei-led consortium completing full-parameter post-training of a 1.6T-parameter DeepSeek-V4-Pro on 1,000+ Ascend 910C chips across 1,500+ iterations with zero interruptions — genuine evidence of sustained large-cluster reliability — but the reporting is explicit this “does not demonstrate the chips can pre-train a frontier model from scratch,” and no source discloses where V4-Pro actually pretrained. Tom’s Hardware/Gigazine/Let’s Data Science [REPORTED] Jun 2026 The honest 2026 verdict: post-training-scale reliability on Ascend is now credibly demonstrated by an external-to-Huawei consortium; full pretraining-from-scratch at frontier scale remains undemonstrated outside Huawei’s own PanGu team.

AMD MI355X/MI400. MLPerf Training v6.0 (June 2026) was AMD’s strongest public showing yet and the first built on Primus, its unified training framework, debuting a production MXFP4 recipe: MI355X came within ~5–6% of B200 on Llama3.1-8B pretraining (FP4) and within ~5% on Llama2-70B LoRA fine-tuning, a 3.46x generational leap over MI300X on the LoRA benchmark. AMD did not submit the new, frontier-relevant DeepSeek-V3-671B MoE benchmark that round. AMD/ROCm blogs [OFFICIAL-BLOG] Jun 2026 The OpenAI-AMD deal (6 GW total, first 1 GW of MI450 from H2 2026) never disambiguates training from inference, and given MI450 deployment doesn’t begin until H2 2026, any AMD-trained frontier model is at best mid-training as of this writing — no publicly disclosed frontier-scale pretraining run exists on AMD silicon as of July 2026. OpenAI/AMD official [OFFICIAL-BLOG] Oct 2025

Is 100k GB200 the Right Assumption?

The sharpest answer available in this corpus is a benchmark absence, not a spec-sheet comparison. When MLPerf Training v6.0 (June 16, 2026) added the industry’s first third-party-audited, standardized large-MoE training benchmark — 671B parameters, DeepSeek-V3-shaped — Nvidia was the only platform to submit a result, in its own words “the only platform to submit on every test.” No AMD, Google/TPU, or AWS/Trainium entry exists for this benchmark, even though AMD submitted to other v6.0 categories that same round and Google is nominally a v6.0 participant. CoreWeave’s winning submission trained DeepSeek-V3 671B to target quality on 8,192 GB300 NVL72 GPUs in 2.02 minutes, with per-GPU throughput rising from 1,298 to 1,648 TFLOPS/GPU in the three months between MLPerf rounds — pure software maturation, no hardware change, and a concrete illustration of how much MFU headroom a mature Nvidia software stack still finds generation-over-generation. MLCommons/Nvidia/CoreWeave [BENCHMARK] Jun 16, 2026 Every other non-Nvidia performance number in this chapter — Trainium’s disclosed FLOPS, TPU’s SemiAnalysis-estimated MFU, Ascend’s PanGu MFU — is vendor-reported or single-lab, not independently reproduced under MLCommons audit rules.

This does not mean the alternatives do not work: Anthropic’s >1M-Trainium2-chip and Google’s Gemini-on-TPU deployments are real, at-scale, production counter-evidence, and PanGu Ultra MoE is a genuine, if narrow, existence proof for Ascend. But as of July 2026, Nvidia is the only platform with a third-party-audited large-MoE training result at all, a materially sharper and more falsifiable claim than the generic “GPUs still dominate” framing. The reader’s answer to “is 100k GB200 the right assumption” should be conditional: yes, as the only platform with an audited data point at this workload class, with three qualifications. First, by the time an actual procurement lands, the relevant conversation has already substantially shifted from GB200 to GB300 — GB300 is 1.6x GB200’s speed at identical scale on the MLPerf DeepSeek-V3 benchmark, and most 2026-vintage TCO modeling in this corpus already uses GB300 as its reference SKU. Second, GB200’s own reliability envelope was still described as immature as recently as August 2025, and no source confirms whether a genuinely months-long, 100,000-GPU-scale frontier pretraining run has yet survived on GB200/GB300-class hardware as of mid-2026. Third, Trainium’s scale trajectory (roughly doubling every six months) is real enough that a program with genuine multi-vendor flexibility should not treat single-sourcing Nvidia as risk-free — it is simply the option with an audited number behind it.

Implications for the 10T/200B Run

  • Use the corrected dense-FP8 peak. 5,000 TFLOPS/GPU (GB200 NVL72), not the frequently-conflated 4,500-TFLOPS HGX B200 figure, as the denominator for every MFU and critical-batch calculation in this book; see Ch16 for the corrected downstream thresholds.
  • Plan MFU around the MoE band, not the dense band. Vendor-published, apples-to-apples data (NVIDIA’s own Megatron-Bridge tables) puts MoE MFU at 9–18% versus 32–39% for dense, on identical hardware — and a genuinely 2%-active 10T/200B design is sparser than any measured data point in this corpus (DeepSeek-V3 at 5.5% active gets 18%), so budget toward the low end, not the middle, absent a specific reason to expect better.
  • Price the reliability tax into TCO, not just into contingency. GB200’s headline perf/TCO advantage over H100 for MoE workloads (up to 50% cheaper) compresses to 20–30% once hot-spare-rack opportunity cost is charged; budget rack-level, not node-level, SLAs and provision accordingly (see Ch19).
  • Treat NVLink6/Rubin as a genuine, quantified alternative if the timeline allows it — the link-count doubling (18→36 links) is specifically valuable for an EP-heavy MoE’s all-to-all traffic, not a generic speed bump — but recognize that Rubin’s TDP, realized HBM4 bandwidth, and CPO scope were all still moving targets as of its most recent public disclosure, and that the same N3/HBM4 supply constraint squeezing GB200/GB300 procurement squeezes Rubin too.
  • Do not budget on an audited non-Nvidia alternative as a hedge. As of MLPerf Training v6.0 (June 2026), no platform besides Nvidia has submitted an independently-audited large-MoE training result; every alternative-silicon number in a vendor’s own favor should be read as the vendor’s best case, not a validated floor.
