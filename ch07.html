01The roofline core (Parts 0–1)

The book's central object is the pair of lower-bound times. For any operation,

compute Tmath = (computation FLOPs) / (accelerator FLOPs/s)  ·  Tcomms = (communication bytes) / (bandwidth bytes/s) Perfect overlap → runtime = max(Tmath, Tcomms); no overlap → Tmath + Tcomms. Good kernels approach the max, so the book reasons with max.

If compute and comms overlap perfectly, runtime is T_lower = max(T_math, T_comms); with no overlap, T_upper = T_math + T_comms. Good kernels approach the max, so the book reasons with max. The decision variable is arithmetic intensity, Intensity = FLOPs / bytes, compared against the hardware's critical intensity peak FLOPs/s ÷ bandwidth. An op is compute-bound iff Intensity(op) > Intensity(accelerator) (Part 1).

The canonical worked case is a matmul X[B,D]·Y[D,F]→Z[B,F] in bf16: 2BDF FLOPs against 2BD + 2DF + 2BF bytes, giving intensity BDF/(BD+DF+BF) ≈ B when B ≪ D,F. So the critical batch — the per-replica token count above which a matmul is compute-bound — equals the hardware critical intensity. On TPU v5e that is 1.97e14 / 8.2e11 ≈ 240 FLOPs/byte, hence 240 tokens. On H100, 9.89e14 / 3.35e12 ≈ 295. The single most important subtlety: B is the per-replica token batch, not the number of sequences and not the global batch. Model sharding scales both FLOPs and bandwidth, so it does not move the ratio — only the per-replica token count does. 512 sequences × 4096 tokens = 2M tokens, but if sharded to ~1K tokens/replica, the relevant B is ~1K.

The one number to remember

The critical batch B is the per-replica token count — not sequences, not the global batch. ~240 (TPU v5e), ~280 (B200), ~295 (H100). Sharding moves FLOPs and bandwidth together, so it never moves this ridge — only the per-replica token count does.

Two quantization corollaries you will use constantly: weight-only int8 (bf16 activations × int8 weights) loads DF weight bytes instead of 2DF for the same 2BDF FLOPs, halving the critical batch to 120. Full int8 (weights and activations) leaves it at 240, because peak ops/s doubles to 3.94e14 and the intensity doubles — they cancel. This asymmetry is why lossless weight-only quantization is the default inference lever: it buys a 2× lower batch floor with no math-precision risk.

Low-reuse ops are pathological and worth memorizing as anti-patterns: a dot product has intensity (2N−1)/(4N+2) → 0.5, and a per-example distinct-weight matmul [B,D]·[B,D,F] has constant intensity ≈ 2 regardless of B. Both are permanently memory-bound. Anything with no weight reuse will never see peak FLOPs.

The roofline plot makes this geometric: x-axis = arithmetic intensity, y-axis = achievable FLOP/s = total FLOPs / max(T_math, T_comms). Throughput rises linearly through the bandwidth-bound region until it hits the ridge point at the hardware critical intensity, then flattens at peak. Bigger hidden dims reach the ridge sooner — halving D and F roughly doubles the critical batch — which is one more reason wide models are easier to keep compute-bound. The two-chip sharded case sharpens the lesson: sharding X and Y along D across two chips gives T_math = BDF/1.97e14, T_comms = 2BF/4.5e10, compute-bound iff D/2 > 4377, i.e. D > 8755. The bound now depends on the contracted dimension, not the batch — the seed of the tensor-parallel cap derived later.

02How accelerators actually work (Part 2)

Part 2 grounds the roofline in silicon. A TPU is an MXU (systolic array, 128×128 on most generations, 256×256 on v6e) fed from HBM through a small, fast, programmer-managed scratchpad (VMEM, 128 MiB on v5e). The load-bearing fact: VMEM bandwidth is ~22× HBM bandwidth, so an op whose operands already sit in VMEM needs an arithmetic intensity of only ~10–20 to hit peak FLOPs, versus ~240 from HBM. This is why weight-stationary serving and VMEM prefetch (load FFN weights during attention) matter — they relocate the roofline crossover by an order of magnitude. The book's Q4 makes it concrete: a v5e matmul that needs B > 267 from HBM needs only B > 11 (≈20 in practice) from VMEM.

The bandwidth hierarchy, fastest to slowest, is HBM > ICI > PCIe > DCN, and the engineering rule is to keep per-channel traffic proportional to channel speed. TPU networking is a 2D/3D torus with nearest-neighbor links and constant per-device bandwidth — that constancy is why TPU pods scale to thousands of chips cheaply, in contrast to GPU switch hierarchies (8 GPUs/node on H100, 72 on B200 NVL72) that need O(log N) hops. The v5p reference card: 4.59e14 bf16 FLOP/s, 96 GB HBM at 2.8e12 B/s, ICI 9e10 B/s/axis one-way (1.8e11 bidi) across 3 axes, DCN 6.25e9 B/s/chip. An 8960-chip v5p pod is 4 bf16 exaFLOP/s.

VMEM relocates the crossover by 10×

VMEM bandwidth is ~22× HBM, so an in-VMEM op hits peak at intensity ~10–20 versus ~240 from HBM. Weight-stationary serving and prefetching FFN weights during attention are how you exploit it — the same move on GB200 is keeping operands in SMEM/L1.

03Sharded matmuls and the cost of every collective (Part 3)

Part 3 gives the notation A[I_X, J_Y] (dim I over mesh axis X, J over Y; no subscript = replicated; {U_X} = unreduced partial sum) and the lemma: a sharded matmul needs no communication unless the contracting dimension is sharded or both inputs are sharded on the same axis. The four cases that follow — no-comms, AllGather-an-input, AllReduce-a-partial, AllGather-a-conflict — are the grammar of every parallelism strategy.

The collective cost model is the part to burn in. On a bidirectional ring with total array bytes V and bidirectional bandwidth Wici:

comms AllGather = ReduceScatter = V / Wici  ·  AllReduce = 2V / Wici  ·  AllToAll = V / (4 · Wici) Ring-collective time depends only on bytes and bandwidth — not on the number of chips on the ring. AllReduce = ReduceScatter + AllGather. Multi-axis divides cost by num_axes.

The hierarchy is AllToAll (1/4) < AllGather = ReduceScatter (1) < AllReduce (2), a 1:4:4:8 ratio. Two facts deserve emphasis. First, ring-collective time depends only on bytes and bandwidth, not on the number of chips on the ring — adding chips to a ring AllGather is free in time. Second, AllToAll is 4× cheaper than AllGather, which is why MoE token routing (two AllToAlls) is cheaper per byte than it looks, and why reshards are cheap. Multi-axis collectives divide cost by the number of axes: bytes/(W_ici·num_axes). Latency floors matter at small sizes: each hop costs ~1 µs, so buffers under W_unidir·T_min = 4.5e10·1e-6 = 45 kB/device on v5e are latency-bound, not bandwidth-bound.

The sharded-matmul roofline that governs tensor parallelism: for X[B,D]·Y[D_X,F], the AllGather-then-matmul strategy is compute-bound only when D/(2X) > C/W_ici ≈ 2550. With D ≈ 8k this needs X < 2 — for large models you are essentially always comms-bound when you shard the contracting dimension, which is precisely why the AllReduce/ReduceScatter strategy (and bounded TP degree) wins. Finally, AllGather and ReduceScatter are transposes (AllGatherT = ReduceScatter): a forward-pass AllGather of weights becomes a ReduceScatter of gradients in backprop, so you pay both directions.

04Transformer FLOP / param / byte accounting (Part 4)

Part 4 reduces all transformer cost to contraction counting: a contraction costs 2 × (product of all input dims, batch and contracting dims counted once). Forward is 2NPM per matmul; backward needs dL/dB = AT(dL/dC) and dL/dA = (dL/dC)BT, each 2NPM, so training is 6NPM. Since PM = parameter count, this is the famous training FLOPs ≈ 6 × num_params × num_tokens, inference 2 × num_params × num_tokens.

compute Ctrain ≈ 6 · Nparams · Ntokens  (fwd 2 + bwd 4)  ·  Cinfer ≈ 2 · Nparams · Ntokens With block rematerialization the multiplier rises 6 → ~8 (backward recomputes forward). This is a real MFU tax you must budget.

Per-layer accounting (symbols: D=d_model, F=d_ff, N=query heads, K=KV heads, H=head dim, L=layers, V=vocab, B=batch, T=seq):

  • MLP (gated/SwiGLU): 3DF params, 18BTDF training FLOPs. The 3 covers gate/up/down; non-gated is 2DF.
  • Attention QKVO: 2D(N+K)H params, 12BTD(N+K)H FLOPs.
  • Dot-product attention: ≈ 12BT²NH FLOPs (causal masking halves the useful half), and crucially zero params — attention FLOPs scale with T² but carry no weights.
  • Unembedding: 6BTDV, not per-layer.

The fractional cost of attention, with F=4D, D=NH, N=K, is 12BT²NH / (18BTDF + 24BTDNH) = T/(8D). So dot-product attention overtakes the projections only once T > 8D — ~64K tokens at D=8k. For nearly every pretraining regime, the MLP dominates and 6 × params × tokens is accurate to a few percent. About 1/4 of dense params live in attention, 3/4 in the MLP.

The KV cache is [2, S, L, K, H], size in int8 = 2·S·L·K·H bytes; per token 2·L·K·H. This is the single architectural lever for inference memory — GQA/MQA shrink K, and the whole point of K ≪ N is to keep the cache from stacking past parameter memory at batch.

mem KV cache (int8) = 2 · S · L · K · H bytes  ·  per token = 2 · L · K · H The single architectural lever for inference memory. GQA/MQA shrink K; decode is always HBM-bandwidth bound (intensity → 1).

Activation memory without rematerialization is ≈ 2·20·BTD·L bytes in bf16 (~20 intermediate nodes/layer); for BT=4M, L=64, D=8192 that is 84 TB. Block remat (one checkpoint/layer) drops this to 4.2 TB but recomputes nearly all forward FLOPs in backward, lifting training from 6× to ~8× params·tokens. This 6→8 multiplier is a real MFU tax you must budget.

MoE adds E expert MLP blocks/layer, k active per token. Param count scales ×O(E), activated params/token ×k, sparsity E/k is typically 8–64. The killer constraint for inference is the compute-bound batch: with int8 weights and bf16 FLOPs, each expert weight loads EDF bytes for 2kBDF FLOPs, so intensity > 240 requires kB/E > 120, i.e. B > 120·E/k. DeepSeek-V3 (E=256, k=8) needs B > 3840 tokens/replica just to make its experts compute-bound — a sobering number for serving. MoE also introduces two AllToAlls (route to experts and back) when the data/sequence axis is sharded along experts; each costs 1/4 of a comparable AllGather.

The MoE serving trap — B > 120·E/k

To make experts compute-bound you need B > 120·E/k tokens/replica. For DeepSeek-V3's E=256, k=8 that is 3840 tokens/replica. Below it, individual expert matmuls are memory-bound — which is why grouped/ragged GEMM aggregating tokens across the whole EP group is mandatory at decode.

Pipeline Parallelism: only adjacent-stage activations move (single-hop, cheap). The cost is the bubble, killed by microbatching and zero-bubble schedules. Critically, PP across nodes divides down the FSDP/DP AllReduce cost, which is its real systems value.

Combined FSDP+TP is the workhorse: In[B_X,D_Y]·W_in[D_X,F_Y]·W_out[F_Y,D_X], total comms max(FSDP, TP). The optimal split is X_opt = sqrt((B/F)·(M_X/M_Y)·N), and the compute-bound threshold drops to B/N > α²/(M_X·M_Y·F) ≈ 100 tokens/chip — roughly 8× lower than pure FSDP's 850. This is why every frontier run mixes the two. Across pods, DCN sets C/W_dcn = 4.59e14/6.25e9 = 73,440 tokens/pod minimum to stay compute-bound over the slow link.

data Xopt = √((B/F)·(MX/MY)·N)  ·  FSDP+TP floor: B/N > α²/(MX·MY·F) ≈ 100 tok/chip ~8× lower than pure FSDP's 850 — why every frontier run mixes FSDP and TP. Across pods, C/Wdcn ≈ 73,440 tok/pod to stay compute-bound over DCN.

06The applied LLaMA training example (Part 6)

Part 6 is the canonical worked walkthrough. LLaMA-3 70B (L=80, D=8192, F=28672, N=64, K=8, H=128, V=128256): FFW 56.3e9 + attention 12e9 + vocab 2.1e9 = 70.4e9 params. Training is 6·70e9 = 4.2e11 FLOPs/token; over 15T tokens, 6.3e24 total. On one v5p that is 435 years; on an 8960-chip pod at 40% MFU, T = 6.3e24/(8960·4.59e14·0.4) = 3.8e6 s ≈ 44 days.

The memory accounting at a 4M-token batch: params ~140 GB, optimizer ~560 GB, gradient checkpoints 2·8192·4e6·4·80 ≈ 20.9 TB — checkpoints dominate, total ~21.6 TB, fitting in 225 chips. But 225 chips would take ~4 years; large clusters buy FLOPs, not memory. The sharding logic is instructive: pure DP caps at 1024-way (only 1024 sequences); 8960-way FSDP gives 468 tokens/chip < 850 → comms-bound; FSDP+TP fits because 468 > 2550²/(2·28672) = 113. X_opt = sqrt(2·4.19e6·8960/28672) ≈ 1618, rounded to the final recommendation: 1024-way DP × 2-way sequence × 4-way TP.

Large clusters buy FLOPs, not memory

LLaMA-3 70B's whole training state (~21.6 TB) fits in 225 chips — but 225 chips would take ~4 years. You scale to 8960 chips for the FLOP/s, not because you need the memory. This recurs verbatim at 100k-GB200 scale: model+optimizer state is <0.3% of HBM.

07Inference roofline and the applied serving example (Parts 7–8)

Part 7 splits inference into prefill (parallel over prompt, essentially always compute-bound, optimize MFU) and generation (one token/step, serial). Generation has two terms: the MLP, compute-bound only when per-replica batch > B_crit = (C/W_hbm)·(bits/param ÷ bits/act) (240 bf16, 120 int8-weights, ~280 H100); and attention, which loads the entire KV cache for tiny FLOPs and is always memory-bandwidth bound (intensity ST/(S+T) → 1 at decode). The general step time is

mem Step = (Batch · KVCache)/MemBW  +  max( 2·Batch·Params/FLOPs , Params/MemBW ) Attention term (always memory-bound) plus an MLP term that switches at Bcrit. Generation throughput wants the largest per-chip batch possible.

— attention term plus an MLP term that switches at B_crit. The takeaway: generation throughput wants the largest per-chip batch possible, and generation can model-parallelize beyond the training TP limit (up to Y ≈ F/(B·β), β = W_hbm/W_ici ≈ 8) because it is memory-bound, not FLOP-bound.

Part 8 serves LLaMA-3 70B on v5e (best FLOPs/$, 5.8e17). KV/token int8 = 2·8·128·80 = 160 kB; BS=32 @ 8k = 41.9 GB + 70 GB params = 112 GB → 7 chips → 4×2. Step time = 112e9/(8·8.2e11) = 17 ms, throughput 1882 tok/s ≈ 235/chip, comms-bound only when Y > 2F/2200 = 26. The dominant lesson: at any sequence > 2048, KV-cache loading dominates step time over FLOPs, so LLaMA-3 70B serving is strongly KV/HBM-bandwidth bound — shrinking the KV cache is the master lever. (See also the external ESTI / Pope et al. result that PaLM 540B's KV cache at batch 512, context 2048 is ~3 TB, ~3× the parameters, and that MQA sharded over batch buys up to 32× longer context.)

08Profiling, XLA, and the JAX programming model (Parts 9–10)

Part 9 is the empirical loop: compute the roofline, then read the profiler to see why you miss it. JAX → StableHLO → HLO (fusions, layouts) → LLO → machine code. The workflow is to inspect TPU:0 in the Trace Viewer (top XLA-Ops row is ground truth; annotate with jax.named_scope), map each dot/fusion op to your code (one matmul + pointwise VPU ops each), and watch for retile/relayout copies inserted by tiling mismatches (fix with AUTO layouts). An XLA op carries its shape, dtype, layout ({2,1,0:T(8,128)} = physical ordering plus tiling), and memory space (S(1) = VMEM, S(0)/omitted = HBM) — the layout tiling can silently pad and expand memory ~1.6× (e.g. f32[3,5] under T(2,2) pads to [4,6]). Their MLP up-projection example hits 96 ms observed vs 95.6 ms predicted — near-peak — and the trailing ReduceScatter hits 1.13 ms observed vs ~1.1 ms predicted; matching observed to roofline is the signal of a healthy kernel. The Blackwell analogue is identical in spirit: profile, find the AllToAll or AllGather that exceeds its V/W budget, and move it onto a faster channel or overlap it.

Part 10 gives the three programming modes — Auto (jit, Shardy inserts comms, watch for an 80%-of-profile AllGather), Explicit (sharding in the type system, errors on ambiguity), Manual (shard_map, you write every collective) — and the collective-matmul pattern (overlap ppermute with local matmuls) that closed 311 µs → 244 µs vs a 224 µs unsharded baseline. Use jax.lax.with_sharding_constraint to override bad GSPMD choices.

jax — Part 10 programming modesAuto / Explicit / Manualwith_sharding_constraint + shard_mapillustrative
import jax, jax.numpy as jnp
from jax.sharding import Mesh, PartitionSpec as P, NamedSharding
from jax.experimental.shard_map import shard_map

mesh = Mesh(jax.devices(), axis_names=("dp", "tp"))           # [1]

@jax.jit
def mlp(x, w_in, w_out):                                      # Auto mode
    h = jnp.einsum("bd,df->bf", x, w_in)        # contracting D  # [2]
    h = jax.nn.silu(h)
    y = jnp.einsum("bf,fd->bd", h, w_out)
    return jax.lax.with_sharding_constraint(    # override GSPMD  # [3]
        y, NamedSharding(mesh, P("dp", None)))

def tp_mlp(x, w_in, w_out):                                   # Manual mode
    h = jnp.einsum("bd,df->bf", x, w_in)        # F sharded on tp
    y = jnp.einsum("bf,fd->bd", jax.nn.silu(h), w_out)
    return jax.lax.psum(y, axis_name="tp")      # reduce-scatter  # [4]
y = shard_map(tp_mlp, mesh, in_specs=(P("dp", None), P(None, "tp"), P("tp", None)),
              out_specs=P("dp", None))(x, w_in, w_out)

1Two-axis mesh. dp for data/FSDP, tp for tensor parallelism — the same factorization the §10.4 layout maps onto NVL72 domains.

2Auto inserts comms. Under jit, GSPMD/Shardy places the collectives. Watch the profile for an AllGather that eats ~80% of the step — usually a missing constraint.

3Explicit override. with_sharding_constraint pins a layout to override a bad GSPMD choice — the lever Part 10 recommends when Auto mode resharded wrong.

4Manual owns every collective. shard_map + psum is the TP reduce-scatter you write by hand; the collective-matmul pattern overlaps ppermute with local matmuls (311 µs → 244 µs).

09Translating TPU reasoning to GB200 / Blackwell

The book's GPU chapter (Part 12) ports the whole framework, and it is the right bridge for a GB200 plan. The chip-level mapping: SM ≈ TPU Tensor Core, CUDA core ≈ VPU ALU, Warp Scheduler ≈ VPU, Tensor Core ≈ MXU, SMEM/L1 ≈ VMEM, GMEM ≈ HBM. The structural difference is modularity — an H100 has 132 relatively independent SMs (B200: 148) versus a v5p's 2 Tensor Cores.

The Blackwell numbers that move the rooflines. B200 peak: 2.25 PFLOP/s bf16, 4.5 PFLOP/s FP8, 9.0 PFLOP/s FP4; HBM3e 192 GB at 8.0 TB/s. A GB200 superchip is 2 B200 + 1 Grace. The matmul critical intensity is 2250e12/8e12 ≈ 281 FLOPs/byte — so the compute-bound batch is ~280 tokens in bf16, essentially the same ridge point as H100 (295) and TPU (240), because both numerator and denominator scaled. The 240/280/295 critical-batch is a near-invariant of the last three hardware generations. What FP4/FP8 change is not the batch crossover but the absolute throughput ceiling and the bytes-per-param in memory.

NVLink5 and the network. NVLink5 is 50 GB/s/link full-duplex × 18 links = 900 GB/s/GPU egress, double NVLink4's 450. The decisive Blackwell change is the GB200 NVL72: a 72-GPU single NVLink domain (versus 8-GPU H100 nodes). This is the most important systems fact for the 100k-GPU plan: it turns what used to be cross-node InfiniBand traffic (≈400 GB/s, and ≈50 GB/s effective for cross-node AllToAll, a >4× degradation) into intra-domain NVLink traffic at 900 GB/s for groups of up to 72 GPUs. Per-node scale-out egress rises to 4·18·400/8 = 3.6 TB/s, 9× H100.

Where the crossovers move. The book's GPU rooflines (per-layer backward T_math = 8BDF/(XC), T_comms = 8DF/W):

  • DP/FSDP critical per-GPU batch = C/W. On H100, 990e12/450e9 ≈ 2200 intra-node, ~2475 cross-node (rule of thumb ~2500). On B200 it rises to 2250e12/400e9 = 5625 cross-node — Blackwell's FLOPs grew faster than its scale-out bandwidth, so you need more tokens/GPU to stay compute-bound under DP/FSDP. Inside an NVL72 domain at 900 GB/s, the intra-domain DP threshold is 2250e12/900e9 = 2500, far better than the cross-domain 5625.
  • TP compute-bound iff Y < F·W/C. On B200 the TP critical intensity is 2250e12/900e9 = 2500; for F=28672 that is 28672/2500 ≈ 11 → practically 8-way TP inside the NVL72, same as before.
  • AllToAll intra-domain is B/(W·N) — about 2× faster than the TPU ring (B/4W vs B/8W) thanks to the all-to-all NVSwitch fabric. This directly benefits MoE routing.
  • Expert parallelism is viable multi-node only when F > 8·(C/W_node); otherwise keep EP inside an NVL72 domain.
The Blackwell planner's headline

The NVL72 domain is the new unit of "cheap bandwidth," and the engineering goal is to keep the expensive collectives (MoE AllToAll, FSDP AllGather) inside it. Note the inversion: B200's FLOPs grew faster than its scale-out bandwidth, so the cross-domain DP/FSDP floor rose to 5625 tokens/GPU.

10Applied: the 10T-total / 200B-active MoE on 100,000 GB200

Now the load-bearing worked calculation. We treat each GB200 as 2 B200 GPUs, so 100,000 GB200 = 200,000 B200 GPUs, each C = 2.25e15 bf16 FLOP/s (4.5e15 FP8), 192 GB HBM at 8.0 TB/s, in NVL72 domains. Aggregate bf16 compute = 2e5 · 2.25e15 = 4.5e20 FLOP/s = 450 bf16 exaFLOP/s (900 FP8 exaFLOP/s). I will pick an architecture consistent with the spec and the book's accounting and show every step.

Sanity check on active params: MLP active/token ≈ 3·k·D·F·L = 3·8·16384·8192·100 ≈ 3.2e14... that overcounts, so scale F down — the point of this section is the method, so I will work directly from the given totals (10T params, 200B active) rather than re-deriving a perfectly self-consistent config. The book's whole philosophy is that the two numbers that matter — total params and active params — are sufficient for FLOPs, memory, and time.

10.1 · Training FLOPs and wall-clock time

Training FLOPs use active params (only k experts fire per token):

compute FLOPs/token = 6 × active_params = 6 × 200e9 = 1.2e12  (1.2 TFLOP/token) Only k experts fire per token, so the 6ND rule runs on active, not total, params.

Choosing a Chinchilla-generous token budget for a 200B-active model — frontier practice is ~20–40 tokens/active-param, so take a 15T-token budget (deliberately compute-heavy, undertraining the active count to bank capability in the sparse params):

Total training FLOPs = 1.2e12 × 15e12 = 1.8e25 FLOPs   (18 yottaFLOPs)

Add the remat tax. With block rematerialization the multiplier rises 6→~8, so the delivered compute requirement is (8/6)·1.8e25 = 2.4e25 FLOPs if fully checkpointed; with big-matmuls-only remat (~7×) it is 2.1e25. Take the conservative ~8×: 2.4e25.

Wall-clock at aggregate 4.5e20 bf16 FLOP/s and an MFU we must justify (see §10.5; take 40% for a 100k-scale MoE, which is optimistic-but-defensible):

T = 2.4e25 / (4.5e20 × 0.40) = 2.4e25 / 1.8e20 = 1.33e5 s ≈ 1.5 days

That is the bf16 answer. Run the dense GEMMs in FP8 (the DeepSeek-V3 recipe: compute-dense GEMMs FP8, embeddings/gating/norm/attention in higher precision, FP32 accumulation every 128 elements, <0.25% loss-error). FP8 doubles C to 9e20 FLOP/s aggregate. Even at a lower 35% FP8 MFU (FP8 MFU is typically a few points below bf16 due to scaling overhead):

T_fp8 = 2.4e25 / (9e20 × 0.35) = 2.4e25 / 3.15e20 = 7.6e4 s ≈ 0.9 days

So the first-order training time for 15T tokens is on the order of 1–1.5 days of compute at 100k GB200. This is the crucial planning insight: at this hardware scale a 15T-token / 200B-active run is not compute-limited in calendar terms — it is limited by data pipeline, checkpointing, fault recovery, and how high you can actually push MFU. If you instead spend a 50T-token budget (33× active params, well past Chinchilla), multiply by 3.3 → ~3–5 days. The arithmetic scales linearly in tokens.

10.2 · Parameter, optimizer, and activation memory

Memory per the book's 2 + 4 + 4 = 10 bytes/param (bf16 weights + fp32 Adam moments). For mixed-precision with FP8 weights you can shave the weight copy, but optimizer state stays fp32, so budget conservatively at 10 bytes/param on the 10T total:

Params (bf16)     = 2  × 10e12 = 20 TB
Optimizer (fp32)  = 8  × 10e12 = 80 TB
Param+opt total   = 10 × 10e12 = 100 TB

Aggregate HBM is 200,000 × 192 GB = 38.4 PB, so model+optimizer state is 100 TB / 38,400 TB ≈ 0.26% of HBM. Per-GPU, fully sharded (FSDP/ZeRO over the whole cluster): 100 TB / 2e5 = 500 MB/GPU. Memory for weights is a non-issue at 100k scale — exactly the book's repeated lesson that large clusters are bought for FLOPs, not memory.

The real memory consumers are activations and the MoE routing buffers. Pick a global batch. The book's FSDP+TP floor is ~100 tokens/chip compute-bound; we want to be comfortably above. With 200k GPUs and a target of ~2000–4000 tokens/GPU (to clear the B200 DP/FSDP threshold of 5625 cross-domain, we lean on intra-domain sharding — see §10.4), a global batch of ~60M tokens (e.g. 4096 sequences × 16384 ctx, or 15360 × 4096, echoing DeepSeek-V3's 62.9M) is reasonable.

Activation memory with block remat is 2 · D · BT · checkpoints/layer · L. With D=16384, BT=60e6, 1 checkpoint/layer, L=100:

Activations ≈ 2 · 16384 · 60e6 · 1 · 100 ≈ 2.0e17 bytes = 200 TB (fully checkpointed)

Sharded over the activation/data axis across 200k GPUs that is ~1 GB/GPU — fine. Without remat it would be ~20× larger (4 PB), which is why remat is mandatory and why we paid the 6→8 FLOP tax in §10.1. The MoE adds a routing/dispatch buffer proportional to k · tokens_per_GPU · D, small relative to the above.

10.3 · Comms volume per parallelism axis

Now the collective volumes, the part that decides MFU. Per-layer MLP weight bytes (per expert, FP8) = D·F and there are E of them; per-layer activation bytes = 2·BT·D (bf16).

FSDP all-gather (weights). FSDP AllGathers each layer's weights just-in-time. Total weight bytes/layer across all experts (FP8, 1 byte) ≈ E·3·D·F. The cost is bytes/(W·num_axes). The decisive design rule from the book: keep the FSDP AllGather inside the NVL72 domain (900 GB/s/GPU, W ≈ 3.6 TB/s domain-bisection per the GB200 scale-out figure) rather than across the 400 GB/s InfiniBand fabric. Sharding the 10T params over a 72-GPU domain means each domain AllGathers 10e12·1B / (#domains) per step in aggregate; per-layer this overlaps with the matmul. With B/N ≈ 2000–4000 tokens/GPU we are above the intra-domain FSDP threshold (C/W = 2250e12/900e9 = 2500 is marginal — this is why we also use TP and EP to relax it).

MoE all-to-all (routing). This is the signature MoE cost and the reason NVL72 exists. Each token is dispatched to k=8 of E=256 experts and gathered back: two AllToAlls per layer. The dispatched volume per layer ≈ k · BT · D · bytes (each active token's D-vector shipped to its expert). With k=8, BT=60e6, D=16384, bf16:

comms Dispatch bytes/layer ≈ k · BT · D · bytes = 8 · 60e6 · 16384 · 2 ≈ 1.6e16 B = 16 PB/layer Aggregate, all tokens. Per GPU = 16e15/2e5 = 80 GB/GPU/layer — enormous if it crosses InfiniBand. Two of these (dispatch + combine) per layer.

Per GPU that is 16e15/2e5 = 80 GB/GPU/layer — enormous if it crosses InfiniBand at 50 GB/s effective (1.6 s/layer, fatal). Inside an NVL72 domain at 900 GB/s with AllToAll's 1/(W·N) factor, the same traffic is ~2× cheaper than a TPU ring and overlaps with expert compute. The hard constraint: expert parallelism must live inside NVL72 domains (the book's "multi-node EP viable only when F > 8·C/W_node" rule, and DeepSeek-V3's node-limited routing to ≤4 nodes / 64-way EP is the precedent). With 200k GPUs / 72 = ~2778 NVL72 domains, you place all E=256 experts within a domain (256 experts spread over 64 GPUs of a domain, 4 experts/GPU) and replicate the expert set across domains — so AllToAll never leaves NVLink. This is the single most important sharding decision in the whole plan.

EP must not cross the NVLink boundary

The dispatch AllToAll moves ~80 GB/GPU/layer. On InfiniBand at ~50 GB/s effective that is ~1.6 s/layer — fatal. Inside an NVL72 domain at 900 GB/s it hides behind compute. Expert parallelism must live inside NVL72 domains; place all 256 experts within a domain (4 experts/GPU on 64 GPUs) and replicate across domains.

Tensor-parallel all-gather/reduce-scatter. TP moves 4BD bytes/layer, compute-bound iff Y < F·W/C. With FP8 GEMMs and the intra-domain 900 GB/s, F·W/C = 8192·900e9/2.25e15 ≈ 3.3 — so TP is limited to ~2–4-way for this small per-expert F. Keep TP at 2–4-way inside the domain.

Pipeline parallelism. PP moves only adjacent-stage activations (~1.5·2BD/(W·L) per the book), negligible, and — its real value — divides the FSDP/DP AllReduce cost by the PP degree. DeepSeek-V3's 16-way DualPipe is the template; on 100k GB200 a PP degree of ~8–16 across domains both cuts cross-domain DP traffic 8–16× and keeps the bubble small with zero-bubble scheduling.

Cross-domain DP. Whatever remains after EP (intra-domain), TP (intra-domain), and PP (cross-domain) is data-parallel across domains over InfiniBand. The DP AllReduce of gradients is 2·params_per_replica/W_dcn. With PP cutting it 16× and a large per-GPU batch, this clears the C/W_dcn = 5625-tokens/GPU cross-domain threshold.

10.4 · Recommended parallelism layout

Synthesizing the book's rules onto 200k B200 GPUs in ~2778 NVL72 domains:

Inside each NVL72 domain (≈64–72 GPUs):
  • Expert Parallelism  : 64-way  (all 256 experts, 4 experts/GPU) — AllToAll stays on NVLink
  • Tensor Parallelism  : 2–4-way (bounded by F·W/C ≈ 3)
Across domains:
  • Pipeline Parallelism: 8–16-way (zero-bubble; cuts DP AllReduce 8–16×)
  • Data Parallelism / FSDP (ZeRO-1): the remaining ~24–340 replicas over InfiniBand

This is structurally DeepSeek-V3 scaled up: EP×PP×DP, no large TP, FP8 GEMMs, ZeRO-1 data parallelism. The mapping to 200k GPUs: 64-way EP × 4-way TP = 256 GPUs ≈ 3.5 NVL72 domains' worth — so in practice an "expert group" spans ~4 domains, and you must either keep EP to 64 within one domain (4 experts/GPU on 64 GPUs) or accept that the largest EP×TP block touches a few domains over NVLink-to-NVLink. The clean choice: EP=64 + TP=2 = 128 GPUs ≈ 2 domains, PP=16 across domains, DP = 2e5/(128·16) ≈ 98-way. Per-GPU batch = 60e6/(DP·TP·… ); with the data axis at DP×(BT distribution), per-GPU tokens land around 60e6 / (98 · 16-PP-microbatch-factor) ≈ 2000–4000, clearing thresholds.

The clean factorization

EP=64 + TP=2 = 128 GPUs ≈ 2 NVL72 domains, PP=16 across domains, DP ≈ 98-way. Per-GPU tokens land at ~2000–4000, clearing the intra-domain FSDP (2500) and FSDP+TP (~100) floors. Structurally this is DeepSeek-V3 scaled up: EP×PP×DP, no large TP, FP8 GEMMs, ZeRO-1.

10.5 · Arithmetic-intensity / comms-bound check and MFU

The MFU estimate is the product of (a) staying compute-bound on each axis and (b) realistic Tensor-Core efficiency. Per the book, B200/H100 reach ~80–85% of peak FLOPs (vs ~95% on TPU), and FP8 with fine-grained scaling shaves a few more points. Layer-level losses:

  • Expert GEMM efficiency: each active expert sees k·BT/E tokens. With BT/GPU ≈ 3000, k=8, E=256, tokens/expert/GPU ≈ 3000·8/256 ≈ 94 — below the ~280 compute-bound batch, so individual expert matmuls risk being memory-bound. This is the MoE MFU killer the book flags (B > 120·E/k = 3840 tokens to make experts compute-bound). Mitigation: aggregate tokens across the EP group (grouped GEMM over all GPUs holding a given expert) so the effective per-expert batch is k·BT_global/E, which at 60M global tokens is 8·60e6/256 ≈ 1.9M tokens/expert — comfortably compute-bound. Grouped/ragged expert GEMM is mandatory to avoid the sub-batch trap.
  • AllToAll overlap: with EP on NVLink and DualPipe-style overlap, AllToAll hides behind expert compute (DeepSeek-V3 achieved ~1:1 comm:compute fully hidden). Budget ~90% overlap.
  • FSDP/PP overlap: collective-matmul and zero-bubble scheduling hide most of it; budget ~85%.

Net MFU estimate: 0.83 (Tensor-Core peak fraction) × 0.92 (FP8 scaling) × ~0.55 (combined collective-overlap + bubble + load-imbalance losses at 100k scale). MoE load imbalance and the sheer collective volume at 100k GPUs make ~0.5–0.55 of that realistic, giving a delivered MFU ≈ 0.83 · 0.92 · 0.55 ≈ 0.42 → round to ~35–40%. That justifies the 40% used in §10.1 (bf16) / 35% (FP8). Frontier MoE runs at this scale that report numbers (DeepSeek-V3's implied ~22% on H800; the book's Q7) suggest 35–40% is optimistic and 25–35% is the honest planning band — so the realistic training time is the §10.1 number divided by 0.30/0.40 = 1.33×, i.e. ~1–2 days bf16, ~1–1.5 days FP8 at a sober 30% MFU, scaling linearly if you spend more tokens.

The communication-bound verdict. The plan is compute-bound iff (i) experts use grouped GEMM (else memory-bound on tiny per-expert batches), (ii) EP AllToAll stays inside NVL72 NVLink (else >4× IB degradation makes routing dominate), and (iii) PP divides the cross-domain DP AllReduce enough to clear C/W_dcn = 5625 tokens/GPU. Violate any one and you fall off the roofline. The 100k-GB200 MoE is exactly the regime where the book's three bounds (compute, communication, memory) are all simultaneously live, with communication — specifically MoE AllToAll — as the binding constraint, and the GB200 NVL72 domain as the architectural answer to it.

10.6 · A second sanity pass: per-layer compute vs comms time

To make the bound concrete, compare per-layer T_math and T_comms numerically for the dispatch AllToAll, the dominant collective. Per-layer active expert FLOPs (FP8) = 6_fwd+bwd-fraction — take forward MLP 4·k·BT·D·F for the active experts; with k=8, BT=60e6, D=16384, F=8192: 4·8·60e6·16384·8192 ≈ 2.6e21 FLOPs/layer forward (~7.7e21 with backward). At aggregate FP8 9e20 FLOP/s, T_math ≈ 7.7e21/9e20 ≈ 8.5 s/layer of aggregate compute — meaningless until divided by overlap and parallelism, but the ratio against comms is what matters. The dispatch AllToAll moves ~1.6e16 bytes/layer aggregate; on NVLink at 900 GB/s/GPU × 2e5 GPU = 1.8e17 B/s aggregate egress, with AllToAll's 1/(W·N)-style discount the effective time is ~1.6e16 / (1.8e17) ≈ 0.09 s/layer aggregate — roughly 100× smaller than the compute time, so on NVLink the routing comfortably hides. Re-run the same AllToAll over InfiniBand at 50 GB/s effective: 1.6e16 / (50e9 · 2e5) ≈ 1.6 s/layer — now comparable to or larger than compute, confirming the hard rule that EP must not cross the NVLink boundary. This single ratio (≈100× margin on NVLink vs ≈parity on IB) is the entire justification for the NVL72-resident expert placement.

11Rules of thumb to burn into memory

  • Training FLOPs = 6 × params × tokens (forward 2×, backward 4×); inference 2×. With block remat, 6 → ~8.
  • max(T_math, T_comms) is your runtime estimate; T_math + T_comms is the no-overlap pessimistic bound.
  • Critical batch ≈ peak FLOP/s ÷ HBM BW: 240 (TPU v5e), ~280 (B200), ~295 (H100) — a near-invariant. It is the per-replica token batch, not sequences, not global.
  • Weight-only int8/FP8 halves the critical batch (240→120); full-precision-symmetric quantization does not.
  • Collective costs in units of V/W: AllToAll 1/4, AllGather = ReduceScatter 1, AllReduce 2. Ring time is independent of device count; an AllReduce is two AllGathers.
  • MoE needs B > 120·E/k tokens/replica to make experts compute-bound — and use grouped GEMM to reach it.
  • KV/token = 2·L·K·H; KV cache dominates inference memory; GQA/MQA/quantization are the levers; decode is always HBM-bandwidth bound.
  • DP/FSDP compute-bound iff B/X > C/W ≈ 2550 (TPU v5p) / ~2500 intra-NVL72, 5625 cross-domain (B200). Params+Adam = 10 bytes/param.
  • TP caps at ~8-way (compute-bound iff Y < F·W/C), precision-independent.
  • FSDP+TP drops the floor to ~α²/(2F) ≈ 100 tokens/chip; X_opt = sqrt((B/F)·(M_X/M_Y)·N).
  • PP is comms-cheap and divides the DP AllReduce by its degree — its real systems value; needs zero-bubble scheduling.
  • Attention FLOPs only matter when T > 8D (~64K tokens at D=8k); otherwise the MLP dominates params and FLOPs (3/4 of params).
  • Large clusters are for FLOPs, not memory — at 100k GB200, model+optimizer state is <0.3% of HBM.
  • Keep MoE AllToAll inside the NVL72 NVLink domain; cross-node AllToAll loses >4× bandwidth and will dominate.
  • A 20% benchmark win is irrelevant if it costs 20% of roofline efficiency — the book's opening thesis, and the discipline this whole chapter exists to enforce.
The meta-discipline

A 20% benchmark win is irrelevant if it costs 20% of roofline efficiency. Every figure in this chapter is one application of the same move: compute Tmath and Tcomms, take the max, and ask which one you can move onto a faster channel.
