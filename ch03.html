Chapter 03 · The network is the training system

Networking and collectives at 100k+ GPUs: MRC, Ultra Ethernet, Spectrum-X, NVLink domains, and the all-to-all problem

For a 10T-total / 200B-active MoE on 100,000 GB200 GPUs the network is not a supporting subsystem — it is the training system. At this scale the model is a communication schedule with arithmetic bolted on, and the gap between a 38% MFU run and a 22% MFU run is almost entirely a function of how well you hide the collectives and how brutally one bad link can stall a synchronous step that 100,000 GPUs are all waiting on.

~28 min read6 figuresgrounded in OpenAI MRC + DeepEP/NCCL-GIN

The expert-parallel (EP) all-to-all that shuttles tokens to their routed experts dominates the inner loop; the data-parallel (DP) and tensor-parallel (TP) gradient/activation reductions dominate the outer loop; and the difference between a fast run and a stalled one is how well you hide these collectives and survive a single bad link. This chapter is about the physical fabric, the transport, and the collective software that make a synchronous all-to-all survivable and fast at this scale.

The architecture is two disjoint worlds, and you must reason about them separately. The scale-up domain is the NVLink5 / NVL72 NVSwitch fabric inside a rack: 72 Blackwell GPUs in one coherent NVLink namespace. The scale-out domain is the 800G RDMA Ethernet (or InfiniBand) plane that stitches ~1,389 NVL72 racks into a 100k-GPU job. The scale-up answer is settled — NVL72/NVLink5 — and the engineering interest is almost entirely in the scale-out plane and in the collective libraries that span the boundary. The central new artifact of mid-2026 is OpenAI's Multipath Reliable Connection (MRC), and the central recurring problem is the MoE all-to-all, so we organize around those.

MRC makes the packet fabric survivable; DeepEP/GIN/MegaMoE make the all-to-all invisible; NVL72 keeps the high-fanout hop on copper at 18× the scale-out bandwidth; and the routing config keeps the RDMA leg small enough that ~50% line-rate efficiency is still enough.

01The scale-up domain: NVL72, NVLink5, and why 72 is the magic number

The GB200 NVL72 is the unit cell. Inside it, NVLink5 gives each Blackwell GPU 1.8 TB/s of bidirectional bandwidth — 18 links × 100 GB/s each, built on 224G SerDes — and the NVSwitch fabric delivers 130 TB/s of aggregate all-to-all bandwidth across the 72-GPU domain, presented as a single flat NVLink namespace (NVIDIA GB200 NVL72). That 1.8 TB/s per GPU is roughly 18× the 800 Gb/s (100 GB/s) per-GPU scale-out bandwidth a ConnectX-8 SuperNIC provides. This bandwidth asymmetry — a ~18:1 ratio between the intra-rack NVLink hop and the inter-rack RDMA hop — is the single most important number for MoE placement, and every routing and parallelism decision downstream is a consequence of it.

The one ratio to remember

NVLink is ~18× faster than RDMA per GPU (1.8 TB/s vs 100 GB/s). Keep the high-fanout part of the all-to-all on copper inside the NVL72 and let the 800G plane see only the irreducible inter-rack residue — every routing and parallelism decision in this chapter is downstream of that ratio.

The compute side deserves a correction that propagates into every roofline you draw. NVIDIA's headline GB200 NVL72 figures — 1,440 PFLOP/s FP4, 720 PFLOP/s FP8, 360 PFLOP/s BF16 per rack — are sparse (2:4 structured sparsity) numbers, footnoted on NVIDIA's own page as "Dense is one-half sparse spec shown." For dense training FLOPs, which is what you actually accumulate during pretraining, the real per-rack peaks are 360 PFLOP/s dense FP8 and 180 PFLOP/s dense BF16, i.e. ~4.5 PFLOP/s dense FP8 and ~2.25 PFLOP/s dense BF16 per B200 GPU (NVIDIA GB200 NVL72). A 100,000-GPU cluster is therefore ~0.45 EFLOP/s dense FP8 peak, not the ~1.0 EFLOP/s you get by counting sparsity. Quote the dense number, state the sparse caveat, and reference MFU (realistically 35–40% FP8 for a well-tuned large MoE in 2025–2026, often lower under heavy routing) against the dense peak — otherwise you over-provision compute relative to network and end up network-bound with idle tensor cores.

Quote dense, not sparse, or you will over-provision compute

The 720/360/180 PFLOP/s rack figures are 2:4-sparse; dense is one-half. Pretraining accumulates dense FLOPs, so a 100k-GPU cluster is ~0.45 EFLOP/s dense FP8, not ~1.0. Provision network against the dense number or your tensor cores sit idle while the fabric saturates.

The reason 72 is the magic number for MoE specifically: it is the largest expert-parallel group you can form where every dispatch/combine hop is an NVLink hop. DeepSeek-style node-limited (group-limited) routing, which caps each token to at most M=4 nodes (DeepSeek-V3), is explicitly designed so an expert group can be co-located inside one NVLink domain; the RDMA leg of the all-to-all then carries only cross-group traffic. On NVL72 you have far more headroom than the 8-GPU HGX nodes DeepSeek-V3 was tuned for, and the right move is to place an entire expert group — and ideally its redundant EPLB replicas — inside a single rack so the high-fanout part of the all-to-all stays on the 130 TB/s NVSwitch fabric and the 800G plane sees only the irreducible inter-rack residue.

02MRC: spray-and-route over RoCE, source-routed by SRv6

The load-bearing scale-out artifact for a 2026 100k-GPU build is OpenAI's Multipath Reliable Connection (MRC), published as arXiv 2605.04333 (50 authors across OpenAI and Microsoft) and contributed to the Open Compute Project as the MRC 1.0 spec (arXiv 2605.04333; OCP MRC 1.0). Understanding MRC is the fastest way to understand where the entire scale-out fabric debate landed by mid-2026.

MRC is not a new transport built from scratch. It extends RoCEv2's Reliable Connection (RC) semantics and keeps the RoCE verbs / queue-pair (QP) abstraction intact, so it slots into the existing NIC and NCCL software stack. Its one radical restriction is the enabler for everything else: an MRC connection supports only write and write-with-immediate — one-sided RDMA writes, no reads, no sends. Why does that unlock packet spraying? Because every data packet of a one-sided write already carries the RDMA virtual address and rkey of its destination. The receiving NIC can therefore DMA each arriving packet directly to its final memory location regardless of arrival order. There is no reorder buffer, because there is nothing to reorder — order is irrelevant when every packet is self-addressing. This is the precise mechanism that kills the central problem of naive multipath RDMA, where out-of-order arrival across sprayed paths forces a reorder buffer that destroys throughput (arXiv 2605.04333).

Spraying and routing. Each packet is tagged with a 32-bit Entropy Value (EV) striped across the UDP source port and the IPv6 flow label. A QP generates a pool of typically 128–256 EVs at startup and rotates through them packet by packet, so a single connection's bytes fan out across up to 256 physical paths. Path selection is static SRv6 source routing using micro-segment IDs (uSIDs, the uN flavor where each switch on the path is explicitly named in the segment list): every EV maps deterministically to exactly one physical path to the destination NIC. Switch forwarding tables are configured at install and essentially never change. This is the reliability thesis: the control plane is dumb and static, so there is no routing-protocol reconvergence to wait on when something breaks (arXiv 2605.04333).

Failover in microseconds. When a link or path fails, the NIC simply stops using the affected EV(s) — it removes them from the rotation. With 256 EVs, a single dead path costs you ~0.4% of your paths and is dropped in microseconds, so the job runs slightly degraded instead of stalling. The slow path — a full QP remap after a NIC↔T0 (top-of-rack) link is lost entirely — is the "few seconds" event, but it is rare and localized. Contrast this with conventional fabrics where a link flap triggers seconds of routing-protocol reconvergence during which a synchronous collective across 100k GPUs simply hangs. This microsecond failover is the actual product, and the reason MRC exists.

Loss and congestion handling, straight from the UEC playbook. MRC disables PFC (Priority Flow Control) entirely — no more PFC-deadlock and head-of-line-blocking pathologies that plague vanilla RoCEv2 at scale. Loss is handled with SACK fast retransmit; ECN is used purely as a load-balancing signal rather than a hard backpressure mechanism; and packet trimming forwards the headers of would-be-dropped packets as priority NACKs, so a sender learns about congestion-induced loss immediately rather than via timeout. This trim-and-spray-and-NACK design is lifted directly from the Ultra Ethernet Consortium's transport (§03).

mrc_qp.cMRC packet spray (illustrative)write-only RCtrimmed
// MRC connection: write-only verbs unlock reorder-free spray
struct mrc_qp {
    uint32_t ev_pool[256];   // 128–256 Entropy Values  // [1]
    int      ev_cursor;
    srv6_usid_path_t path_for_ev[256];  // static, set at install  // [2]
};

ssize_t mrc_post_write(struct mrc_qp *qp, void *src, size_t len,
                       uint64_t remote_addr, uint32_t rkey) {  // [3]
    for (size_t off = 0; off < len; off += MTU) {
        uint32_t ev = qp->ev_pool[qp->ev_cursor++ % 256];  // rotate per packet
        if (ev_is_dead(qp, ev)) continue;                  // failover: skip EV  // [4]
        pkt_t p = { .udp_src = ev & 0xffff,                // EV → UDP src + flow label
                    .ipv6_flow = (ev >> 16),
                    .srv6 = qp->path_for_ev[ev],           // deterministic path
                    .rdma_addr = remote_addr + off,        // self-addressing  // [5]
                    .rkey = rkey, .imm = WRITE_IMM };
        nic_tx(&p, src + off, MTU);                        // no reads, no sends
    }
}

2Static SRv6. Each EV maps deterministically to one uSID source-routed path; forwarding tables are set at install and never reconverge.

3Write-only verbs. Only write/write-with-immediate — every packet carries the destination address, so order is irrelevant.

4Microsecond failover. A dead path just drops its EV from rotation (~0.4% of paths) — no reconvergence, the job runs slightly degraded.

5No reorder buffer. The NIC DMAs each self-addressing packet straight to its final memory location regardless of arrival order.

Hardware and deployment. MRC's SRv6 + spray design is implemented across NVIDIA ConnectX-8 (800G), AMD Pollara (400G) and Vulcano (800G), and Broadcom Thor Ultra NICs, with the SRv6 forwarding handled by NVIDIA Spectrum-4/5 and Broadcom Tomahawk 5 switch silicon — explicitly multi-vendor, which is the point. It is deployed in production on OpenAI's OCI Abilene site and Microsoft Fairwater GB200 superclusters (OCP MRC 1.0). For a greenfield 100k build, MRC over Spectrum-X / Tomahawk-5 is the baseline scale-out transport, and the multi-vendor NIC/switch support is precisely what lets you dual-source and avoid being captive to a single supplier's lead times.

InfiniBand XDR via Quantum-X800 (the Q3400 switch, 144×800G ports) remains the deterministic gold standard: ~1 µs small-message latency, hardware adaptive routing, and crucially SHARPv4 in-network reduction delivering 14.4 TFLOPS of in-switch compute with claims of up to 9× traffic reduction on allreduce (NVIDIA SHARP). Its weaknesses are that it is NVIDIA-only and the most expensive per-port, and that the determinism advantage it historically held over Ethernet is exactly what MRC was engineered to erase.

Vanilla RoCEv2 is the cautionary tale: the ECMP hash-collision problem (a flow gets pinned to one path and collides) and PFC deadlock are the two pathologies that the entire spray-fabric movement exists to fix. Spectrum-X is NVIDIA's answer — RoCE plus adaptive routing and congestion control — and it is reported to come within ~5% of IB NDR on 8-node NCCL allreduce. Spectrum-X is the switch substrate MRC frequently runs on.

Ultra Ethernet (UEC 1.0, published June 2025) defines the Ultra Ethernet Transport (UET), which brings packet spraying with coordinated path selection (not bare ECMP hashing), Link Layer Retry (LLR) and Credit-Based Flow Control (CBFC) to give Ethernet InfiniBand-like lossless behavior, plus new sender- and receiver-driven congestion control (UEC 1.0). The cleanest way to position MRC is as OpenAI's productionized superset of UEC: it takes UEC's spray + trim + congestion-control machinery and adds two things UET handles differently — SRv6 source routing (deterministic per-EV paths instead of UET's coordinated-but-still-fabric-chosen paths) and the write-only-RDMA reorder-free trick (UET instead carries its own reordering model). Tom Herbert's protocol-engineer review of UET 1.0 is worth reading for the design tensions in UET's reordering and congestion-control internals that MRC's choices were specifically made to sidestep (Herbert, UET review).

Recommendation for the 100k GB200 build. The hyperscalers have already voted, and you should follow them: Ethernet-based spray fabrics (MRC / UEC / Spectrum-X), not InfiniBand, for the scale-out plane. The drivers are multi-vendor sourcing (no single-supplier captivity at 100k-NIC volume), blast-radius isolation, and the fact that MRC closes the determinism gap that historically justified IB's premium. Keep IB-style SHARP in the toolbox via the NVLink-SHARP path (§06), not via an IB scale-out fabric. The ~5 µs MRC latency floor vs IB's ~1 µs is a real regression, but for bandwidth-bound MoE all-to-all on large messages it is irrelevant — and the microsecond failover advantage at 100k GPUs dwarfs the per-message latency difference.

04Topology: rail-optimized fat-tree vs multi-plane (Oracle Acceleron)

Two structurally different scale-out topologies are on the table, and the choice interacts directly with MRC.

Rail-optimized fat-tree is the classic NVIDIA reference architecture. GPU i in every node connects to leaf switch i, organized into Scalable Units of 32 nodes, so that same-rail traffic crosses exactly one leaf and only cross-rail traffic climbs to the spine. With ConnectX-8 SuperNICs at 800 Gb/s/GPU, each NVL72 rack offers 57.6 Tb/s of scale-out bandwidth (72 × 800 Gb/s) (NVIDIA GB200 NVL72). The rail structure is a good match for collectives where ranks aligned on the same rail dominate the traffic, but it has the failure characteristic that a leaf or spine failure degrades the whole fabric until reconvergence.

Oracle Acceleron multiplanar is the structurally different bet and the most relevant non-NVIDIA primary source. Instead of one fabric, Acceleron splits each GPU's NIC across multiple fully-disjoint network planes, connecting a single GPU to several switches simultaneously (Oracle Acceleron). The payoffs are: (1) elimination of fate-sharing — a plane failure reroutes instantly onto surviving planes instead of degrading the whole job; (2) a flatter fabric with fewer tiers, which lowers latency, power, and cost; and (3) it is RoCEv2-based, so it composes with the spray transports above. OCI's Zettascale10 targets up to 800,000 GPUs / 16 zettaFLOPS, H2 2026, on this architecture.

The key insight is that multiplanar and MRC are complementary, not competing: MRC sprays within the redundant planes that Acceleron provides. Disjoint planes give MRC's microsecond EV-failover a richer substrate — when an entire plane degrades, you fail over to a wholly independent plane rather than just dropping EVs within one fabric. For a 100k greenfield build, baseline a disjoint multi-plane fabric (Acceleron-style) running MRC, against a single-plane rail-optimized fat-tree, and prefer the former. The fate-sharing elimination is worth the extra NIC ports and cabling complexity at this scale, because at 100k GPUs the probability that some fabric element is degraded at any given moment approaches one, and a single-plane fabric forces every such event into the job's critical path.

05The contrast that matters: Google ICI + optical circuit switching

Everything above is packet-switched Ethernet. The architectural antithesis — and the forward-looking signal you should track — is Google's TPU fabric and its emerging GPU-side analogs.

TPU ICI (Inter-Chip Interconnect) is a 3D torus of optical-circuit-switched cubes: 4×4×4 = 64 TPUs per cube, up to 1.2 TB/s/chip, with MEMS-based optical circuit switches (OCS) — 2D mirror arrays / beam steering — reconfiguring the torus on a per-job basis. OCP's April 2026 OCS white paper and Google's own numbers cite ~40% power and ~30% cost reduction versus an equivalent electrical-packet switching tier, with the OCS handling only the inter-cube optical links while intra-cube links stay copper (OCP OCS white paper). The defining property is that circuit-switched means zero in-network congestion: you build a contention-free topology that exactly matches the collective, rather than statistically load-balancing a packet fabric and hoping spraying smooths the tail. The MEMS reconfiguration is slow (milliseconds), so it is used for job-granularity topology, not per-collective.

The genuinely forward-looking resource — and the one most directly relevant to MoE — is PCCL (arXiv 2509.15450, Cornell, Sep 2025) (PCCL). PCCL does per-collective-round topology reconfiguration on a photonic scale-up fabric, using MZI (Mach-Zehnder interferometer) switches on optical interposers that reconfigure in microseconds rather than the milliseconds of MEMS. The thesis is inverted from a decade of collective research: "collective-aware topologies, not topology-aware collectives." Instead of synthesizing a clever algorithm for a fixed topology, you reconfigure the topology to match each collective. On a 4×4×4 torus, PCCL is 7.5× faster on a 32 MB all-to-all and up to 2.5–3× faster on allreduce versus TACCL/TACOS/NCCL-class baselines, yielding 1.3× end-to-end training throughput.

The explicit motivation is MoE, and it names the exact problem this chapter circles: MoE "alternates between latency-sensitive AllToAll requiring low-diameter topologies and bandwidth-hungry AllReduce favoring ring-like high-diameter topologies" — no fixed topology serves both, costing 2–4× from congestion plus dilation. This is the deep structural reason the all-to-all problem is hard: the EP dispatch/combine wants a low-diameter, high-radix topology to minimize hop count for small scattered messages, while the DP/TP allreduce wants a ring-like high-diameter topology to maximize bisection bandwidth for large contiguous reductions. A packet fabric — MRC included — picks one compromise topology and lives with both being suboptimal.

The forward bet: photonic scale-up not yet productized

MRC and IB make the packet fabric survivable and fast-enough; they do not solve the all-to-all-vs-allreduce topology conflict at its root. PCCL/photonic scale-up does. For mid-2026 you build on MRC because photonic scale-up is not yet productized at 100k scale, but architect software (collective abstractions, expert placement) so a future photonic scale-up domain is a drop-in — that is where the all-to-all problem actually gets solved.

06The collective stack: NCCL, NVSHMEM, IBGDA, DeepEP, GIN, SHARP

The fabric is inert without the collective software that drives it. For a GB200 MoE there are three load-bearing layers, and they map onto the two domains (NVLink scale-up, RDMA scale-out) explicitly.

DeepEP — the device-initiated sparse all-to-all substrate

DeepEP is the reference EP communication library and the right mental model for dispatch/combine. The original (V1) design split into two kernel families. High-throughput (HT) / normal kernels (training and prefill) use NVSHMEM, run ~24 QPs, and do asymmetric-domain forwarding: a token destined for a remote expert goes over IB/RDMA to the matching in-node index on the destination rack, then hops over NVLink to the actual target expert — overlapping the RDMA and NVLink legs so both domains saturate concurrently. Low-latency (LL) kernels (decode) use a pure-RDMA full mesh over IBGDA (InfiniBand GPUDirect Async, where the GPU programs the NIC doorbell directly with no CPU in the loop), 8–16 QPs, with a hook-based comm/compute overlap that consumes zero SMs (DeepEP legacy docs). The whole HT path saturates both NVLink and RDMA with only ~20–24 SMs.

The V2 rewrite is the more important development for GB200. It unifies HT and LL kernels behind a single ElasticBuffer interface, drops SM consumption from 24 → 4–6 SMs, and — critically — replaces the NVSHMEM transport with an NCCL "Gin" backend, giving you NCCL's elasticity and fault tolerance under the same low-latency device-initiated all-to-all. V2 adds SM100 (Blackwell) support with measured 726/740 GB/s dispatch/combine over NVLink at EP8 (max-perf, 64 SMs) and 643/675 GB/s at the minimal-SM config; SM90/CX7 RDMA is ~90 GB/s at EP8×2 (DeepEP README). The NVIDIA-authored hybrid-ep branch is the one to track for GB200: it uses TMA copies and warp-level pipelining to minimize SM occupancy while saturating both NVLink and RDMA, plus experimental NIXL/UCX integration.

NCCL 2.28 GIN — DeepEP-style device-initiated RDMA, in NCCL

NVIDIA's NCCL 2.28 introduces a Device API with three modes (NCCL GIN, arXiv 2511.15076): LSA (Load/Store Accessible) for NVLink/PCIe, Multimem for NVLink-SHARP multicast/reduce, and GIN (GPU-Initiated Networking) for network RDMA. GIN has two backends: a GDAKI backend where DOCA GPUNetIO lets GPU threads program the NIC directly (the IBGDA idea, generalized), and a Proxy backend using lock-free GPU→CPU queues with 64-byte descriptors that works on any RDMA NIC. GIN is integrated with DeepEP and pplx-kernels, so you get NVSHMEM-class latency plus NCCL's collective ecosystem, elasticity, and fault tolerance. For a 100k-GPU run where fault tolerance is not optional, this is the strategically correct substrate — it lets you ride NCCL's mature failure-recovery while getting device-initiated all-to-all performance.

SHARP — in-network allreduce offload

SHARP offloads the allreduce side (the DP/TP gradient sync that overlaps with the EP all-to-all). NCCL 2.27 added both NVLink-SHARP and IB-SHARP; Blackwell NVLink-SHARP does 7.2 TB/s in-domain allreduce, 4× Hopper, and IB SHARPv4 on Quantum-X800 adds 14.4 TFLOPS of in-switch compute (NVIDIA SHARP). The architectural point for an Ethernet scale-out build: you lose IB-SHARP on the scale-out plane (you chose Ethernet), but you keep NVLink-SHARP inside each NVL72, which is where most of your high-bandwidth allreduce traffic should be confined anyway via TP-within-rack placement. Run the gradient allreduce hierarchically — NVLink-SHARP within rack, ring/tree allreduce across racks over MRC — and overlap it with the EP all-to-all of the next micro-batch.

07The MoE all-to-all bottleneck: quantifying it on this fabric

Now the central problem. Take DeepSeek-V3-class routing as the template: hidden dimension 7,168, 256 routed experts, top-8, FP8 dispatch / BF16 combine (DeepSeek-V3).

Volume math. Per token per MoE layer, dispatch moves the top-8 copies of the 7,168-dim activation outbound, and combine returns them inbound. The asymmetry is structural: dispatch in FP8 is 8 × 7,168 × 1 ≈ 57 KB up, while combine in BF16 is 8 × 7,168 × 2 ≈ 115 KB down. The general formula is 2 · top_k · d_model · bytes bidirectional per token per layer; FP8 dispatch halves the up leg. This is exactly why DeepEP reports higher combine bandwidth ceilings than dispatch — the combine leg moves twice the bytes. For a micro-batch of 8,192 tokens per EP-rank, dispatch+combine is ~1.4 GB of cross-fabric traffic per layer per rank, multiplied across ~58 MoE layers.

comms AllToAll bytes / token / layer = 2 · topk · dmodel · bytes Doubled for dispatch + combine. With topk=8, dmodel=7168: FP8 dispatch ≈ 57 KB up, BF16 combine ≈ 115 KB down. At 8,192 tokens/rank that is ~1.4 GB cross-fabric per layer per rank. The term that gates MoE MFU.

What the fabric actually delivers. This is where the dossier numbers turn from comforting to sobering. DeepEP's published internode RDMA throughput (H800 / CX-7, 50-node) is only 43–58 GB/s per GPU on dispatch/combine — i.e. the all-to-all sustains ~50–60% of the 400G NIC line rate, and that ceiling is set by spray efficiency and expert-skew, not raw link rate (DeepEP legacy docs). Worse, per-token internode bandwidth falls as expert count grows, because each token fans out to more remote NVLink domains: the LL pure-RDMA path goes from 77 µs / 98 GB/s at 8 experts to 194 µs / 39 GB/s at 256 experts, with combine latency 1.5–1.8× the dispatch latency. The normal-kernel internode RDMA peaks around 58 GB/s at EP32 and sags to ~50 GB/s at EP64.

On 800G ConnectX-8 with MRC spraying 256 paths you roughly double the absolute number — a ~90–110 GB/s/GPU envelope is plausible, consistent with the LL-kernel's 98–127 GB/s NVLink-assisted figures and DeepEP-V2's measured ~90 GB/s RDMA at EP8×2. But the fraction of line rate stays stubbornly at ~50–60%, because the bottleneck migrates from link bandwidth to all-to-all incast and load imbalance: many senders hit one receiver simultaneously (incast), and a hot expert receives disproportionate traffic (skew). Doubling the link rate does not double the effective all-to-all throughput when the limiter is the receiver's incast queue and the slowest (hottest) expert's serialization.

Concretely, at 100 GB/s/GPU effective: the ~115 KB combine per token per layer is ~1.15 µs of serialized fabric time per token per layer if unoverlapped. That number, multiplied by tokens and layers, is catastrophic if exposed. Which is the entire point: the game is overlap, not bandwidth.

The fraction of line rate, not the absolute, is the planning number

Doubling 400G → 800G roughly doubles the absolute GB/s but leaves the all-to-all stuck at ~50–60% of line rate, because the limiter is incast + expert skew, not link bandwidth. Provision for incast and skew, not for line rate — and remember the real game is overlap, not bandwidth.

08How the all-to-all is actually hidden

Three mechanisms, layered, hide these microseconds under expert compute.

DualPipe scheduling (V3). Each forward/backward chunk is decomposed into four phases — attention, all-to-all dispatch, MLP, all-to-all combine — with backward further split into backward-for-input and backward-for-weights, and a hand-tuned schedule overlaps the all-to-all of one micro-batch with the GEMMs of another (DeepSeek-V3). The bubble count drops to (PP/2−1)(F&B+B−3W) versus 1F1B's (PP−1)(F+B). The cost is two resident parameter copies (the bidirectional schedule), which is cheap because EP already shards the experts. The comms side reserves 20 SMs across 10 warp-specialized channels with custom PTX and auto-tuned chunk sizes to limit L2 interference — that 20-SM tax is exactly what DeepEP-V2 (4–6 SMs) and the V4 mega-kernel are built to eliminate.

The fundamental overlap condition. DeepSeek-V4 frames it crisply: you need roughly 6,144 FLOPs/Byte for the MoE compute to fully hide the communication (DeepSeek-V4). The expert GEMM does ~6·h·d FLOPs per token-expert against ~3·h bytes of communication, so the ratio is set by the expert intermediate dimension d. If your experts are too narrow relative to your network, the comm cannot be hidden no matter how clever the schedule — which is an argument for wider, fewer experts when the network is the binding constraint, and a direct input to your expert-sizing decision.

compute ratio to beat = (6 · h · d) FLOPs / (3 · h) bytes ≈ 6144 FLOP/byte Stay above this and the expert GEMM hides the all-to-all under compute. The ratio is set by the expert intermediate dimension d — narrow experts cannot hide comms, an argument for wider, fewer experts when the network binds.

Device-initiated overlap (DeepEP hook + GIN). DeepEP's hook mechanism issues the RDMA and returns, letting the grouped-GEMM proceed; the transfer completes asynchronously and consumes zero SMs. GIN's device-initiated puts do the same inside NCCL. This is what buries the ~1 µs/token/layer fabric time under the expert grouped-GEMM.

The V4 endgame — a single fused mega-kernel. DeepSeek-V4 (arXiv 2606.19348) replaces the DualPipe + DeepEP two-piece overlap with a single fused MoE mega-kernel ("MegaMoE", open-sourced in DeepGEMM) that fuses EP-dispatch → first FP8×FP4 expert GEMM → SwiGLU+requant → second FP8×FP4 GEMM → EP-combine into one symmetric-memory kernel (DeepSeek-V4). The mechanism is expert "waves": the current wave computes on the tensor cores while the next wave's tokens transfer over NVLink and finished experts' results are sent back, all concurrently — finer-grained than the prior Comet-style overlap. On SM100 it uses three warp roles (dispatch / UMMA-non-epilogue / epilogue), Tensor Memory, UE8M0 2D-TMA scale descriptors, and a MegaMoEScheduler that prioritizes NVLink-active tiles (DeepGEMM MegaMoE). Measured speedup is 1.50–1.73× general, up to 1.96× latency-sensitive over the non-fused baseline. For a 100k GB200 build this is the target: the fused kernel makes the all-to-all genuinely invisible by interleaving transfer and MMA at wave granularity rather than at kernel-boundary granularity.

Ship the V4 MegaMoE fused kernel as the overlap target

MegaMoE deletes DualPipe's 20-SM overlap tax and the two-piece dispatch/GEMM/combine handoff in one move, for 1.5–1.96×, by interleaving NVLink transfer and tensor-core MMA at wave granularity rather than kernel-boundary granularity. This is what makes the all-to-all genuinely invisible on GB200.

LL decode budgets. For the inference/decode side, the LL-kernel dispatch latencies of 77–194 µs and combine of 114–314 µs (8 → 256 experts) are the budget you must hide behind expert compute. If your per-expert compute is smaller than this, decode is comms-bound and you need EPLB replication of hot experts plus larger batched decode to amortize.

09What MRC actually buys the all-to-all: tail suppression

A crucial clarification: MRC's contribution to the all-to-all is not throughput — it is tail-latency suppression. At 100k GPUs a synchronous training step waits on the slowest all-to-all transfer across the whole job. In a conventional fabric, a single link flap spikes the p99.9 of these transfers to seconds during routing reconvergence, and that one stall propagates to all 100k GPUs. MRC's static SRv6 + EV-spray design means one dead path out of 256 costs ~0.4% of paths and is dropped in microseconds, so the p99.9 tail no longer spikes to seconds on a single link event (arXiv 2605.04333). For a synchronous 100k-GPU step, tail-latency suppression is worth more than peak bandwidth, and that is precisely the problem MRC was built to solve. You provision bandwidth with 800G NICs and spraying; you provision step-time stability with MRC's failover semantics.

MRC buys p99.9 tail suppression, not peak bandwidth

A synchronous 100k-GPU step waits on the slowest transfer. MRC keeps a single link event from spiking the p99.9 to seconds — you provision bandwidth with 800G NICs and spraying, and step-time stability with MRC's microsecond EV-failover. At this scale the second matters more.

10Placement and routing co-design: keeping the RDMA leg small

The networking and MoE-systems layers are not separable; the routing config determines the fabric load. The levers, in priority order for a 100k GB200 build:

  • Node-limited (M=4) routing caps each token's internode fan-out so an expert group fits one NVL72 domain; the RDMA leg then carries only cross-group residue (DeepSeek-V3). On NVL72's 72-GPU domain you have more headroom than the 8-GPU HGX assumption, so co-locate whole expert groups (plus replicas) in-rack.
  • Auxiliary-loss-free bias balancing keeps expert load even so the all-to-all incast stays bounded: a per-expert bias added to top-K selection scores only, nudged by ±γ (γ=0.001 for the first 14.3T tokens, 0.0 for the final 500B in V3). Without this, expert skew destroys the ~50–60% line-rate efficiency further. Watch DeepEP issue #427: LL dispatch can silently corrupt under heavily imbalanced load — exactly the early-training regime before the balancer converges (DeepEP #427).
  • EPLB redundant-expert replication at deployment is essentially mandatory at 100k scale: replicate the hottest experts so incast spreads across replicas. The newer LPLB solves per-batch dynamic redistribution as an LP over the redundant-expert topology via a single-SM interior-point method in ~100 µs/batch, fed by live DeepEP buffer stats (LPLB) — the research-frontier online variant.
  • Dropless ragged buffers sized at the min(ep_degree, global_experts/top_k) worst-case bound — never worse than 256/8 = 32× for a V3-style config — or ragged_buffer_factor≈3 as the near-dropless memory-bounded compromise (MaxText moe.py). In MaxText this rides jax.lax.ragged_all_to_all with offsets/sizes from get_all_to_all_params, dispatching the grouped GEMM through ragged_dot / megablox / tokamax.
Step-0 hazard — DeepEP #427 imbalance corruption

LL dispatch can silently corrupt activations under heavily imbalanced expert load — exactly the first few thousand steps before your auxiliary-loss-free bias balancer converges. Run the high-throughput path early, warm up the balancer aggressively, or use hash-routed early layers (immune to #427) as a clean warm-up.

The MaxText EP all-to-all is the most legible open reference for the JAX path. The dispatch/combine is built not from a dense one-hot all-to-all but from jax.lax.ragged_all_to_all, which moves only the valid (variable-length) per-expert token runs:

# per-shard group sizes -> ragged all-to-all offset/size metadata
def get_all_to_all_params(group_sizes, shard_id, ep_degree):
    base = group_sizes.reshape(ep_degree, -1)
    send_sizes = base[shard_id]            # rows we ship out
    recv_sizes = base[:, shard_id]         # rows we receive
    in_off  = jnp.concatenate([z, jnp.cumsum(send_sizes)[:-1]])
    out_off = jnp.concatenate([z, jnp.cumsum(recv_sizes)[:-1]])
    return in_off, send_sizes, out_off, recv_sizes

# dispatch leg: move only valid per-expert runs (no dense BSEC mask)
x_sorted, sort_idx, group_sizes = permute(x, top_k_indices)     # custom-VJP sort

The unifying principle: every routing decision is a fabric-load decision. The network team and the model team must co-design, because a routing change that doubles internode fan-out doubles the RDMA leg, and a 50% line-rate fabric has no headroom to absorb that.

11Quantified recommendation for the 10T / 200B / 100k-GB200 run

Putting it together for the target system:

scale-up
NVL72 / NVLink5
1.8 TB/s/GPU, 130 TB/s/rack all-to-all. Confine TP + intra-group EP in-rack; NVLink-SHARP (7.2 TB/s) for in-rack gradient reduction.
scale-out
MRC over Spectrum-X / Tomahawk-5
~770 Gb/s/NIC, 57.6 Tb/s/rack, disjoint Acceleron-style multi-plane. Not single-plane InfiniBand.
collectives
DeepEP-V2 / hybrid-ep + MegaMoE
Fused dispatch-GEMM-combine, riding NCCL 2.28 GIN for elasticity; SHARP for in-rack allreduce.
  • Scale-up: NVL72 / NVLink5, 1.8 TB/s/GPU, 130 TB/s/rack aggregate all-to-all. Confine TP and intra-expert-group EP entirely inside the rack. Use NVLink-SHARP (7.2 TB/s in-domain allreduce) for in-rack gradient reduction.
  • Scale-out: 800G spray fabric — MRC over Spectrum-X / Tomahawk-5 on disjoint Acceleron-style multi-plane fabric, ~770 Gb/s/NIC achievable, 57.6 Tb/s/rack scale-out. Not single-plane InfiniBand. Two-tier multi-plane Clos, 3-switch longest path, scales past your 100k requirement toward the 131,072-GPU envelope.
  • Bandwidth budget: ~1.4 GB cross-fabric per layer per rank for a V3-class config at 8,192 tokens/rank; effective ~90–110 GB/s/GPU on the RDMA leg (~50–60% of 800G); the fraction of line rate, not the absolute, is the planning number — provision for incast and skew, not for line rate.
  • Collectives: DeepEP-V2 / hybrid-ep + DeepGEMM MegaMoE for the fused dispatch-GEMM-combine path, riding NCCL 2.28 GIN for elasticity and fault tolerance. SHARP for allreduce offload in-rack.
  • Overlap target: hit ≥6,144 FLOPs/Byte so the all-to-all hides fully; size experts wide enough that the expert GEMM dominates the dispatch/combine cost. The V4 fused mega-kernel's wave-level interleave (1.5–1.96× over non-fused) is the mechanism.
  • Reliability: MRC's microsecond EV-failover is the load-bearing property — at 100k GPUs you are buying p99.9 tail suppression, not peak bandwidth. Provision disjoint planes so plane-level failures also fail over instantly.
  • Forward bet: architect collective abstractions so a future photonic scale-up domain (PCCL-style per-collective-round reconfiguration, 7.5× all-to-all) drops in. That, not MRC, is where the all-to-all-vs-allreduce topology conflict ultimately dissolves.
The summary judgment

MRC makes the packet fabric survivable; DeepEP/GIN/MegaMoE make the all-to-all invisible; NVL72 keeps the high-fanout hop on copper at 18× the scale-out bandwidth; and the routing config keeps the RDMA leg small enough that ~50% line-rate efficiency is still enough. None of these individually solves the all-to-all problem — collectively, they make a synchronous 200B-active MoE step across 100,000 GPUs run without stalling. The root-cause solution to the topology conflict is photonic and still a generation away.
