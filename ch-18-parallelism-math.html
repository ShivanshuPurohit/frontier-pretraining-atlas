Every parallelism decision in this design eventually reduces to the same inequality: is the per-GPU compute time longer or shorter than the communication time the chosen sharding scheme imposes. At 100,000 GPUs training a 10T-total/200B-active MoE, that inequality gets evaluated dozens of times across five interacting axes — data, expert, tensor, pipeline, and (for long-context stages) sequence parallelism — and getting any one of them wrong by a small constant factor is the difference between 40% MFU and 15%. This chapter derives the crossovers explicitly, corrects a subtle GB200-vs-HGX-B200 arithmetic error that has propagated through the theory literature, and ends in three fully-worked candidate layouts for the strawman model specified in Ch7.

The roofline algebra, restated for GPUs

Every formula in this chapter reduces to T_math = FLOPs/C versus T_comms = Bytes/W, with the operation compute-bound once arithmetic intensity exceeds the hardware’s critical ratio C/W. This is the JAX Scaling Book’s organizing move, extended from TPU tori to GPU fat-trees in its GPU chapter (Aug 2025). The four sharding primitives on a GPU fat-tree: AllGather/ReduceScatter cost T=V/W regardless of shard count; AllReduce is exactly 2V/W (a ReduceScatter plus an AllGather); intra-node AllToAll costs T≈B/(N·W) for an N-GPU domain — note this is 1/N, not the TPU torus’s 1/4, because full-crossbar NVSwitch connectivity beats a torus’s partial connectivity, and conflating the two is a documented source of error when porting TPU intuition to GPU clusters. Cross-node AllToAll for M nodes degrades to T≈B/(M·W_node), more than 4x worse than the single-node case because it cannot exploit full bisection bandwidth the way AllReduce can.

For a single dense matmul, this produces the familiar single-chip compute-bound floor: batch size must exceed roughly C/W_hbm tokens, which works out to B>295 on H100 and B>281 on B200 in bf16 (B>312 using the corrected GB200 figures — 2.5 PFLOPS over 8 TB/s — the source’s “B200” row being the HGX SKU per the reconciliation below) — essentially unchanged across three hardware generations because FLOPs and HBM bandwidth have scaled together. The dense-model DP/FSDP/TP crossovers the parallelism math below builds on: DP and FSDP are compute-bound once B/X > C/W_ici; TP is compute-bound once Y < F·W_ici/C (Y=TP degree, F=FFN width). Stas Bekman’s ml-engineering independently arrives at the same threshold as flat field wisdom (“TP ≤ GPUs per node,” typically 8-16-way) — the crossover pre-evaluated for typical F and frozen as a rule of thumb. Both of these are dense-model results (Ch7 and the MoE derivations below extend them); the Scaling Book’s main training-parallelism chapter explicitly excludes expert parallelism from its scope, pushing all MoE-aware formulas into the GPU chapter appendix — which is where the numbers this chapter actually needs live.

GB200 ground truth used throughout this chapter (full derivation in Ch16): the authoritative per-GPU dense FP8 Tensor Core peak for GB200 NVL72 is 5.0 PFLOPS, not the 4.5 PFLOPS the Scaling Book’s summary table implies — that table silently reports the air-cooled HGX B200 SKU’s number under an undifferentiated “B200” label, an error confirmed against NVIDIA’s own side-by-side datasheet (doc #3384703) and cross-validated against two independent NVIDIA product pages and a third-party technical wiki. Dense BF16 is 2.5 PFLOPS/GPU (not 2.25); dense FP4 is 10.0 PFLOPS/GPU (not 9.0) — a uniform +11.1% correction across every tensor-core precision, tracking GB200’s 1,200W liquid-cooled envelope against HGX B200’s 1,000W air-cooled part on the identical Blackwell die. The mechanism isn’t a single clean clock multiplier, though: back-calculating from published FLOPs/core-count gives only a ~6.7% vector-ALU clock delta (≈2.1GHz GB200 vs ≈1.98GHz HGX B200) — well short of the +11.1% tensor-core gap — implying either a more aggressive sustained-clock floor specifically under saturated Tensor-Core load at 1,200W, independently-rounded marketing figures on the two SKUs, or (most likely) both; NVIDIA publishes no official clock speed for either part. NVLink5 gives 900 GB/s full-duplex per GPU, unchanged in per-GPU terms whether the domain is 8 GPUs (HGX B200) or 72 GPUs (GB200 NVL72) — the domain-size increase from 8→72 is the whole story, not a per-link speed change. Scale-out bandwidth per GPU is the wildcard: ~50 GB/s/GPU on ConnectX-7/400Gbps fabrics (the implicit baseline in most GPU-chapter formulas) versus ~100 GB/s/GPU on ConnectX-8/800Gbps XDR fabrics deployed on GB200 racks from mid-2025 onward — a 2x swing that halves or doubles every cross-domain threshold below, and the more likely default for a 2026-commissioned 100k-GPU build. Every calculation below uses the corrected 5.0e15 FLOPs/s/GPU dense FP8 figure.

MoE-specific formulas: the E/k inflation and the ragged AllToAll

The GPU chapter derives two closed-form results absent from the dense-model chapters. First, MoE data-parallel critical batch inflates by exactly the sparsity ratio E/k: B/X > (E/k)·C/W, because FSDP/ZeRO-3-style sharding must AllGather all E experts’ weights each step to support routing that only uses k of them — the worked example given is “the new OpenAI OSS model” (E=128, k=4, E/k=32), which needs 79,200 tokens/GPU cross-node, described in the source text as “a kind of ridiculously high number.” Second, the ragged (top-k) AllToAll cost for MoE dispatch/combine is T≈min(k/N,1)·B/(N·W) — the ordinary AllToAll formula scaled down by the fraction of the domain each token actually touches.

Third, and the most consequential derivation for this chapter: the expert-parallelism compute-bound viability threshold generalizes the H100-specific F > 8·C/W_node (8 = H100 node size) to F_min = C/W_scaleout for a domain of any size n, because W_node_total = n·W_per_gpu and the n cancels algebraically. This threshold is domain-size invariant — it depends only on per-GPU scale-out bandwidth, not on how many GPUs sit inside the cheap NVLink domain. Using the corrected GB200 numbers: F_min = 5.0e15/50e9 = 100,000 on ConnectX-7 fabrics, or 5.0e15/100e9 = 50,000 on ConnectX-8. Neither source states this invariance explicitly; it falls directly out of generalizing the book’s own H100-specific formula, and it is easy to state backwards — GB200’s real MoE advantage is entirely about how much EP is “free” inside the 72-GPU domain before F_min becomes relevant, not about F_min itself moving.

Applying this to the Ch7 strawman (512 routed experts, top-8, one shared expert, d_model≈8192, d_expert≈1400): the architecturally correct sparsity ratio for the weight-AllGather formula is E/k = 512/8 = 64 — not the naive total/active parameter ratio of 10T/200B=50 that a first-pass estimate might reach for. The two numbers differ because total/active blends in the always-active components (attention, the one shared expert, embedding/output head, and the strawman’s 1-3 dense layers) that dilute the pure routed-expert sparsity down from 64 toward 50 — a useful internal consistency check, since diluting toward 50 rather than away from it is exactly the direction the arithmetic should go. This chapter uses 64 throughout, since it is the ratio that actually governs weight-movement cost; anyone cross-referencing the coarser 50-based figures used elsewhere should apply a ~1.28x scaling factor.

And the EP viability threshold on expert width is unchanged by the sparsity ratio (F_min is a per-expert-width condition, not a routing-fanout condition): 100,000 (CX-7) or 50,000 (CX-8), as derived above. The strawman’s d_expert≈1400 sits ~36x below the CX-8 threshold (and ~71x below the stricter CX-7 threshold) — architecturally indistinguishable from DeepSeek V3’s F=2048, which sits ~24x below CX-8 (~49x below CX-7) once corrected for the true GB200 peak. This is not a strawman-specific problem. The Ling paper’s granularity optimum (G≈12, Ant’s definition G=2·d_model/d_expert, stable across FLOPs budgets 1e18-1e20 and confirmed across 300+ trained models up to 28B params — Towards Greater Leverage, Jul 2025) and DeepSeek’s own precedent both push expert width in exactly the direction that makes flat cross-domain EP comms-bound. So does the post-snapshot Kimi K3, whose 16-of-896 expert configuration pushes granularity past anything in this chapter’s tables (Ch3’s addendum). Narrow, numerous experts are good for the Ch7/Ch13 scaling-law story and bad for this chapter’s network story, and the two literatures don’t currently talk to each other on this point. The practical consequence, worked through below: node/domain-limited routing (DeepSeek’s ≤4-node cap, generalized here to a ≤1-domain cap) remains structurally necessary at 10T scale, not a 2024-era workaround that a bigger cluster outgrows.

Why pure DP/FSDP is mathematically dead — and why ZeRO-1 escapes the same trap

At 100,000 GPUs with a plausible global batch in the 10-60M token range (bracketed by LLaMA-3’s 16M and DeepSeek-V3’s 63M token batches), per-GPU batch is roughly 100-600 tokens. Compare against the intra-domain FSDP threshold of 355,556 tokens/GPU derived above: per-GPU batch is 3-4 orders of magnitude below even the cheapest (single-domain, NVLink-speed) FSDP threshold, before cross-domain bandwidth is even considered. Pure DP or ZeRO-3-style FSDP, applied naively to the full 10T-parameter model, is not a suboptimal choice at this scale — it is comms-bound by several orders of magnitude at any batch size a 100k-GPU cluster could plausibly run. EP+PP+TP composition is therefore not an optimization; it is the only mathematically viable regime, exactly mirroring what DeepSeek V3 already demonstrates (64-way EP, 16-way PP, 2-way DP) at roughly 1/50th the GPU count and comparable total sparsity (DeepSeek-V3 technical report, Dec 2024).

But this raises an apparent puzzle: DeepSeek V3’s DP axis is not zero — it runs 2-way DP on top of EP64×PP16, and a 100k-GPU cluster needs a DP axis running into the hundreds of ways just to use all its GPUs. Why doesn’t that DP axis hit the same E/k wall? The resolution, synthesizing three sources that don’t individually state it: the E/k inflation is specifically a weight-AllGather-style (FSDP/ZeRO-3) artifact. It exists because FSDP must materialize all E experts’ weights on demand each forward pass to support k-sparse routing across an unpredictable token stream. DeepSeek’s actual DP choice is ZeRO-1 — optimizer-state sharding only, with the full (already EP- and PP-sharded) local weight shard resident on every DP replica at all times, synced via periodic gradient AllReduce rather than per-step weight AllGather (report §3.2). Because EP has already physically distributed the 512 experts across the EP group before DP replication happens, a GPU’s local shard already excludes the E−k experts it doesn’t hold — there is no “gather all E, use k” waste left for the DP axis to pay for. This matches Stas Bekman’s independent, hard-won field rule that ZeRO combined with PP should almost always be ZeRO stage 1 only, since PP’s small microbatches make ZeRO-2/3’s extra reduce-scatter proportionally expensive relative to its marginal memory savings — arrived at from BLOOM/IDEFICS-scale empirical tuning rather than from the Scaling Book’s algebra, but landing on the identical conclusion.

The ZeRO-1 DP axis is therefore free of the weight-gather inflation — but not of sparsity entirely, and the distinction deserves to be stated more carefully than the literature usually does. Gradient AllReduce volume scales with the total parameters resident on a replica’s shard, while compute scales with active parameters only — so the E/k ratio reappears in the gradient-sync condition with full force. The compute-bound floor for hiding the reduce behind the backward pass is not the naive B/X > C/W ≈ 50,000 tokens per replica, but roughly B/X ≳ (2/3)·(P_total/P_active)·(C/W) ≈ 1.7M tokens per replica on ConnectX-8 at this host’s ~50x sparsity (the 2/3 because only the backward pass is available for overlap). That floor has teeth for this chapter’s own recommendation: Layout 2 below, at DP=192 and a 40M-token global batch, delivers only ~208,000 tokens per replica — an ~8x shortfall — meaning its expert-gradient AllReduce cannot be fully hidden and its achievable MFU is capped near the pessimistic end of Ch23’s planning band regardless of kernel maturity. The escape routes are structural, not software: a small expert-data-parallel degree (DeepSeek’s own EDP=2 confines expert-gradient sync to two replicas), FP8 gradient compression (halving the traffic), or a larger global batch. So the load-bearing distinction stands, with one amendment: weight/optimizer memory sharding is EP+PP’s job; DP is a replication factor layered on top via ZeRO-1 — but it must be sized against a gradient-sync floor that inherits the full E/k ratio, even though the weight-gather term does not.

The EP-inside-NVL72 opportunity, and its hard limit

GB200 NVL72’s structural contribution to MoE training is domain size, not per-link bandwidth: NVLink5 gives the same 900 GB/s/GPU whether the domain has 8 GPUs (HGX B200) or 72 (NVL72) — the win is putting 9x more GPUs behind the cheap all-to-all domain before falling back to the slower scale-out fabric. This is confirmed empirically, not just algebraically, by Megatron-Core’s own measured dispatch/combine latency table (hidden=7168, seqlen=4096, 256 experts) in Scalable Training of MoE Models with Megatron Core (Mar 2026):

GB200’s HybridEP kernel stays essentially flat (391→675µs) as EP scales 8→64 because the entire domain fits inside NVLink; H100’s plain all-to-all blows up 7-8x as EP crosses node boundaries onto InfiniBand. Consistent with this, the same paper reports EP all-to-all consuming only ~20% of iteration time on GB200 (intra-domain EP64) versus 40-60% on H100 (cross-node EP64); even after dispatcher optimization the overhead is still 30-40% of iteration time for DeepSeek-V3-scale EP64 (§4.2, §9.2.2) — this residual is not zero even in the favorable GB200 regime, a fact worth carrying into the predicted-MFU numbers below.

The limit is the one derived above: F_min doesn’t move. NVL72’s 72-GPU domain changes how much EP is free (up to ~64-72-way, versus H100’s 8-way), not whether an expert of a given width is comms-bound. For a practical layout, this argues for EP=64 rather than the full EP=72: it divides the strawman’s 512 experts evenly (8 experts/GPU), and it matches a real-world operational pattern — GB200 NVL72 racks as deployed in 2025 commonly run 64-of-72 active GPUs with the remaining 8 held as hot spares for backplane/GPU failures, a reliability practice documented independently of any MoE-specific design choice (per SemiAnalysis’s “H100 vs GB200 NVL72 Training Benchmarks”, Aug 2025, paywalled). Sizing EP to leave spare headroom inside the domain is a free design choice that costs nothing in the comms model and buys operational slack Ch21 covers in more depth.

TP domain sizing: Y < F·W/C, and why “TP ≤ 72” is a trap

Stas Bekman’s flat field rule — “TP size ≤ GPUs per node” — is, evaluated against the Scaling Book’s derived Y < F·W_ici/C threshold at typical F, exactly the source of the commonly-quoted “8-16-way” TP ceiling. On an 8-GPU H100 node this rule and the derived threshold happen to coincide, inviting the (wrong) generalization that GB200 NVL72’s 72-GPU domain raises the ceiling to TP=72. It does not: the underlying Y < F·W/C ratio is unaffected by domain size, since both F and the C/W ratio are architecture and hardware properties independent of how many GPUs the NVLink fabric spans. Recomputing directly with the corrected GB200 numbers, for a dense FFN of width F=4×d_model=32,768 (relevant only to the strawman’s 1-3 non-MoE layers per Ch7): Y < F/(C/W) = 32,768/(5.0e15/900e9) = 32,768/5,556 ≈ 5.9. TP should stay below roughly 6-way even inside the cheap 72-GPU domain — a tighter ceiling than the generic “8-16-way” folk number, driven by this model’s particular d_model choice.

For the strawman’s routed-expert layers, this question is largely moot: expert-TP (splitting an individual expert’s already-narrow d_expert≈1400 matrix further across GPUs) is explicitly the wrong move — Megatron-Core’s own Parallel Folding paper (Apr 2025) frames this as the core “dense-sparse mismatch” MoE parallelism has to solve, since MoE “wants high EP and low (usually 1) expert-TP, because sharding an already-small expert matrix with TP just fragments it further.” Parallel Folding’s mechanism — decoupling attention-layer parallelism (TP×CP×DP×PP) from MoE-layer parallelism (ETP×EP×EDP×PP), sharing only PP for gradient-flow correctness — is exactly the tool that lets a layout run ETP=1 (no expert-level TP at all) while independently choosing whatever small TP degree the attention block’s arithmetic intensity supports. This matches DeepSeek V3’s own choice to skip tensor parallelism entirely during training, and it matches Megatron-Core’s own published reference configuration for DeepSeek-V3-scale training on GB200: TP1/PP4/EP64 (the Megatron-Bridge performance summary, 26.06 container). The layouts below follow this precedent: TP=1 everywhere except where a long-context stage’s attention block specifically benefits from tensor- or sequence-level sharding (see below).

Pipeline schedules: bubble formulas and the overlap landscape

The naive 1F1B bubble fraction is r=(p-1)/m (p=pipeline depth, m=microbatch count); interleaving with v virtual model-chunks per GPU reduces this to r=(p-1)/(v·m), trading v-fold more communication for v-fold smaller bubble (the Ultra-Scale Playbook, 2025). DeepSeek’s DualPipe goes further, splitting each backward chunk into separate input-gradient and weight-gradient micro-ops and feeding the pipeline from both ends simultaneously to hide cross-node all-to-all fully behind compute — its published bubble formula is (PP/2−1)(F&B+B−3W), paid for with 2x parameter memory per device (two model copies live on each PP rank) and PP+1 activation memory, a cost DeepSeek judged acceptable specifically because a large EP degree already keeps per-GPU parameter count small (DeepSeek-V3 report, Table 2). Megatron-Core did not adopt DualPipe’s code directly; instead combined_1f1b.py implements a “conceptually DualPipe-like” merged-forward-backward 1F1B overlap on top of the standard schedule, achieving a comparable bubble/memory profile without the 2x parameter-memory tax — a still-open GitHub thread on this exact design (Megatron-LM issue #1524) asks whether the VPP-based version requires more inter-node communication than true DualPipe under equivalent bubble rates, unresolved as of the issue’s last comment.

For a 90-100-layer model (Ch7’s strawman), Megatron-Core’s Flexible Asymmetric VPP — which allows arbitrary per-virtual-stage layer type and count, rather than the traditional uniform-layer-count constraint — is directly relevant: DeepSeek V3’s own PP=16/VPP=2 layout assigns embedding+3 dense layers to rank 0 (cost-matched to 2 MoE layers), 2 MoE layers per VPP stage to ranks 1-13, the MTP module alone to rank 14, and the loss layer alone to rank 15 (Megatron-Core MoE report, Table 10). The same tool lets a 96-100-layer, MTP-augmented strawman balance genuinely uneven per-stage costs (dense layers, MoE layers, and 2-3 MTP modules per Ch7’s depth-2-or-3 recommendation) rather than forcing a naive uniform split.

Activation recomputation economics

Full activation checkpointing trades a well-quantified throughput tax for a large memory win: for a Llama-3.1-8B-shaped block at seqlen=32768, bs=1, activations without checkpointing run 224 GiB versus 31 GiB with it — a 7.2x reduction — for a throughput cost of roughly 20-25%, “up to 30-40% per newer papers” (ml-engineering, training/performance). Selective recomputation (recomputing only cheap-to-recompute, expensive-to-store ops like attention softmax/dropout rather than the full block) claws back most of this overhead while retaining most of the memory win, and is the approach Megatron’s own sequence-parallel + selective-recompute combination validated at 530B-parameter, 2,240-A100 scale — 54.2% MFU versus a 42.1% full-recompute baseline, +29% relative (Reducing Activation Recomputation in Large Transformer Models, 2022). At 200B-active-parameter scale with PP-imposed small microbatches and a long-context extension stage reaching 32-128K tokens (Ch7’s curriculum), some form of recomputation is close to mandatory rather than optional — but the exact tax for this specific block structure (MoE FFN plus MLA-and-sparse-attention plus MTP heads) is not directly measured anywhere public. The 20-40% range above is a dense-model anchor to budget against, not a validated MoE-specific number, and it is the single largest source of uncertainty in the predicted-MFU figures below.

Before recomputation policy can be chosen, the more basic question — does 10T of parameters even fit in 186 GB per GPU — deserves the two-minute audit this literature always skips. (The audit is our own arithmetic on the Layout 2 configuration below.) At EP64×PP8, each GPU holds 512 experts ÷ 64 ranks × 12 of 96 layers of routed-expert weight — about 19.5B routed parameters — plus its PP-slice of the EP-replicated spine (attention, shared expert, dense layers, embedding/MTP shards), a few billion more: call it ~24B parameters resident per GPU. That is ~48 GB of BF16 weights (half that if compute weights are held in FP8 with masters in the optimizer shard), plus ~48 GB of BF16 gradients, which under ZeRO-1 must stay fully resident between backward and the sharded update. The optimizer state — the term that dominates every naive 10T memory estimate — is the one thing that essentially vanishes at this layout: the full-model 80 TB of AdamW state (Ch9) divided by EP64×PP8 and then by DP=192 comes to under 2 GB per GPU (half that under Muon). Static state therefore totals roughly 75–100 GB depending on the precision recipe, leaving 85–110 GB of the 186 GB for activations — enough that selective recomputation (the +29%-throughput regime of the Megatron result above) closes the budget for p=8 in-flight microbatches at 4–8K context, where full recomputation would only be forced by a much deeper pipeline or a naive no-recompute policy. Three corollaries. The “10T parameters cannot fit” intuition dissolves entirely once EP×PP has done its 512-way sharding — memory is not why this layout is shaped as it is. The real memory consumer to engineer against is gradients-plus-activations, which is why FP8 gradient storage (halving 48 GB) buys more than any optimizer-state trick at this DP degree. And the headroom that remains is not slack — it is what absorbs routing-imbalance transients, when a hot expert’s token batch briefly inflates one rank’s activation footprint (the MegaScale-Data 6.9x microbatch-FLOP-imbalance phenomenon of Ch15, in memory rather than time). GB300’s 288 GB (Ch16) widens every term of this audit and is most valuable at exactly the long-context stage where the activation term grows into the budget.

Sequence and context parallelism for long-context stages

Attention FLOPs only dominate the per-layer FLOPs budget once context length exceeds roughly 8×d_model (the Scaling Book’s Transformer Math chapter, Feb 2025); at d_model≈8192 that crossover sits at T>65,536 tokens — squarely inside Ch7’s planned context-extension stage (32-128K tokens), meaning sequence/context parallelism stops being optional exactly where the curriculum says the model needs to go. Among the six cataloged SP implementations, DeepSpeed-Ulysses’s comms volume scales down with parallelism degree (4Nh/P) while Megatron-LM’s native sequence parallelism stays flat (4Nh) regardless of P (ml-engineering, model-parallelism). Ulysses’s core primitive — an all-to-all that redistributes the sequence dimension across GPUs and gathers back the full sequence per local attention head — is architecturally the same shape as MoE’s own dispatch/combine all-to-all, making it the natural SP pairing for a ZeRO+EP-based stack rather than Megatron-style SP, which would add fixed, non-shrinking traffic on top of EP’s own all-to-all rather than sharing the same comms budget logic. Ring-attention variants (Liu et al.’s ring-passed K/V, Colossal-AI’s ring self-attention, DistFlashAttn’s load-balanced variant) remain live alternatives with less production precedent at this scale.

Stas’s composition rules as a field-experience cross-check

The Scaling Book’s crossovers are first-principles derivations; ml-engineering’s decision tree is compressed field experience from BLOOM-176B and IDEFICS-80B, and the two should agree once evaluated at the same constants. Walking the tree for a 10T-total model: it categorically does not fit on a single node (ruling out the “try ZeRO-with-replicas first” branch, which assumes the model fits within one node’s memory), leaving the multi-node branches — “fast inter-node connectivity: PP+TP+DP” or, if still memory-constrained, “DP+PP+TP+ZeRO-1.” Both land on exactly the EP(-substituting-for-much-of-TP)+PP+ZeRO-1 composition this chapter derives from the roofline algebra independently — a genuine convergence between a from-first-principles theory source and a from-scratch-trainings-run practitioner source that neither document states explicitly.

Three worked candidate layouts

Layout 1 — the anti-pattern, shown to make the “pure DP is dead” argument concrete. EP=8 (single H100-style node-equivalent, deliberately under-using the 72-GPU domain), DP≈12,288 to reach 100k GPUs, PP and TP minimal. At E/k=64 and any realistic per-domain bandwidth, the FSDP-style critical batch this configuration needs is in the millions-to-tens-of-millions of tokens per GPU — even the most permissive cross-domain figure derived above (3.2M tokens/GPU at ConnectX-8) times a DP degree in the thousands implies a global batch requirement several orders of magnitude beyond anything in the LLaMA-3-to-DeepSeek-V3 range. This configuration does not train at a usable MFU; it is included to show numerically why “just use more DP” fails, not as a real candidate.

Layout 2 — DeepSeek-style scaled up, the bulk-training (4-8K context) recommendation. EP=64 (fits inside one NVL72 domain, 8 of the strawman’s 512 experts per GPU, leaving 8-GPU hot-spare headroom), PP=8 physical stages with VPP=4 (Flexible Asymmetric VPP, 32 effective pipeline slots for ~96 layers ≈ 3 layers/slot), TP=1, DP=192 via ZeRO-1. Total: 64×8×192 = 98,304 GPUs (≈1,365 NVL72 domains), leaving headroom below the 100k budget for evaluation, checkpoint-coordination, and spare capacity. Interleaved-bubble check: keeping r=(p-1)/(v·m) under 10% with p=8, v=4 requires m≥18 microbatches per flush — comfortably achievable. ZeRO-1 compute-bound check on the DP axis: 50,000 tokens/GPU (ConnectX-8) is easily cleared by 18-24 microbatches of a few thousand tokens each. Predicted global batch, order of magnitude: 10-15M tokens, bracketed by LLaMA-3’s 16M and DeepSeek-V3’s 63M. Predicted MFU: the closest measured empirical anchor is Megatron-Core’s own DeepSeek-V3-685B benchmark on GB200 at PP4×VPP4×EP64/256 GPUs — 1,048 TFLOPS/GPU sustained MXFP8, which is 21.0% FP8 MFU against the corrected 5.0 PFLOPS denominator (857 TFLOPS/GPU BF16, 34.3% BF16 MFU against the corrected 2.5 PFLOPS denominator; Table 11, normalized against Ch16’s corrected peaks). The strawman’s narrower experts (d_expert≈1400 versus DeepSeek’s 2048, roughly 30% narrower) push arithmetic intensity down further, and 512 experts versus 256 adds routing/permutation overhead on top of the ~9% Megatron-Core already measures post-fusion; both effects push predicted MFU below the anchor. This is an extrapolation, not a measurement: a defensible planning range is 15-19% FP8 MFU (≈30-38% BF16-equivalent), with the DeepSeek-V3-on-GB200 figure as the optimistic bound and the activation-recomputation tax (20-40%, above) layered on separately as a further, not-yet-included, throughput cost.

Layout 2 also exposes a structural effect that, as far as we can tell, is stated nowhere in the parallelism literature — it belongs in the open rather than buried in a kernel footnote. (The derivation is ours.) Call it expert-batch starvation: the average per-expert grouped-GEMM batch is M = B_global·k/(E·DP·m) — global batch times routed fraction, divided across DP replicas and pipeline microbatches. DeepSeek-V3 never had to think about this term because its DP degree was 2: at a ~60M-token batch, EP64 and E=256, its per-expert M sat in the thousands-to-tens-of-thousands for any plausible microbatch count. Layout 2 divides a critical-batch-bounded 10-15M-token global batch by DP=192 and m≥18 (the microbatch count its own bubble math requires), leaving M ≈ 45-70 tokens per expert per GEMM call as the average case — squarely in the small-M regime where the one measured nearby datapoint (M=128 → 190 TFLOPS on H800, ~12% of the same library’s well-shaped peak, Ch10) says grouped-GEMM efficiency collapses, and where Ch17’s documented 10x naive-to-tuned Blackwell headroom is sampled at its bad end. The general law is worth stating because it does not appear in the Scaling Book, the Megatron papers, or the MoE-design literature, each of which owns only one factor of the denominator: at fixed global batch, per-expert GEMM efficiency degrades inversely with DP degree times microbatch count — so scaling a granular MoE to more GPUs makes every expert’s matmul thinner even when all the communication is perfectly hidden. The gradient-noise ceiling (Ch8) caps B_global; the bubble formula forces m up with pipeline depth; E/k is fixed by the architecture’s granularity bet (Ch7); DP is whatever is left after EP and PP saturate their domains. Every escape route is therefore a real design trade, not a tuning knob: raise the global batch toward (or past) the critical-batch ceiling — which is precisely the move Muon’s large-batch tolerance (Ch9) underwrites, and a better explanation than fashion for why 2026 frontier batches drift toward 60-100M tokens; shrink m and buy back the bubble with DualPipe-style dual injection rather than microbatch count; or coarsen the granularity bet itself, trading Ch7’s EL-law compounding for fatter GEMMs. A 100k-GPU MoE program that discovers this arithmetic after freezing E, k, and the optimizer has already spent its escape routes — this single interaction, more than any kernel gap in Ch17, is why architecture, optimizer, and parallelism (Ch7, Ch9, this chapter) are one decision, not three.

Layout 3 — long-context extension stage (32-128K tokens), Ch7’s curriculum tail. Starting from Layout 2’s EP64×PP8 unit, insert Ulysses-style context parallelism CP=4 and shrink DP correspondingly: EP64×PP8×CP4×DP48 = 98,304 GPUs (same total). At 128K context, the T>8D crossover derived above means attention FLOPs are solidly dominant, making CP load-bearing rather than optional. Two countervailing effects on predicted MFU relative to Layout 2: Megatron-Core’s own long-context measurements (§6.5) show DeepSeek-V3-style (dense-attention-heavy) architectures losing MFU at 256K context (88% of short-context MFU on H100) while SDPA-kernel-efficient architectures like Qwen3-235B-A22B actually gain MFU at long context (129% of short-context). The strawman’s DSA-style trainable sparse attention (Ch7) — O(Lk) rather than O(L²) — argues for a milder version of the Qwen3 pattern rather than the DeepSeek-V3 pattern, since sparse attention’s cost doesn’t scale with L the way dense attention’s does. Net predicted MFU: roughly 85-95% of Layout 2’s figure, i.e., in the low-to-mid-teens percent FP8 — but this combines two extrapolations (Layout 2’s own uncertainty, plus the DSA-tempers-long-context-cost argument) and should be treated as the most speculative number in this chapter.

Two placement facts about Layout 3 deserve to be stated because the layout table hides them. (Both are our own working-out of the layout geometry.) First, CP4 necessarily crosses NVL72 domains: each pipeline stage’s EP64 already fills a domain, so the four CP ranks sharing a sequence live in four different racks, and the Ulysses all-to-all rides the scale-out fabric, not NVLink. (The alternative — packing CP4×EP16 inside one domain — puts 32 experts ≈ 78B routed parameters on each GPU, which the memory audit above rules out at 186 GB.) The volume arithmetic makes this material rather than cosmetic: Ulysses moves roughly 4·tokens·d_model bytes per attention layer against dispatch-plus-combine’s 2k·tokens·d_model — about a quarter of the MoE volume at top-8 — but at one-ninth the bandwidth (100 vs 900 GB/s per GPU), so its time cost is roughly 2x the entire intra-domain MoE dispatch per layer unless overlapped, partially tempered by MLA’s compressed KV shrinking the K/V share of the transfer (Ch6). Second, nobody has published how context parallelism composes with trainable-sparse attention at all: Ulysses and ring variants both assume every query can reach every key, but under DSA each query’s top-k selected tokens live on arbitrary CP ranks, so the natural implementation gathers the compressed/indexer representation globally and fetches only selected KV — a different communication pattern from either standard scheme, with volume scaling on k rather than sequence length. DeepSeek necessarily built this for V4’s 1M-token training (as Moonshot did for K3’s), but neither has documented it; a run adopting Ch7’s DSA-style attention should treat CP-composed-with-sparse-attention as a build item with no public reference implementation, not a solved configuration.

Implications for the 10T/200B run

The arithmetic in this chapter yields six load-bearing conclusions for the run plan. First, EP degree should be sized to the NVL72 domain (64-way, leaving hot-spare headroom) and never allowed to flatten across domains at this expert width — the strawman’s d_expert≈1400 sits tens of times below the compute-bound EP viability threshold on any realistic scale-out fabric, so domain-limited (or DeepSeek-style node-capped) routing is a permanent architectural constraint, not a scale-dependent one that a bigger cluster escapes. Second, ZeRO-1 — not ZeRO-3/FSDP — is the correct DP-axis primitive precisely because EP has already done the expensive weight-sharding; treating this as a free choice rather than a structural necessity is the single most common way a first-pass parallelism plan would waste an order of magnitude of communication budget. Third, tensor parallelism belongs almost nowhere in this layout: not on the expert matrices (already too narrow), and only marginally (Y<6) on the strawman’s few dense layers — Parallel Folding is the mechanism that makes “TP=1 for MoE, small TP for attention if at all” a composable choice rather than an all-or-nothing one. Fourth, the predicted 15-19% FP8 MFU for the bulk-training layout is a real number with a real empirical anchor (Megatron-Core’s measured GB200/DeepSeek-V3 benchmark), but it sits below the more optimistic MFU figures sometimes quoted for GB200 elsewhere in the frontier-lab literature — Ch23’s money math should treat this chapter’s bottom-up derivation as a conservative floor to reconcile against, not a number to average away. Fifth — derived here rather than inherited from any source — the gradient-sync floor above means the DP axis is not free even under ZeRO-1: at this host’s sparsity, a layout must buy roughly 1.7M tokens per replica per step (via small EDP, FP8 gradients, or global batch) before its expert-gradient AllReduce hides behind compute, and a layout that ignores this ships with a calculable, structural MFU ceiling in the low teens that no amount of kernel maturation will lift. Sixth, and cutting deepest: expert-batch starvation — M = B_global·k/(E·DP·m) — means the run’s grouped-GEMM efficiency is set jointly by the architecture’s granularity (Ch7), the optimizer’s batch tolerance (Ch9), the gradient-noise ceiling (Ch8), and this chapter’s DP and microbatch choices; at Layout 2’s own numbers the average expert GEMM sees only ~45-70 tokens, and the cheapest escape (a Muon-underwritten 60-100M-token global batch) must be decided before the architecture and optimizer are frozen, because it is those chapters’ choices that determine whether the batch can get there.
