OpenAI, Anthropic, Google DeepMind, Meta, xAI, and Microsoft AI have collectively said more about their pretraining infrastructure across 2024-2026 than at any point since GPT-3 — and almost none of it through the channel you’d expect. Model cards stayed thin. The substance arrived through OCP networking papers, AWS product-launch blogs, sworn court testimony, a podcast where a chief scientist forgot to be careful, and one extraordinarily well-sourced paid newsletter that got a single Meta post-mortem section right when everyone else missed it. Here is that record, lab by lab, every figure dated, with the sourcing problems stated in the same breath as the claims.

OpenAI

The deepest public look into OpenAI’s production training fabric is a networking-standards contribution, not anything published about a model. MRC (Multipath Reliable Connection) — co-developed with AMD, Broadcom, Intel, Microsoft, and Nvidia, and published as the Open Compute Project contribution “Resilient AI Supercomputer Networking using MRC and SRv6” (OpenAI, “Supercomputer networking to accelerate large scale AI training”, May 2026; the post itself 403’d on direct fetch, with details corroborated via MarkTechPost and a Towards Data Science deep-dive) — extends RoCE with SRv6 source routing developed alongside Ultra Ethernet Consortium techniques. Each 800 Gb/s NIC splits into eight independent 100 Gb/s “planes” and sprays a single flow’s packets across all eight, rather than trusting ECMP hashing to spread load. Routing state lives at the NIC — 128-256 Entropy Values per Queue Pair — so switches hold zero forwarding state, versus 100,000+ FIB entries conventionally. Health-assessment cycles run at 1-2ms, which makes route convergence “1,000 to 30,000x faster” than traditional routing. Two choices stand out as engineering doctrine rather than spec-sheet detail: OpenAI deliberately disabled switch-layer adaptive routing “because [they] didn’t want two adaptive routing mechanisms interacting,” and dropped PFC entirely in favor of lossy Ethernet with selective ACKs and packet trimming. The topology is two-tier, supports “131,072 GPUs at full bisection bandwidth” on roughly 2/3 the optics and 3/5 the switches of a conventional 3-tier design, and has a worst-case path of 3 hops versus 5-7. It runs in production across Oracle’s Stargate Abilene site and Microsoft’s Fairwater Atlanta/Wisconsin sites, with jobs surviving simultaneous transceiver failures and core-switch reboots without interruption. This is the closest thing the closed frontier has produced to a systems paper (Ch20).

What scaling pretraining past 100,000 GPUs actually felt like is on the record too, in the “Pre-Training GPT-4.5” retrospective (YouTube, April 11, 2025) — Sam Altman interviewing Chief Systems Architect Amin Tootoonchian, pretraining lead Alex Paino, and data-efficiency lead Daniel Selsam. No direct transcript was recoverable; everything below triangulates three independent secondary write-ups that substantially agree. The run scaled from roughly 10,000 GPUs (GPT-4-class) to over 100,000, which introduced qualitatively new failure modes and forced a shift to multi-cluster state management — the closest corroboration of cross-datacenter training at OpenAI, though no source gives an inter-cluster bandwidth or latency figure, so the multi-cluster claim is paraphrase, not spec. The now-famous bug is a systems parable: a PyTorch torch.sum edge case surfaced as “illegal memory access” errors roughly once per 100-1,000 steps, reproducible only via a specific Triton-kernel/torch-fallback interaction, spraying seemingly unrelated symptoms; the team ranked hypotheses by vote, the least-favored one turned out correct, and once patched, “all varied error symptoms disappeared simultaneously.” The most substantively important line, repeated near-verbatim across sources: “we’re no longer compute constrained on the best models we can produce.” One source frames the human-vs-model data-efficiency gap at “100,000x” — an imprecise secondhand figure; a more careful paraphrase gives the underlying hypothesis: “the volume of high-quality training data isn’t growing as fast as compute… meaningful concepts in data are sparse and power-law distributed.” GPT-4.5 comes out as roughly 10x GPT-4’s pretraining compute, “hundreds of people” over roughly two years — against the claim that a GPT-4-class model could now be reproduced by “5-10 people.”

Independent reporting sharpens the picture. OpenAI ran at least two large training attempts for what became GPT-4.5, each spanning several months, with language-task gains landing but coding/math gains falling short; Sutskever, in the same coverage: “The data is not growing because we have but one internet” (WSJ via Fortune, Feb 25, 2025). A six-month run of this class is estimated at roughly half a billion dollars in compute alone, with a “GPT-5-scale” cloud-compute figure floated near $1 billion. Epoch AI’s tracker, pulled July 12, 2026, still ranks GPT-4.5 at 3.8e26 FLOP (“Likely”) as OpenAI’s best-evidenced run, with GPT-5 at only 6.6e25 FLOP (“Speculative”) — a 5-6x reduction, the opposite of the historical 100x-per-generation pattern (Epoch AI, notable_ai_models.csv). Epoch attributes the reversal to a September 2024 RL/reasoning breakthrough that let cheap post-training substitute for expensive pretraining, pricing GPT-4.5 at “$200M pretrain + $2M post-train” against GPT-5’s “$20M pretrain” (Epoch AI, “Why GPT-5 used less training compute than GPT-4.5”). No OpenAI model since GPT-4.5 has an Epoch FLOP estimate at all as of the July 2026 pull. A persistent rumor sourced to SemiAnalysis via Hacker News claims OpenAI “has not completed a successful full-scale pre-training run… since GPT-4o in May 2024” — single-hop-sourced, undercut by GPT-4.5’s undisputed status as a real new pretrain, and best treated with skepticism. And one number arrived under oath rather than through PR: OpenAI President Greg Brockman testified in Musk v. OpenAI that OpenAI plans to spend $50 billion on computing power in 2026, up from “tens of billions of dollars” recently and “roughly $30 million in 2017” (Bloomberg, May 5, 2026, from the trial record).

Stargate reduces to a few planning-relevant numbers. The $500B program targets >9 GW by 2029 across seven US sites — for scale, against roughly “20 million H100-equivalents” of global 2025 AI compute (Epoch AI, “OpenAI Stargate: where the US sites stand”, April 2026). Abilene sat at roughly 0.3 GW / ~250,000 H100-equivalents as of April 2026, targeting 1.2 GW across 8 buildings by Q4 2026 (DCD/Epoch aggregation); Larry Ellison’s claim that the site will “eventually house more than 450,000 Nvidia GB200 GPUs” is an Oracle number, not OpenAI’s (via DCD, June 2026). The most instructive detail is a refusal: OpenAI declined a planned 600 MW/2.1 GW Abilene expansion in early-to-mid 2026, redirecting capacity to avoid mixing Blackwell with incoming Vera Rubin hardware on one site (Tom’s Hardware, citing DCD) — a disclosed engineering decision about hardware-generation staging that any multi-year buildout has to make.

Anthropic

Anthropic runs the inverse disclosure policy: compute numbers live exclusively in partnership press releases, never in model or system cards — consistent across every Claude generation. The AWS side: Project Rainier is disclosed at “nearly half a million” Trainium2 chips, “70% larger than any other AI computing platform in AWS history,” organized into EC2 UltraClusters of 64-chip UltraServers (4 servers × 16 chips, NeuronLink intra-server, EFA inter-server), giving Anthropic “more than five times the compute power” of its prior setup (AWS, “AWS activates Project Rainier”, ~Oct 2025). Amazon’s later corroboration clarifies “~500K” describes Rainier specifically; the trajectory was “more than 1 million” Trainium2 chips for training and inference combined across AWS datacenters by end of 2026 (Amazon posts, plus Yahoo Finance reporting). Trainium3 (AWS re:Invent 2025, Dec 2025) specs at 2.52 PFLOPS MXFP8/chip, 144GB HBM3e, 144-chip UltraServers at 362 PFLOPS, a stated path to “up to 1 million Trainium3 chips” in one application, and 40% better energy efficiency than Trainium2; Anthropic is named an early adopter. Anthropic’s own framing commits “more than $100 billion over the next ten years” to AWS, securing “up to 5GW,” with “nearly 1GW total of Trainium2 and Trainium3” targeted online by end of 2026 (Anthropic).

The Google side is where the interesting arithmetic lives. The official disclosure: “up to one million TPUs,” “well over a gigawatt” online in 2026, “tens of billions of dollars” (Anthropic, “Expanding our use of Google Cloud TPUs and Services”, Oct 23, 2025). A more granular reconstruction: Phase 1 = 400k TPUv7 Ironwoods bought outright ($10B, Broadcom-brokered); Phase 2 = 600k rented via GCP ($42B of RPO); Google’s equity stake capped at 15% with no voting rights, preserving arm’s-length hardware dealing; Anthropic’s effective cost estimated at ~$1.60/TPU-hour (SemiAnalysis, “Google TPUv7: The 900lb Gorilla In the Room,” Nov 28, 2025). The same piece works out why the multi-vendor bet pencils: against a ~30% Nvidia training-MFU rule of thumb, Google/Anthropic need only ~15-19% TPU MFU to match GB300’s cost per effective FLOP — and Anthropic’s realized ~40% MFU implies roughly 52% lower TCO per effective PFLOP than GB300 NVL72 even after GCP’s margin. By mid-2026, at least three non-reconciling dollar/GW figures circulate for the cumulative relationship — the Oct 2025 framing above; an April 7, 2026 Google-Broadcom follow-on adding “3.5 GW starting 2027” (TechCrunch); and Bloomberg’s May 18, 2026 cumulative “up to $40 billion… 5 gigawatts… up to one million Ironwood chips” — best read as tranche-by-tranche snapshots of a moving negotiation, not a ledger.

The system cards, meanwhile, describe pretraining data sources — web crawl, licensed third-party data, Anthropic-generated synthetic data, and (recently) opted-in user data — and state that customer conversations are not used for training by default (the Claude Sonnet 4.5 card, Sept 2025, and earlier; read via Simon Willison’s summaries and Stanford CRFM’s Foundation Model Transparency Index). No GPU/TPU/Trainium counts, FLOPs, or energy figures appear in any Claude system card. The split is total: capabilities in the cards, compute in the press releases.

For macro numbers, the clearest CEO-level source is Dario Amodei on Dwarkesh, “We are near the end of the exponential”: “The amount of compute the industry is building this year is probably, call it, 10-15 gigawatts. It goes up by roughly 3x a year.” On the training-inference split: “Let’s say half of your compute is for training and half of your compute is for inference” — an industry generalization, not Anthropic-specific. On cost: “Each gigawatt costs maybe $10 billion, on the order of $10-15 billion a year.” Amodei votes for “raw compute, data quantity, data quality/distribution, training duration, scalable objective functions, and numerical stability” over “novel algorithmic techniques” — a bitter-lesson stance in direct tension with Sutskever’s “age of research” framing — while declining to give Anthropic’s exact spend beyond “comparable to what the biggest players in the game are buying.” One attribution worth pinning down, because it circulates wrong: the “~$1 million RL spend” figure originates in Amodei’s own essay as paraphrased on Dwarkesh’s show — not, as often misattributed, a Sholto Douglas disclosure — and describes a period when the team was deliberately not compute-limited on RL: a “tech tree” strategy of buying algorithmic certainty before committing large compute. Set against 2026’s RL-dominant allocations, it is a striking reminder of how recently that phase ended.

Google DeepMind

The largest single infrastructure disclosure from any lab is Google’s Virgo Network and TPU 8t/8i package from Cloud Next (Google Cloud Blog, “Introducing Virgo megascale data center fabric” and “TPU 8t and TPU 8i technical deep dive”, April 22, 2026). TPU 8t (training-optimized, Broadcom-codesigned) delivers 12.6 PFLOPs peak FP4 per chip, 216GB HBM3e at 6,528 GB/s, with native FP4 doubling MXU throughput versus FP8. A superpod packs 9,600 liquid-cooled chips for 121 FP4 ExaFLOPs peak — roughly 3x the prior-generation (Ironwood) pod. TPU 8i (inference-optimized, MediaTek-codesigned) uses a “Boardfly” ICI topology cutting network diameter from 16 hops to 7 at 1,024-chip scale. Virgo — the scale-out fabric, not a chip — links 134,000 TPU 8t chips in one non-blocking fabric per site at 47 Pb/s bisectional bandwidth on a flat two-layer topology. The stated rationale matters as much as the specs: “Training demands now exceed the power and space of a single data center, requiring unified, multi-data-center domains” — a dated, on-the-record acknowledgment that single-DC training no longer suffices at the frontier, echoing Microsoft’s Russinovich (Ch20). And the headline claim: with JAX + Pathways, Google states it can orchestrate “more than 1 million TPU chips in a single training cluster” — two to three orders of magnitude beyond the Gemini-era ceiling in stated ambition.

That earlier ceiling is documented in the Gemini 1.0 Technical Report — still the most quantified goodput/reliability disclosure any closed lab has produced as of mid-2026, and routinely misattributed to the Gemini 1.5 report, which carries no comparable systems section (Gemini Team, arXiv:2312.11805, December 2023). TPUv4 SuperPods of 4,096 chips carried a dedicated optical circuit switch that could reconfigure 4×4×4 cubes into arbitrary 3D torus topologies in ~10 seconds. Gemini Ultra trained “across multiple sites, and multiple clusters within those sites” (Thomas Kurian, on record), combining SuperPods via Google’s Jupiter OCS — model-parallel within superpods, data-parallel across them — with Google stating that “network latencies and bandwidths are sufficient to support the commonly used synchronous training paradigm” even across sites. The number every subsequent goodput claim gets benchmarked against: goodput — time computing useful steps over elapsed job time — “for the largest-scale training job increased from 85 to 97 percent.” Google explicitly expected SDC events “every week or two” at this scale, mitigated by deterministic replay plus proactive SDC scanners on idle machines, and moved recovery away from periodic disk checkpointing toward redundant in-memory copies of model state — the earliest explicit disclosure of the pattern Ch21 treats as a design default.

Some of the best Google disclosure arrives by proxy. “How to Scale Your Model,” the JAX-ML/DeepMind scaling textbook (2025, Jacob Austin, Sholto Douglas, and colleagues), carries Jeff Dean’s endorsement on X — “Training our most capable Gemini models relies heavily on our JAX software stack + Google’s TPU hardware platforms” — confirming JAX+TPU remains the production Gemini path. And Dean and Noam Shazeer, on Dwarkesh (Feb 12, 2025), gave the plainest confirmation anywhere that cross-datacenter synchronous pretraining is routine at Google. Dean: “We’re already doing it… in the Gemini 1.5 tech report, we said we used multiple metro areas and trained with some of the compute in each place,” with the constraint being purely a timing budget — “as long as the gradient accumulation and communication of the parameters across metro areas happens fast enough relative to the step time, you’re golden.” Shazeer: “As long as you can sync all of the parameters… in the time it takes to do one step, you’re pretty good.” That is a materially stronger claim than “async is possible”: the gate is bandwidth relative to step time, not algorithm. Dean also volunteered a candid MoE critique — “current problem… it’s still a very regular structure where each of the experts is the same size. The paths merge back together very fast” — relevant to the granularity choices in Ch7 and Ch9.

On data scarcity, Dean pushed back directly: “I think everyone has this view that we’re running out of training data… I’m not too worried about that as an impediment to making progress” — citing untapped video, synthetic generation, and multi-epoch passes as substitutes for unique tokens. This is Google’s explicit counter to Sutskever — disputing not finiteness, but that finiteness binds. The disagreement went fully on the record: Oriol Vinyals posted that Gemini 3’s pretraining delta over 2.5 was the largest jump the team has seen, “contra the popular belief that scaling is over,” explicitly referencing a NeurIPS ’25 talk shared with Sutskever and Quoc Le (~Nov 2025; the post’s exact wording is unfetched, so treat the paraphrase as provisional) — a rare dated public disagreement between frontier-lab principals from the same panel, and one Ch8 returns to.

Meta

Meta’s Llama 3 paper remains the richest single frontier infrastructure disclosure as of mid-2026 — read its infra chapter as a methods appendix, not a marketing document (Meta, “The Llama 3 Herd of Models,” arXiv:2407.21783, July 2024). The 405B model trained on up to 16,384 of the 24,576 H100 GPUs in Meta’s RoCE cluster, on 400 Gbps per-GPU interconnect across a 3-tier Clos fabric (Arista 7800 + Minipack2, 1:7 oversubscription between pods). Storage ran on Tectonic — 240PB across 7,500 SSD servers, 2TB/s sustained, 7TB/s peak. Meta discloses an actual MFU range: 38-43% BF16, dropping to ~41% at the 16K-GPU configuration from reduced per-GPU batch size — one of the only frontier labs to publish a real MFU figure for a named model. Parallelism was explicit 4D: TP + PP + CP (context parallelism) + DP, e.g. TP=8, CP=1, PP=16, DP=64-128. Over a 54-day window the run logged 419 unexpected component-failure interruptions (~1 every 3 hours), 78% confirmed hardware, GPU/NVLink failures the largest category — yet maintained >90% effective training time. One rare concrete finding: diurnal 1-2% throughput variation from ambient temperature effects on GPU voltage/frequency scaling (Ch21). NCCLX, Meta’s internal NCCL fork, is credited as central to failure localization.

Llama 4 Behemoth’s public footprint is thin by design: ~2T total parameters, 288B active, 16 experts; Scout and Maverick shipped, Behemoth never did (Meta AI blog, April 2025). The only hard efficiency number ever released: “while pre-training our Llama 4 Behemoth model using FP8 and 32K GPUs, we achieved 390 TFLOPs/GPU” — only 2x Llama-3-405B’s throughput-per-GPU despite ~5x the active parameters, implying FP8 + MoE sparsity doing substantial efficiency work. The family-wide mixture is stated at “more than 30 trillion tokens,” spanning text/image/video and 200 languages.

What actually killed Behemoth did not come from Meta. The “Llama Behemoth 4 Failure Post-Mortem” section of SemiAnalysis’s “Meta Superintelligence – Leadership, Compute, Talent, and Data” (Dylan Patel, Jeremie Eliahou Ontiveros, Wei Zhou, July 11, 2025) names four concrete root causes — the most specific public attribution of blame for any frontier MoE pretrain failure anywhere in the record. First, chunked attention. Behemoth used Meta’s chunked local-attention scheme — independently confirmed via HuggingFace’s transformers Llama4 config: attention_chunk_size=8192, a full-context NoPE layer only every 4th layer, a 3:1 local:global ratio. SemiAnalysis: “Behemoth’s implementation of chunked attention chasing efficiency created blind spots, especially at block boundaries. This impacts the model’s ability to develop reasoning abilities as chain of thought exceeds one chunk in length” — and, worse, Meta “didn’t even have the proper long context evaluations or testing infrastructure set up to determine that chunked attention would not work.” That is an evals-before-scale-up governance gap, not just an architecture bet gone wrong.

Second — and the single most load-bearing claim in the post-mortem for any MoE design: Meta reportedly switched Behemoth’s router from expert-choice (EC) to token-choice (TC) mid-run, and “the performance drop from the switch resulted in a model that was meaningfully worse than a model fully trained on TC.” The switch itself — not merely the initial choice of EC — gets the blame; SemiAnalysis calls EC→TC transitions “not unusual for EC models” generally, but asserts Meta’s execution produced an unusually large, unquantified loss. No independent source corroborates the claim or its magnitude — it is single-sourced — but it is architecturally coherent (experts specialized under EC’s balanced distribution must re-adapt to TC’s skewed, popularity-driven assignment), and it is the most specific “here is exactly what not to do” MoE-routing data point in the public record (Ch7, Ch9). Third, data quality: Meta reportedly switched its crawl source mid-run, from Common Crawl-based corpora to a newly built internal crawler, and “struggled to clean and deduplicate the new data stream” because “the processes hadn’t been stress-tested at scale” — an ingestion-pipeline swap mid-pretrain, on immature dedup infrastructure (Ch12-Ch13). SemiAnalysis separately claims Meta uses no YouTube data at all, unlike OpenAI and DeepSeek by its account — unquantified, and unsourced even within the piece. Fourth, scaling-strategy and coordination failure: “competing research directions and a lack of leadership to decide which direction was the most productive path forward. Certain model architecture choices did not have proper ablations but were thrown into the model. This led to poorly managed scaling ladders” — substantiating, at a process level, the reporting of executives blaming the Llama 4 team and the non-public LMArena-optimized submission controversy (WSJ via Axios/Computerworld/SiliconANGLE/Fortune, May 15, 2025). A bonus aside from the same piece, unverified elsewhere: OpenAI’s GPT-4.5 team discovered mid-run that its “uncontaminated” code-monorepo validation set had itself been partly copy-pasted from public data, so apparent generalization was partly memorization.

The aftermath follows from the causes. Maverick and Scout were distilled from Behemoth’s logits — “bypassing some of the flawed architectural choices of the larger model… [but] they aren’t best-in-class for their size” — which explains their underwhelming reception as downstream of Behemoth’s problems. The $14.3B Scale AI transaction is framed explicitly as fixing the data root cause, “especially the SEAL lab which specializes in evals.” Behemoth was never resumed; converging reporting has Meta Superintelligence Labs, under Alexandr Wang, “starting anew,” with MSL’s first ship being Muse Spark (April 8, 2026) and a single-source forecast of May 2027 for its next true flagship (aggregated by FutureSearch, citing Reuters). Zuckerberg confirmed on Dwarkesh (Aug 2025) that Behemoth exceeded 2T parameters and that “we’ve had to build a bunch of infrastructure just to be able to post-train it ourselves.” The compute posture stayed aggressive regardless: Prometheus (Ohio) expanded from ~1GW to >3GW within two years via a rapid “Tent” datacenter design — 27 datacenters across 6 campuses up to 80km apart, linked by a 22 Pb/s “AI-Backbone” network — and Hyperion (Louisiana) targets 5GW (SemiAnalysis, “The Future of Meta Superintelligence: A 1 Year Progress Update”). Meta also discloses the latency tiers driving its sync-vs-async choice: 1-10µs within a region, 500µs+ across ~100km+, the latter “requiring asynchronous training strategies” (SemiAnalysis).

xAI

xAI’s disclosure is mostly about speed of construction, and the numbers are worth keeping even where the framing needs deflating. Colossus 1 (Memphis) is documented at roughly 200,000 H100-class GPUs, its initial 100,000-GPU phase built in a widely reported ~122 days. Colossus 2 went from a bare 1M-sq-ft warehouse (acquired March 2025) to 200MW of cooling capacity within six months — 119 air-cooled chillers on site by Aug 22, 2025, enough for roughly 110,000 GB200 NVL72-equivalent GPUs; “xAI built in six months what took 15 months for Oracle, Crusoe and OpenAI” (SemiAnalysis, “xAI’s Colossus 2 — First Gigawatt Datacenter In The World,” Sep 16, 2025). Power runs partly through an offsite Southaven, Mississippi gas-turbine hub (a former Duke Energy site) under a temporary 12-month no-permit approval, with Tesla Megapacks bridging the two sites; Solaris Energy Infrastructure supplies rental turbines (xAI is 67% of its 1,700MW orderbook — 1,140MW — with a 50.1%/49.9% Solaris/xAI joint venture owning 900MW of that), guiding toward >1.1GW fully operating by Q2 2027. Note the title inflation: the piece’s “First Gigawatt Datacenter” is a target its own text places roughly 21 months out from publication, not a reached milestone, whatever secondary aggregation implied. The financing is unusual enough to flag: the ~555,000-GPU, ~$18-20B buildout runs as ~$7.5B equity (including up to $2B from Nvidia) plus $12-12.5B of GPU-collateralized debt via a Valor Equity Partners-run SPV that buys chips and leases them back to xAI — a structure The Information frames as compensating for a weaker balance sheet, with lenders reportedly pushing 3-year repayment caps (WSJ and The Information, July 2025 onward).

Networking runs on Nvidia Spectrum-X Ethernet, not InfiniBand, claiming 95% data throughput with near-zero packet loss while syncing ~200,000 GPUs (Nvidia Newsroom). The RL strategy is genuinely distinct: rather than concentrating on verifiable code/math domains like OpenAI and Anthropic, xAI bets on Ani (an anime-persona companion product) as “one of the most diverse RL environments,” treating human engagement itself as the reward signal — “It’s debatable whether this leads to intelligence gains any time soon,” SemiAnalysis notes, but it is a different midtraining/RL-data strategy and worth tracking as one. Grok compute claims deserve standing skepticism: Musk states Grok 3 used “10x” Grok 2’s compute and has floated a ~10T-parameter Grok 5, with xAI simultaneously training “seven models ranging 1T-10T params” — all self-reported, none independently verified, with Grok 5’s own slip from Q1→Q2→Q3 2026 evidencing the reliability of the channel.

Microsoft MAI

Microsoft’s in-house model effort is deliberately small relative to everything above. MAI-1-preview, an in-house mixture-of-experts model, was pre-trained and post-trained on ~15,000 Nvidia H100 GPUs — two orders of magnitude below Stargate/Colossus/Rainier scale, comparable to Mistral Large 3’s disclosed 3,000 H200s (Microsoft AI, “Two in-house models in support of our mission”, Aug/Sept 2025). No parameter, token, or MFU figures were disclosed. SemiAnalysis scores it at 38 on LMArena and puts Microsoft’s internal AI training compute spend scaling toward **$16B annualized** (“Microsoft & OpenAI, 2023-25: From All-In to the Big Pause,” Nov 2025). The custom-silicon record is a string of setbacks: Maia 100 (2023) never reached high-volume production; Maia 200’s tape-out slipped to late 2024 and is “assessed a failure internally,” with software development stopped entirely; Maia 300 (2nm) is the earliest credible next attempt, “late 2027” at best. The fallback is IP access (excluding consumer hardware) to OpenAI’s own Broadcom-designed “Titan” ASIC line (SemiAnalysis).

Where Microsoft is genuinely strong is the datacenter layer, covered in depth in Ch16 and Ch20. Fairwater Wisconsin and Atlanta operate as one logical training fabric via a dedicated “AI WAN” built on 120,000+ new fiber miles in a year, with Azure CTO Mark Russinovich on record: “The amount of infrastructure required now to train these models is not just one datacenter, not two, but multiples of that… Nobody’s really run into that problem yet because they haven’t gotten to the scale we’re at now” (Microsoft, “Infinite scale: The architecture behind the Azure AI superfactory”, Nov 12, 2025). Each Fairwater campus pairs a ~300MW GPU building (>150k GB200 GPUs) with a 48MW CPU/storage building — a ~1:6 CPU:GPU power ratio SemiAnalysis expects to worsen in the Rubin generation. Fairwater 2 Atlanta’s rail-only topology — splitting each compute tray’s 3,200G into 32 planes rather than per-GPU — reaches 524,288 GPUs on a 2-layer network using 24,576 switches (the same 21.3 GPU/switch ratio as Stargate’s 131,072-GPU network, at 4x the GPU count), at the cost that same-tray GPUs can no longer talk over the scale-out network and must route through NVLink, handled by Microsoft’s own MRC implementation (Ch20) (SemiAnalysis, “Microsoft’s AI Strategy Deconstructed,” Nov 2025). Satya Nadella, on Dwarkesh (Nov 2025), gave the buildout philosophy in one line: “you want to be scaling in time as opposed to scale once and then be stuck with it.”

The pattern across six labs

Table 2.1 collects the load-bearing disclosed numbers. Note how unevenly they cluster — hardware topology is disclosed far more freely than data, routing, or failure attribution.

Three patterns fall out of the assembled record. First, networking is the disclosure of choice: five of six labs have put real, quantified fabric detail on the record (MRC, Virgo, Fairwater, Llama 3’s Clos topology, Colossus’s Spectrum-X), because a fabric paper reads as an industry-collaboration story rather than a competitive-secrets leak. Second, failure attribution is almost entirely absent from official channels. Where it exists at all, it comes from a single paid-newsletter analyst team working from partial access — Meta’s Behemoth account is the only detailed technical postmortem of a frontier MoE pretrain failure in the record, and it is second-hand, single-sourced, and unquantified on its most load-bearing claim. Third, headline compute-scale numbers are increasingly unreliable as a measure of current frontier position: Epoch’s tracker has no FLOP estimate for Gemini 3 Pro, any Claude Opus 4.x model, Claude Sonnet 4/4.5, or GPT-5.1 as of its July 2026 pull, and its “Confident”-rated large-scale estimates belong not to any lab in this chapter but to DeepSeek, GLM, and Kimi (Ch3) — precisely because those labs publish real technical reports. The closed frontier is not only closed on data and algorithms; as of mid-2026 it is functionally closed on the compute-scale question too.

Implications for the 10T/200B run

Four disclosures here should shape planning assumptions downstream. MRC’s design choices — NIC-side routing state, refusal to stack two adaptive-routing layers, static SRv6 paths — are the most concrete production answer yet to the fabric-design question Ch18 and Ch20 have to resolve independently, since no framework ships this out of the box. The Gemini 1.0 report’s 85%→97% goodput improvement via in-memory state replication is the cleanest existence proof that a 10T/200B run’s fault-tolerance layer (Ch21) should treat in-memory redundancy as the default, not a fallback. Meta’s Behemoth postmortem is the most actionable cautionary tale in the whole record: do not switch routing paradigms mid-run, do not swap data-ingestion pipelines mid-run without stress-testing dedup at target scale, and build long-context/reasoning evals before locking in a local-attention-majority architecture, not after (Ch7, Ch12). And every lab here treats compute-scale disclosure as a marketing lever, not a transparency obligation — the ratio of hardware-topology disclosure to failure-mode disclosure runs roughly ten to one. A team at 10T/200B scale should expect to generate its own failure taxonomy from scratch: the public record from six labs that have done this amounts to one paper (Llama 3), one paid-newsletter postmortem (Behemoth), and a scattering of podcast asides. That is the argument for Ch4’s DeepSeek deep-dive and Ch22’s operations chapter — the open stack and the war-story genre are what’s left to learn from.
