Every parallelism formula in Ch16 and every optimizer schedule in Ch7 presupposes an input that this chapter treats as anything but given: 25-50T tokens of text, arriving on schedule, at a quality bar that survives Ch11’s filters. By mid-2026 that assumption is the shakiest load-bearing wall in the whole plan. The open web is enclosing in real time, the two labs with the cleanest legal book-acquisition playbooks got there by either destroying merchandise at industrial scale or settling for $1.5B, and the public estimate of the entire addressable human-text stock is measured in the same units as a single frontier training run’s appetite. This chapter reconciles what a 10T-parameter, 200B-active MoE actually needs against what demonstrably exists, and specifies what a new entrant would have to build to get it.

The own-crawler economy: who crawls, how much succeeds, and what that reveals

By 2026 every frontier lab operates a dedicated, named, robots.txt-addressable crawler fleet distinct from search indexing — GPTBot/OAI-SearchBot/ChatGPT-User (OpenAI), ClaudeBot/Claude-SearchBot/claude-web (Anthropic), Google-Extended/Googlebot/GoogleOther (Google), Meta-ExternalAgent (Meta), Amazonbot, Bytespider (ByteDance), PetalBot (Huawei) — directly measurable via Cloudflare’s Radar bot-traffic telemetry, which classifies verified bots by declared purpose (training / search / user-action) [OFFICIAL-BLOG, blog.cloudflare.com/from-googlebot-to-gptbot, 06/2025].

The volume numbers move fast and reshuffle hard. Cloudflare’s May 2025 snapshot put request-volume share at Googlebot 50%, Bingbot 8.7%, GPTBot 7.7%, ClaudeBot 5.4%, GoogleOther 4.3%, Amazonbot 4.2%, Bytespider 2.9% [OFFICIAL-BLOG, 06/2025]. Year-over-year growth from May 2024 to May 2025 was wildly uneven: GPTBot +305%, ChatGPT-User +2,825%, PerplexityBot +157,490% off a tiny base — while ClaudeBot fell -46%, Bytespider fell -85%, Amazonbot fell -35% [OFFICIAL-BLOG, 06/2025]. Among AI-only crawlers specifically, Bytespider’s share collapsed from 42% to 7.2% over the same window while GPTBot’s rose from 5% to 30% [OFFICIAL-BLOG, 06/2025]. That picture inverted again within a year: by June 2026, ClaudeBot surged +66% month-over-month to roughly 19.8-20% of AI-bot traffic, becoming the #2 AI crawler behind Googlebot and ahead of both Meta-ExternalAgent (~10.2%) and GPTBot (~9.4-9.6%); with Claude-SearchBot added, Anthropic’s combined footprint reached ~23%, nearly matching Googlebot’s 24.9-27.5% share of AI-bot-classified traffic in Q2 2026 [CREDIBLE-HEARSAY, Cloudflare Radar via secondary aggregation, 06-07/2026]. Bytespider’s brief 2025 resurgence reversed again, dropping back to ~7.3% by June 2026 [CREDIBLE-HEARSAY, 06-07/2026]. The month-to-month volatility — GPTBot and ClaudeBot trading the #1 AI-crawler spot repeatedly across April-June 2026 — is a genuinely underappreciated operational signal: it suggests labs are throttling and bursting crawl fleets on a cadence plausibly tied to training-run data-refresh cycles, rather than running steady-state background crawls, meaning a competitor’s crawl telemetry is itself a leading indicator of their training calendar.

Crawl-purpose breakdown confirms training dominance is durable, not a one-time spike: 52.3% of AI crawler requests in the 28 days to June 22, 2026 were for model training, versus only 2.6% live user fetches [OFFICIAL-BLOG, Cloudflare Radar, 06/2026], consistent with Cloudflare’s July 2025 finding that training crawling ran 79-82% of total AI crawl volume across Jan-Jul 2025, up from 72% in July 2024 [OFFICIAL-BLOG, blog.cloudflare.com/crawlers-click-ai-bots-training, 08/2025].

The single sharpest metric of extractive-versus-reciprocal crawling behavior is the crawl-to-refer ratio — pages fetched per human visitor eventually referred back — and Anthropic is the extreme outlier. Cloudflare measured Anthropic’s ratio at 286,930:1 in January 2025, falling to a still-enormous 38,065:1 by July 2025, against OpenAI’s much flatter 1,217:1 → 1,091:1 over the same window [OFFICIAL-BLOG, 08/2025]. This gap is a direct artifact of ClaudeBot being almost purely training-oriented with negligible consumer-search product driving referrals back, versus OpenAI’s ChatGPT search integration — and it is exactly the kind of asymmetry that plausibly shaped both site-level blocking decisions and the political pressure behind Cloudflare’s September 2026 default-block policy (below).

Vercel’s hosted-site network corroborates the picture independently (December 2024) [OFFICIAL-BLOG, vercel.com/blog/the-rise-of-the-ai-crawler]: Googlebot 4.5B fetches/month, GPTBot 569M, Claude 370M, AppleBot 314M, PerplexityBot 24.4M — GPTBot plus Claude combined at roughly 20% of Googlebot’s volume. AI crawlers post markedly higher 404 rates than Googlebot (ChatGPT 34.82%, Claude 34.16%, versus Googlebot’s 8.22%) [OFFICIAL-BLOG, 12/2024], a sign these fleets still hit stale or broken URL lists more than a mature scheduler should — an engineering-maturity gap the greenfield spec below should not replicate.

Common Crawl’s slow-motion enclosure

The foundational academic study of the commons closing is Longpre et al., “Consent in Crisis: The Rapid Decline of the AI Data Commons” (Data Provenance Initiative / MIT Media Lab) [PAPER, arXiv:2407.14933, 07/2024]. Auditing more than 14,000 domains across C4, RefinedWeb, and Dolma, the paper finds that over 28% of the most actively-maintained, high-quality C4 source domains are now fully robots.txt-disallowed, and roughly 45% of C4’s underlying data is now restricted by Terms of Service even where robots.txt stays silent — a split-brain problem, since TOS restrictions are legally live but technically invisible to a crawler that respects only robots.txt. The paper dates the inflection to fall 2023, coincident with OpenAI and Google publishing dedicated AI-training user-agent strings that made selective blocking legible for the first time.

By 2025-2026 the trend has compounded, per secondary aggregation of Cloudflare’s own robots.txt corpus scans [CREDIBLE-HEARSAY throughout this paragraph — SEO/content-marketing sites reprocessing Radar data, not primary researchers, treat point estimates with caution]: AI-blocking among “reputable” sites rose from roughly 23% (September 2023) to roughly 60% (May 2025), median blocking site now disallowing about 15.5 distinct AI user-agents; more than 2.5M sites had fully disallowed AI training and more than 1M Cloudflare customers had activated AI-crawler blocking as of August 2025. The Q1 2026 DISALLOW-rule frequency ranking is the sharpest single data point in this beat: GPTBot is the most-blocked bot (5.52% of all disallow rules), then CCBot (5.08%) — Common Crawl’s own bot is blocked nearly as often as OpenAI’s dedicated training crawler — then ClaudeBot (4.88%), Google-Extended (4.44%), Bytespider (4.23%) [CREDIBLE-HEARSAY, Q1/2026]. This directly undercuts the assumption that “just use Common Crawl” sidesteps the consent fight: CC is caught in the same dragnet, at nearly the same rate, as the labs it supposedly serves as a neutral alternative to.

Common Crawl remains large in raw terms — current monthly snapshots run roughly 2.0-2.2B pages and 350-400 TiB uncompressed (CC-MAIN-2026-25, June 2026: 2.10B pages; CC-MAIN-2026-21, May 2026: 2.16B pages), cumulative corpus in the hundreds-of-petabytes / 300B+-page range since 2008 [OFFICIAL/PRIMARY, commoncrawl.org, verified 07/2026] — but the quality-weighted usable fraction is shrinking, because the domains that matter most for quality filtering (news, forums, reference sites, precisely the ones with well-maintained robots.txt) are disproportionately represented among the newly-disallowed. Whether CCBot actually honors a disallow directed at AI-training bots specifically, or continues crawling under a “research use” theory, is not resolved by any source found in this pass; the best available inference is that CC respects Disallow: CCBot literally, meaning the 5.08% blocked-rule figure translates fairly directly into lost quality-domain coverage in every corpus (C4, RefinedWeb, FineWeb, DCLM) built on top of it — a medium-confidence inference, not a confirmed policy (see Ch11 for what those downstream corpora look like once built).

Cloudflare’s own structural interventions compound the picture. Pay Per Crawl (private beta July 1, 2025, GA August 2025) lets a domain owner set a flat per-request price and return HTTP 402 Payment Required to non-paying crawlers; named early adopters include Condé Nast, Time, AP, BuzzFeed, Reddit, Pinterest, and Stack Overflow, and by 2026 Cloudflare reports customers network-wide sending more than 1 billion HTTP 402 responses per day to AI crawlers — a lower bound on crawl attempts now actively monetization-gated [OFFICIAL-BLOG, blog.cloudflare.com/introducing-pay-per-crawl, 07/2025]. The Content Signals Policy (September 24, 2025) adds three machine-readable robots.txt signals — search, ai-input, ai-train — auto-injected into managed robots.txt, defaulting to search=yes, ai-train=no, live on more than 3.8 million domains as of mid-2026 [OFFICIAL-BLOG, blog.cloudflare.com/content-signals-policy, 09/2025]. Google has reportedly stated the signal has no binding effect on its crawling behavior [CREDIBLE-HEARSAY, reported via SEO trade press; not independently re-verified — flag accordingly], underscoring these remain voluntary courtesy signals: a crawler operator can ignore ai-train=no with no more consequence than ignoring classic robots.txt Disallow today — legal and reputational risk only, not a technical barrier.

The most consequential near-term change is dated: starting September 15, 2026, “mixed-use” crawlers blending search, agent, and training signals will be blocked by default from any ad-carrying page, for all new customers, new sites, and existing free-tier accounts — pure search crawlers are exempted, and Google-Extended remains the AI-training opt-out channel [OFFICIAL-BLOG + TechCrunch, 07/2026]. Cloudflare’s stated rationale: more than 50% of AI crawler traffic re-fetches unchanged pages, and per CEO Matthew Prince, “the majority of traffic on the internet is now non-human” [OFFICIAL-BLOG, 07/2026]. Cloudflare is separately piloting “Pay Per Use” — compensation tied to actual content citation rather than raw fetch count, with partners Ceramic.ai and You.com, broad availability undated as of July 2026 [OFFICIAL-BLOG, 2026]. The direction is unambiguous: crawl access is moving from an opt-out-by-default commons to an opt-in, metered, increasingly enforced marketplace, and a run planned for late 2026 or 2027 should assume the September 15 change is live for a meaningful fraction of the commercial web by the time crawling starts.

The licensing economy: real money, bounded scale

Licensing deals are a real revenue stream for publishers and a real legal-risk-mitigation play for labs, but they do not move the token-volume needle for a 25-50T-token budget. The T&F and Wiley deals drew significant author backlash over the absence of opt-out or author compensation mechanisms — a governance failure mode a new entrant negotiating similar deals should design around explicitly. OpenAI alone reportedly holds roughly 20 verified publisher partnerships covering 160+ outlets in 20+ languages, per an independent aggregator not independently re-verified [CREDIBLE-HEARSAY]. But aggregate industry-wide licensing spend is dwarfed by a different budget line: multiple sources converge on each frontier lab spending on the order of $1B/year on human-provided training and evaluation data via Scale AI, Surge AI, and Mercor [CREDIBLE-HEARSAY, 2026]. Licensing deals are principally about real-time grounding, brand safety, and pre-empting lawsuits — not about closing a multi-trillion-token pretraining gap; budget the line item as legal and product expense, not data-volume strategy.

Books: two legal templates, one clean and one expensive

The clearest bifurcation in 2025-2026 data law separates how you acquire copyrighted text from whether training on it is fair use, and Bartz v. Anthropic produced the reference case. In June 2025, Judge William Alsup (N.D. Cal.) ruled that training on legally acquired books is “quintessentially transformative” and protected fair use — but that downloading and retaining pirated copies, even of books never used for training, is independently infringing, separate from any training use [legal filing, widely reported, 06/2025].

Anthropic’s own acquisition pipeline — internally codenamed “Project Panama” and surfaced via litigation discovery, reported by the Washington Post (01/2026), The Bookseller, and Futurism [CREDIBLE-HEARSAY] — is the cleanest available template for a new entrant. The company purchased books in bulk, sometimes tens of thousands at a time, from resellers such as Better World Books and World of Books, then destructively scanned them: hydraulic cutting machines to debind, production-grade scanners, with the now-unbound, unsellable physical remains sent to recycling. Internal documents reportedly cite throughput of up to 8,000 books and 2 million pages per day, and an internal note stating “We don’t want it to be known that we are working on this” [CREDIBLE-HEARSAY, 01/2026]. The judge’s fair-use finding rested partly on three facts: the books were purchased rather than pirated, scanning was done in-house, and physical destruction prevented resale or redistribution of the source — a legally clean but operationally and reputationally expensive template, an industrial physical-logistics operation, not a script.

The other half of the case is cautionary. Anthropic separately settled for $1.5B (announced August 2025, court approval through September 2025) [widely reported] covering roughly 500,000 pirated works from LibGen and the lesser-known “PiLiMi” shadow library that the company held but had not necessarily used for training — the largest copyright settlement in US history, roughly $3,000 per class work. Anthropic settled this despite having won the fair-use argument for training use itself; the exposure came entirely from the piracy-acquisition claim, where statutory damages could reach $150,000 per work, implying theoretical liability above $70B once the class was certified. The lesson for a new entrant is explicit and three-part: purchase physical or licensed-digital copies rather than downloading shadow-library dumps; digitize in-house, destructively or DRM-respectingly; and do not retain pirated copies even “just in case” — retention itself, independent of training use, is the primary post-Bartz liability surface.

Meta’s parallel exposure is diverging toward risk, not settling toward safety. Kadrey v. Meta (June 2025, Judge Chhabria) found Meta’s training use fair use, but on a narrow, fact-specific record (plaintiffs failed to show market harm on that record), and Chhabria explicitly signaled future plaintiffs could win with better evidence. Discovery established Meta employees torrented roughly 82TB (Kadrey record) to as much as 267TB+ (per a newer suit’s allegations) of pirated books and journals via LibGen, Z-Library, and Anna’s Archive, including — per the newer suit — deliberately masking IP addresses while torrenting and stripping copyright management information, a distinct DMCA §1202 claim independent of fair use [court filings via press]. On May 5, 2026, five major publishers (Elsevier, Cengage, Hachette, Macmillan, McGraw Hill) plus author Scott Turow filed a fresh class action alleging Meta considered and abandoned licensing negotiations in April 2023 on direct instruction from Zuckerberg — evidence, if proven, that cuts hard against fair use’s good-faith factor [PAPER-ADJACENT/LEGAL, 05/2026]. As of July 2026 this is unresolved litigation, not settled law: a new entrant should not read Kadrey’s summary judgment as a green light for pirated-corpus acquisition, since the acquisition method itself now faces a second, more targeted legal challenge independent of the fair-use question.

A licensed, non-litigation alternative worth flagging alongside these two cases: Harvard’s Institutional Books 1.0, released by the Institutional Data Initiative in June 2025, digitizes 983,004 public-domain volumes from Harvard Library’s collections (part of the original Google Books scanning effort), spanning more than 250 languages and roughly 400M pages, tokenized to 242B tokens with OpenAI’s o200k_base scheme [PAPER, arXiv:2506.08300, 06/2025]. It is architecturally distinct from every web-crawl or PDF corpus in this chapter — pre-2006-digitized institutional scan OCR, refined, with historical-document register and out-of-copyright-era language distributions no Common-Crawl-derived corpus can supply. The catch for a commercial 10T-parameter run: despite the public-domain status of the underlying books, the initial release carries a non-commercial license, reflecting unresolved uncertainty around subsequent, non-Harvard re-digitizations of the same works — meaning it cannot simply be ingested today without a separate licensing conversation, unlike Common Pile v0.1’s openly-licensed sources (see Ch11 for that corpus’s specific, benchmark-identifiable quality gap on commonsense-reasoning tasks) [PAPER, 06/2025].

The gray zones: YouTube, audio, and the expert-data channel

A parallel, non-web data supply chain has scaled to a size that should be treated as available capacity, not hypothetical headroom. NYT reporting (widely cited, not independently re-verified in the underlying research pass — [CREDIBLE-HEARSAY]) states that OpenAI, having assessed by 2021 it was running short of high-quality text, built Whisper specifically to transcribe audio and video at scale, and used it to transcribe over 1 million hours of YouTube video as GPT-4-era training data, with internal awareness this arguably violated YouTube’s terms of service and a decision to proceed on a fair-use theory anyway [CREDIBLE-HEARSAY, ~2024 reporting]. This is distinct from Whisper’s own published training corpus — the original model used 680,000 hours of multilingual/multitask supervised audio [OFFICIAL-BLOG, openai.com/index/whisper, 09/2022], and large-v3 scaled to 1M hours weakly-labeled plus 4M hours Whisper-v2-pseudo-labeled [OFFICIAL-BLOG, 11/2023] — two separate million-hour-scale audio datasets easy to conflate. The operational lesson: audio/video-derived text is a proven, board-approved-scale channel, but carries the same ToS/consent exposure as web scraping — available capacity with real legal tail-risk, not free lunch (Ch9 covers the audio/video-native pretraining case where this becomes a first-class modality rather than a text-extraction source).

By 2026 the human-data vendor market has bifurcated from cheap crowd labeling toward credentialed specialist networks explicitly marketed for pretraining-scale use, not just RLHF: Mercor (roughly 300,000 vetted experts, “off-the-shelf, license-ready” pretraining datasets), Scale AI’s Expert Match, Surge AI’s premium expert pipelines [CREDIBLE-HEARSAY, 2025-2026]. This is now materially a data-generation function: expert-written worked solutions, textbook-style explanations, and domain Q&A at this scale function as a synthetic-adjacent, high-density pretraining source distinct from both web crawl and licensed-publisher archives. The aggregate spend figure — each frontier lab reportedly on the order of $1B/year — is the same number cited for licensing context above, and it is worth stating plainly: this vendor channel, not publisher licensing, is where the serious money in acquired (as opposed to crawled) data actually goes.

The stock math: reconciling Epoch AI against a 25-50T budget

Epoch AI’s “Will We Run Out of Data? Limits of LLM Scaling Based on Human-Generated Data” (originally November 2022, substantially revised June 2024) remains the load-bearing public estimate of the addressable stock [PAPER, arXiv:2211.04325, 06/2024]. The revised numbers: an effective stock of quality- and repetition-adjusted human-generated public text of roughly 300 trillion tokens, 90% confidence interval 100T-1000T, and an 80% confidence interval for full exhaustion between 2026 and 2032. The 2024 revision roughly quintupled the 2022 estimate (which had projected exhaustion by 2024), for two operationally relevant reasons: careful filtering makes raw web data outperform curated corpora more than expected, and multi-epoch repetition — several epochs “without significant degradation” — expands the effective stock 2-5x over the naive single-pass count. At Chinchilla-style compute-optimal training the stock supports models up to roughly 5×10^28 FLOP, a level Epoch projected around 2028, though this FLOP-year mapping predates the overtraining trends that have since dominated frontier practice and should be treated as dated. Under aggressive overtraining — 100x past Chinchilla-optimal token:parameter ratio — the stock could be exhausted as early as 2025; Epoch’s own model flags that overtraining, exactly the strategy frontier labs have pursued since Llama 3 on dense/effective-parameter terms, burns through the stock fastest.

Reconciling this against the reader’s stated 25-50T-token target requires being explicit about what “tokens” means. Two hundred billion active parameters at even a moderate 20-40x overtraining ratio — well below extreme small-model ratios like Llama 3 8B’s roughly 1875x tokens/param, but plausible for a frontier-scale active-parameter budget — implies a token target in the 4-8T range on active-parameter Chinchilla logic alone (Ch6 covers the scaling-law machinery behind this arithmetic). The 25-50T figure is consistent with aggressive overtraining on active parameters, multi-epoch repetition of a smaller high-quality core, and/or heavy synthetic augmentation — all three of which the Epoch framework treats as stock-multipliers, not stock-consumers. At 25-50T unique-equivalent tokens, a 10T-parameter MoE would consume roughly 8-17% of Epoch’s central 300T-token estimate in a single run — plausible only if multi-epoch counting is used generously (true unique tokens closer to 10-20T, repeated 2-3x) and/or synthetic/rephrased data supplies a meaningful fraction of the nominal count. The instruction that follows: treat “25-50T tokens” as requiring an explicit synthetic/multi-epoch accounting from day one, not as 25-50T tokens of freshly-crawled unique human text — that volume of unique, quality-filtered fresh crawl very likely does not exist.

The corpus-scale evidence assembled elsewhere in this research base supports that conclusion directly. No single open web-crawl-derived corpus tops out above the low-teens of trillions of tokens even at maximal scale — Essential-Web v1.0 reaches 24T tokens across 23.6B documents from Common Crawl [PAPER, arXiv:2506.14111, 06/2025], and DCLM’s own raw pool of 240T tokens filters down to 3.8T retained (2.6T used for the headline run) [PAPER, arXiv:2406.11794, 06/2024] — a filtering ratio of roughly 1-1.6% retained, the single most concrete illustration of how far raw crawl volume overstates usable stock (Ch11 covers the filtering-pipeline mechanics behind that ratio). Nemotron-CC’s full pipeline, including 2T tokens of synthetic augmentation, reaches 6.3T [OFFICIAL-BLOG+PAPER, arXiv:2412.02595, 12/2024], and its v2 successor reaches 6,585.8B tokens with 879.1B + 1,629.1B tokens of additional synthetic rephrase [OFFICIAL/HF, 2025]. Frontier runs are already consuming a meaningful fraction of what’s publicly documented to exist: Qwen3 at 36T tokens across three curriculum stages [PAPER, arXiv:2505.09388, 05/2025], Kimi K2 at 15.5T [PAPER, arXiv:2507.20534, 07/2025], DeepSeek-V3 at 14.8T [PAPER, arXiv:2412.19437, 12/2024]. Multi-epoch repetition is consequently not a fallback for a 25-50T-token run but a structural necessity: the load-bearing scaling law remains Muennighoff et al.’s finding that up to roughly 4 epochs causes negligible degradation, meaningful gains persist to roughly 16 epochs, and value is largely exhausted by roughly 40 epochs [PAPER, “Scaling Data-Constrained Language Models,” 2023, cited widely through 2025-26]. A 2026 refinement extends the domain-mixture scaling-law framework to model per-domain repetition as a first-class variable once any single mixture component becomes data-limited, rather than a single global scalar [PAPER, arXiv:2605.12715]. Kimi K2’s own ablation is the sharpest single data point on the alternative to brute-force repetition: 10x knowledge-domain rephrasing lifts SimpleQA from 23.8% to 28.9%, beating naive 10-epoch repetition on the same underlying text [PAPER, arXiv:2507.20534, 07/2025] — synthetic rephrasing is now a demonstrated superior alternative to repetition for knowledge-dense subsets, not merely a hedge against running out of unique tokens.

DatologyAI’s BeyondWeb result quantifies the same substitution from the compute-efficiency side: a rephrased-web recipe beats Cosmopedia by up to 5.1pp and Nemotron-Synth by 2.6pp average accuracy across 14 benchmarks, with a 3B model on the recipe beating an 8B baseline at equal token budget, and 7.7x training speedup against open web data [OFFICIAL-BLOG, datologyai.com/blog/beyondweb, 08/2025]. As a data-efficiency multiplier rather than a parameter-count multiplier: for a fixed token budget, a synthetic-rephrasing pipeline can substitute for roughly 2.5x more parameters — a lever a 10T/200B design should weight explicitly against simply adding more raw, unrephrased crawl tokens, bearing directly on the ladder-recipe cost accounting in Ch6.

Two additional stock-side data points sit genuinely outside standard web-crawl accounting. FinePDFs (HuggingFace, September 2025) demonstrates PDFs as a systematically denser source than HTML web text — 475M documents, 1,733 languages, 3T tokens total (1.1T English alone), median document length roughly 5.3k characters, about 2x typical web corpora, with a much heavier tail (95th percentile ~68k characters versus 11-13k for HTML-derived corpora) [PAPER/OFFICIAL-BLOG, huggingfacefw-finepdfsblog.hf.space, 09/2025]. A 1.67B-parameter ablation shows a FinePDFs/FineWeb-Edu/DCLM blend at just a 0.25 mixing ratio beating Nemotron-CC-v2, the prior public-SOTA baseline [OFFICIAL-BLOG, 09/2025]. CCI4.0 (BAAI, June 2025) illustrates a different kind of stock-stretching: a 35T-token bilingual corpus that reuses Nemotron-CC’s already-curated 22.5TB English subset wholesale and adds a from-scratch 5.2TB Chinese pipeline, rather than re-deriving an independent English pipeline [PAPER, arXiv:2506.07463, 06/2025] — a reminder that the effective global stock is not additive across labs’ independent crawls the way a naive sum would suggest, since major curated English pools are increasingly reused rather than re-crawled.

Building a greenfield crawler in 2026: the technical spec

Extraction quality is now a measured, first-order lever on effective stock, not commoditized the way most practitioners assume. DCLM’s own ablation found resiliparse and trafilatura both beat raw WET-file extraction by at least 2.5 CORE points, similar to each other in downstream quality but resiliparse roughly 8x faster — why DCLM standardized on it for cost, not quality, reasons [PAPER, arXiv:2406.11794, NeurIPS 2024 D&B track, 06/2024]. More surprising: a February 2026 Apple/Stanford/UW paper found that at a fixed post-curation filtering pipeline, resiliparse, trafilatura, and jusText each keep substantially different pages — only 39% of surviving pages are kept by more than one extractor, 61% survive via exactly one [PAPER, arXiv:2602.19548, 02/2026]. At 7B-model/1x-epoch scale from the identical underlying WARC pool: resiliparse yields 193B tokens (Core_V2 42.6, MMLU 39.5), trafilatura 135B tokens (41.9, 37.7), jusText 124B tokens (41.2, 34.5) — a greater than 50% token-yield spread between extractors on identical input [PAPER, 02/2026]. Taking the union of multiple extractors’ output increases DCLM-Baseline token yield by up to 71%, falling to roughly 58% after cross-extractor deduplication, at equal benchmark performance [PAPER, 02/2026] — running two or three extractors in parallel and deduping the union is a close-to-free yield increase essentially no major open pipeline appears to be doing as of publication. A November 2025 Shanghai AI Lab/OpenDataLab paper (AICC) goes further, replacing heuristic extraction with a 0.6B-parameter sequence-labeling model (MinerU-HTML) that lifts ROUGE-F1 content-match from trafilatura’s 63.6% to 81.8%, with far better structured-content preservation (code blocks 90.9%, formulas 94.0%, categories heuristic extractors are known to mangle) [PAPER, arXiv:2511.16397, 11/2025], producing a 7.3T-token corpus from just two CC snapshots and a measured +1.08pp average benchmark gain over an identically-processed trafilatura baseline [PAPER, 11/2025]. Whether any frontier lab already runs a proprietary model-based extractor is undisclosed by all of them, but given the magnitude of these gaps it would be surprising if none has — a genuine open question for anyone benchmarking against published open-corpus numbers.

JS-rendering economics are the second design axis, and they are memory-bound rather than compute-bound: the reported rule of thumb is roughly 250MB RAM per headless-Chrome browser context, so 1,000 concurrent rendered pages costs roughly 250GB RAM, and practitioners converge on Chromium’s multi-context-per-process model plus horizontal scale-out across many small nodes as both faster and cheaper than fewer large machines [CREDIBLE-HEARSAY, practitioner-blog consensus, 06/2026]. Webrecorder’s Browsertrix (open-source, Puppeteer/CDP-driven, Docker-packaged, billing by browser-window-time) is the closest thing to a standard reference implementation for high-fidelity JS-rendering crawl-to-WARC pipelines outside the major labs [CODE/OFFICIAL, github.com/webrecorder/browsertrix-crawler, 07/2026]. This matters structurally because Common Crawl itself does not render JS at all — CCBot is Nutch-based (Hadoop/MapReduce scheduling, WARC output, adaptive rate-limiting) and fetches raw HTTP responses only [OFFICIAL, commoncrawl.org engineering documentation] — systematically under-representing JS-heavy modern sites (SPAs, paywalled news, app-shell architectures) relative to a lab running its own headless-render fleet, a gap arguably as important a reason frontier labs run proprietary crawlers as the robots.txt-blocking gap above.

Politeness and scheduling round out the spec. Common Crawl’s architecture (Hadoop/Bigtop on spot EC2, historically roughly 20 nodes at 2 cores/32GB RAM for the fetch stage) makes strict per-host fetch-delay queuing, not compute, the actual throughput bottleneck [OFFICIAL, commoncrawl.org engineering writeups]. A greenfield 2026 crawler needs the same primitives, plus one CC lacks: per-host adaptive rate limiting respecting Retry-After/429 and empirically inferring safe QPS; a separately-scheduled freshness/recrawl policy — Cloudflare’s >50% unchanged-page-refetch finding indicts the industry’s recrawl-scheduling maturity, implying a change-detection/etag-aware scheduler is not yet standard even at major labs [OFFICIAL-BLOG, 07/2026]; crawl-time dedup via URL-canonicalization plus content-hash/SimHash ahead of the offline pass in Ch11; and a content-signals parser for Cloudflare’s search/ai-input/ai-train extension alongside classic robots.txt, unenforced as it remains. Brave Search’s independently-built index and Mojeek’s smaller but fully independent crawler are useful existence-proofs that non-Google-scale independent web indexes remain viable in 2026, though neither publishes a page-count metric comparable to Common Crawl’s.

Implications for the 10T/200B run

The data-supply chain analysis compresses to a small number of hard planning constraints:

  • Do not budget on Common Crawl staying a free, comprehensive commons. CCBot’s 5.08% DISALLOW-rule share (nearly matching GPTBot’s 5.52%) and the September 15, 2026 Cloudflare default-block change mean the effective quality-adjusted CC yield will keep shrinking through the run’s crawl-planning window; treat any CC-derived corpus size as a ceiling that degrades monthly, not a fixed asset.
  • Budget an explicit multi-epoch and synthetic-augmentation accounting for the token target, not a unique-token count. A 25-50T nominal token budget against Epoch’s ~300T central stock estimate is only defensible if the true unique-token core is closer to 10-20T, repeated 2-3x and/or extended via rephrasing on the Kimi K2/BeyondWeb model — plan the data pipeline around this from the start rather than discovering the shortfall mid-run.
  • Treat book acquisition as a build-vs-license decision with a known cost structure on both sides. The Anthropic Project Panama template (purchase, in-house destructive digitize, no retention of pirated material) is legally clean but requires standing up an industrial physical-logistics operation; Harvard’s Institutional Books 1.0 offers 242B tokens of comparable historical-register text but needs a separate commercial-licensing conversation before use.
  • Do not treat licensing deals as a data-volume lever. Aggregate industry licensing spend is a rounding error against the ~$1B/year each frontier lab separately spends on human-provided data via Scale/Surge/Mercor; size the licensing budget for legal risk mitigation and product integration, not token count.
  • Run 2-3 text extractors in parallel and dedup the union. The measured 50-71% token-yield spread between resiliparse, trafilatura, and jusText on identical WARC input is close to free to capture and is not standard practice even at major labs — one of the few remaining near-costless yield increases available before touching filtering strategy proper (Ch11).
  • Design the crawler’s freshness scheduler before its throughput. Cloudflare’s >50% unchanged-page-refetch finding indicates raw crawl QPS is not the binding constraint most fleets face; a change-detection/etag-aware recrawl policy is higher-leverage than additional crawl capacity.
  • Treat YouTube/audio transcription as available but legally live capacity, appropriate for the multimodal-native design discussed in Ch9, and size any reliance on it against the same ToS/consent exposure pattern that is currently reshaping web crawling itself.
