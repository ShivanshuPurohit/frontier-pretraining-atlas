# Chapter 16 — The Parallelism Math for 10T/200B on 100k GB200

Every parallelism decision in this book eventually reduces to the same inequality: is the per-GPU compute time longer or shorter than the communication time the chosen sharding scheme imposes. At 100,000 GPUs training a 10T-total/200B-active MoE, that inequality gets evaluated dozens of times across five interacting axes — data, expert, tensor, pipeline, and (for long-context stages) sequence parallelism — and getting any one of them wrong by a small constant factor is the difference between 40% MFU and 15%. This chapter derives the crossovers explicitly, corrects a subtle GB200-vs-HGX-B200 arithmetic error that has propagated through the theory literature this book draws on, and ends in three fully-worked candidate layouts for the strawman model specified in Ch5.

## The roofline algebra, restated for GPUs

Every formula in this chapter reduces to `T_math = FLOPs/C` versus `T_comms = Bytes/W`, with the operation compute-bound once arithmetic intensity exceeds the hardware's critical ratio `C/W`. This is the JAX Scaling Book's organizing move, extended from TPU tori to GPU fat-trees in its GPU chapter [How to Scale Your Model, GPU chapter](https://jax-ml.github.io/scaling-book/gpus/) [OFFICIAL-BLOG, 2025-08]. The four sharding primitives on a GPU fat-tree: AllGather/ReduceScatter cost `T=V/W` regardless of shard count; AllReduce is exactly `2V/W` (a ReduceScatter plus an AllGather); intra-node AllToAll costs `T≈B/(N·W)` for an N-GPU domain — note this is `1/N`, not the TPU torus's `1/4`, because full-crossbar NVSwitch connectivity beats a torus's partial connectivity, and conflating the two is a documented source of error when porting TPU intuition to GPU clusters [How to Scale Your Model, GPU chapter](https://jax-ml.github.io/scaling-book/gpus/) [OFFICIAL-BLOG, 2025-08]. Cross-node AllToAll for M nodes degrades to `T≈B/(M·W_node)`, more than 4x worse than the single-node case because it cannot exploit full bisection bandwidth the way AllReduce can.

For a single dense matmul, this produces the familiar single-chip compute-bound floor: batch size must exceed roughly `C/W_hbm` tokens, which works out to B>295 on H100 and B>281 on B200 in bf16 — essentially unchanged across three hardware generations because FLOPs and HBM bandwidth have scaled together [How to Scale Your Model, GPU chapter](https://jax-ml.github.io/scaling-book/gpus/) [OFFICIAL-BLOG, 2025-08]. The dense-model DP/FSDP/TP crossovers this book's parallelism math builds on: DP and FSDP are compute-bound once `B/X > C/W_ici`; TP is compute-bound once `Y < F·W_ici/C` (Y=TP degree, F=FFN width) — Stas Bekman's ml-engineering independently arrives at the same threshold as flat field wisdom ("TP ≤ GPUs per node," typically 8-16-way), which is this crossover pre-evaluated for typical F and frozen as a rule of thumb [ml-engineering, model-parallelism](https://github.com/stas00/ml-engineering) [CODE, 2026-07]. Both of these are dense-model results (Ch5's architecture chapter and this book's own MoE derivations below extend them); the Scaling Book's main training-parallelism chapter explicitly excludes expert parallelism from its scope, pushing all MoE-aware formulas into the GPU chapter appendix — which is where the numbers this chapter actually needs live.

**GB200 ground truth used throughout this chapter** (full derivation in Ch14): the authoritative per-GPU dense FP8 Tensor Core peak for GB200 NVL72 is **5.0 PFLOPS**, not the 4.5 PFLOPS the Scaling Book's summary table implies — that table silently reports the air-cooled HGX B200 SKU's number under an undifferentiated "B200" label, an error confirmed against NVIDIA's own side-by-side datasheet (doc #3384703) and cross-validated against two independent NVIDIA product pages and a third-party technical wiki [NVIDIA Blackwell datasheet](https://openzeka.com) [OFFICIAL-DOCS, verified 2026-07-13]. Dense BF16 is 2.5 PFLOPS/GPU (not 2.25); dense FP4 is 10.0 PFLOPS/GPU (not 9.0) — a uniform +11.1% correction across every tensor-core precision, tracking GB200's 1,200W liquid-cooled envelope against HGX B200's 1,000W air-cooled part on the identical Blackwell die. The mechanism isn't a single clean clock multiplier, though: back-calculating from published FLOPs/core-count gives only a ~6.7% vector-ALU clock delta (≈2.1GHz GB200 vs ≈1.98GHz HGX B200) — well short of the +11.1% tensor-core gap — implying either a more aggressive sustained-clock floor specifically under saturated Tensor-Core load at 1,200W, independently-rounded marketing figures on the two SKUs, or (most likely) both; NVIDIA publishes no official clock speed for either part. NVLink5 gives 900 GB/s full-duplex per GPU, unchanged in per-GPU terms whether the domain is 8 GPUs (HGX B200) or 72 GPUs (GB200 NVL72) — the domain-size increase from 8→72 is the whole story, not a per-link speed change. Scale-out bandwidth per GPU is the wildcard: ~50 GB/s/GPU on ConnectX-7/400Gbps fabrics (the implicit baseline in most GPU-chapter formulas) versus ~100 GB/s/GPU on ConnectX-8/800Gbps XDR fabrics deployed on GB200 racks from mid-2025 onward — a 2x swing that halves or doubles every cross-domain threshold below, and the more likely default for a 2026-commissioned 100k-GPU build [How to Scale Your Model + industry reporting] [CREDIBLE-HEARSAY, 2025]. Every calculation below uses the corrected 5.0e15 FLOPs/s/GPU dense FP8 figure.

## MoE-specific formulas: the E/k inflation and the ragged AllToAll

The GPU chapter derives two closed-form results absent from the dense-model chapters. First, **MoE data-parallel critical batch inflates by exactly the sparsity ratio E/k**: `B/X > (E/k)·C/W`, because FSDP/ZeRO-3-style sharding must AllGather all E experts' weights each step to support routing that only uses k of them — the worked example given is "the new OpenAI OSS model" (E=128, k=4, E/k=32), which needs 79,200 tokens/GPU cross-node, described in the source text as "a kind of ridiculously high number" [How to Scale Your Model, GPU chapter](https://jax-ml.github.io/scaling-book/gpus/) [OFFICIAL-BLOG, 2025-08]. Second, the **ragged (top-k) AllToAll cost** for MoE dispatch/combine is `T≈min(k/N,1)·B/(N·W)` — the ordinary AllToAll formula scaled down by the fraction of the domain each token actually touches.

Third, and the most consequential derivation for this chapter: the **expert-parallelism compute-bound viability threshold** generalizes the H100-specific `F > 8·C/W_node` (8 = H100 node size) to `F_min = C/W_scaleout` for a domain of any size n, because `W_node_total = n·W_per_gpu` and the n cancels algebraically. **This threshold is domain-size invariant** — it depends only on per-GPU scale-out bandwidth, not on how many GPUs sit inside the cheap NVLink domain. Using the corrected GB200 numbers: `F_min = 5.0e15/50e9 = 100,000` on ConnectX-7 fabrics, or `5.0e15/100e9 = 50,000` on ConnectX-8. Neither source states this invariance explicitly; it falls directly out of generalizing the book's own H100-specific formula, and it is easy to state backwards — GB200's real MoE advantage is entirely about how much EP is "free" inside the 72-GPU domain before F_min becomes relevant, not about F_min itself moving.

**Applying this to the Ch5 strawman** (512 routed experts, top-8, one shared expert, d_model≈8192, d_expert≈1400): the architecturally correct sparsity ratio for the weight-AllGather formula is `E/k = 512/8 = 64` — not the naive total/active parameter ratio of 10T/200B=50 that a first-pass estimate might reach for. The two numbers differ because total/active blends in the always-active components (attention, the one shared expert, embedding/output head, and the strawman's 1-3 dense layers) that dilute the pure routed-expert sparsity down from 64 toward 50 — a useful internal consistency check, since diluting *toward* 50 rather than away from it is exactly the direction the arithmetic should go. This chapter uses 64 throughout, since it is the ratio that actually governs weight-movement cost; readers cross-referencing the coarser 50-based figures elsewhere in this book should apply a ~1.28x scaling factor.

Recomputing the intra-domain and cross-domain critical batches at E/k=64 and the corrected 5.0e15 FLOPs/s/GPU:

| Regime | Bandwidth | Critical batch |
|---|---|---|
| Intra-domain (72-GPU NVL72, NVLink5) | 900 GB/s/GPU | 64 × 5.0e15/900e9 = **355,556 tokens/GPU** |
| Cross-domain, ConnectX-7 | 50 GB/s/GPU | 64 × 100,000 = **6,400,000 tokens/GPU** |
| Cross-domain, ConnectX-8 | 100 GB/s/GPU | 64 × 50,000 = **3,200,000 tokens/GPU** |

And the EP viability threshold on expert width is unchanged by the sparsity ratio (F_min is a per-expert-width condition, not a routing-fanout condition): 100,000 (CX-7) or 50,000 (CX-8), as derived above. The strawman's d_expert≈1400 sits **~36x below the CX-8 threshold (and ~71x below the stricter CX-7 threshold)** — architecturally indistinguishable from DeepSeek V3's F=2048, which sits **~24x below CX-8 (~49x below CX-7)** once corrected for the true GB200 peak [scaling-book §10 + this reconciliation]. This is not a strawman-specific problem; the Ling paper's granularity optimum (G≈12, Ant's definition G=2·d_model/d_expert, stable across FLOPs budgets 1e18-1e20 and confirmed across 300+ trained models up to 28B params [Towards Greater Leverage, arXiv:2507.17702](https://arxiv.org/abs/2507.17702) [PAPER, 2025-07]) and DeepSeek's own precedent both push expert width in exactly the direction that makes flat cross-domain EP comms-bound — narrow, numerous experts are good for the Ch5/Ch11 scaling-law story and bad for this chapter's network story, and the two literatures don't currently talk to each other on this point. The practical consequence, worked through below, is that node/domain-limited routing (DeepSeek's ≤4-node cap, generalized here to a ≤1-domain cap) remains structurally necessary at 10T scale, not a 2024-era workaround that a bigger cluster outgrows.

## Why pure DP/FSDP is mathematically dead — and why ZeRO-1 escapes the same trap

At 100,000 GPUs with a plausible global batch in the 10-60M token range (bracketed by LLaMA-3's 16M and DeepSeek-V3's 63M token batches), per-GPU batch is roughly 100-600 tokens. Compare against the intra-domain FSDP threshold of 355,556 tokens/GPU derived above: **per-GPU batch is 3-4 orders of magnitude below even the cheapest (single-domain, NVLink-speed) FSDP threshold**, before cross-domain bandwidth is even considered. Pure DP or ZeRO-3-style FSDP, applied naively to the full 10T-parameter model, is not a suboptimal choice at this scale — it is comms-bound by several orders of magnitude at any batch size a 100k-GPU cluster could plausibly run. EP+PP+TP composition is therefore not an optimization; it is the only mathematically viable regime, exactly mirroring what DeepSeek V3 already demonstrates (64-way EP, 16-way PP, 2-way DP) at roughly 1/50th the GPU count and comparable total sparsity [DeepSeek-V3 Technical Report](https://arxiv.org/abs/2412.19437) [PAPER, 2024-12].

But this raises an apparent puzzle: DeepSeek V3's DP axis is not zero — it runs 2-way DP on top of EP64×PP16, and the reader's cluster will need a DP axis running into the hundreds of ways just to reach 100,000 GPUs. Why doesn't *that* DP axis hit the same E/k wall? The resolution, synthesizing three sources that don't individually state it: **the E/k inflation is specifically a weight-AllGather-style (FSDP/ZeRO-3) artifact.** It exists because FSDP must materialize all E experts' weights on demand each forward pass to support k-sparse routing across an unpredictable token stream. DeepSeek's actual DP choice is **ZeRO-1** — optimizer-state sharding only, with the full (already EP- and PP-sharded) local weight shard resident on every DP replica at all times, synced via periodic gradient AllReduce rather than per-step weight AllGather [DeepSeek-V3 Technical Report §3.2](https://arxiv.org/abs/2412.19437) [PAPER, 2024-12]. Because EP has already physically distributed the 512 experts across the EP group before DP replication happens, a GPU's local shard already excludes the E−k experts it doesn't hold — there is no "gather all E, use k" waste left for the DP axis to pay for. This matches Stas Bekman's independent, hard-won field rule that ZeRO combined with PP should almost always be ZeRO stage 1 only, since PP's small microbatches make ZeRO-2/3's extra reduce-scatter proportionally expensive relative to its marginal memory savings [ml-engineering, model-parallelism](https://github.com/stas00/ml-engineering) [CODE, 2026-07] — arrived at from BLOOM/IDEFICS-scale empirical tuning rather than from the Scaling Book's algebra, but landing on the identical conclusion.

The ZeRO-1 DP axis is therefore governed by the ordinary, non-inflated compute-bound condition `B/X > C/W`, using whichever fabric the DP replicas actually communicate over. For a layout where DP replicas span NVL72 domain boundaries (the typical case once EP+PP already consume most of a domain), that's the cross-domain figure: `5.0e15/100e9 = 50,000 tokens/GPU` on ConnectX-8 — three orders of magnitude more forgiving than the FSDP threshold, and comfortably satisfiable by any reasonable microbatch × gradient-accumulation-steps product. This is the load-bearing distinction the rest of this chapter's layouts depend on: **weight/optimizer memory sharding is EP+PP's job; DP is a replication factor layered on top via ZeRO-1, sized only to satisfy a modest gradient-sync compute-bound floor, not the punishing E/k-inflated one.**

## The EP-inside-NVL72 opportunity, and its hard limit

GB200 NVL72's structural contribution to MoE training is domain size, not per-link bandwidth: NVLink5 gives the same 900 GB/s/GPU whether the domain has 8 GPUs (HGX B200) or 72 (NVL72) — the win is putting 9x more GPUs behind the cheap all-to-all domain before falling back to the slower scale-out fabric [How to Scale Your Model, GPU chapter](https://jax-ml.github.io/scaling-book/gpus/) [OFFICIAL-BLOG, 2025-08]. This is confirmed empirically, not just algebraically, by Megatron-Core's own measured dispatch/combine latency table (hidden=7168, seqlen=4096, 256 experts) [Scalable Training of MoE Models with Megatron Core, arXiv:2603.07685](https://arxiv.org/abs/2603.07685) [PAPER, 2026-03]:

| EP size | GB200 HybridEP dispatch (µs) | H100 all-to-all dispatch (µs) |
|---|---|---|
| 8 | 391 | 1,265 |
| 16 | 578 | 5,774 |
| 32 | 612 | 8,059 |
| 64 | 675 | 9,164 |

GB200's HybridEP kernel stays essentially flat (391→675µs) as EP scales 8→64 because the entire domain fits inside NVLink; H100's plain all-to-all blows up 7-8x as EP crosses node boundaries onto InfiniBand. Consistent with this, the same paper reports EP all-to-all consuming only ~20% of iteration time on GB200 (intra-domain EP64) versus 40-60% on H100 (cross-node EP64), even after dispatcher optimization the overhead is still 30-40% of iteration time for DeepSeek-V3-scale EP64 [arXiv:2603.07685 §4.2, §9.2.2] [PAPER, 2026-03] — this residual is not zero even in the favorable GB200 regime, a fact worth carrying into the predicted-MFU numbers below.

The limit is the one derived above: F_min doesn't move. NVL72's 72-GPU domain changes how much EP is free (up to ~64-72-way, versus H100's 8-way), not whether an expert of a given width is comms-bound. For a practical layout, this argues for **EP=64** rather than the full EP=72: it divides the strawman's 512 experts evenly (8 experts/GPU), and it matches a real-world operational pattern — GB200 NVL72 racks as deployed in 2025 commonly run 64-of-72 active GPUs with the remaining 8 held as hot spares for backplane/GPU failures, a reliability practice documented independently of any MoE-specific design choice [SemiAnalysis, H100 vs GB200 NVL72 Training Benchmarks](https://semianalysis.com) [SA-PAID, 2025-08]. Sizing EP to leave spare headroom inside the domain is a free design choice that costs nothing in the comms model and buys operational slack Ch19 covers in more depth.

## TP domain sizing: Y < F·W/C, and why "TP ≤ 72" is a trap

Stas Bekman's flat field rule — "TP size ≤ GPUs per node" — is, evaluated against the Scaling Book's derived `Y < F·W_ici/C` threshold at typical F, exactly the source of the commonly-quoted "8-16-way" TP ceiling [ml-engineering, model-parallelism](https://github.com/stas00/ml-engineering) [CODE, 2026-07]. On an 8-GPU H100 node this rule and the derived threshold happen to coincide, inviting the (wrong) generalization that GB200 NVL72's 72-GPU domain raises the ceiling to TP=72. It does not: the underlying `Y < F·W/C` ratio is unaffected by domain size, since both F and the C/W ratio are architecture and hardware properties independent of how many GPUs the NVLink fabric spans. Recomputing directly with the corrected GB200 numbers, for a dense FFN of width F=4×d_model=32,768 (relevant only to the strawman's 1-3 non-MoE layers per Ch5): `Y < F/(C/W) = 32,768/(5.0e15/900e9) = 32,768/5,556 ≈ 5.9`. TP should stay below roughly 6-way even inside the cheap 72-GPU domain — a tighter ceiling than the generic "8-16-way" folk number, driven by this model's particular d_model choice.

For the strawman's routed-expert layers, this question is largely moot: expert-TP (splitting an individual expert's already-narrow d_expert≈1400 matrix further across GPUs) is explicitly the wrong move — Megatron-Core's own Parallel Folding paper frames this as the core "dense-sparse mismatch" MoE parallelism has to solve, since MoE "wants high EP and low (usually 1) expert-TP, because sharding an already-small expert matrix with TP just fragments it further" [Parallel Folding paper, arXiv:2504.14960](https://arxiv.org/abs/2504.14960) [PAPER, 2025-04]. Parallel Folding's mechanism — decoupling attention-layer parallelism (TP×CP×DP×PP) from MoE-layer parallelism (ETP×EP×EDP×PP), sharing only PP for gradient-flow correctness — is exactly the tool that lets a layout run ETP=1 (no expert-level TP at all) while independently choosing whatever small TP degree the attention block's arithmetic intensity supports. This matches DeepSeek V3's own choice to skip tensor parallelism entirely during training [DeepSeek-V3 Technical Report §3.2](https://arxiv.org/abs/2412.19437) [PAPER, 2024-12], and it matches Megatron-Core's own published reference configuration for DeepSeek-V3-scale training on GB200: TP1/PP4/EP64 [NeMo/Megatron-Bridge performance summary, 26.06 container] [OFFICIAL-DOCS, 2026-07]. The layouts below follow this precedent: TP=1 everywhere except where a long-context stage's attention block specifically benefits from tensor- or sequence-level sharding (see below).

## Pipeline schedules: bubble formulas and the overlap landscape

The naive 1F1B bubble fraction is `r=(p-1)/m` (p=pipeline depth, m=microbatch count); interleaving with v virtual model-chunks per GPU reduces this to `r=(p-1)/(v·m)`, trading v-fold more communication for v-fold smaller bubble [Ultra-Scale Playbook](https://huggingface.co/spaces/nanotron/ultrascale-playbook) [OFFICIAL-BLOG, 2025]. DeepSeek's DualPipe goes further, splitting each backward chunk into separate input-gradient and weight-gradient micro-ops and feeding the pipeline from both ends simultaneously to hide cross-node all-to-all fully behind compute — its published bubble formula is `(PP/2−1)(F&B+B−3W)`, paid for with 2x parameter memory per device (two model copies live on each PP rank) and `PP+1` activation memory, a cost DeepSeek judged acceptable specifically because a large EP degree already keeps per-GPU parameter count small [DeepSeek-V3 Technical Report Table 2](https://arxiv.org/abs/2412.19437) [PAPER, 2024-12]. Megatron-Core did not adopt DualPipe's code directly; instead `combined_1f1b.py` implements a "conceptually DualPipe-like" merged-forward-backward 1F1B overlap on top of the standard schedule, achieving a comparable bubble/memory profile without the 2x parameter-memory tax — a still-open GitHub thread on this exact design (issue #1524) asks whether the VPP-based version requires more inter-node communication than true DualPipe under equivalent bubble rates, unresolved as of the issue's last comment [github.com/NVIDIA/Megatron-LM](https://github.com/NVIDIA/Megatron-LM) [CODE/ISSUE, 2026].

For a 90-100-layer model (Ch5's strawman), Megatron-Core's **Flexible Asymmetric VPP** — which allows arbitrary per-virtual-stage layer type and count, rather than the traditional uniform-layer-count constraint — is directly relevant: DeepSeek V3's own PP=16/VPP=2 layout assigns embedding+3 dense layers to rank 0 (cost-matched to 2 MoE layers), 2 MoE layers per VPP stage to ranks 1-13, the MTP module alone to rank 14, and the loss layer alone to rank 15 [Scalable Training of MoE Models with Megatron Core, Table 10](https://arxiv.org/abs/2603.07685) [PAPER, 2026-03]. The same tool lets a 96-100-layer, MTP-augmented strawman balance genuinely uneven per-stage costs (dense layers, MoE layers, and 2-3 MTP modules per Ch5's depth-2-or-3 recommendation) rather than forcing a naive uniform split.

## Activation recomputation economics

Full activation checkpointing trades a well-quantified throughput tax for a large memory win: for a Llama-3.1-8B-shaped block at seqlen=32768, bs=1, activations without checkpointing run 224 GiB versus 31 GiB with it — a 7.2x reduction — for a throughput cost of roughly 20-25%, "up to 30-40% per newer papers" [ml-engineering, training/performance](https://github.com/stas00/ml-engineering) [CODE, 2026-07]. Selective recomputation (recomputing only cheap-to-recompute, expensive-to-store ops like attention softmax/dropout rather than the full block) claws back most of this overhead while retaining most of the memory win, and is the approach Megatron's own sequence-parallel + selective-recompute combination validated at 530B-parameter, 2,240-A100 scale (54.2% MFU versus a 42.1% full-recompute baseline, +29% relative) [Reducing Activation Recomputation in Large Transformer Models, arXiv:2205.05198](https://arxiv.org/abs/2205.05198) [PAPER, 2022]. At 200B-active-parameter scale with PP-imposed small microbatches and a long-context extension stage reaching 32-128K tokens (Ch5's curriculum), some form of recomputation is close to mandatory rather than optional — but the exact tax for this specific block structure (MoE FFN plus MLA-and-sparse-attention plus MTP heads) is not directly measured anywhere in this book's source corpus; the 20-40% range above is a dense-model anchor to budget against, not a validated MoE-specific number, and should be treated as the single largest source of uncertainty in the predicted-MFU figures below.

## Sequence and context parallelism for long-context stages

Attention FLOPs only dominate the per-layer FLOPs budget once context length exceeds roughly `8×d_model` [How to Scale Your Model, Transformer Math](https://jax-ml.github.io/scaling-book/transformers/) [OFFICIAL-BLOG, 2025-02]; at d_model≈8192 that crossover sits at T>65,536 tokens — squarely inside Ch5's planned context-extension stage (32-128K tokens), meaning sequence/context parallelism stops being optional exactly where the curriculum says the model needs to go. Among the six cataloged SP implementations, DeepSpeed-Ulysses's comms volume scales down with parallelism degree (`4Nh/P`) while Megatron-LM's native sequence parallelism stays flat (`4Nh`) regardless of P [ml-engineering, model-parallelism](https://github.com/stas00/ml-engineering) [CODE, 2026-07]. Ulysses's core primitive — an all-to-all that redistributes the sequence dimension across GPUs and gathers back the full sequence per local attention head — is architecturally the same shape as MoE's own dispatch/combine all-to-all, making it the natural SP pairing for a ZeRO+EP-based stack rather than Megatron-style SP, which would add fixed, non-shrinking traffic on top of EP's own all-to-all rather than sharing the same comms budget logic. Ring-attention variants (Liu et al.'s ring-passed K/V, Colossal-AI's ring self-attention, DistFlashAttn's load-balanced variant) remain live alternatives with less production precedent at this scale.

## Stas's composition rules as a field-experience cross-check

The Scaling Book's crossovers are first-principles derivations; ml-engineering's decision tree is compressed field experience from BLOOM-176B and IDEFICS-80B, and the two should agree once evaluated at the same constants [ml-engineering, model-parallelism](https://github.com/stas00/ml-engineering) [CODE, 2026-07]. Walking the tree for a 10T-total model: it categorically does not fit on a single node (ruling out the "try ZeRO-with-replicas first" branch, which assumes the model fits within one node's memory), leaving the multi-node branches — "fast inter-node connectivity: PP+TP+DP" or, if still memory-constrained, "DP+PP+TP+ZeRO-1." Both land on exactly the EP(-substituting-for-much-of-TP)+PP+ZeRO-1 composition this chapter derives from the roofline algebra independently, a genuine convergence between a from-first-principles theory source and a from-scratch-trainings-run practitioner source that neither document states explicitly.

## Three worked candidate layouts

**Layout 1 — the anti-pattern, shown to make the "pure DP is dead" argument concrete.** EP=8 (single H100-style node-equivalent, deliberately under-using the 72-GPU domain), DP≈12,288 to reach 100k GPUs, PP and TP minimal. At E/k=64 and any realistic per-domain bandwidth, the FSDP-style critical batch this configuration needs is in the millions-to-tens-of-millions of tokens *per GPU* — even the most permissive cross-domain figure derived above (3.2M tokens/GPU at ConnectX-8) times a DP degree in the thousands implies a global batch requirement several orders of magnitude beyond anything in the LLaMA-3-to-DeepSeek-V3 range. This configuration does not train at a usable MFU; it is included to show numerically why "just use more DP" fails, not as a real candidate.

**Layout 2 — DeepSeek-style scaled up, the bulk-training (4-8K context) recommendation.** EP=64 (fits inside one NVL72 domain, 8 of the strawman's 512 experts per GPU, leaving 8-GPU hot-spare headroom), PP=8 physical stages with VPP=4 (Flexible Asymmetric VPP, 32 effective pipeline slots for ~96 layers ≈ 3 layers/slot), TP=1, DP=192 via ZeRO-1. Total: 64×8×192 = 98,304 GPUs (≈1,365 NVL72 domains), leaving headroom below the 100k budget for evaluation, checkpoint-coordination, and spare capacity. Interleaved-bubble check: keeping `r=(p-1)/(v·m)` under 10% with p=8, v=4 requires m≥18 microbatches per flush — comfortably achievable. ZeRO-1 compute-bound check on the DP axis: 50,000 tokens/GPU (ConnectX-8) is easily cleared by 18-24 microbatches of a few thousand tokens each. Predicted global batch, order of magnitude: 10-15M tokens, bracketed by LLaMA-3's 16M and DeepSeek-V3's 63M. **Predicted MFU**: the closest measured empirical anchor is Megatron-Core's own DeepSeek-V3-685B benchmark on GB200 at PP4×VPP4×EP64/256 GPUs — 1,048 TFLOPS/GPU sustained MXFP8, which is 21.0% FP8 MFU against the corrected 5.0 PFLOPS denominator (857 TFLOPS/GPU BF16, 34.3% BF16 MFU against the corrected 2.5 PFLOPS denominator) [arXiv:2603.07685 Table 11](https://arxiv.org/abs/2603.07685) + [GB200 FLOPs reconciliation] [PAPER, 2026-03]. The strawman's narrower experts (d_expert≈1400 versus DeepSeek's 2048, roughly 30% narrower) push arithmetic intensity down further, and 512 experts versus 256 adds routing/permutation overhead on top of the ~9% Megatron-Core already measures post-fusion; both effects push predicted MFU below the anchor. This is an extrapolation, not a measurement: a defensible planning range is **15-19% FP8 MFU (≈30-38% BF16-equivalent)**, with the DeepSeek-V3-on-GB200 figure as the optimistic bound and the activation-recomputation tax (20-40%, above) layered on separately as a further, not-yet-included, throughput cost.

**Layout 3 — long-context extension stage (32-128K tokens), Ch5's curriculum tail.** Starting from Layout 2's EP64×PP8 unit, insert Ulysses-style context parallelism CP=4 and shrink DP correspondingly: EP64×PP8×CP4×DP48 = 98,304 GPUs (same total). At 128K context, the T>8D crossover derived above means attention FLOPs are solidly dominant, making CP load-bearing rather than optional. Two countervailing effects on predicted MFU relative to Layout 2: Megatron-Core's own long-context measurements show DeepSeek-V3-style (dense-attention-heavy) architectures losing MFU at 256K context (88% of short-context MFU on H100) while SDPA-kernel-efficient architectures like Qwen3-235B-A22B actually *gain* MFU at long context (129% of short-context) [arXiv:2603.07685 §6.5] [PAPER, 2026-03]. The strawman's DSA-style trainable sparse attention (Ch5) — O(Lk) rather than O(L²) — argues for a milder version of the Qwen3 pattern rather than the DeepSeek-V3 pattern, since sparse attention's cost doesn't scale with L the way dense attention's does. Net **predicted MFU: roughly 85-95% of Layout 2's figure**, i.e., in the low-to-mid-teens percent FP8 — but this combines two extrapolations (Layout 2's own uncertainty, plus the DSA-tempers-long-context-cost argument) and should be treated as the most speculative number in this chapter.

## Implications for the 10T/200B run

The arithmetic in this chapter yields four load-bearing conclusions for the run plan. First, EP degree should be sized to the NVL72 domain (64-way, leaving hot-spare headroom) and never allowed to flatten across domains at this expert width — the strawman's d_expert≈1400 sits tens of times below the compute-bound EP viability threshold on any realistic scale-out fabric, so domain-limited (or DeepSeek-style node-capped) routing is a permanent architectural constraint, not a scale-dependent one that a bigger cluster escapes. Second, ZeRO-1 — not ZeRO-3/FSDP — is the correct DP-axis primitive precisely because EP has already done the expensive weight-sharding; treating this as a free choice rather than a structural necessity is the single most common way a first-pass parallelism plan would waste an order of magnitude of communication budget. Third, tensor parallelism belongs almost nowhere in this layout: not on the expert matrices (already too narrow), and only marginally (Y<6) on the strawman's few dense layers — Parallel Folding is the mechanism that makes "TP=1 for MoE, small TP for attention if at all" a composable choice rather than an all-or-nothing one. Fourth, the predicted 15-19% FP8 MFU for the bulk-training layout is a real number with a real empirical anchor (Megatron-Core's measured GB200/DeepSeek-V3 benchmark), but it sits below the more optimistic MFU figures sometimes quoted for GB200 elsewhere in the frontier-lab literature — Ch21's money-math chapter should treat this chapter's bottom-up derivation as a conservative floor to reconcile against, not a number to average away.
