# Chapter 5 — The MoE Design Space and a 10T/200B Strawman

Every other chapter in this book measures a lab's disclosures against a fixed architecture; this one has to fix the architecture. By July 2026 the frontier has converged on more of the MoE design space than most retrospectives credit — dropless routing, aux-loss-free balancing, and multi-token prediction are no longer live debates — but the axes that actually determine whether a 10T-total/200B-active host trains stably at 100,000 GB200s (sparsity ratio, attention mechanism, expert granularity) are still fought over in public, with at least one frontier lab publishing a named retreat from its own architecture. This chapter reads that fight from the primary sources and ends where the brief demands: a fully justified spec table.

## The Sparsity Frontier: How Far Does 2% Go?

The foundational result is Krajewski and Ludziejewski's fine-grained MoE paper ([Scaling Laws for Fine-Grained Mixture of Experts](https://arxiv.org/abs/2402.07871), ICML 2024) [PAPER, 02/2024], which introduced granularity G as an independent scaling axis and showed that matching expert width to a dense model's 4x-widened FFN — the naive default — is suboptimal at nearly every compute budget. The load-bearing 2025-2026 refinement is Ant Group's Ling team paper, [Towards Greater Leverage: Scaling Laws for Efficient Mixture-of-Experts Language Models](https://arxiv.org/abs/2507.17702) [PAPER, 07/2025], which trains over 300 models up to 28B parameters and defines Efficiency Leverage (EL) as the compute-multiplier an MoE configuration buys over a dense equivalent at matched loss. Its central finding: "for a fixed computational cost, greater model sparsity (i.e., lower activation ratio) leads to higher parameter efficiency," monotonically across every FLOPs budget tested (3e17-3e20), down to the lowest ratio tried, 0.8%, with no knee, and "the EL advantage is amplified at larger scales." Apple's companion paper, [Parameters vs FLOPs: Scaling Laws for Optimal Sparsity for Mixture-of-Experts Language Models](https://arxiv.org/abs/2501.12370) [PAPER, 07/2025 v3], reaches the same conclusion differently: compute-optimal sparsity S* (fraction of inactive experts) converges toward 1 as budget and model size grow, with active parameters per example *decreasing* for compute-optimal models even as total parameters grow.

Read naively, both papers say a 2% activation ratio is conservative, not aggressive. Two qualifications complicate that. First, both fitted ranges top out around 1e20-1e22 FLOPs, while a 10T/200B host trained on tens of trillions of tokens implies C ≈ 6·N_a·D ≈ 6×(2×10^11)×(4×10^13) ≈ 5×10^25 FLOPs — three to seven orders of magnitude beyond the fitted range, so extrapolation governs here, not interpolation. Second, Abnar et al. add a caveat the Ling paper doesn't emphasize: holding pretraining perplexity constant, sparser models underperform on reasoning-heavy tasks, since FLOPs-per-example matters independently of loss there even where it barely matters in-distribution. A companion paper, [Optimal Sparsity of MoE Language Models for Reasoning Tasks](https://arxiv.org/abs/2508.18672) [PAPER, 08/2025], reinforces this: reasoning accuracy scales with active compute, not total capacity.

This is almost certainly why real 2025-2026 flagships sit less sparse than the loss-optimal frontier would push them: DeepSeek-V3 5.5% (256+1, top-8, 671B/37B, [DeepSeek-V3](https://arxiv.org/abs/2412.19437) [PAPER, 12/2024]), Kimi K2 ~3.1% (384+1, top-8, 1.04T/32.6B, [Kimi K2](https://arxiv.org/abs/2507.20534) [PAPER, 07/2025]), Ling-1T ~3.5% (256+1, top-8, 1T/51B, [Ling-1T](https://arxiv.org/abs/2510.22115) [PAPER, 10/2025]), GLM-5 ~5.4% (256+1, top-8, 744B/40B, [GLM-5](https://arxiv.org/abs/2602.15763) [PAPER, 2026]), Qwen3-235B-A22B 9.4% (128, top-8, no shared, [Qwen3](https://arxiv.org/abs/2505.09388) [PAPER, 05/2025]), MiniMax-M2 ~4.3% (256, top-8, sigmoid gating, [MiniMax-M2](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.26494) [PAPER, 11/2025]). All cluster 3-5.5%, none within an order of magnitude of the Ling study's 0.8% floor. A 2% activation ratio sits between two regimes — sparser than every shipped flagship, nowhere near the loss-optimal extreme — a defensible hedge against the reasoning-task penalty, not a number either paper independently validates.

Granularity resolves a real terminological trap. Ludziejewski's original G is 4·d_model/d_expert; Ant's paper redefines G as 2·d_model/d_expert so each expert is the same absolute size for a given G-value — the two papers' G-numbers are not interchangeable, a mismatch the Ling paper flags explicitly (§6.1) as the reason its finding looks contradictory to a naive reading of Ludziejewski. Under Ant's definition, granularity is genuinely U-shaped, not monotone, with a stable optimum at **G≈12** across FLOPs 1e18-1e20; poor routing balance shifts the optimum coarser, so the granularity/routing-quality interaction is load-bearing. Shared-expert ratio is U-shaped too, with a compute-dependent optimum: S=16.7% for C≤1e20 FLOPs, dropping to S=8.3% for C>1e20 — since any real 10T-host run clears 1e20 FLOPs by orders of magnitude, the paper's practical translation is blunt: "the optimal design choice is to use the lowest possible non-zero sharing ratio... heuristically implemented by setting the number of shared experts to one." DeepSeek-V3/V3.2, Kimi K2, and Ling-1T all in fact ship exactly one shared expert; Qwen3 removed shared experts entirely in its 235B-A22B flagship, while Qwen3-Next brought exactly one back.

Two secondary findings matter for the strawman: attention should consume 30-40% of total FLOPs, a "robust" near-optimal range; and replacing the first 1-3 of roughly 60 layers with dense (non-MoE) FFN is near-EL-neutral but reduces routing imbalance, with the optimal dense-layer count growing from 0 at 1e18 FLOPs to 2-3 of 60 at 3e20 FLOPs. Fitted hyperparameter laws diverge from dense practice: MoE's optimal LR is η^opt = 1.1576·C^(-0.1529), batch B^opt = 0.0694·C^0.3644, versus dense η^opt = 2.2177·C^(-0.1649), B^opt = 0.1039·C^0.3493 — MoE wants a larger batch and lower LR at matched compute, attributed to sparse per-expert gradient statistics needing more averaging. Allocation follows the same asymmetry: MoE M^opt = 0.1915·C^0.5095, D^opt = 5.2232·C^0.4905 versus dense M^opt = 0.0655·C^0.5422, D^opt = 15.2582·C^0.4578 — the compute-optimal MoE model is smaller in active FLOPs/token but trained on relatively more tokens. The framework validates end to end with Ling-mini-beta (0.85B active/17.5B total, 3.1% activation, G=12), matching or beating a 6.1B dense comparator on an identical 1T-token dataset — a predicted >7x efficiency leverage confirmed.

## Routing Mechanics: Sigmoid, Aux-Loss-Free, and the Balancing Forks

DeepSeek-V3's router uses sigmoid affinity scoring with post-hoc normalization over the selected top-k, and aux-loss-free load balancing via a per-expert bias term added only to the routing decision — not the gate value — updated each step as bias += γ·sign(load_error), with **γ=0.001 for the first 14.3T tokens, frozen to 0 for the final ~500B tokens** ([DeepSeek-V3](https://arxiv.org/abs/2412.19437) §2.1.2 [PAPER, 12/2024]). A small sequence-wise balance loss at α=0.0001 backstops pathological within-sequence imbalance without acting as the primary force — the paper is explicit that "too large an auxiliary loss will impair model performance." Result: zero token drops across the full run. Ling-1T ships essentially the same recipe, and GLM-4.5 publishes an almost identical schedule — bias rate 0.001 for the first 15T tokens then 0.0, sequence-level weight 0.0001 ([GLM-4.5](https://arxiv.org/abs/2508.06471) §2.4 [PAPER, 08/2025]) — evidence this exact recipe, not just the general idea, has propagated cross-lab.

Qwen3 forks deliberately: it removes shared experts (present in Qwen2.5-MoE) and replaces DeepSeek's bias mechanism with a "global-batch load balancing loss" (Qiu et al., 2025) computed over a global batch, intended to "encourage expert specialization" ([Qwen3](https://arxiv.org/abs/2505.09388) [PAPER, 05/2025]). MiniMax-M2 forks a third way: sigmoid gating with learnable per-expert bias, chosen because sigmoid removes softmax's zero-sum constraint, letting several experts activate confidently at once and smoothing routing dynamics enough to lean much less on any auxiliary loss ([MiniMax-M2](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.26494) §2 [PAPER, 11/2025]). Two incompatible balancing philosophies coexist in frontier production, and DeepSeek-V4 adds a fourth wrinkle — the gate activation moves from sigmoid to √(softplus(·)), unreplicated outside DeepSeek as of this writing ([DeepSeek-V4](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.19348) [PAPER, 06/2026]).

Expert-choice routing — experts ranking and selecting tokens, rather than the reverse — remains dead for autoregressive pretraining despite a real 2025-2026 literature of workarounds (predictor-based causal thresholds, Lory's segment routing, SeqTopK, ReMoE, top-p adaptive routing): the causality problem — EC needs a future-token ranking unavailable to an autoregressive decoder — remains fundamentally unsolved, not merely unfashionable; token-choice top-k is fully dominant. On the systems side, dropless-versus-capacity-factor is closed: MegaBlocks ([arXiv:2211.15841](https://arxiv.org/abs/2211.15841), Databricks/Stanford [PAPER, 2022]) established block-sparse dropless MoE, beating Tutel's best capacity-factor config by up to 40% and 2.4x over dense Megatron-LM. By 2026 the live frontier is kernel efficiency for dropless under expert parallelism, per AMD's Primus-Turbo team: "a well-tuned grouped-GEMM kernel is the single most important primitive for fast dropless MoE training" [OFFICIAL-BLOG/CODE, 06/2026] — a Ch15 question; the strawman below simply assumes dropless.

## Attention for a 200B-Active Host

### MLA versus GQA: the theory and the hardware case

The efficiency argument for Multi-head Latent Attention is, at bottom, a memory-bandwidth argument, not a parameter-efficiency one. DeepSeek's own hardware-reflections paper reframes LLM decode as GEMV-bound, not GEMM-bound — "the computation shifts from GEMM to GEMV, which has a much lower compute-to-memory ratio" — and reports MLA cutting per-token KV-cache footprint from 516 KB (LLaMA-3.1-style MHA) to **70.272 KB** for DeepSeek-V3, a 7.28x reduction [PAPER, arXiv:2505.09343]. A separate comparative study puts DeepSeek-V3-class MLA (~70 KB/token) against comparable GQA models (192-328 KB/token) — a 2.7-4.7x reduction [CREDIBLE-HEARSAY, 2025], narrowing but not disappearing under aggressive GQA cache compression.

The more rigorous case comes from Su Jianlin, the RoPE inventor and a Moonshot AI researcher whose blog kexue.fm functions as a running lab notebook for Kimi's pretraining team, often posting derivations before the corresponding technical report — QK-Clip appeared there ten days before the Kimi K2 report that used it as a core stability technique ([QK-Clip / MuonClip](https://kexue.fm/archives/11126) [BLOG, 07/2025], Ch7). His two-part MLA analysis ([#20](https://kexue.fm/archives/10907) [BLOG, 05/2025], [#21](https://kexue.fm/archives/11111) [BLOG, 07/2025]) claims, from outside DeepSeek: "under equal training and inference cost, MLA may be the best-performing Full Attention variant." Controlled ablations decompose the advantage into three separable effects: larger effective head_dims (128+64 combined ≈ 192-equivalent) is the single biggest lever — bigger than adding heads or widening the MLP at matched params; Partial RoPE helps independently; KV-sharing helps but is smallest and least validated at scale. The theoretical argument (#21): under NoPE, any of MHA/GQA/MQA is expressible as some head_dims-K, KV-shared MQA via matrix absorption, so for a fixed cache budget the best decode-time model is head_dims-equal-to-cache-size KV-shared MQA, while for fixed compute (train/prefill) the best is the widest-head, non-shared MHA. MLA is the LoRA-style bridge — down-project then up-project, simultaneously MHA-192-ish at train/prefill and MQA-576-ish at decode with the same weights, at no real cost since decode is never compute-bound. Practically: the free variables that matter are head_dims (push past 128) and Partial RoPE fraction, not "copy DeepSeek's MLA verbatim or don't." A wide-head_dims, Partial-RoPE GQA variant (Su's GQA2-(192+64)-S2, with VO-RoPE) came within 0.003 nats of MLA's own loss at MLA's cache budget — a validated near-equal alternative.

### Hybrid sliding-window and trainable sparse attention

Three 2025 designs converge on similar local:global ratios for cheap long-context handling: gpt-oss alternates GQA sliding-window and full-attention layers [OFFICIAL-BLOG, 08/2025]; Gemma 3 uses a 5:1 local:global ratio (1024-token local windows, RoPE base 10k local / 1M global), costing minimal perplexity versus Gemma 2's 1:1 while needing full-context K/V in only 1/6 of layers ([Gemma 3](https://arxiv.org/abs/2503.19786) [PAPER, 03/2025]); Llama 4's iRoPE alternates ~3:1 RoPE-chunked local attention against NoPE full-attention layers, targeting "infinite" length generalization [OFFICIAL-BLOG, 2025].

The more consequential development is trainable *sparse* attention, where DeepSeek has shipped a second full generation. Native Sparse Attention ([NSA](https://arxiv.org/abs/2502.11089), DeepSeek-AI + Peking University, ACL 2025 Best Paper [PAPER, 02/2025]) combines coarse compressed tokens, fine-grained selected blocks, and a local window via learned gating; its largest reported scale (27B active) was never confirmed at frontier size. MoBA ([arXiv:2502.13189](https://arxiv.org/abs/2502.13189), Moonshot AI [PAPER+CODE, 02/2025]) applies MoE-style routing to attention itself — queries sparsely select KV blocks — and is confirmed as the mechanism actually serving Kimi's production long-context traffic.

DeepSeek Sparse Attention (DSA), from the DeepSeek-V3.2-Exp technical report [PAPER, primary PDF, 09/2025], is the most fully-specified mechanism here. A small FP8 "lightning indexer" computes I_{t,s} = Σ_j w^I_{t,j}·ReLU(q^I_{t,j}·k^I_s), then retrieves only the top-**k=2048** KV entries by score; main attention runs over just those, cutting complexity from O(L²) to O(Lk). It runs under MLA in MQA mode, sharing each compressed KV latent across query heads. DSA is retrofitted, not trained from scratch: a dense warm-up freezes everything but the indexer, training it via KL-divergence against the main model's own summed attention scores (LR=1e-3, 2.1B tokens), then sparse training unfreezes everything (LR=7.3e-6, 943.7B tokens). Measured payoff (H800, $2/GPU-hr, 128K context): prefill drops from ~$0.7 to ~$0.2/M tokens, decode from ~$2.1 to ~$0.4, with quality flat against V3.1-Terminus (MMLU-Pro identical at 85.0). GLM-5 independently states it uses "DeepSeek Sparse Attention (DSA)" by name — the clearest documented cross-lab propagation of a DeepSeek attention mechanism ([GLM-5](https://arxiv.org/abs/2602.15763) [PAPER, 2026]). DeepSeek-V4 extends the idea to two compression tiers — Compressed Sparse Attention (top-k selection over compressed KV entries) and Heavily Compressed Attention (dense attention over a much-more-heavily-compressed sequence) — both with shared-KV MQA, Partial RoPE, and an attention-sink term; at 1M context, V4-Pro needs only 27% of V3.2's inference FLOPs and 10% of its KV cache ([DeepSeek-V4](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.19348) [PAPER, 06/2026]).

### The linear-hybrid verdict

The genuinely unsettled question, as of July 2026, is whether linear-recurrent attention belongs in a frontier stack at all — and the evidence is split in a way directly informative for a 100k-GPU commitment. Qwen3-Next uses a 3:1 Gated DeltaNet:Gated Attention hybrid across 48 layers, claiming under 10% of dense Qwen3-235B-A22B's training cost at comparable quality [OFFICIAL-BLOG, 09/2025]. Kimi-Linear ([KDA](https://arxiv.org/abs/2510.26692), Moonshot [PAPER, 10/2025]) uses a 3:1 KDA:full-attention interleave, cutting KV-cache up to 75% and delivering up to 6x decode throughput at 1M context in a 48B/3B model trained to 5.7T tokens, with open-sourced FlashKDA kernels giving 1.72-2.22x prefill speedup on H20 [CODE, 04/2026].

Against this, MiniMax published an explicit, named postmortem on abandoning linear attention. M1 used Lightning (linear) attention in a 1-full-per-7-linear hybrid at 456B/45.9B ([MiniMax-M1](https://arxiv.org/abs/2506.13585) [PAPER, 06/2025]); M2 reverted to 100% full attention across all 62 layers. The team's blog is specific about why: on saturated benchmarks the hybrid "looked just as good as pure full attention," but at larger scale "clear deficits in complex, multi-hop reasoning tasks" emerged — the validation benchmarks were themselves the blind spot. RL training separately exposed "serious precision issues": linear attention is "far more sensitive to numerical precision than full attention," a direct liability for low-precision KV/state storage. Infrastructurally, linear variants are "memory-bound, even during training, and without extreme IO optimization, the system fails to utilize the GPU's available FLOPs" — the theoretical compute-favorable crossover doesn't materialize without simultaneously solving prefix caching, speculative decoding, and low-precision state storage together. MiniMax's summary: full attention "wins because it's the least fragile choice across tasks, model sizes, and inference stacks," not because efficient attention is dead but because it "isn't mature enough to be the default" [OFFICIAL-BLOG, huggingface.co/blog/MiniMax-AI, 11/2025]. Tellingly, MiniMax M3 reportedly reintroduces a new *sparse*-attention mechanism rather than returning to linear-recurrent compression [CREDIBLE-HEARSAY, 2026] — even MiniMax's own trajectory bends toward selection-based sparsity.

The frontier is bifurcating between trainable *sparse* (NSA→MoBA→DSA→CSA/HCA) and *linear-hybrid* (GDN→KDA), with sparse-selection accumulating more cross-lab adoption at the largest scales (DeepSeek→GLM-5) while linear-hybrid stays attractive at mid-scale but carries one documented production retreat citing failure modes — memory-bound training, precision fragility, serving-stack friction — most punishing at 100k-GPU scale. This tips the strawman toward MLA-plus-trainable-sparse-attention and away from linear-hybrid, though Ch9 revisits whether multimodal-native pretraining changes the calculus for very long video/audio contexts.

## Multi-Token Prediction as Default Practice

MTP is close to universal in 2025-2026 frontier releases — DeepSeek-V3/V3.2/V4, Kimi K2, Qwen3-Next, MiMo-V2-Flash, Ling-1T, and Nemotron-3 Super/Ultra all use it — and its value expanded in 2026 beyond speculative decoding. DeepSeek-V3 implements MTP as a cascaded chain of modules (not Gloeckle-style parallel independent heads), sharing the embedding and output head with the main model; loss weight is 0.3 for the first 10T tokens, 0.1 for the remaining 4.8T ([DeepSeek-V3](https://arxiv.org/abs/2412.19437) §2.2 [PAPER, 12/2024]), giving 85-90% second-token acceptance and ~1.8x decode TPS [CREDIBLE-HEARSAY, 2025]. Kimi K2's cautionary tale is purely practical: its initially released MTP checkpoint had only ~39% acceptance because the weights weren't self-distilled against the exact base checkpoint; properly matched, MTP reaches 80-90% acceptance [CREDIBLE-HEARSAY, 2025-2026] — MTP quality is checkpoint-matching-sensitive, not architecture-sensitive alone.

The genuinely new 2026 framing comes from Xiaomi's MiMo-V2-Flash, a 3-layer, 0.33B-param-per-block MTP stack achieving 3.6-token acceptance length and 2.6x decode speedup, positioned explicitly as an **RL-training accelerator**: "MTP enables efficient and effective RL with small batches by scaling token-level parallelism instead of batch size" ([MiMo-V2-Flash](https://arxiv.org/abs/2601.02780) [PAPER, 01/2026]) — directly relevant to the reader's post-pretraining phase, not just inference.

Nemotron-3 Super and Ultra take a structurally different approach: 2 MTP heads with **shared weights across prediction offsets**, rather than independent fixed-offset heads. Fixed-offset heads suffer a train/inference mismatch when reused recursively for deeper speculative drafts (trained on ground-truth hidden states, conditioned at inference on self-generated ones); a shared-weight head generalizes better under recursive self-conditioning. On SPEED-Bench at draft length 7, Super achieves 3.45 tokens/verification-step, ahead of Qwen3-Next's 3.33 and DeepSeek-R1's 2.70, with the gap widening at deeper draft positions ([Nemotron 3 Super](https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.12374) Table 2 [PAPER, 04/2026]). MTP loss-scaling is 0.3 for Super, 0.1 (0.05/block) for Ultra — a hyperparameter that interacts badly with a BF16 gradient-reduction optimization during Ultra's pretraining, discussed in Ch7.

## Nemotron-3: The Hybrid Mamba-MoE Divergent Branch

Every architecture discussed so far shares one commitment: a Transformer backbone with MoE FFN layers and some flavor of attention compression. NVIDIA's Nemotron 3 family — Nano (31.6B/3.2B, [arXiv:2512.20848](https://arxiv.org/abs/2512.20848) [PAPER, 12/2025]), Super (120.6B/12.7B, [arXiv:2604.12374](https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.12374) [PAPER, 04/2026]), Ultra (550B/55B, [arXiv:2606.15007](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.15007) [PAPER, 06/2026]) — is the production counter-example: a **hybrid Mamba-Transformer-MoE** backbone where the majority of layers are state-space (Mamba) and only a minority (6 of 52 in Nano) are self-attention "global anchor" layers, wrapped around a novel MoE-compression mechanism called LatentMoE.

LatentMoE ([arXiv:2601.18089](https://arxiv.org/abs/2601.18089), NVIDIA [PAPER, 01/2026]) starts from a GB200 roofline (10 PFLOPS FP4, 8 TB/s HBM, 900 GB/s unidirectional NVLink): at throughput-serving batch sizes, standard-MoE communication-to-compute ratio for a Qwen3-235B-A22B-shaped model is roughly 9x — all-to-all dominates compute — while at low-latency batch sizes MoE is memory-bandwidth-bound on expert-weight reads. From derived design principles (communication volume scales as d_model×K independent of expert width; quality tracks K·m via a Barron-function expressivity argument; scaling N and K by the same factor α exponentially expands the combinatorial expert-selection space), d_model is the only axis that can shrink without hurting bandwidth, communication, or the K·m quality budget. LatentMoE down-projects each token from d to a latent ℓ<d (typically α=d/ℓ=4x) before routing and all expert FFN compute, then up-projects back — holding communication volume and per-expert weight-load bytes constant while quadratically expanding the expert-combination space if N and K scale together by α. Validated at 95B-total/8B-active on 300B tokens, it beats a matched-active/matched-total baseline on every metric (MMLU-Pro 34.91 vs. 29.26); at 73B-total/8B-active hybrid Mamba-MoE scale on 1T tokens the gain holds (52.87 vs. 48.30). Projected to trillion-parameter scale via an Effective Parameter Multiplier framework, a hypothetical Kimi-K2-1T-LatentMoE variant would need roughly 350B fewer parameters than standard MoE to match accuracy, with the standard-MoE competitor running 1.24-3.46x slower at matched accuracy.

Ultra ships this at 550B total/55B active, 108 layers, d_model=8192, 512 experts at top-22, ℓ=2048 (α=4) — distinct from every model above in two ways at once: state-space hybridization replacing most self-attention, and latent-space MoE dispatch compression replacing granularity/activation-ratio tuning as the primary sparsity lever. It represents NVIDIA's actual production bet, and is in principle combinable with MLA-style KV-cache compression, though no source here documents that attempted. Two disclosure gaps matter for a planner: Super and Ultra pretrain natively in NVFP4 (Ch8's territory, though architecturally relevant since it's why LatentMoE's latent projections and MTP heads stay in BF16 while the bulk of linear layers run native NVFP4 GEMMs); and none of the four papers discloses MFU, GPU count, or step time for the main pretraining run — a striking silence given how much other operational detail (RL infrastructure failure rates, exact LR schedules) they do publish. LatentMoE's accuracy claims are validated only to 73B-total/8B-active on 1T tokens and projected, not measured, at trillion-parameter scale, and Ultra's own run suffered two named divergences (one root-caused to a BF16-gradient-reduction/MTP-loss-scale interaction, one left "Undetermined," mitigated only by cutting the token budget) — evidence this branch is differently-risked, not lower-risk, than the Transformer-MoE mainline.

## Vocabulary, Tokenization, and the Embeddings-versus-Experts Tension

Vocabulary scaling has its own laws, and frontier practice is systematically under-provisioned relative to them. [Scaling Laws with Vocabulary](https://arxiv.org/abs/2407.13623) (Tao et al., NeurIPS 2024 [PAPER, 07/2024]) finds optimal vocabulary size grows as a power law in compute, more slowly than non-vocabulary parameters but still faster than shipped practice: "the optimal vocabulary size of Llama2-70B should have been at least 216K, seven times larger than its actual 32K," with 32K→43K at matched 2.3e21 FLOPs moving ARC-Challenge 29.1→32.0. [Over-Tokenized Transformer](https://arxiv.org/abs/2501.16975) [PAPER, 01/2025] reinforces the direction. A fresh January 2026 result, [Scaling Embeddings Outperforms Scaling Experts](https://arxiv.org/abs/2601.21204) [PAPER, 01/2026], cuts directly against the "more/finer experts" consensus above: for a fixed budget, growing embedding/vocabulary capacity can beat growing expert count — a genuine, unresolved tension, since the two sides of that trade were never fit jointly.

Real frontier vocab sizes cluster below what the laws suggest is optimal: Kimi K2 160K, Ling-1T 156K BBPE, Qwen3 151,669 BBPE, MiniMax M2 200,064, gpt-oss ~200K (o200k_harmony). None has pushed past ~200K. Two more radical alternatives remain unshipped at frontier scale: Byte Latent Transformer ([BLT](https://arxiv.org/abs/2412.09871), Meta, ACL 2025 [PAPER, 12/2024]) uses entropy-based dynamic byte patching, showing better inference-FLOP scaling than tokenization at fixed cost up to 8B params/4T bytes; [SuperBPE](https://arxiv.org/abs/2503.13423) (Liu et al., COLM 2025 [PAPER, 03/2025]) lifts the whitespace pretoken-boundary constraint to learn "superword" tokens, cutting token counts up to 33% and beating BPE baselines on 30 tasks at 8B scale (+8.2% MMLU, 27% more efficient at inference). Neither is in a named 2025-2026 flagship — both are a live opportunity for a from-scratch build, not a proven-at-scale choice, and betting the tokenizer on either is a higher-variance decision than vocabulary size alone.

## Depth, Width, and the Layer-Count Census

The one dedicated depth-versus-width paper here, [The Depth Delusion: Why Transformers Should Be Wider, Not Deeper](https://arxiv.org/abs/2601.20994) (Fahim & Karim [PAPER, 01/2026]), needs its provenance stated before its numbers are used: a two-author preprint validated only on **dense** transformers from 17M to 7B params (30 architectures, R²=0.922) — not a frontier-lab publication, not MoE-specific. Its findings are still a useful hypothesis to stress-test: optimal depth D*∝C^0.12, width W*∝C^0.34 (width should scale 2.8x faster than depth), and a "critical depth" D_crit∝W^0.44 beyond which more layers *increase* loss via "gradient starvation" — their own 7B-scale 64-layer model underperformed a 32-layer model by 0.12 nats despite more parameters, and the paper speculates several dense frontier models sit 3.6-4.9x past their theoretical critical depth. Treat this as a caution against blindly stacking layers, not validated exponents for a 10T MoE.

Real frontier layer counts show no clean monotone trend against total parameters: DeepSeek-V3 and Kimi K2 both hold 61 layers at d_model=7168 across a 671B-to-1T range; GLM-4.5 (355B/32B) runs d_model=5120 with 96 attention heads — 2.5x more than a hidden-dim-matched baseline — deliberately trading width for depth because "deeper models exhibited better reasoning capacity" (the research notes do not record GLM-4.5's exact layer count) ([GLM-4.5](https://arxiv.org/abs/2508.06471) §2.1 [PAPER, 08/2025]); GLM-5 and Ling-1T both run 80 layers, continuing that deeper-narrower philosophy; Qwen3-Next holds 48; MiniMax-M2 uses 62 at d_model=3072; gpt-oss-120b uses 36. Nemotron-3 Ultra runs deepest at 108 layers — but mixes Mamba and attention blocks, not directly comparable. Depth is currently lab-specific, not a settled scaling-law output.

## Multimodal-Native Constraints on the Text Backbone

Ch9 owns multimodal-native pretraining; the narrower question here is whether building multimodal-native from step one changes any decision above. The consequential datapoint is Baidu's own reversal: ERNIE 4.5 (June 2025) shipped **modality-isolated MoE** with a router-orthogonalization loss to prevent routing collapse when a text-only router is naively extended [PAPER, ERNIE 4.5 Technical Report, 06/2025]; ERNIE 5.0 (February 2026) abandoned that for "modality-agnostic expert routing... in contrast to modality-isolated routing strategies in our previous models" ([ERNIE 5.0](https://arxiv.org/abs/2602.04705) [PAPER, 02/2026]). This means the router/sparsity/granularity choices above don't need modification for an anticipated multimodal-native build — the majority pattern (Llama 4, Kimi-VL, Qwen3-VL) already feeds a continuous encoder into the same modality-unaware expert pool as text. What does change is EP/TP/PP topology: NVIDIA's heterogeneous-parallelism paper for multimodal training ([arXiv:2605.27678](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.27678) [PAPER, 05/2026]) shows a shared layout across encoder and LLM costs real throughput (colocated heterogeneous execution improves TFLOPS/GPU up to 49.3%) — a Ch16/Ch9 question, not a change to the architecture chosen here.

## Upcycling versus From-Scratch

NVIDIA's dense-to-MoE upcycling technique (2024) used virtual-group initialization to expand a dense Nemotron-4 15B, beating continued dense training (67.6 vs. 65.3 MMLU) [CREDIBLE-HEARSAY, 10/2024] — the largest documented upcycling success, still several orders of magnitude below frontier scale. 2025-2026 upcycling papers (Marco-MoE, Dense2MoE, SPRI) target multilingual, on-device, or data-constrained regimes, not frontier pretraining. Every flagship surveyed here — DeepSeek V3/V3.2/V4, Kimi K2, Qwen3/Qwen3-Next, Ling-1T, GLM-4.5/5, MiniMax M2, Nemotron 3 — was trained MoE-from-scratch. For a 10T-total host, from-scratch is the only empirically demonstrated path at this scale.

## Implications for the 10T/200B Run

The architecture this chapter lands on — MLA with wide effective head-dims and Partial RoPE, DSA-style trainable sparse selection instead of linear-hybrid attention, sigmoid-plus-aux-loss-free routing with one shared expert at G≈12, 2% activation as a deliberate hedge, and 2-3 shared-weight MTP modules — is defensible from primary sources to roughly 1T total parameters and genuine extrapolation beyond that, by the notes' own admission on the sparsity and depth rows. Three consequences follow for the rest of this book. First, granularity and expert-count choices here set the EP topology math in Ch16 — 512-640 experts at top-8 is a materially different all-to-all shape than DeepSeek's 256 or Kimi's 384, propagating into the communication-to-compute ratios Ch18 budgets for. Second, sparse-selection over linear-recurrent keeps kernel dependencies inside the FlashAttention/DSA lineage Ch15 catalogs, rather than the still-immature linear-attention kernel and serving stack MiniMax's postmortem warns against betting a 100k-GPU commitment on. Third, the Nemotron-3 hybrid Mamba-MoE branch is differently-risked enough — given two named divergences in NVIDIA's own largest run — that it belongs in Ch22's gap list as a hedge track worth ablating before the run is locked, not a rejected alternative.
