# Chapter 16 — GB200 NVL72 Ground Truth and the Alternative Silicon Question

Every FLOPs-per-dollar argument from here through Ch23 rests on a single contested number: what one GB200 GPU can actually do, sustained, on a sparse-MoE workload. That number turns out to be surprisingly hard to pin down even from primary sources — NVIDIA's own reference documents blur the SKUs, and careful secondary sources, the JAX Scaling Book among them, silently substitute the wrong SKU's peak FLOPs into GB200-labeled calculations. This chapter reconciles that conflict, walks the only rigorous public before/after benchmark of GB200 against H100, and then asks the question assumed away so far: whether 100,000 GB200-class GPUs is even the right unit of account for a run that will not start moving tokens until well into 2026 or 2027.

## The Reconciled Spec Table: What "GB200 NVL72" Actually Means

GB200 NVL72 and HGX B200 are the same reticle-limited Blackwell die — 208B transistors, TSMC 4NP — configured at different power envelopes and cooling classes. NVIDIA's own side-by-side datasheet ([document #3384703](https://openzeka.com/wp-content/uploads/2025/02/blackwell-datasheet.pdf), Dec 2024) puts them in one table, with a single footnote that resolves most of the confusion in downstream literature: every Tensor Core figure in the table except FP64 is a 2:4 structured-sparse number, and the dense figure relevant to ordinary training rooflines is exactly half.

| Metric (dense, non-sparse) | GB200 NVL72 (1,200W, liquid) | HGX B200 (1,000W, air) |
|---|---|---|
| FP4 Tensor Core | 10,000 TFLOPS | 9,000 TFLOPS |
| FP8/FP6 Tensor Core | **5,000 TFLOPS** | 4,500 TFLOPS |
| BF16/FP16 Tensor Core | 2,500 TFLOPS | 2,250 TFLOPS |
| TF32 Tensor Core | 1,250 TFLOPS | 1,125 TFLOPS |
| FP32 (vector, non-tensor) | 80 TFLOPS | 75 TFLOPS |
| FP64/FP64 Tensor Core | 40 TFLOPS | 37 TFLOPS |
| GPU memory / bandwidth | **186 GB HBM3e / 8.0 TB/s** | 180 GB HBM3e / 7.7 TB/s |
| Max configurable TDP | 1,200 W | 1,000 W |

The correction across every tensor-core precision is a clean, uniform +11.1% (5,000/4,500 = 2,500/2,250 = 10,000/9,000 = 10/9 exactly), but the vector-ALU path (FP32/FP64) only gains 6.7–8.1%. Back-calculating an implied clock from the vector path (≈1.98 GHz HGX vs ≈2.1 GHz NVL72 GB200) predicts only a ~4,770 TFLOPS dense-FP8 GB200 figure if the same ~6% gap applied uniformly to tensor cores — not the actual 5,000. NVIDIA publishes no official clock speed for either SKU, and the gap between the tensor-core ratio and the vector-ALU ratio is unexplained in any source we could locate. The likely mechanism is a more aggressive sustained-clock floor specifically under Tensor-Core-saturated load at 1,200W — but that is our inference, not a cited fact.

The distinction matters practically because it is genuinely easy to get wrong, and good sources get it wrong. The JAX Scaling Book's undifferentiated "B200" FLOPs table is unambiguously the HGX dense figure (2.3e15 BF16, 4.5e15 FP8, 9.0e15 FP4 — matching HGX within rounding), and dropping those numbers into a GB200 NVL72 cluster analysis silently understates the machine; Ch18's critical-batch and EP-viability thresholds are computed against the corrected figure. The same trap produces wrong MFU numbers: a 46.6% GB200 MFU for DeepSeek-V3-scale training circulates from dividing a measured 1,048 TFLOPS/GPU by the HGX 2,250-TFLOPS BF16 denominator — but the measurement is MXFP8 per the Megatron report's own Table 11, so the correct denominator is the 5,000-TFLOPS FP8/MXFP8 peak, giving 21.0% (Ch17 and Ch18 carry the corrected number). The scaling book's own text is internally inconsistent on this point — its main FLOPs table uses HGX-class numbers while a worked example three sections later reasons from an 80-TFLOPS FP32 figure that is unambiguously the GB200 number, without ever flagging the SKU switch ([jax-ml/scaling-book, gpus.md](https://github.com/jax-ml/scaling-book/blob/main/gpus.md)). Every GB200 FLOPs figure from here on uses the corrected 5,000/2,500/10,000 TFLOPS dense FP8/BF16/FP4 figures above; any number tracing to an un-disambiguated "B200" citation should be treated as suspect.

A third SKU, B100 (700W, a drop-in HGX H100-baseboard-compatible part), exists specifically as a compatibility bridge and is generally excluded from benchmarking literature because of reported yield/throttling problems that kept it from hitting claimed specs — worth knowing only so a stray "B100 TFLOPS" search result is not conflated with either number above ([glennklockwood.com](https://www.glennklockwood.com/garden/processors/b200), Apr 2026).

The fourth SKU is the one most 2026-vintage procurement conversations actually concern: **GB300 NVL72 (Blackwell Ultra, B300)**, which the MLPerf and TCO sections below lean on repeatedly and which therefore deserves its own spec paragraph rather than a passing mention. Blackwell Ultra's headline changes are HBM capacity (288 GB per GPU, 12-Hi stacks, up from 186 GB), a dense-FP4 uplift of roughly 1.5x (to ~15 PFLOPS/GPU), and a per-GPU power envelope pushed toward ~1.4 kW — while NVIDIA quotes no corresponding FP8/BF16 tensor-core uplift, which is why the MFU normalizations in Ch17 use the same 5,000-TFLOPS dense-FP8 denominator for both GB200 and GB300 (NVIDIA's Blackwell Ultra materials, 2025–2026). That makes the two measured GB300-vs-GB200 training deltas on record mutually illuminating rather than contradictory. The container-matched Megatron-Core tables show +18% sustained MXFP8 throughput on DeepSeek-V3-scale training (1,233 vs 1,048 TFLOPS/GPU) — about what a power/clock bump plus memory-capacity slack (larger microbatches, less recomputation pressure) should buy with no new FP8 silicon. The "GB300 is 1.6x GB200" MLPerf framing, by contrast, compares submissions that differ in software vintage and configuration as well as silicon. For a training-TCO model, the +18% container-matched figure is the honest hardware delta; the balance of the 1.6x is software maturation that GB200 owners also receive (our own reconciliation of the Megatron-Core report's Table 11, arXiv:2603.07685, against the MLPerf v6.0 submissions). The 288-GB capacity point is the quietly decisive spec for the 10T/200B design point: at 200B active parameters with MTP heads and long-context activation footprints (Ch18's memory audit), the extra 102 GB per GPU is worth more than any FLOPs delta — it buys back recomputation tax directly, which compounds into MFU at a rate no clock bump matches.

One spec-table absence in the discussion so far deserves correcting, because it is GB200's most architecturally distinctive feature and the one HGX B200 does not share at any price: **the Grace CPU and its coherent memory pool**. Each GB200 superchip pairs one Grace (72 Neoverse V2 cores, 480 GB LPDDR5X at roughly 0.5 TB/s) with two Blackwell GPUs over NVLink-C2C — the same 900 GB/s-class coherent link introduced on GH200, an order of magnitude beyond a PCIe Gen5 x16 host link — giving a full NVL72 rack 36 Grace sockets and roughly **17.3 TB of host LPDDR against 13.4 TB of HBM** (72 × 186 GB). The host pool is *larger than the rack's entire HBM complement*, addressable at NVLink-class bandwidth, and cache-coherent. For this run that is not a spec-sheet curiosity; it is the substrate for three load-bearing subsystems: local-first asynchronous checkpoint drain (Ch21's architecture wants a fast, capacious staging tier exactly one hop from HBM — 140 TB of optimizer-plus-weight state spread across 1,365 racks is ~100 GB per rack-share, under 1% of the rack's host pool), optimizer-state or EMA offload (the DeepSeek-V3 CPU-EMA practice, Ch9, is effectively free when the CPU is 900 GB/s away instead of across PCIe), and the dataloader's host staging path (Ch15). An HGX-B200-based design gets the same tensor cores but pays PCIe prices for every one of these host round-trips — a difference invisible in the FLOPs table above and material to the checkpoint-cadence math in Ch21. ## MFU Over Software Releases: The SemiAnalysis H100-vs-GB200 Benchmark

The single most load-bearing empirical document for this chapter is SemiAnalysis's ["H100 vs GB200 NVL72 Training Benchmarks — Power, TCO, and Reliability Analysis, Software Improvement Over Time"](https://newsletter.semianalysis.com/) (Dylan Patel & Daniel Nishball, Aug 20, 2025; paywalled), built on NVIDIA's own DGXC benchmarking-team scripts run on NVIDIA's internal H100 EOS reference cluster, using NeMo Megatron-LM — explicitly not native PyTorch/FSDP2, a methodological gap the authors themselves flag (pp. 2-3) since many end users do not run NeMo Megatron-LM in production.

The document contains **zero theoretical-peak TFLOPS figures for either chip anywhere in its 27 pages** — its entire analysis runs in measured tokens/s/GPU, %MFU (without disclosing the FLOPs-peak denominator), $/M-tokens, and J/token. Anyone pairing this article's MFU% with a peak-FLOPs figure from elsewhere must import that denominator independently; wherever one is needed alongside these numbers below, it is the corrected 5,000/2,500 TFLOPS GB200 dense FP8/BF16 figure established above.

**Capex/TCO baseline** (pp. 3-4). H100 server ≈$190k (all-in ≈$250k); GB200 NVL72 rack ≈$3.1M (all-in ≈$3.9M). Across buyer tiers, GB200's all-in capex-per-GPU runs 1.6–1.7x H100's, and combined TCO is ~1.6x — meaning GB200 needs ≥1.6x the effective throughput just to break even on perf/TCO, before reliability is factored in at all.

**Software-only MFU gains on fixed hardware are large and fast** (pp. 9-13). On a static 128×H100 cluster training GPT-3 175B (TP=4, seq 2,048, global batch 500k tokens), BF16 MFU rose 34%→54% and FP8 MFU rose 29.5%→39.5% between Jan and Dec 2024, from CUDA-stack/NCCL software improvements alone — no hardware change. This translated to FP8 pretraining cost falling from 72¢ to 54.2¢ per million tokens ($218k→$162k to replicate GPT-3's original run) and energy from 3.63 to 2.46 J/token.

**Dense-model weak scaling is close to flat, but MoE is where GB200 actually earns its premium** (pp. 15-20). Llama3-405B FP8 MFU held ~43% and BF16 ~54% across 576–2,304 H100 GPUs; Llama3-70B FP8 MFU instead *degraded* with scale (38.1%→35.5% from 64 to 2,048 GPUs) while BF16 stayed comparatively flat (54.5%→53.7%) — a reproducible signal that small-scale FP8 MFU should not be extrapolated naively to 100k-GPU regimes. DeepSeek-670B (MoE) landed at just 16.6% BF16 MFU on H100, versus 54.5% for dense Llama3-405B, attributed to massive dispatch/combine communication for token routing (comm volume scales with top-k — routing to 4 experts instead of 1 roughly quadruples traffic). On GB200, DeepSeek-670B throughput went from a barely-compelling 1.3x H100's tokens/s/GPU in May 2025 to 2.5x by July 2025 (a 1.5x perf/TCO advantage), with NVIDIA's Dec-2025 roadmap projecting 4.7x. The mechanism is structural: GB200's 72-GPU NVLink domain makes 64-GPU all-to-all collectives — the exact primitive MoE dispatch/combine needs — run **18x faster** than on H100. Llama4 Maverick 400B MoE (17B active) showed an even sharper GB200 advantage in FP8: 2.4x H100's throughput, a 50% perf/TCO win ($0.061 vs $0.093/M tokens).

This is the chapter's central mechanistic finding for a 10T/200B MoE program: GB200's economic case over H100 is overwhelmingly a *sparse-MoE, all-to-all-bound* story, not a generic compute-density story. A dense-model buyer gets a much weaker case for GB200 over plain HGX B200 — SemiAnalysis's own closing verdict on the dense case (pp. 24-26) is blunt: *"It appears the vanilla B200 is better performance TCO than GB200 NVL72 for pretraining normal sized models. As model sizes scale or reinforcement learning comes in, GB200 NVL72 will take a lead again."*

**Independently, NVIDIA's own published Megatron-Bridge performance tables** (dense-FP8-peak-normalized, using the corrected 5,000-TFLOPS GB200 denominator) show the same dense-vs-MoE split from a different angle: dense Llama models land in a tight 32–39% MFU band across H100/B200/GB200, while MoE models on the *identical* hardware land at 9–18%.

|---|---|---|---|
| Llama3.1-405B (dense) | 38.6% | 37.2% | 36.8% |
| DeepSeek-V3 (MoE, 5.5% active, EP64) | 17.1% | 13.1% | 18.0% |

The GB200 36.8% Llama3.1-405B figure cross-validates against NVIDIA's own separately-stated claim of "sustained FP8 training MFU on NVL72: 38–46% of peak" — though that specific range could not be traced to any retrievable primary source (not the SemiAnalysis PDF, not NVIDIA's own GB200 developer blog), and should be treated as directionally consistent rather than independently confirmed.

A separate, from-first-principles reconstruction of DeepSeek-V3's actual delivered MFU — using the paper's own 2.664M H800-GPU-hour pretraining figure, 14.8T tokens, 37B active parameters, and the standard 6·N·D FLOPs approximation against H800-SXM's 1,979-TFLOPS FP8 peak — gives **~17.3% MFU**, consistent with the NVIDIA-table pattern above ([DeepSeek-AI, arXiv:2412.19437](https://arxiv.org/abs/2412.19437); the reconstruction is our own arithmetic). Ch4's finer-grained reconstruction, built from DeepSeek's own disclosed 250-GFLOPs/token figure rather than the 6ND shortcut, lands at ≈19.5% — the ~13% delta is exactly the attention FLOPs that 6·N·D omits, and Ch23 reconciles the two conventions explicitly; both are the same finding at different accounting resolutions. A widely-circulating "58% MFU for DeepSeek-V3" figure does not survive this reconstruction and appears to be a misreading; it should not be treated as fact in any downstream synthesis.

## Reliability: NVL72 in the Field

GB200 NVL72's mean time between interruption (MTBI) is measurably worse than H100's — 1,000–3,000 GPU-days versus 2,000–5,000 GPU-days — compounded by a much larger blast radius per event: a switch-tray or backplane fault takes down the entire 72-GPU rack, not an 8-GPU node (SemiAnalysis, pp. 20-23). The mechanical root cause is concrete: each rack's four copper backplane cartridges connect via Paladin HD connectors rated for only **200 mating cycles** before the gold plating must be discarded, and root-causing a backplane signal-integrity fault ("XID 149") can take hours because the ambiguity spans three possible connector interfaces. The repair-time ladder is steep: compute-tray swap 1–4 hours (job continues on other trays); switch-tray swap 1–4 hours but requires a full-rack drain; backplane cartridge swap **8–24 hours**. Operators run 64-of-72 GPUs live with 8 held as hot spares, but the Slurm `--segment` flag that pins 64-of-72 provides no protection against switch-tray or backplane events, which drain the whole rack regardless — genuine resilience requires provisioning entire spare racks, not just spare GPUs within a rack.

Pricing in this reliability tax materially changes the GB200 economic case: Llama4 Maverick's headline 50%-cheaper-per-token GB200 advantage falls to 20% cheaper once the 8-of-72 hot-spare GPUs are charged as opportunity cost, or 30% cheaper if only fully-idle spare racks are charged (pp. 24-25). A separately-documented firmware saga corroborates the operational maturity gap: GB200 NVL72's early NVLink backplane cabling/sync issues, which affected AWS's, GCP's, and Meta's initial NVL36x2 deployments broadly, were traced to a firmware bug (not hardware) but took **6–7 months** to resolve — engineering-sample units shipped November 2024, with a stable firmware v1.3 not released until roughly spring/summer 2025. Top providers (CoreWeave, Oracle) have since moved to rack-level SLAs at 99% uptime rather than component/node-level SLAs, which effectively requires provisioning hot-spare racks as standard practice (per SemiAnalysis's "ClusterMAX 2.0," Nov 2025, paywalled).

For calibration on what "good" reliability looks like at scale even on mature Hopper-class hardware: Meta's 54-day, 16–24k-GPU Llama3-405B run recorded 466 job interruptions (419 hardware failures), an implied MTBF of ~2,111 H100-days — over 5x better than the sub-400-GPU-day MTBF "gold/silver"-tier neoclouds reportedly deliver on smaller clusters (same source). As of the Aug 2025 benchmark's own writing, **no large-scale frontier training run had yet completed on GB200 NVL72 at all** — a dated claim we could not confirm or refute for mid-2026, but worth carrying forward as a live open question. As of late 2025 the largest publicly-verified GB-generation MLPerf submission was ~5,120 GPUs (v5.1, Nov 2025); by MLPerf v6.0 (June 2026, discussed below) that ceiling moved to 8,192 GPUs, but even the newer figure is a ten-minute benchmark, not a months-long pretrain — a materially lower reliability bar than sustaining a 10T-parameter run across 100,000 GPUs for months.

## Power, Cooling, and the Rack Envelope

NVIDIA's own GB200 NVL72 SuperPOD reference architecture defines one Scalable Unit as 8 racks / 576 GPUs at a stated 1.2 MW TDP — implying ≈150 kW/rack on average, though the document never states this per-rack figure directly, only the SU-level total ([GB200 SuperPOD RA](https://docs.nvidia.com/dgx-superpod/reference-architecture-scalable-infrastructure-gb200/latest/), updated Nov 2025). Real deployed racks diverge from this reference by a wide margin: Meta's Catalina platform, its OCP contribution built on the same NVL72 GPU domain, draws only **93.5 kW/rack** at its highest disclosed power tier — genuinely lower than NVIDIA's own implied figure, the opposite of the "hyperscalers push power density higher" intuition, reflecting Meta's different rack composition (OCP-form-factor NICs instead of PCIe CEM cards, a doubled 1:1 Grace-CPU-to-GPU ratio that forces the 72-GPU domain across two physical racks rather than one) — per the [Hot Chips 2025 talk](https://hc2025.hotchips.org/) by Arnold & Bowman, "Meta's Catalina Platform (NVL72)," Aug 24, 2025. Cooling on the GB200 RA is stated only as "hybrid direct-liquid and air cooling" without a quantified split; Meta discloses more granularity — purpose-built Facility Liquid Cooling for liquid-native datacenters plus an "Air Assisted Liquid Cooling" retrofit path for air-cooled facilities, governed by a per-rack Rack Management Controller with a four-tier leak-detection response.

A power problem that no per-rack figure captures, and that a 100k-GPU planner must budget at the *substation* rather than the rack: synchronous training makes the entire cluster's power draw oscillate in lockstep. Every GPU alternates between compute-saturated phases (near-TDP draw) and communication/synchronization phases (a large fraction of TDP shed) on sub-second timescales, and because the parallelism keeps all 100,000 GPUs in phase, the swings sum coherently instead of averaging out. Meta's Llama 3 paper states this plainly for a mere 16k-GPU job: instantaneous power swings of **tens of megawatts** across the cluster from job start/stop and checkpoint-save phases, "stretching the limits of the power grid" ([arXiv:2407.21783](https://arxiv.org/abs/2407.21783), Jul 2024) — and a ~150 MW GB200-class cluster running the same duty cycle is a grid-event-scale oscillator by construction. The field's mitigations are now themselves part of the hardware conversation: xAI's Memphis buildout reportedly interposed Tesla Megapack battery storage specifically to buffer training-load transients off the utility interconnect (industry reporting, 2024–2025), and NVIDIA added **power smoothing** to the GB300 generation — rack-level energy storage plus firmware-governed ramp limiting that caps the cluster's grid-visible peak-to-trough excursion, framed in NVIDIA's own materials as cutting peak grid demand by up to ~30% for synchronous AI workloads. The planning consequence: the utility interconnect and the datacenter's electrical protection must be engineered for the *oscillation envelope*, not the average draw, and a GB300-based build gets a meaningful piece of that engineering on-board — one more quiet argument, alongside the 288 GB HBM, for why the procurement conversation has drifted from GB200 to GB300 (Ch22 picks up the operational side).

A genuine planning trap sits in NVIDIA's own storage-bandwidth guidance: the top-tier ("Enhanced") per-GPU storage-bandwidth figure in the GB200 RA is **≈217 GBps read per 1,000 GPUs**, roughly half the H100 RA's top-tier ("Best") **≈488 GBps per 1,000 GPUs** — NVIDIA's own reference sizing dropped materially between generations even though GB200-class checkpoints and datasets are unambiguously larger (both RAs' Table 6, updated Nov 2025; the per-1,000-GPU normalization is our own arithmetic on the two tables). This should be read as a signal that NVIDIA's published sizing assumes dense/lower-active-parameter H100-and-GB200-era workloads, not validated for a 200B-active/10T-total design point whose checkpoint payload scales with *total*, not active, parameter count (see Ch21 for the full checkpoint-math treatment).

Finally, NVIDIA's own compute-fabric reference tables have a hard, disclosed ceiling: the GB200 RA fully tables a non-blocking two-tier InfiniBand fabric only to **16 Scalable Units / 9,216 GPUs**, beyond which the document adds an undertabled third "core" tier and directs readers to "contact your NVIDIA representative" ([GB200 SuperPOD RA, Table 3](https://docs.nvidia.com/dgx-superpod/reference-architecture-scalable-infrastructure-gb200/latest/)). Every hyperscaler's 100k+-GPU fabric — OCI's 131,072-GPU Supercluster, Meta's DSF, Azure's MRC, AWS's EFA "10P10U," Google's Jupiter — is proprietary in-house engineering built past that point, not an extension of a published NVIDIA reference design (see Ch20 for the full fabric-by-fabric treatment). No single vendor-published reference architecture covers the full 100,000-GPU scale this run targets.

## Rubin Timing: Build Now or Wait

The build-now-vs-wait-for-Rubin question turns on one architectural fact that is easy to miss in FLOPs-headline comparisons: NVLink6 doubles link count relative to NVLink5, not just per-lane rate.

| NVLink 6 | 2 @ 25.0 GBps | **36** | 1,800 GBps | 3,600 GBps | Rubin |

Source: the [ml-engineering repo's network notes](https://github.com/stas00/ml-engineering) (July 2026).

Because per-lane rate stays fixed at 25 GBps/lane between NVLink5 and NVLink6, the entire generational jump comes from doubling link count — and because PCIe stays at generation 6 across both Blackwell and Rubin, the NVLink-over-PCIe advantage roughly doubles from ~7x (Hopper, Blackwell) to ~15x (Rubin). For an EP-heavy 10T/200B MoE design, this is directly relevant, not a generic bandwidth footnote: NVLink-domain all-to-all bandwidth is the dominant lever on MoE dispatch/combine MFU (per the DeepSeek-670B 18x-faster-collective finding above), and Rubin doubles the raw headroom on exactly that primitive relative to Blackwell.

But Rubin's own spec sheet is still moving as of its most recent public reveal, in directions that complicate a simple "wait for the bigger number" argument (per SemiAnalysis's "Vera Rubin – Extreme Co-Design," Feb 2026, and "ISSCC 2026 Roundup," Apr 2026, both paywalled). Rubin GPU: 35 PFLOPS dense FP4 (3.5x GB200), but BF16/TF32 tensor-core width only grows 1.6x over Blackwell — a signal that NVIDIA expects training and inference to keep migrating to FP8/FP4, not that BF16 workloads get a proportionate speedup. HBM4 target is 22 TB/s at 10.8 GT/s pin speed, but memory suppliers are reportedly struggling to hit that above-JEDEC-spec ask — Samsung's own ISSCC 2026 disclosure shows only ~50% front-end yield on the 1c DRAM node through 2025, and the informed prior is initial shipments land closer to ~20 TB/s. TDP was raised late in the design cycle from 1,800W to 2,300W (Max-P), and VR NVL72 rack TDP reaches **180–220 kW versus GB200/GB300's 120–140 kW**, with NVIDIA's roadmap explicitly targeting 1 MW/rack within a couple of years.

NVIDIA's own GTC 2026 recap (per SemiAnalysis's coverage, Mar 2026) resolves a real ambiguity about whether Rubin forces a co-packaged-optics transition on a 2026–2027 cluster design: it does not, at least not yet. Rubin NVL72 and Rubin Ultra NVL72/144/288 stay entirely copper for scale-up; only Rubin Ultra NVL576 (8 racks bridged) and the following Feynman NVL1152 generation introduce CPO, and only rack-to-rack, not within a rack — copper remains NVIDIA's medium of choice as long as physically possible, because pushing NVLink SerDes rates further is itself described as "a challenging feat" requiring unproven bidirectional echo cancellation.

The broader supply-side signal cuts against an aggressive wait-for-Rubin strategy: AI workloads (accelerator + host CPU + networking) are modeled to consume just under 60% of TSMC N3 wafer output in 2026, rising to 86% in 2027, with effective N3 utilization projected to exceed 100% of nameplate capacity in H2 2026 — every major accelerator family converges onto the same constrained N3 process, and HBM (already ~3x the wafer-per-bit cost of commodity DRAM, rising to 4x with HBM4) competes directly with server DRAM for the same fab capacity (per SemiAnalysis's "The Great AI Silicon Shortage," Mar 2026). Read together: GB200 is field-validated, if imperfectly, and its dense-FP8/BF16 peak, HBM capacity, and rack power envelope are settled numbers; Rubin's headline FP4/HBM4/NVLink6 numbers are directionally compelling — especially for a genuinely EP-heavy MoE, where the NVLink6 link-count doubling matters more than any other single Rubin spec — but its TDP, realized HBM4 bandwidth, and CPO scope were still moving as of its most recent disclosure, and it sits behind the same N3/HBM4 supply constraint squeezing every other 2026–2027 accelerator family. A program with a 2026 procurement deadline is choosing GB200/GB300 not because it is unambiguously superior on paper, but because it is the only option with settled specs and a maturing reliability track record; a program with genuine flexibility to slip into 2027 has a real, quantified reason — not just a hype-cycle reason — to wait for Rubin specifically because of the EP all-to-all doubling. See Ch18 for how this delta would reshape the parallelism math if the build timeline allows it.

## The Alternative Silicon Verdicts

**AWS Trainium2/3 (Project Rainier).** Anthropic's disclosed Trainium2 chip count roughly doubled in six months — from "nearly half a million" (Oct 2025) to a present-tense **"currently over one million Trainium2 chips"** in the [Anthropic/Amazon announcement of April 20, 2026](https://www.anthropic.com/news/anthropic-amazon-compute), which also confirms an internal Trainium4 designation already inside the AWS-Anthropic contract. Trainium2 is 96 GiB HBM3 @ 2.9 TB/s, 1,299 FP8/667 BF16 TFLOPS/chip — a 3.85x raw-FLOPs gap versus GB200 per chip, with SemiAnalysis's blunt framing (in "Amazon's AI Self Sufficiency") that "400k Trainium2 is less raw FLOPs than a 100k GB200 cluster." The 64-chip Trn2 UltraServer's 3D torus is asymmetric — the Z-axis carries only 64 GB/s versus 128 GB/s on X/Y, a topological wart invisible in spec-sheet comparisons that a TPU's symmetric ICI mesh does not share. Software maturity is the real open question, per SemiAnalysis's ["AWS Trainium3 Deep Dive"](https://newsletter.semianalysis.com/p/aws-trainium3-deep-dive-a-potential) (Dec 2025): NKI only reached Beta→Stable in April 2026 — roughly 16 months after Trainium2's late-2024 GA — with AWS's own launch blog admitting hand-tuned kernel development requires expertise "few teams can afford"; PyTorch/XLA is being fully retired the same window for a ground-up native-PyTorch/TorchNeuron rewrite; and the NeuronX Collective Communication Library's all-to-all gap — the exact primitive MoE dispatch/combine needs — has reportedly been outstanding since roughly 2022. Vendor-disclosed MFU brackets the gap directly: Trainium3 Day-0 PyTorch-native/torch.compile hits only 20–30% BF16 MFU on MoE workloads, rising to ~40% (DeepSeek-670B-class) with hand-written NKI kernels. No public Trainium2/3 MFU number exists for a DeepSeek/Kimi-scale MoE pretraining run — the same disclosure gap that recurs on every non-Nvidia platform.

**Google TPU v7 (Ironwood) / TPU 8t.** Sundar Pichai stated [on X](https://x.com/sundarpichai) (Nov 6, 2025) that Google trains and serves its own frontier models on Ironwood, but no Google source names a specific Gemini generation with citable specificity — even SemiAnalysis's own deep-dive, while confirming "Gemini 3 was trained entirely on TPUs," does not specify which generation. Google then bifurcated training and inference silicon outright five months later: TPU 8t (training, 9,600 chips/pod, 3x Ironwood's processing power) versus TPU 8i (inference, 1,152-chip pods, 3x on-chip SRAM), announced at [Cloud Next](https://blog.google/), April 22, 2026 — a fast reversal of the "one chip trains and serves" framing Ironwood used at GA five months earlier. SemiAnalysis's MFU/TCO breakeven math (["TPUv7: Google Takes a Swing at the King"](https://newsletter.semianalysis.com/p/tpuv7-google-takes-a-swing-at-the), Nov 2025) is the sharpest quantified case for TPU economics available: at Nvidia's ~30% assumed training MFU baseline, Google needs only ~15% MFU (internal) or ~19% (Anthropic's GCP-priced floor) to match GB300's cost-per-effective-FLOP, and Anthropic's realized ~40% MFU implies ~52% lower TCO per effective PFLOP once GCP's external margin is included. Apple pretrains its cloud foundation model on the latest-generation TPU while serving its largest cloud model on Nvidia GPUs hosted *inside* Google Cloud ([Apple Machine Learning Research](https://machinelearning.apple.com/), Jun 2026) — a clean illustration that training-chip and inference-chip choices diverge even within one model family. TPU is the most credible non-Nvidia *software-maturity* story here (MaxText/Pathways, a decade of Google-internal hardening), but it shares the same MLPerf-absence gap as every other non-Nvidia platform (below).

**Huawei Ascend / CloudMatrix 384.** Ascend 910C is a dual-die package, 128 GB HBM2e @ 6.4 TB/s, built on SMIC 7nm(N+2) — but the binding constraint is national-level HBM supply, not chip design: CXMT's domestic HBM output caps China at roughly 250,000–300,000 Ascend-910C-equivalent chips/year, well short of Huawei's own 600,000-unit 2026 target, as the TSMC-fabricated die bank that has cushioned production runs dry within the 2026 window (per SemiAnalysis's "Huawei Ascend Production Ramp," 2025–2026). The single most concrete named-lab data point on Ascend's frontier-*pretraining* readiness remains DeepSeek's reported R2 experience: urged to shift training to Ascend, the attempt reportedly hit unstable performance, slow interconnect, and immature CANN tooling, and DeepSeek reverted to Nvidia for training while keeping Ascend for inference only ([Tom's Hardware/TrendForce reporting](https://www.tomshardware.com/), Aug 2025). The counter-datapoint is Huawei's own team: PanGu Ultra MoE, 718B parameters trained on 6,000 Ascend NPUs, reached **30.0% MFU** after optimization (up from 18.9% at baseline), via simulation-driven architecture co-design tuned specifically to Ascend's NPU characteristics ([arXiv:2505.04519](https://arxiv.org/abs/2505.04519), May 2025). A June 2026 update shows a Huawei-led consortium completing full-parameter *post-training* of a 1.6T-parameter DeepSeek-V4-Pro on 1,000+ Ascend 910C chips across 1,500+ iterations with zero interruptions — genuine evidence of sustained large-cluster reliability — but the reporting is explicit this "does not demonstrate the chips can pre-train a frontier model from scratch," and no source discloses where V4-Pro actually pretrained. The honest 2026 verdict: post-training-scale reliability on Ascend is now credibly demonstrated by an external-to-Huawei consortium; full pretraining-from-scratch at frontier scale remains undemonstrated outside Huawei's own PanGu team.

**AMD MI355X/MI400.** MLPerf Training v6.0 (June 2026) was AMD's strongest public showing yet and the first built on Primus, its unified training framework, debuting a production MXFP4 recipe: MI355X came within ~5–6% of B200 on Llama3.1-8B pretraining (FP4) and within ~5% on Llama2-70B LoRA fine-tuning, a 3.46x generational leap over MI300X on the LoRA benchmark ([AMD/ROCm blogs](https://rocm.blogs.amd.com/), Jun 2026). AMD did **not** submit the new, frontier-relevant DeepSeek-V3-671B MoE benchmark that round. The [OpenAI-AMD deal](https://openai.com/index/openai-amd-strategic-partnership/) (Oct 2025; 6 GW total, first 1 GW of MI450 from H2 2026) never disambiguates training from inference, and given MI450 deployment doesn't begin until H2 2026, any AMD-trained frontier model is at best mid-training as of mid-2026 — no publicly disclosed frontier-scale pretraining run exists on AMD silicon as of July 2026.

## Is 100k GB200 the Right Assumption?

The sharpest available answer is a benchmark absence, not a spec-sheet comparison. When MLPerf Training v6.0 (June 16, 2026) added the industry's first third-party-audited, standardized large-MoE training benchmark — 671B parameters, DeepSeek-V3-shaped — **Nvidia was the only platform to submit a result**, in its own words "the only platform to submit on every test" ([MLCommons](https://mlcommons.org/)). No AMD, Google/TPU, or AWS/Trainium entry exists for this benchmark, even though AMD submitted to other v6.0 categories that same round and Google is nominally a v6.0 participant. CoreWeave's winning submission trained DeepSeek-V3 671B to target quality on 8,192 GB300 NVL72 GPUs in 2.02 minutes, with per-GPU throughput rising from 1,298 to 1,648 TFLOPS/GPU across NeMo container releases 25.11 → 26.06 over roughly three months (DeepSeek-V3 was new in the v6.0 round, so this is container-to-container progress, not an MLPerf round-over-round comparison) — pure software maturation, no hardware change, and a concrete illustration of how much MFU headroom a mature Nvidia software stack still finds generation-over-generation. Every other non-Nvidia performance number in this chapter — Trainium's disclosed FLOPS, TPU's SemiAnalysis-estimated MFU, Ascend's PanGu MFU — is vendor-reported or single-lab, not independently reproduced under MLCommons audit rules.

This does not mean the alternatives do not work: Anthropic's >1M-Trainium2-chip and Google's Gemini-on-TPU deployments are real, at-scale, production counter-evidence, and PanGu Ultra MoE is a genuine, if narrow, existence proof for Ascend. But as of July 2026, **Nvidia is the only platform with a third-party-audited large-MoE training result at all** — a materially sharper and more falsifiable claim than the generic "GPUs still dominate" framing. So the answer to "is 100k GB200 the right assumption" is conditional: yes, as the only platform with an audited data point at this workload class, with three qualifications. First, by the time an actual procurement lands, the relevant conversation has already substantially shifted from GB200 to GB300 — GB300 is 1.6x GB200's speed at identical scale on the MLPerf DeepSeek-V3 benchmark, and most 2026-vintage TCO modeling (Ch23 included) already uses GB300 as its reference SKU. Second, GB200's own reliability envelope was still described as immature as recently as August 2025, and no source confirms whether a genuinely months-long, 100,000-GPU-scale frontier pretraining run has yet survived on GB200/GB300-class hardware as of mid-2026. Third, Trainium's scale trajectory (roughly doubling every six months) is real enough that a program with genuine multi-vendor flexibility should not treat single-sourcing Nvidia as risk-free — it is simply the option with an audited number behind it.

## Implications for the 10T/200B Run

- **Use the corrected dense-FP8 peak.** 5,000 TFLOPS/GPU (GB200 NVL72), not the frequently-conflated 4,500-TFLOPS HGX B200 figure, as the denominator for every downstream MFU and critical-batch calculation; see Ch18 for the corrected thresholds.
- **Plan MFU around the MoE band, not the dense band.** Vendor-published, apples-to-apples data (NVIDIA's own Megatron-Bridge tables) puts MoE MFU at 9–18% versus 32–39% for dense, on identical hardware — and a genuinely 2%-active 10T/200B design is sparser than any measured public data point (DeepSeek-V3 at 5.5% active gets 18%), so budget toward the low end, not the middle, absent a specific reason to expect better.
- **Price the reliability tax into TCO, not just into contingency.** GB200's headline perf/TCO advantage over H100 for MoE workloads (up to 50% cheaper) compresses to 20–30% once hot-spare-rack opportunity cost is charged; budget rack-level, not node-level, SLAs and provision accordingly (see Ch21).
- **Treat NVLink6/Rubin as a genuine, quantified alternative if the timeline allows it** — the link-count doubling (18→36 links) is specifically valuable for an EP-heavy MoE's all-to-all traffic, not a generic speed bump — but recognize that Rubin's TDP, realized HBM4 bandwidth, and CPO scope were all still moving targets as of its most recent public disclosure, and that the same N3/HBM4 supply constraint squeezing GB200/GB300 procurement squeezes Rubin too.
- **Do not budget on an audited non-Nvidia alternative as a hedge.** As of MLPerf Training v6.0 (June 2026), no platform besides Nvidia has submitted an independently-audited large-MoE training result; every alternative-silicon number in a vendor's own favor should be read as the vendor's best case, not a validated floor.
