# Chapter 9 — Optimizers and Stability at 10T Parameters

The optimizer line in a training-run design document is usually the shortest, least-argued-over entry in the whole spec — until the run is one-shot, costs several billion dollars, and the largest public validation of either candidate optimizer sits at a fraction of the target active-parameter count. For a 10T-total/200B-active MoE, the optimizer choice is a bet made under genuine extrapolation, and 2025-2026 supplied the first real evidence base for making it rather than guessing. This chapter lays out that evidence: Muon's mechanics and trillion-parameter track record, the distributed-systems engineering required to run Newton-Schulz at 100,000-GPU scale, the adversarial re-benchmarks that punctured and then partially restored Muon's headline speedup claims, the strict validation ceiling no optimizer has cleared above roughly 50B active MoE parameters, and the stability toolkit — QK-Norm, QK-Clip, spike taxonomies, automated rollback — any choice will need regardless.

## Muon Mechanics and the Trillion-Parameter Evidence Base

Muon (MomentUm Orthogonalized by Newton-Schulz), due to Keller Jordan (2024), takes an SGD-momentum update matrix and replaces it with its nearest semi-orthogonal matrix via a quintic Newton-Schulz (NS) iteration rather than an exact SVD (kellerjordan.github.io/posts/muon, verified July 2026). Five fixed-coefficient NS steps (`a=3.4445, b=-4.7750, c=2.0315`) drive singular values toward 1, run stably in bf16, and apply independently per 2D parameter matrix — never to embeddings, the LM head, or scalar/vector parameters, which stay on AdamW in every production implementation on record (arXiv:2502.16982; arXiv:2507.20534). FLOP overhead scales as `Θ(T·m/B)`, measured at roughly 0.7% at NanoGPT-speedrun scale and estimated at ~0.5% at Llama-405B scale (same source).

Su Jianlin's kexue.fm — effectively Moonshot AI's public research diary — supplies the cleanest first-principles account: an ideal optimizer's update should minimize `Tr(GᵀΔW)` subject to a stability bound `ρ(ΔW)≤η` under some norm; SGD is the Frobenius-norm solution, Muon the spectral-norm solution, a strictly tighter bound on output perturbation (kexue.fm/archives/10739, Feb 2025). Su also proves the special case β=0 of 2018's Shampoo is exactly `msign(G)` — Muon and Shampoo coincide at that limit (kexue.fm/archives/10592, Dec 2024).

**Moonlight** (Moonshot; arXiv:2502.16982, Feb 2025) is the load-bearing scaling-law evidence: fitted curves give **Muon: 2.506·C^(-0.052) vs AdamW: 2.608·C^(-0.054)**, translating to the claim that Muon needs only **~52% of AdamW's training FLOPs** to match loss at compute-optimal allocation. One caution before reusing that number: the two fitted curves as quoted do not reproduce the ~52% figure — solving them for matched loss gives a compute ratio that drifts with C and lands far from a 2x saving — so either the coefficients or the headline has been garbled in transmission. Treat ~52% as the paper's own headline claim, and re-extract the coefficients from the paper before putting them in any budget model. Two changes were required to scale raw Muon past small models: weight decay (orthogonalized updates carry no natural shrinkage the way AdamW's adaptive denominator does), and a per-parameter update-scale correction, since raw NS output has RMS `≈1/√max(A,B)` for an `[A,B]` matrix — Moonlight rescales each update by `0.2·√max(A,B)` to match AdamW's typical update RMS, letting teams reuse existing LR schedules without per-layer-shape re-tuning. Su flags at least four distinct Muon scaling-factor conventions circulating in the wild, and warns that conflating them — or inverting `d_in`/`d_out` between PyTorch's and Keras's conventions, a bug he documents as present in Keras 3.12's built-in Muon — is a common, silent source of bad results; with every convention but Moonlight's, peak LR needs roughly a 10x multiplier over Adam's (kexue.fm/archives/11416, Nov 2025). Moonlight itself: 2.24B active/15.29B total MoE, 5.7T tokens, weight decay 0.1, momentum 0.95. Muon-trained weights show measurably higher singular-value entropy than Adam-trained weights — the same signal Su later uses to explain Muon's MaxLogit-explosion propensity, though he has since begun publicly questioning whether higher entropy is actually good (kexue.fm/archives/11767, May 2026).

**Kimi K2** (1.04T total/32.6B active, MLA attention, 15.5T tokens) is the largest confirmed pure-Muon pretraining run and the origin of MuonClip (arXiv:2507.20534, July 2025). During a 9B-active ablation, max pre-softmax attention logits "rapidly exceed 1000" under vanilla Muon, "more frequently with Muon but less with AdamW." Soft-capping bounds only the *effect* of logit growth, and QK-Norm is "incompatible with MLA's non-materialized keys," since MLA's compressed KV representation isn't directly normalizable at inference time. **QK-Clip** operates in weight space, post-hoc: after each Muon step, if a per-head max softmax input `S_max^h` exceeds τ=100, rescale that head's Q/K weights by `γ_h = min(1, τ/S_max^h)` — `√γ_h` to the head-specific MLA components, `γ_h` to the rotary component, with the shared rotary component untouched to avoid cross-head interference. Su's account, published ten days before the K2 report, adds telemetry the paper doesn't: across ~220,000 training steps, the first head crossed τ around step 7,000, and a roughly 70,000-step "tug of war" between Muon (pushing MaxLogit up) and QK-Clip (pulling it down) preceded all heads settling below τ, after which QK-Clip essentially stopped firing. In Su's reading, that is evidence weight decay alone would eventually have solved the problem — but QK-Clip is what lets the model survive early training (kexue.fm/archives/11126, July 2025). He credits DeepSeek's Auxiliary-Loss-Free MoE balancing as QK-Clip's conceptual ancestor, and flags that per-head clipping requires reassembling logically-per-head slices of a sharded weight matrix — "not hard on top of Muon, noticeably more complex on top of Adam-style per-shard state." K2's Figure 3 shows the full per-step loss curve for the entire 15.5T-token run with no visible spikes — one of three "zero loss spikes" data points now on record (with DeepSeek-V3/AdamW and, implicitly, DeepSeek-V4/Muon), a sharp break from PaLM-era norms of roughly twenty manually-handled spikes per run (arXiv:2204.02311).

**DeepSeek-V4** is the single most consequential — and least publicized relative to K2 — data point in this chapter. DeepSeek-V3 used plain AdamW (β1=0.9, β2=0.95, weight decay 0.1) and reported zero loss spikes and zero rollbacks across its full 14.8T-token run (arXiv:2412.19437, Dec 2024). **DeepSeek-V4 switched to Muon** — "we employ the Muon optimizer for the majority of modules in DeepSeek-V4 series due to its faster convergence and improved training stability" — reusing prior AdamW hyperparameters after the RMS-matching rescale, and running a hybrid Newton-Schulz scheme of 8 fast iterations plus 2 stabilizing iterations rather than the standard fixed-coefficient 5 (arXiv:2606.19348, June 2026). V4-Pro (1.6T total/49B active) and V4-Flash (284B total/13B active) trained on 32-33T tokens, more than double V3's budget. Where K2 solves attention-logit explosion with a post-hoc weight-space clip, V4 solves it architecturally, applying RMSNorm directly to attention queries and KV entries — no QK-Clip needed. This suggests QK-Clip may be a workaround for MLA's incompatibility with standard QK-Norm rather than a generically necessary Muon companion — an inference from DeepSeek's architectural choice, not a confirmed causal finding by either lab, since neither has published the counterfactual (QK-Clip retrofitted onto a non-MLA model, or direct RMSNorm-on-QK retrofitted onto MLA) that would isolate the variable. The right stability mechanism depends on attention architecture (Ch7), not optimizer alone. Two other named V4 techniques, "Anticipatory Routing" and "SwiGLU Clamping," lack enough surviving detail to describe precisely; treat both as open questions. On distributed engineering, V4 bin-packs dense-parameter matrices to ZeRO ranks and **optimizes each expert independently**, quantizing MoE gradients to bf16 during synchronization — detail Ch4 covers in full. No spike count is disclosed for V4; given DeepSeek's stability discipline, the silence is more plausibly "still very few" than an admission — but that is an inference, not a fact.

**GLM-4.5/4.6/4.7** (355B total/32B active, Muon + QK-Norm) supply the most complete published Muon hyperparameter recipe of any lab: NS iterations N=5, momentum μ=0.95, update RMS scaled to 0.2, Muon on all parameters except embeddings/bias/RMSNorm, weight decay 0.1, no dropout (arXiv:2508.06471 §2.4, Aug 2025). Su confirms this is the Moonlight-Muon variant specifically — cross-lab propagation of Moonshot's implementation into Zhipu's stack via blog post and open code (kexue.fm/archives/11416, Nov 2025). GLM-4.5 also found WSD-scheduled models underfit relative to cosine decay on SimpleQA/MMLU and chose cosine deliberately. "Muon: Training and Trade-offs with Latent Attention and MoE" independently confirms the same three-way split found in Moonlight, K2, and DeepSeek-V4 — router on Adam, experts on Muon, embeddings/norms/LM head on Adam, embarrassingly parallel across experts since NS has no cross-expert coupling (arXiv:2509.24406, Sep 2025). This is now close to a converged default for any lab adopting Muon.

## Distributed Newton-Schulz at 10T Parameters

Running NS orthogonalization across a tensor-parallel-sharded momentum buffer at 100,000-GPU scale is a solved-but-nontrivial engineering problem, not an open research question, as of mid-2026. NVIDIA's Megatron Core "Emerging-Optimizers" library ships three sharding modes: **duplicated** (all-gather momentum across the TP domain once, every GPU runs full NS locally — one comm op, redundant compute, latency-optimized), **distributed** (NS itself distributed across TP ranks, an all-reduce after each iteration's first matmul), and **blockwise** (no cross-GPU communication; each GPU orthogonalizes only its local block, approximate) (developer.nvidia.com, 2026). A companion **layer-wise distributed optimizer** assigns whole layers, not individual elements, to DP ranks, so each GPU holds complete matrices for its layers and NS runs without cross-rank gather. Measured throughput on GB300 NVL72 is close to a wash against AdamW and, by one accounting convention, favorable:

| Config | AdamW (TFLOPs/s) | Muon (TFLOPs/s) | Muon overhead |
|---|---|---|---|
| Kimi-K2-shaped | 1051 | 1080 | 51 TFLOPs/s (~4.7% of total; ~2.8% net *throughput gain* when NS matmul FLOPs are counted toward MFU) |
| Qwen3-30B-A3B-shaped | 713 | 721 | 35 TFLOPs/s (~4.9% of total; ~1.1% net gain) |

The "MFU is higher with Muon" framing is a methodological subtlety: part of the reported gain is an artifact of counting NS's own matmuls as useful FLOPs, though raw throughput is still favorable either way. `torch.optim.Muon` shipped in PyTorch 2.9 core but does not itself support distributed training — the Megatron sharding layer above is a necessary addition, exposing a `match_rms_adamw` flag implementing Moonshot's RMS-rescaling trick directly (PyTorch docs; GitHub issue #148819). Megatron-LM's roadmap targets layer-wise distributed Muon with `torch_dist` checkpoint format in Q1 2026 and Muon+Megatron-FSDP integration in Q2 2026 (github.com/NVIDIA/Megatron-LM issues #4003/#4997).

The gap that matters most for a 10T/200B design: **no explicit MoE-expert-parallel-interaction detail surfaces anywhere in the available NVIDIA documentation**, despite EP-heavy configurations appearing in the library's own experimental setup. The Qwen3-30B-A3B-shaped measurement is an MoE model showing overhead proportionally similar to the dense case — reassuring evidence per-expert NS doesn't blow up disproportionately with expert count — but not a measurement anywhere close to 200B-active, high-expert-count.

**Dion** (Microsoft; arXiv:2504.05295, April 2025) is the leading alternative distributed design, replacing NS with amortized power iteration on a low-rank approximation of the momentum buffer via decoupled momentum buffers, so orthonormalization never needs a fully-materialized gradient matrix on any device — compatible with simultaneous DDP+FSDP+TP. Validated only to 3B parameters, with benefits reported to improve with scale, but no adoption in any named frontier model as of mid-2026. A research proposal, not a production choice.

One memory angle for the HBM budget: **8-bit blockwise-quantized Muon optimizer states** report up to 62% memory reduction with zero measured accuracy loss to 2.7B parameters, since Muon's momentum-only state is uniquely compatible with simple linear quantization (arXiv:2509.23106, Sep 2025) — a single-paper result with no cross-lab or larger-scale replication, so treat it as a provisional lever pending independent confirmation; still under-discussed, and relevant where 200B-active optimizer-state memory is itself a serious HBM line item. The raw arithmetic deserves stating, because it is one of Muon's least-advertised advantages at this scale: a 10T-total-parameter model carries 80 TB of AdamW optimizer state at FP32 (first and second moments) against 40 TB for Muon's momentum alone. ZeRO sharding makes either fit in aggregate HBM easily at 100k GPUs — but the halved state is a direct halving of the optimizer-state portion of every checkpoint write, restore, and cross-region replication this run will perform tens of thousands of times (Ch21's checkpoint-cadence math inherits this line item directly).

A first-principles note on *why* the Muon-for-experts pattern keeps winning, beyond the benchmark record — our own synthesis, not a sourced claim. A routed expert's weight matrix sees only ~k/n of the batch's tokens per step, so its gradient — and accumulated momentum — is both noisier and effectively lower-rank than a dense layer's, concentrated in whatever few directions this step's routed tokens excited. Adam's per-coordinate normalization responds to that concentration by amplifying exactly the excited coordinates; orthogonalization instead equalizes update energy across all singular directions, acting as a shape-aware trust region that stops any single routing batch from dominating an expert's trajectory. This is the same diagnosis that Ling's fitted laws express as "sparse per-expert gradients prefer larger batches and lower LR," and that Hunyuan-Large patches diagonally with its 0.31x expert-specific LR — three independent responses (bigger batch, smaller LR, orthogonalized update) to the one underlying fact that per-expert gradient statistics are starved relative to dense layers. It also predicts where Muon-for-MoE should be stress-tested hardest: the highest-granularity, lowest-tokens-per-expert-per-step corner of the design space — precisely the fine-grained-scaling regime where arXiv:2605.14200 reports muP-for-MoE transfer becoming unreliable.

## The Adversarial Re-Benchmarks and the Critical-Batch-Size Case

The "Muon is 2x AdamW" headline that circulated through 2025 does not survive careful re-benchmarking intact. "Fantastic Pretraining Optimizers and Where to Find Them" retested 11 optimizers with fair per-optimizer tuning and found matrix-based optimizers' advantage over AdamW shrinks to roughly **1.1x at 1.2B parameters**, with claimed 2x speedups in prior papers traced to under-tuned AdamW baselines (arXiv:2509.02046, Sep 2025). A NeurIPS 2025 follow-up partially reverses that: with proper muP-based hyperparameter transfer, Muon and Shampoo sustain **1.4x and 1.3x compute-equivalent speedups** over AdamW up to 1.4B parameters (arXiv:2512.05620, Dec 2025), and a June 2026 "Hyperball" follow-up pushes Muon's advantage to **20-30% and growing with scale** (arXiv:2606.16899). The lesson: any single-paper speedup number is provisional until it survives adversarial re-tuning, and the most recent evidence tilts back toward a real, scale-persistent Muon edge — but "persistent to 1.4B parameters" is still five orders of magnitude below a 200B-active target.

Essential AI's "Practical Efficiency of Muon" supplies the claim most relevant to a 100,000-GPU plan, since it's about batch size rather than parameter count: **Muon needs 10-15% fewer tokens than AdamW to reach the same loss at batch sizes up to 16M tokens, and the relative advantage is non-decreasing — often growing — as batch size increases past the point where AdamW's own data efficiency starts degrading** (the critical-batch-size regime) (arXiv:2505.02222, May 2025). At tested scales (100M-4B parameters, batch 128K-16M tokens, Gemma3 architecture), the absolute AdamW token deficit reaches ~2B extra tokens — the strongest evidence that Muon's data-efficiency edge doesn't erode at exactly the extreme-batch regime a 100,000-GPU plan is forced into. The paper's **muP telescoping protocol** (doubling calibration width while shrinking the search grid by `4^(-1/k)` per doubling, tuning overhead `O(C·log N)`) reports transfer holding to 3.7B for both peak LR and weight decay — a claim contested at MoE-specific scale below. Their recipe: Muon on all matrix layers, AdamW for embeddings/norms, tune only peak LR and weight decay.

The `modded-nanogpt` speedrun repository — cloned and inspected directly — is a load-bearing, dated primary source for which optimizer micro-techniques survive contact with a competitive, third-party-timed benchmark (Prime Intellect times every record; FineWeb; 8×H100) (github.com/KellerJordan/modded-nanogpt, verified July 13, 2026). Muon has been present in every one of 84 records since #3 (Oct 4, 2024), and the record has fallen from 45 minutes to 1.32 minutes: Polar Express replaced Newton-Schulz at #38 (Sep 29, 2025); NorMuon was adopted at #41 (Oct 24, 2025), 18 days after its arXiv posting; per-head-pair Q/K orthogonalization landed at #80 (April 8, 2026). The README's own point — any contributor was free to reset the baseline to well-tuned AdamW across "12 records set by 7 different researchers" and didn't — is revealed preference under adversarial, third-party-audited conditions. NorMuon adds per-neuron second-order-statistics normalization on top of orthogonalization, reporting 21.74% fewer steps than AdamW at 1.1B parameters and roughly half of AdamW's memory footprint (arXiv:2510.05491, Oct 2025) — unvalidated, like everything here, above the 1-2B class.

## AdamW: The Incumbent's Actual 2025-2026 Track Record

AdamW remains the only optimizer with a disclosed, completed validation above 400B parameters, and it is worth tabulating what frontier labs actually shipped with rather than assuming the field has moved on:

| DeepSeek-V3 | 671B total/37B active | β1=0.9, β2=0.95, wd=0.1, grad-clip 1.0, LR warmup 2000 steps→2.2e-4, constant to 10T tokens then cosine decay; 14.8T tokens; zero spikes, zero rollbacks | arXiv:2412.19437, Dec 2024 |

Adafactor (Shazeer & Stern, 2018) was Google's standard for T5 and the unfactored PaLM variant, but no public evidence has surfaced of Adafactor use in any Google frontier model since — Gemini's optimizer remains undisclosed. A recurring secondary-literature claim that "higher-order preconditioning has been applied in large-scale training runs, such as Gemini-1.5 Flash" traces back to citations of Google's own Distributed Shampoo research, not to a primary source confirming Shampoo actually trained Gemini; unconfirmed as of July 2026. The qualitative pattern across every AdamW-disclosing lab is close to universal — β1=0.9, β2=0.95, weight decay 0.1, gradient clip 1.0 — varying mainly in peak LR, warmup length, and batch-ramp schedule. The 2025-2026 shift is Muon displacing AdamW specifically for matrix parameters; no lab has gone "all-Muon," and AdamW remains the fallback for embeddings, norms, router, and head in every Muon-using system on record, including DeepSeek-V4.

## Shampoo, SOAP, and the Preconditioning Road Not (Yet) Taken

MLCommons AlgoPerf 2024 remains the strongest controlled evidence that non-diagonal preconditioning beats Adam on wall-clock, not just step-count: **Distributed Shampoo (Meta) won the external-tuning ruleset, 28% faster wall-clock than the baseline**, while Schedule-Free AdamW won the self-tuning ruleset (mlcommons.org, Aug 2024). Despite this, no frontier lab has publicly confirmed using Distributed Shampoo, SOAP, or PSGD for a >10B-parameter production run; Meta's actively-developed `optimizers/distributed_shampoo` repository signals continued internal interest (github.com/facebookresearch/optimizers) — signal, not deployment. MARS/MARS-M and Lion likewise show no frontier-scale validation, Lion specifically degrading with scale in the adversarial benchmark above and surviving only as a niche in vision-language contrastive pretraining (arXiv:2411.10438; arXiv:2510.21800; arXiv:2509.02046). Schedule-free approaches (ScheduleFree+; arXiv:2605.19095, June 2026) claim a 31% improvement over WSD at 1000 tokens/param but have no confirmed frontier adoption. On the theory frontier, Thinking Machines' "Modular Manifolds" (Bernstein, Sep 2025) constrains weight matrices to the Stiefel manifold and derives "Manifold Muon" via dual ascent plus `msign` retraction, with a composability calculus for budgeting per-layer LR — a rival to muP, validated only on toy models (thinkingmachines.ai/blog/modular-manifolds). Scion (arXiv:2502.07529) generalizes the family to arbitrary norm-ball linear-minimization oracles, reporting ~30% fewer ViT/ImageNet epochs with momentum interpolating directly into weight decay — again with no MoE-scale validation. None of this is production-ready for a 10T run; worth tracking, not betting on.

## The Strict Validation Ceiling

Being strict about "publicly validated" (named model, disclosed optimizer, disclosed scale, completed run) and strict about *active*, not total, parameters, the record as of mid-2026 is stark:

| Optimizer | Largest confirmed active-param validation | Model | Active params | Source |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| AdamW (dense) | 405B | Llama 3.1 405B | 405B | arXiv:2407.21783 |
| Adafactor (dense) | 540B | PaLM | 540B | arXiv:2204.02311 |
| AdamW (MoE) | 52B | Hunyuan-Large | 52B | arXiv:2411.02265 (DeepSeek-V3, 37B active, is the better-documented run) |
| Muon/MuonClip (MoE) | 49B | DeepSeek-V4-Pro | 49B | arXiv:2606.19348 |
| Muon (MoE) | 32.6B | Kimi K2 | 32.6B | arXiv:2507.20534 |
| Muon+QK-Norm (MoE) | ~32B | GLM-4.6/4.7 | 32B | arXiv:2508.06471 |
| Distributed Shampoo | benchmark-only | AlgoPerf submissions | ≤~1B class | mlcommons.org |
| Muon/Shampoo (HP-transfer-corrected) | 1.4B | Llama-arch research runs | 1.4B | arXiv:2512.05620 |

No optimizer choice has a public, disclosed, completed-training validation above ~50B active parameters for an MoE, or above ~540B for a dense model. A 200B-active design sits **4x past the largest validated MoE active-parameter count for Muon**. GPT-4 (rumored ~280B active of ~1.76T total, widely repeated 2023-2026 but never confirmed) and Llama 4 Behemoth (288B active target, never shipped) are the closest "almost-precedents," and neither has a disclosed optimizer. Comparisons citing *total* parameters — where 1-2T totals are now unremarkable — systematically launder over this active-parameter gap.

Three days past the snapshot, the ceiling likely moved: Kimi K3's launch materials name "Per-Head Muon" in its training stack at 2.8T total parameters (launch coverage, July 2026, pending the technical report). What "Per-Head Muon" is has not been disclosed. The plausible reading — orthogonalizing attention projections at per-head granularity rather than per-matrix, promoting MuonClip's per-head bookkeeping into the update rule itself — would make it the production echo of the per-head-pair Q/K orthogonalization the modded-nanogpt speedrun adopted at record #80 three months earlier, but that identification is a guess until the report lands. If K3's active-parameter count lands anywhere near the ~50B-class its 16-of-896 topology suggests, the Muon-MoE validation ceiling above shifts from 49B to K3's figure. And Yang Zhilin's GTC 2026 framing — Muon(Clip) as Moonshot's explicit replacement for the Adam pillar, with the ~2x-over-AdamW efficiency claim repeated on NVIDIA's main stage — should still be priced at this chapter's re-benchmarked 1.1-1.4x, not the stage number (Ch3 covers the talk; the re-benchmark section above covers why).

## The 2026 Stability Toolkit

The field has converged on "normalize Q/K somehow" as the dominant attention-stability strategy, and abandoned pure logit soft-capping:

| Technique | Status | Who uses it | Source |
|---|---|---|---|
| QK-Norm (RMSNorm on Q/K pre-softmax) | Dominant, replacing soft-cap | OLMo2, Gemma3, GLM-4.5/4.6/4.7, DeepSeek-V4 (direct-RMSNorm variant) | arXiv:2501.00656; arXiv:2503.19786 |
| QK-Clip (weight-space post-hoc rescale) | Kimi-specific, needed because MLA breaks QK-Norm | Kimi K2/K2.5 | arXiv:2507.20534 |
| Logit soft-capping | Deprecated — Gemma 3 explicitly dropped it | Gemma 2 (legacy only) | arXiv:2503.19786 |
| Z-loss | Standard secondary stabilizer, keeps output logits bounded | OLMo2, widely cited as general practice | arXiv:2501.00656 |
| Embedding-grad-shrink / WeSaR reparameterization | Active area | GLM (embedding-grad-shrink); WeSaR is research-stage | arXiv:2410.05052 |
| AdaGC (adaptive per-tensor clipping) | Research-validated to 10B-A1.4B, not yet confirmed frontier-adopted | Tested: Llama2-7B, Mixtral 8x1B, ERNIE 10B-A1.4B | arXiv:2502.11034 |

A numerics-level, architecture-independent complement: Su Jianlin reviews evidence for a BF16 round-to-even failure — a "two big, one tiny, same-sign" accumulation pattern structurally common in softmax-attention output, producing a systematic positive rounding bias that compounds over training (kexue.fm/archives/11371, Oct 2025, reviewing an external paper). The mitigation widens the softmax denominator's subtracted max (`S − β·S_max`, β≥2) so residual terms underflow cleanly; Su flags it as complementary to, not a substitute for, QK-Clip/QK-Norm.

Spike response has moved from PaLM's fully manual playbook — roughly 20 spikes on the 540B run, each handled by rolling back ~100 steps and skipping the triggering batch (arXiv:2204.02311, 2022) — to automated, tiered systems distinguishing narrow, self-recovering spikes from wide, persistent ones, escalating only the latter to full rollback. AdaGC operationalizes this with per-tensor adaptive thresholds from an EMA of each tensor's clipped-gradient-norm history, reducing spike incidence to zero and improving downstream accuracy 1.3-2.5% over global clipping (arXiv:2502.11034, Feb 2025). Root-cause attribution is now a named discipline: "Flare" categorizes divergence into data, numerics, and optimizer-state buckets (arXiv:2502.05413, Feb 2025), and OLMo2 traced a concrete data-side cause — long repeated n-gram sequences — to an n-gram-repeat filter added to the pipeline (arXiv:2501.00656, Jan 2025). Not every spike is an optimizer problem.

Stas Bekman's `ml-engineering` supplies a complementary, field-tested taxonomy: seven named spike patterns, the most dangerous of which is a **silent repeat-data false-plateau**. A PyTorch-Lightning DataSampler resume bug caused exact data repetition across stitched training segments, producing what looked like loss *improvement* — caught only by manually re-stitching wandb runs to find the "progress" was pure memorization (`training/instabilities/training-loss-patterns.md`, github.com/stas00/ml-engineering, verified July 2026). It is the one pattern with no visible anomaly, likeliest to survive undetected through the dozens-to-hundreds of resumes a 10T-MoE run will require; the only defense is deliberately testing early resumes for repeat data. The same source's STD-init recommendation — `sqrt(1/(3·NHIDDEN))` over Megatron's naive `0.02` default — is credited as one reason BLOOM-176B succeeded where a pre-BLOOM 104B fp16 attempt with the default init never got past a few thousand steps. And a study of Adam divergence up to 546B parameters found the fix for time-correlated divergence is **setting epsilon to 0**, not raising it (arXiv:2304.09871, cited in ml-engineering).

Worth stating plainly: **full-run EMA of weights maintained continuously throughout pretraining is rarer than the literature implies, but not absent** — DeepSeek-V3 discloses a continuously-maintained CPU-side EMA of model weights across the full run, which is exactly the counterexample (arXiv:2412.19437). What most labs use instead is post-hoc averaging of only the last few checkpoints — a final-model-quality technique, not a continuous-training-time stability tool — and the V3 precedent shows the continuous variant costs only host memory and asynchronous copies, not step time.

## WSD vs Cosine, muP-for-MoE Transfer, and Per-Block LR

The field has not converged on a single LR-schedule shape, and the disagreement is recent and live. DeepSeek-V3 holds LR constant after a 2000-step warmup until 10T of its 14.8T tokens, then cosine-decays the remainder; Kimi K2 does the identical shape (constant 2e-4 for 10T of 15.5T tokens, then cosine decay to 2e-5). Both label this inconsistently — K2 calls it "WSD," V3 doesn't name it — but functionally both are WSD variants with a cosine-shaped decay phase. Nemotron-3 uses an explicit WSD schedule. The practical payoff, per the broader WSD literature, is that the token budget doesn't need to be committed at step zero — teams can extend the stable phase and branch checkpoints for different downstream budgets (Ch14). Llama 3.1 405B instead uses pure cosine from step 1 (peak 8e-5, 8000-step warmup, decay to 8e-7 over 1.2M steps) — the schools coexist, WSD-style concentrated in 2025-2026 Chinese-lab reports, pure cosine still present in 2024-era Meta practice. **GLM-4.5 directly contradicts the WSD trend**: its team found WSD-scheduled models underfit relative to cosine decay on SimpleQA/MMLU — "indicating underfitting" — and chose cosine deliberately (arXiv:2508.06471 §2.4). Ant's Ling-1T uses a third mechanism, "Warmup-Stable-Merge," avoiding LR decay entirely via checkpoint merging (arXiv:2510.22115 §2.2). Three frontier labs, three answers, inside a twelve-month window — though part of the disagreement may be semantic rather than purely empirical: GLM's ablation compared its own named "WSD" schedule against cosine, not literally against K2's or V3's constant-then-cosine shape, and since K2/V3 are WSD-shaped only in function (both decline to use the label), it is not established that GLM's underfitting result would reproduce against the specific schedule K2 and V3 actually ran. No settled consensus for a 10T design to inherit uncritically either way.

muP-for-MoE transfer — does a learning rate fitted at small expert count and width hold as both scale up? — is genuinely contested as of mid-2026. In favor: dedicated derivations (arXiv:2508.09752) provide theoretical feature-learning guarantees across width, and a June 2026 follow-up (arXiv:2605.14200) reports transfer holding *unless* fine-grained granular expert scaling is introduced — it works scaling expert width, becomes unreliable scaling expert count, with optimal LR shifting slightly downward as count increases even where transfer nominally holds. Essential AI separately reports transfer holding to 3.7B for both LR and weight decay (arXiv:2505.02222). Against it: a Qwen-affiliated paper tested muTransfer against a fitted power-law `LR(N,D) = 38.4588·N^(-0.2219)·D^(-0.3509)` on a Qwen3-MoE (12B total/1.3B active) and found the fitted law beat muTransfer by 1.28%/2.23% on MMLU/CMMLU after 500B tokens — and found **no significant gain from fine-grained per-module LR tuning** once QK-Norm is present (arXiv:2601.05049, Jan 2026). Both of the most scale-relevant papers report real deviations from clean transfer in the large-expert-count regime a 10T/200B design occupies — treat muP-for-MoE transfer as directionally useful for narrowing a search space, not a scaling-law substitute. On per-block LR, the field has converged toward *not* needing separate LR beyond a two-group split (Adam-group: embeddings/norms/router/head; Muon-group: everything else) — Essential AI and the Qwen paper agree despite disagreeing on almost everything else. Hunyuan-Large's expert-specific LR scaling (shared expert at the standard rate, specialized experts at ≈0.31x, justified by unequal per-iteration token load) is the one documented counterexample (arXiv:2411.02265 §2.4).

## Batch Ramping

Batch-size ramping over the first several hundred billion to a few trillion tokens is universal: DeepSeek-V3 ramps 3072→15360 sequences over the first 469B tokens; Llama 3.1 405B doubles 4M→8M→16M tokens at 252M and 2.87T tokens; GLM-4.5 ramps 16M→64M tokens over the first 500B tokens. BLOOM-176B's ramp — GBS=16 at only 8 TFLOPs/s (PP-bubble-dominated), GBS=192 at 73 TFLOPs/s, GBS=2048 at 150 TFLOPs/s — shows strongly diminishing marginal throughput gain well before GBS saturates memory (ml-engineering, `training/hparams.md`). That is a 2022-era number, but the *pattern* is worth re-verifying rather than assuming linear gains at 10T-MoE scale, where PP is also structurally required (Ch18). Weight decay is near-universal at 0.1 across AdamW and Muon alike — and for Muon it is not merely regularization: per Moonlight's proof, it keeps `‖W‖` bounded and MaxLogit growth in check.

## Implications for the 10T/200B Run

**Safe choice: AdamW**, with the recipe DeepSeek-V3, Qwen3, and Llama 3 converged on — β1=0.9, β2=0.95, weight decay 0.1, grad clip 1.0, a WSD-shaped or pure-cosine schedule with a fitted-scaling-law peak LR rather than naive muTransfer, batch ramp over the first ~500B-3T tokens, QK-Norm for attention stability. This is the only choice with a disclosed, completed validation above 400B active-or-dense parameters. The cost is real: leaving Muon's FLOPs/token efficiency on the table, and inheriting AdamW's own critical-batch-size degradation risk at 100,000-GPU global-batch scale.

**Alpha choice: Muon (with a QK-Clip or RMSNorm-on-QK-equivalent stability companion), applied to all 2D matrix parameters, AdamW retained for embeddings/norms/router/head.** This is no longer a research bet — it is the choice made by the two largest confirmed MoE pretraining runs on record, Kimi K2 at 32.6B active and, more tellingly, DeepSeek's own V3→V4 transition: the lab with arguably the best track record for MoE stability, having validated AdamW at 37B active with zero spikes, deliberately moved to Muon next. The risk is pure scale extrapolation: every public Muon validation tops out at 49B active; 200B is a 4x extrapolation with no precedent for *any* optimizer at that count on an MoE. Three risks need de-risking: (a) attention-logit blowup at larger active width, solved differently by every lab that has hit it — validate your own variant, don't assume K2's or DeepSeek-V4's transfers untested; (b) distributed NS at your specific EP/TP/PP topology, where the MoE-EP interaction is exactly the undocumented gap in NVIDIA's tooling; (c) muP-for-MoE LR transfer reliability at high expert count, which the 2026 evidence says is not solved.

**The de-risking ablation ladder**:

1. **50M-300M active, ~10B tokens, architecture-matched, 4-way sweep** — AdamW vs. Muon vs. Muon+your-chosen-QK-stability-mechanism vs. Muon+QK-Clip-exact-as-K2. Confirm attention-logit blowup reproduces at small scale (per K2's report of hitting it at 9B active) and that your fix suppresses it; cheap enough (<$50K at 2026 GB200 rates) to run all four arms to convergence.
2. **1B-4B active, ~50-100B tokens, muP-telescoping (Essential AI) and fitted-scaling-law (Qwen) protocols side by side** on the actual target architecture — the two disagreed in the literature because they were tested on different architectures; a head-to-head at your own MoE shape is required before trusting either for the final LR.
3. **8-15B active, ~1-2T tokens, full distributed-Muon systems test at production EP/TP/PP topology** — validate the NS-sharding-mode choice against your fabric and expert-parallel degree, and get the first read on whether GB300-class Muon throughput parity holds at very high active-param, very high expert-count. NVIDIA's own Qwen3-30B GB300 measurement has distributed Muon at 721 TFLOP/s versus AdamW's 713 — slightly *ahead* — so the question is whether that parity survives scale, not how much overhead to budget; that regime sits outside every public measurement.
4. **~50-80B active (>1x the largest public Muon validation of 49B), 3-5T tokens** — full end-to-end rehearsal, the primary go/no-go gate: if attention-stability, muP-transfer, and distributed-NS-overhead all hold clean here, the marginal risk of continuing to 200B active becomes "does the same shape of finding continue" rather than "will something qualitatively new break."

If step 4 shows any qualitative instability that doesn't trace to a known, fixable cause, fall back to AdamW for the full run. The FLOPs/token cost of that fallback is bounded — and should be priced realistically at roughly 1.1-1.4x, not the 2x that a naive reading of Moonlight's ~52% headline implies: the headline is a compute-optimal-frontier claim whose own fitted curves do not reproduce it (see above), and independent re-benchmarks put Muon's practical advantage at matched wall-clock in the tens of percent, not a doubling. The downside is a known, priced inefficiency either way. An unrecovered instability discovered only at 100,000-GPU full scale is not bounded the same way, and that asymmetry — a priced fallback against an unpriced failure mode — should govern the final decision more than any individual benchmark in this chapter.
