# The State of Frontier Pretraining, July 2026

### What it takes to pretrain a 10-trillion-total / 200-billion-active MoE on roughly 100,000 NVIDIA GB200 GPUs

---

## The Question

What does a team actually need to know, decide, and build before committing a 10-trillion-total/200-billion-active mixture-of-experts model to a training run on approximately 100,000 NVIDIA GB200 GPUs? That is the question everything here works toward, as rigorously as the July 2026 evidence allows. No lab has published that run. No lab has published anything within one to three orders of magnitude of it end-to-end — DeepSeek-V3 proved its recipe at 2,048 GPUs, Kimi K2 at 1.04 trillion total parameters, Meta at 16,384 GPUs, and Llama 4 Behemoth was shelved before it finished. What exists instead is a scattered, uneven, often contradictory record: engineering blogs about racks and fabrics, technical reports that stopped after one generation, sworn courtroom testimony, unscripted podcast asides, community microbenchmarks of silicon the vendor never fully specified, and open-weight Chinese labs whose technical reports are, by a wide margin, the most detailed infrastructure disclosures the field has produced. The job here is to assemble that record into a single coherent, load-bearing reference — not to paper over its gaps, but to state them as precisely as the claims it does support.

The raw material is primary-source: arXiv papers, official lab blog posts and technical reports, transcribed podcast interviews, conference talks and community benchmarks, a private archive of paywalled SemiAnalysis reports, and code-level reading of the repositories a real build would actually run — Megatron-LM, TorchTitan, MaxText, DeepSeek's DeepGEMM/DeepEP/EPLB/3FS, Stas Bekman's `ml-engineering`. Every claim is cited to a named source and dated; Chapter 1 sets out the sourcing discipline in full, including how to catch a rumor hardening into consensus, and Chapter 25 indexes every source consulted. Everything reflects a July 13, 2026 snapshot. Frontier disclosure moves fast in the infrastructure layer and glacially in the model-disclosure layer, so treat every dated claim as current only as of its date — and treat the chapters on *how to evaluate a new claim* (1 and 25 above all) as the ones with the longest shelf life. One dated post-snapshot addendum — Kimi K3, released July 16 — closes Chapter 3, marked as such rather than silently folded in; its consequences are traced through Chapters 5, 7, 9, 10, and 18 under the same dated flags.

## Executive Summary

Frontier pretraining in mid-2026 is defined less by any single new technique than by a wide and increasingly settled convergence on infrastructure, sitting directly on top of an architecture and data layer that is still genuinely contested — and a closed-lab disclosure regime that has gotten *less* informative even as the open-weight Chinese labs have gotten more so. By the end, the 10T/200B question resolves into a short list of positions the evidence actually supports, a longer list it only weakly supports, and an explicit accounting of what nobody outside five or six buildings on Earth currently knows (Ch1).

**The disclosure pattern is itself a load-bearing finding.** Closed labs are extraordinarily voluble about physical infrastructure — chip counts, network topologies, power and cooling, buildout timelines — because that category of disclosure serves competitive signaling and investor confidence without revealing data mixture, architecture, or hyperparameters. It has produced the richest disclosure record the field has ever generated: OpenAI's MRC networking protocol, Microsoft's Fairwater superfactory, Google's Virgo Network and TPU 8t claims, Anthropic's Trainium/TPU partnership cadence (Ch2). None of that candor extends to what any specific flagship model actually consumed to train. One technical-report-grade disclosure per lab, typically early in a lineage, followed by infrastructure-only disclosure for every subsequent generation, is close to a universal pattern (Ch1, Ch2). Meta's Llama 3 paper and Google's Gemini 1.0/1.5 reports remain, respectively, the richest infrastructure and systems disclosures their labs have produced, unmatched by anything since — and the goodput, SuperPod, and silent-data-corruption systems detail sits in the December 2023 Gemini 1.0 report specifically, not the better-remembered 1.5. Against that silence, the open-weight Chinese labs — DeepSeek above all, but also Kimi, GLM, Ling, and, distinctively, Baidu's non-Megatron PaddlePaddle stack — supply the field's only frontier-adjacent, technical-report-grade, independently-triangulable infrastructure numbers (Ch3, Ch4). That is a genuine evidentiary inversion, and it shapes everything downstream: the best-documented recipes are not the recipes running at the current capability frontier, and a planner has to read around that gap rather than pretend it isn't there.

**What has converged.** On precision, FP8 blockwise training — DeepSeek-V3's 1×128/128×128-tile, E4M3, CUDA-core-accumulation-fixed recipe — is now close to a default rather than a novelty, and MXFP8 is validated at real scale (8B params/15T tokens) with a corroborating ~41% throughput result on Blackwell — though that figure is MXFP8 grouped-GEMMs plus DeepEP overlap combined, on the expert GEMMs only, not a pure-format speedup (Ch10). NVFP4 as a pretraining format remains a real existence proof (12B/10T tokens) but two orders of magnitude below frontier scale and unresolved on stochastic-vs-round-to-nearest rounding — a pilot, not a bet-the-run choice, as of this writing — even as, three days before the snapshot closed, NVFP4 crossed into production in the RL-rollout and serving loop, with an open recipe showing BF16-parity reward curves behind a bit-exact trainer/sampler contract (Ch10). On optimizers, Muon has moved from a curiosity to the field's most-watched alpha bet, anchored by the single most consequential data point in the optimizer record: DeepSeek's own switch from AdamW (V3) to Muon (V4) (Ch4, Ch9). On MoE mechanics, dropless routing via grouped GEMM, aux-loss-free load balancing with a dynamic per-expert bias term, sigmoid-affinity routing, and multi-token prediction are no longer live debates — they are shipped, cross-lab-replicated defaults, and the thirteen-plus-lab comparison table in Chapter 3 shows FP8 near-universal and Muon spreading fast across the Chinese-lab tier specifically (Ch3, Ch7). On attention, the frontier is visibly consolidating around trainable sparse selection: DeepSeek's NSA→MoBA→DSA lineage, independently adopted by GLM-5, and joined in June 2026 by MiniMax's MSA. MiniMax is also the lab whose named, quantified M1→M2 retreat from linear attention remains the field's only closed-loop postmortem — its M3 re-entered long context via block-sparse softmax rather than recurrence — while the linear-recurrent hybrid camp (Kimi's KDA lineage, Qwen3-Next's Gated DeltaNet) holds a committed minority position. That fork is genuinely live, not settled (Ch6, Ch7, Ch4). On hardware, GB200 NVL72's real dense FP8 peak is 5,000 TFLOPS/GPU, not the 4,500-TFLOPS HGX B200 figure that even careful sources silently substitute; this single correction ripples into every MFU, cost, and roofline calculation in Chapters 10, 18, 20, 21, and 23 (Ch16). On networking, NVL72 (72 GPUs, 130 TB/s aggregate) is the industry's first genuinely standardized scale-up unit, and the OpenAI/Microsoft/AMD/Broadcom/NVIDIA Multipath Reliable Connection protocol is production-validated at 42,000-GPU scale with a stated 131,072-GPU target — the closest thing to a shared reference design for the scale-out fabric above it (Ch20). And several problems that looked open turn out to be functionally solved: sequence packing wastes 0.001-0.003% of tokens (Ch15), a 100T-token corpus can be fully deduplicated in about a day on 32 H100s (Ch13), and raw token dataloading caps out three to four orders of magnitude below any storage system's real throughput — at 100,000 GPUs, orchestration risk, not bandwidth, is what binds (Ch15).

**What is still genuinely contested.** The activation ratio for a 10T/200B host is the single most consequential open architecture question: the loss-optimal scaling-law extrapolation (Ant Group, Apple) pushes toward sparser than any shipped flagship, but both papers' fitted ranges stop three to seven orders of magnitude short of this host's compute, and sparser models measurably underperform on reasoning tasks at matched loss — 2% activation is a defensible hedge, not a validated optimum (Ch7). No optimizer, Muon or AdamW, has a public, completed validation above roughly 50 billion active parameters for any MoE — a 200B-active design is genuinely unprecedented territory, and the honest answer is a safe default (AdamW) plus a staged de-risking ladder toward the higher-upside bet (Muon), not a settled choice (Ch9). No joint closed-form solution exists anywhere in the public literature for optimal EP+TP+PP+FSDP layout on GB200's two-tier NVLink-domain/scale-out-fabric hierarchy — the JAX Scaling Book and the HuggingFace Ultra-Scale Playbook each handle individual parallelism axes well, and neither derives a true joint optimum, a gap Chapter 18 closes by combination rather than citation. Achieved GEMM efficiency at real MoE training shapes, on GB200, in MXFP8 or NVFP4, at frontier scale, does not appear anywhere in the reachable public record — every number found is either an isolated microbenchmark tile or an H800/Hopper-generation figure (Ch10, Ch17). And the field's best-evidenced compute figures are reliably its most stale ones: DeepSeek-V3's 3.3e24 FLOP is Epoch AI's only "Confident"-rated large training-run estimate in existence, precisely because DeepSeek is not the lab currently defining the capability frontier — no source anywhere offers a FLOP estimate, GPU count, or duration for Gemini 3 Pro, Claude Opus 4.x, or GPT-5.5/5.6 and its successors (Ch1). Whether a new, GPT-4.5-class-or-larger pretraining run is in flight right now at any closed US lab is the single most consequential question for anyone planning this run, and it is precisely the question the 2026 evidence base cannot answer (Ch1, Ch25).

A second cluster of open questions sits closer to the ground a build actually has to stand on. No production system anywhere in the record has demonstrated the checkpoint bandwidth a naive reading of a 10T/200B host's 140TB Adam state would seem to require; the systems that get closest do it by architecture — local-first, async-drain, peer-replicated — not by brute-force bandwidth (Ch21). No validated elastic-pretraining path exists past a few hundred devices, three to four orders of magnitude below target scale, which forces a fixed-world-size, warm-standby fault-tolerance design rather than the elastic-membership systems the research literature is more excited about (Ch21). No public source gives a validated hot-spare ratio for a 100,000-GPU-class fleet. No controlled ablation anywhere isolates whether MoE genuinely wants a pretraining data mixture different from a compute-matched dense model's — every major Chinese-lab report is silent on the question, which may reflect genuine mixture-invariance or may simply mean nobody has published the experiment (Ch14, Ch25). And the training-logbook genre that made OPT and BLOOM's postmortems so useful has essentially vanished from frontier commercial labs — DeepSeek's entire public disclosure on training-run drama is a one-sentence "zero loss spikes" claim — leaving Chapter 22's operational texture with no frontier-scale precedent to read, only extrapolation from smaller, still-instructive runs (Ch22, Ch25).

**The ten decisions**, each argued in full in its chapter:

1. **Activation ratio: commit to ~2% as a hedge, not an optimum**, sparser than every shipped flagship but short of the loss-optimal extrapolation, chosen specifically to buy back the reasoning-task penalty sparser models pay at matched loss (Ch7).
2. **Attention: MLA with wide effective head-dimensions and Partial RoPE, plus a DSA-style trainable sparse indexer, not a linear-recurrent hybrid** — the memory-bandwidth case is now theoretically grounded (Su Jianlin's ablations) and MiniMax's own retreat from linear attention is the sharpest available warning against betting a 100k-GPU commitment on that branch (Ch4, Ch7).
3. **Optimizer: AdamW as the safe default, Muon as the alpha bet, staged through an explicit four-step de-risking ladder with a defined fallback trigger** — no MoE optimizer choice has been validated above ~50B active parameters, and this is a one-shot commitment (Ch9).
4. **Precision: FP8 blockwise is bet-the-run-ready; MXFP8 is pilot-only pending full-model kernel integration; NVFP4 is a 2027-or-later bet, not a 2026 one** — with FP32 router logits as the one item on this list that is unambiguous and costs nothing (Ch10).
5. **Parallelism: ZeRO-1 plus expert parallelism, not pure DP/FSDP**, which is comms-bound by three to four orders of magnitude at any realistic batch size for this host's expert count — because EP has already sharded expert weights before DP replication occurs — though gradient traffic still scales with total parameters, not active ones, so the E/k inflation survives in gradient-sync volume and sets a residual data-parallel batch floor of roughly 1.7M tokens per replica on CX-8-class fabrics (Ch18).
6. **Framework: fork Megatron-Core rather than build from scratch or bet on TorchTitan/MaxText as-is** — it is the only codebase with a primary-source MoE-at-scale paper and a production dispatcher backend tuned for GB200, but the fork needs specific, enumerated modifications before it survives 100,000 GPUs (Ch19, Ch24).
7. **Hardware: 100,000 GB200 NVL72 is a defensible bet, conditioned on treating rack-level acceptance testing and a dedicated NVL72 hardware-diagnostics team as gating prerequisites, not post-delivery hygiene** — GB200's measured MTBI runs roughly twice as bad per GPU-day as H100's, with a 9x larger blast radius (Ch16, Ch21).
8. **Data: budget for 25-50T tokens as a multi-epoch-and-synthetic-accounted total, not a fresh-unique-crawl target** — Epoch's ~300T-token stock estimate does not translate to that much genuinely fresh, high-quality, deduplicated text, and own-crawler infrastructure has a 12-plus-month independent lead time (Ch12, Ch13).
9. **Curriculum: a costed, staged 28T-token pipeline — pretrain, midtrain, long-context extension, final anneal — with stage-specific abort criteria**, built on the one piece of token-level ground truth the field has published (Ai2's Dolmino mixture, a documented +36.3-point Math jump from 1.7% of total tokens) while treating the mixture-transfer literature's ~1-8B-parameter validation ceiling as an honest limitation, not a solved problem (Ch14).
10. **Reliability: build a hierarchical, peer-replication checkpoint stack targeting sub-minute recovery on 140TB of Adam state, and plan for a fixed-world-size warm-standby architecture rather than elastic membership**, which remains unvalidated past a few hundred devices — with an explicit 85-93% goodput target as the standing operational bar (Ch21).

Read together, these ten decisions describe a build that borrows the DeepSeek stack's FP8 recipe, aux-loss-free balancing, and EPLB-style expert placement nearly verbatim (Ch4), while requiring substantial, novel engineering everywhere the DeepSeek stack's own 2,048-GPU, H800-specific, single-generation validation stops short of this host's scale, hardware, and parameter count. It is a build that treats Megatron-Core as inheritance rather than foundation. It budgets a scaling-laws program whose cost is roughly fixed in absolute terms — about 2-25% of the final run at this scale, not the 30-60% smaller-model practice would extrapolate to — before the architecture is even frozen (Ch8, Ch24). And it spends real organizational lead time — twelve to fourteen months, in several places — on capabilities (a hardware-diagnostics team, an own-crawler pipeline, a peer-replication checkpoint stack) that have no vendor SKU and cannot be bought at any price once the cluster clock starts running (Ch24).

The honest throughline, restated at length in Chapter 24 and turned into an annotated ledger in Chapter 25: every system examined here has been validated somewhere between one and three orders of magnitude below the target — DeepSeek's recipe at 2,048 GPUs, Kimi's optimizer at 1.04T total parameters, Meta's goodput at 16,384 GPUs, MRC's production fabric at 75,000 of a stated 131,072-GPU design target. The distance between the nearest available evidence and an actual 10T/200B plan cannot be closed by assertion. Where triangulation across independent sources closes it, the chapters say so; where nothing can close it, they say that too, claim by claim. That discipline — not any single number in these pages — is what a real 10T/200B program should carry forward.
