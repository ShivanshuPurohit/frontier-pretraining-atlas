# The State of Frontier Pretraining, July 2026

### A technical planning reference for a 10-trillion-total / 200-billion-active MoE pretraining run on roughly 100,000 GB200 GPUs

---

## What This Book Is

This book exists to answer one question as rigorously as the July 2026 evidence base allows: what does a team actually need to know, decide, and build before committing a 10-trillion-total/200-billion-active mixture-of-experts model to a training run on approximately 100,000 NVIDIA GB200 GPUs. No lab has published that run. No lab has published anything within one to three orders of magnitude of it end-to-end — DeepSeek-V3 proved its recipe at 2,048 GPUs, Kimi K2 at 1.04 trillion total parameters, Meta at 16,384 GPUs, Llama 4 Behemoth was shelved before it finished. What exists instead is a scattered, uneven, and often contradictory record: engineering blogs about racks and fabrics, technical reports that stopped after one generation, sworn courtroom testimony, unscripted podcast asides, community microbenchmarks of silicon the vendor never fully specified, and open-weight Chinese labs whose technical reports are, by a wide margin, the most detailed infrastructure disclosures the field has produced. This book's job is to assemble that record into a single coherent, load-bearing reference — not to paper over its gaps, but to state them as precisely as the claims it does support.

It was built by a multi-agent research fleet working in two rounds over a 42-file, roughly 332,000-word corpus of primary sources: arXiv papers, official lab blog posts and technical reports, transcribed podcast interviews, conference talks and community benchmarks, a private archive of paywalled SemiAnalysis reports and articles, and direct code-level analysis of the repositories (Megatron-LM, TorchTitan, MaxText, DeepSeek's DeepGEMM/DeepEP/EPLB/3FS, Stas Bekman's `ml-engineering`) a real build would actually run. Round one covers the load-bearing frameworks, the DeepSeek and Chinese-lab reference stacks, architecture and scaling-law literature, precision, networking, fault tolerance, and benchmarks. Round two closes gaps round one's own critique surfaced: a dedicated GB200-FLOPS reconciliation that corrects a spec-sheet conflation propagating through the corpus's own earlier notes, multimodal integration, Baidu/PaddlePaddle, the Llama 4 Behemoth failure post-mortem, Nemotron 3's hybrid Mamba-MoE architecture, three further deep-dives into Bekman's operational writing, open-lab war stories, and historical training postmortems from OPT and BLOOM forward. Every claim in every chapter carries a provenance tag identifying how it reached this page — a discipline explained in full in Chapter 1 and applied without exception through Chapter 23. This is a July 13, 2026 snapshot. Frontier disclosure moves fast in the infrastructure layer and glacially in the model-disclosure layer; treat every dated claim as current only as of that date, and treat the chapters that describe *how* to evaluate a new claim (Chapters 1 and 23 above all) as the parts of this book with the longest shelf life.

## How to Read This Book

### The Provenance Tier Legend

Every citation in this book carries one of the following tags, defined in full in Chapter 1 and indexed in full in Chapter 23. The tag is carried through unaltered from the underlying research rather than upgraded or downgraded by editorial judgment at write-time — a claim tagged [RUMOR] in the source note stays [RUMOR] in the chapter that uses it.

| Tag | Source type | What it buys you | What it doesn't |
|---|---|---|---|
| **[PAPER]** | arXiv preprint or peer-reviewed technical report with disclosed methodology | Reproducible detail, author accountability, often exact numbers | Currency — most [PAPER]-grade disclosures are 6-18 months stale on arrival |
| **[OFFICIAL-BLOG]** / **[OFFICIAL-STATEMENT]** / **[OFFICIAL X POST]** | Company-sanctioned written disclosure | Legal review, an entity that can be held to the claim later | Completeness — selected for competitive signaling, not full disclosure |
| **[OFFICIAL-VIDEO]** / **[OFFICIAL-INTERVIEW]** / **[SPOKEN-CLAIM]** | Lab-sanctioned but unscripted remarks | Candor written material rarely matches | Verbatim fidelity when recovered via secondhand summary rather than direct transcript |
| **[CREDIBLE-HEARSAY]** / **[REPORTED-BY:outlet]** | Journalism with named institutional sourcing (WSJ, Bloomberg, Reuters, The Information, SemiAnalysis) | Editorial and legal review, anonymous-sourcing discipline | Independent verifiability — underlying sources are, by construction, unnamed |
| **[EPOCH-ESTIMATE]** / **[ANALYSIS]** | Explicit, methodologically disclosed estimation | A visible, auditable chain of inference, self-rated confidence | Ground truth — an estimate built on an estimate built on a leak |
| **[BENCHMARK]** / **[TALK]** / **[DOC]** / **[CODE]** | Conference talks, community microbenchmarks, open specs, repositories | Measured-not-claimed numbers, incentives orthogonal to PR | Institutional accountability — no legal department stands behind a GPU MODE lecture |
| **[RUMOR]** / **[FORUM]** | Single-source, unsourced, or self-reported by a party with an obvious incentive to exaggerate | Nothing verifiable | Everything — retained only when explicitly flagged, never laundered into fact |

A second convention runs alongside tagging: convergence as an explicit, deliberate upgrade mechanism. Where several independent secondary accounts of an unfetchable primary source substantially agree on specifics, this book treats the convergent core as higher-confidence paraphrase and flags anything appearing in only one account as lower-confidence. Chapter 1 works this method in full on a dated, concrete example — the "Gemini 3 trained exclusively on Ironwood" claim, traced from a single unnamed-sources blog post through four recycling outlets to a false sense of consensus.

## Executive Summary

Frontier pretraining in mid-2026 is defined less by any single new technique than by a wide and increasingly settled convergence on infrastructure, sitting directly on top of an architecture and data layer that is still genuinely contested — and a closed-lab disclosure regime that has gotten *less* informative even as the open-weight Chinese labs have gotten more so. A reader planning a 10T-total/200B-active run should walk away from this book with a short list of positions the evidence actually supports, a longer list of positions the evidence only weakly supports and flags as such, and an explicit accounting of what nobody outside five or six buildings on Earth currently knows (Ch1).

**The disclosure landscape itself is a load-bearing finding.** Closed labs are extraordinarily voluble about physical infrastructure — chip counts, network topologies, power and cooling, buildout timelines — because that category of disclosure serves competitive signaling and investor confidence without revealing data mixture, architecture, or hyperparameters, and it produces the richest disclosure record the field has ever generated: OpenAI's MRC networking protocol, Microsoft's Fairwater superfactory, Google's Virgo Network and TPU 8t claims, Anthropic's Trainium/TPU partnership cadence (Ch2). None of that candor extends to what any specific flagship model actually consumed to train. One technical-report-grade disclosure per lab, typically early in a lineage, followed by infrastructure-only disclosure for every subsequent generation, is close to a universal pattern (Ch1, Ch2) — Meta's Llama 3 paper and Google's Gemini 1.5 report remain, respectively, the richest infrastructure and systems disclosures their labs have produced, unmatched by anything since. Against that silence, the open-weight Chinese labs — DeepSeek above all, but also Kimi, GLM, Ling, and, distinctively, Baidu's non-Megatron PaddlePaddle stack — supply the field's only frontier-adjacent, technical-report-grade, independently-triangulable infrastructure numbers (Ch3, Ch4), a genuine evidentiary inversion this book leans on throughout: the best-documented recipes are not the recipes running at the current capability frontier, and a planner has to read around that gap rather than pretend it isn't there.

**What has converged.** On precision, FP8 blockwise training — DeepSeek-V3's 1×128/128×128-tile, E4M3, CUDA-core-accumulation-fixed recipe — is now close to a default rather than a novelty, and MXFP8 is validated at real scale (8B params/15T tokens) with independently corroborated ~41% throughput gains on Blackwell (Ch8). NVFP4 remains a real existence proof (12B/10T tokens) but two orders of magnitude below frontier scale and unresolved on stochastic-vs-round-to-nearest rounding — a pilot, not a bet-the-run choice, as of this writing (Ch8). On optimizers, Muon has moved from a curiosity to the field's most-watched alpha bet, anchored by the single most consequential data point in this book's optimizer evidence: DeepSeek's own switch from AdamW (V3) to Muon (V4) (Ch4, Ch7). On MoE mechanics, dropless routing via grouped GEMM, aux-loss-free load balancing with a dynamic per-expert bias term, sigmoid-affinity routing, and multi-token prediction are no longer live debates — they are shipped, cross-lab-replicated defaults, and a thirteen-plus-lab comparison table in Chapter 3 shows FP8 near-universal and Muon spreading fast across the Chinese-lab tier specifically (Ch3, Ch5). On attention, the frontier is visibly bifurcating toward trainable sparse selection (DeepSeek's NSA→MoBA→DSA lineage, now independently adopted by GLM-5) and away from linear-recurrent hybrids, a shift driven by MiniMax's own named, quantified retreat from linear attention between M1 and M2 — a rare closed-loop postmortem citing memory-bound training, precision fragility, and serving-stack immaturity as the specific failure modes (Ch5, Ch4). On hardware, GB200 NVL72's real dense FP8 peak is 5,000 TFLOPS/GPU, not the 4,500-TFLOPS HGX B200 figure that even careful sources — including two notes in this book's own corpus before a dedicated reconciliation pass caught it — silently substitute; this single correction ripples into every MFU, cost, and roofline calculation in Chapters 8, 16, 18, 19, and 21 (Ch14). On networking, NVL72 (72 GPUs, 130 TB/s aggregate) is the industry's first genuinely standardized scale-up unit, and the OpenAI/Microsoft/AMD/Broadcom/NVIDIA Multipath Reliable Connection protocol is production-validated at 42,000-GPU scale with a stated 131,072-GPU target — the closest thing to a shared reference design for the scale-out fabric above it (Ch18). And on the systems layer generally, several problems this book expected to still be open turned out to be functionally solved: sequence packing wastes 0.001-0.003% of tokens (Ch13), a 100T-token corpus can be fully deduplicated in a single afternoon on 32 H100s (Ch11), and raw token dataloading caps out three to four orders of magnitude below any storage system's real throughput, meaning it is orchestration risk — not bandwidth — that binds at 100,000 GPUs (Ch13).

**What is still genuinely contested.** The activation ratio for a 10T/200B host is this book's single most consequential open architecture question: the loss-optimal scaling-law extrapolation (Ant Group, Apple) pushes toward sparser than any shipped flagship, but both papers' fitted ranges stop three to seven orders of magnitude short of this host's compute, and sparser models measurably underperform on reasoning tasks at matched loss — 2% activation is this book's defensible hedge, not a validated optimum (Ch5). No optimizer, Muon or AdamW, has a public, completed validation above roughly 50 billion active parameters for any MoE — a 200B-active design is genuinely unprecedented territory, and the honest answer is a safe default (AdamW) plus a staged de-risking ladder toward the higher-upside bet (Muon), not a settled choice (Ch7). No joint closed-form solution exists anywhere in the public literature for optimal EP+TP+PP+FSDP layout on GB200's two-tier NVLink-domain/scale-out-fabric hierarchy — both the JAX Scaling Book and the HuggingFace Ultra-Scale Playbook handle individual parallelism axes well and none derives a true joint optimum, a gap this book's own chapters had to close by combination rather than citation (Ch16). Achieved GEMM efficiency at real MoE training shapes, on GB200, in MXFP8 or NVFP4, at frontier scale, does not appear anywhere in the public record this research could reach — every number found is either an isolated microbenchmark tile or an H800/Hopper-generation figure (Ch8, Ch15). And the field's best-evidenced compute figures are reliably its most stale ones: DeepSeek-V3's 3.3e24 FLOP is Epoch AI's only "Confident"-rated large training-run estimate in existence, precisely because DeepSeek is not the lab currently defining the capability frontier — no source anywhere in this corpus offers a FLOP estimate, GPU count, or duration for Gemini 3 Pro, Claude Opus 4.x, or GPT-5.5/5.6 and its successors (Ch1). Whether a new, GPT-4.5-class-or-larger pretraining run is in flight right now at any closed US lab is the single most consequential question a planner reading this book would want answered, and it is precisely the question the 2026 evidence base cannot answer (Ch1, Ch23).

A second cluster of open questions sits closer to the ground a build actually has to stand on. No production system anywhere in this corpus has demonstrated the checkpoint bandwidth a naive reading of a 10T/200B host's 140TB Adam state would seem to require, and the systems that get closest do it by architecture — local-first, async-drain, peer-replicated — not by brute-force bandwidth (Ch19). No validated elastic-pretraining path exists past a few hundred devices, three to four orders of magnitude below target scale, which forces a fixed-world-size, warm-standby fault-tolerance design rather than the elastic-membership systems the research literature is more excited about (Ch19). No public source gives a validated hot-spare ratio for a 100,000-GPU-class fleet, and no controlled ablation anywhere in the corpus isolates whether MoE genuinely wants a pretraining data mixture different from a compute-matched dense model's — a question every major Chinese-lab report is silent on, which may reflect genuine mixture-invariance or may simply reflect nobody having published the experiment (Ch12, Ch23). The training-logbook genre that made OPT and BLOOM's postmortems so useful has essentially vanished from frontier commercial labs — DeepSeek's entire public disclosure on training-run drama is a one-sentence "zero loss spikes" claim — leaving a reader wanting Chapter 20's texture at 10T scale with no frontier-scale precedent to read, only extrapolation from smaller, still-instructive runs (Ch20, Ch23).

**The reader's ten-decision shortlist**, each pointing to the chapter that argues it in full:

1. **Activation ratio: commit to ~2% as a hedge, not an optimum**, sparser than every shipped flagship but short of the loss-optimal extrapolation, chosen specifically to buy back the reasoning-task penalty sparser models pay at matched loss (Ch5).
2. **Attention: MLA with wide effective head-dimensions and Partial RoPE, plus a DSA-style trainable sparse indexer, not a linear-recurrent hybrid** — the memory-bandwidth case is now theoretically grounded (Su Jianlin's ablations) and MiniMax's own retreat from linear attention is the sharpest available warning against betting a 100k-GPU commitment on that branch (Ch4, Ch5).
3. **Optimizer: AdamW as the safe default, Muon as the alpha bet, staged through an explicit four-step de-risking ladder with a defined fallback trigger** — no MoE optimizer choice has been validated above ~50B active parameters, and this is a one-shot commitment (Ch7).
4. **Precision: FP8 blockwise is bet-the-run-ready; MXFP8 is pilot-only pending full-model kernel integration; NVFP4 is a 2027-or-later bet, not a 2026 one** — with FP32 router logits as the one item on this list that is unambiguous and costs nothing (Ch8).
5. **Parallelism: ZeRO-1 plus expert parallelism, not pure DP/FSDP**, which is comms-bound by three to four orders of magnitude at any realistic batch size for this host's expert count — because EP has already sharded expert weights before DP replication occurs (Ch16).
6. **Framework: fork Megatron-Core rather than build from scratch or bet on TorchTitan/MaxText as-is** — it is the only codebase with a primary-source MoE-at-scale paper and a production dispatcher backend tuned for GB200, but the fork needs specific, enumerated modifications before it survives 100,000 GPUs (Ch17, Ch22).
7. **Hardware: 100,000 GB200 NVL72 is a defensible bet, conditioned on treating rack-level acceptance testing and a dedicated NVL72 hardware-diagnostics team as gating prerequisites, not post-delivery hygiene** — GB200's measured MTBI runs roughly twice as bad per GPU-day as H100's, with a 9x larger blast radius (Ch14, Ch19).
8. **Data: budget for 25-50T tokens as a multi-epoch-and-synthetic-accounted total, not a fresh-unique-crawl target** — Epoch's ~300T-token stock estimate does not translate to that much genuinely fresh, high-quality, deduplicated text, and own-crawler infrastructure has a 12-plus-month independent lead time (Ch10, Ch11).
9. **Curriculum: a costed, staged 28T-token pipeline — pretrain, midtrain, long-context extension, final anneal — with stage-specific abort criteria**, built on the one piece of token-level ground truth the field has published (Ai2's Dolmino mixture, a documented +36.3-point Math jump from 1.7% of total tokens) while treating the mixture-transfer literature's ~1-8B-parameter validation ceiling as an honest limitation, not a solved problem (Ch12).
10. **Reliability: build a hierarchical, peer-replication checkpoint stack targeting sub-minute recovery on 140TB of Adam state, and plan for a fixed-world-size warm-standby architecture rather than elastic membership**, which remains unvalidated past a few hundred devices — with an explicit 85-93% goodput target as the standing operational bar (Ch19).

Read together, these ten decisions describe a build that borrows the DeepSeek stack's FP8 recipe, aux-loss-free balancing, and EPLB-style expert placement nearly verbatim (Ch4), while requiring substantial, novel engineering everywhere the DeepSeek stack's own 2,048-GPU, H800-specific, single-generation validation stops short of this host's scale, hardware, and parameter count. It is a build that treats Megatron-Core as inheritance rather than foundation, that budgets 30-60% of its final-run compute on a scaling-laws program before the architecture is even frozen (Ch6, Ch22), and that spends real organizational lead time — twelve to fourteen months, in several places — on capabilities (a hardware-diagnostics team, an own-crawler pipeline, a peer-replication checkpoint stack) that have no vendor SKU and cannot be bought at any price once the cluster clock starts running (Ch22).

The book's honest throughline, restated at length in Chapter 22 and made into an annotated ledger in Chapter 23, is that every system examined here has been validated somewhere between one and three orders of magnitude below the target — DeepSeek's recipe at 2,048 GPUs, Kimi's optimizer at 1.04T total parameters, Meta's goodput at 16,384 GPUs, MRC's production fabric at 75,000 of a stated 131,072-GPU design target — and the distance between the nearest available evidence and an actual 10T/200B plan is not a gap this book can close by assertion. It closes it, where it can, by triangulation across independent sources, and states plainly, chapter by chapter and tagged claim by tagged claim, everywhere it cannot. That discipline — not any single number in this book — is what a real 10T/200B program should carry forward from it.
