# Chapter 20 — Networking at 100k: NVL72 Domains, Fabrics, MRC, and Collectives

Every other chapter in the infrastructure arc treats the network as a budget line: a bandwidth number to divide FLOPs by, a latency term to hide behind overlap. This chapter is about where those numbers actually come from, and about the uncomfortable fact that the network fabric a 10T/200B-active MoE run depends on above roughly 10,000 GPUs does not exist as a single vendor-published design — it has to be assembled from five hyperscalers' independently-converged, mostly-proprietary engineering, cross-checked against one open academic-industrial standard still finding its silicon and one very new open transport spec that only became public in May 2026.

## The NVL72 Domain: Mechanics and What It Changes

The atomic scale-up unit for the entire GB200/GB300 generation is now settled industry-wide: 72 Blackwell GPUs, 1.8 TB/s NVLink5 per GPU (18 links × 100 GB/s bidirectional each), roughly 130 TB/s of aggregate switched bandwidth through nine NVSwitch trays — arithmetically consistent as 72 × 1.8 TB/s (NVIDIA's own docs, 2025–2026). Physically this is 5,184 copper cables forming a backplane compute trays "bite into" on insertion, using Amphenol Paladin HD 224G connectors — the 900 GB/s unidirectional NVLink5 figure is per GPU across its 18 links, not a per-connector-pair rating. Every cloud that has published a GB200 reference design — Azure's ND GB200 v6, GCP's A4X, AWS's P6e-GB200 UltraServer, NVIDIA's own SuperPOD RA — converges on the identical 72-GPU/1.8-TB/s-per-GPU/≈28.8-Tbps-scale-out-per-rack figure, making NVL72 the first genuinely standardized scale-up unit of the accelerator era rather than something each cloud independently re-derives.

That standardization is not yet matched by operational maturity. SemiAnalysis's field reporting from mid-2025 states plainly that the NVLink copper backplane, even after extensive burn-in, "is not that reliable," that diagnostic tooling lags the hardware, and that no large-scale synchronous training run had yet completed on NVL72 at a leading lab or CSP as of August 2025. NVIDIA's own DGX GB200 release notes corroborate active firmware work here — recent updates address "NVLink Reduction" accounting and "Head-of-queue Lifetime Limit" interactions when NVLink Recovery is enabled, now on by default, implying it was not reliably on earlier. Separately, GB200/GB300 backplane and cable NVLink sync issues were traced to a firmware bug (not hardware) that took six to seven months to resolve — early-ship units shipped November 2024, stable firmware v1.3 only landing spring/summer 2025, affecting AWS's, GCP's, and Meta's early NVL36x2 deployments broadly (per SemiAnalysis's ClusterMAX reporting). For a build committing capital to a specific NVL72 delivery window, this is a live risk, not settled history.

The domain's real significance for a 10T/200B MoE is architectural, not just electrical. With 72 GPUs sharing one non-blocking NVLink fabric, expert-parallel (EP) dispatch and combine can execute entirely inside NVLink for EP degrees up to 72, rather than crossing into RDMA/scale-out territory the way EP traffic had to at EP≤8 in the H100-node era. Meta's NCCLX paper documents a host-kernel coordinated mode in which the MoE all-to-all lets the CUDA kernel perform the NVLink copy in parallel with CPU-driven RDMA whenever there's no data dependency between the two hops — EP dispatch inside NVL72 and DP/PP traffic on RDMA can genuinely overlap rather than serialize (arXiv:2510.20171, Oct 2025). DeepSeek's DeepEP embodies the identical idea one layer down: an asymmetric-domain forwarding kernel routes tokens IB→matching-index-GPU, then NVLink→target expert, overlapping the two hops on roughly 20 SMs. Tensor-parallel degree nominally inherits the same headroom — the *fabric* now permits TP up to 72 without leaving the NVLink domain, versus TP≤8 previously — but that is a statement about where the wires run, not about what is efficient: Ch18's arithmetic-intensity derivation shows useful TP for this design caps near 6-way regardless of domain size, and treating "TP ≤ 72" as the new rule is exactly the trap that chapter names. The honest reshaping of the classic 3D layout is narrower and stronger: EP (the collective that actually needs the all-to-all crossbar) lives inside NVL72, TP stays small or absent, and PP and DP get pushed onto the RDMA scale-out fabric (full composition math is Ch18's territory; this chapter establishes only the fabric boundary that math has to respect).

## Scale-Out at 100k: The Production Fleet and the Reference-Architecture Gap

NVIDIA's own published reference architectures quietly stop being non-blocking beyond roughly 10,000 GPUs. The GB200 NVL72 SuperPOD RA's compute InfiniBand fabric tables out cleanly to 16 Scalable Units — 9,216 GPUs — but it is a three-tier leaf/spine/core fat-tree (32 leaf + 24 spine per SU), not a two-tier design: the core tier appears as soon as a second SU joins the fabric (18 core switches at 2 SU/1,152 GPUs), scaling to 512 leaf / 384 spine / 144 core at the full 16-SU/9,216-GPU configuration. The separate storage/Ethernet fabric adds its own additional "super-spine" tier beyond 4 SUs to preserve non-blocking storage access as the SuperPOD scales toward 16 SUs, and beyond 16 SU the document says only "contact your NVIDIA representative" (the RA document, Nov 2025). Every fabric operating past that line — OCI's 131,072-GPU Supercluster, Meta's 100K+ clusters, Azure Fairwater, AWS's Rainier — runs on a proprietary hyperscaler invention (MRC/SRv6, Disaggregated Scheduled Fabric, EFA "10P10U," Oracle Acceleron RoCE), not an NVIDIA-blessed blueprint. This is arguably the single most load-bearing fact in this chapter: there is no vendor-published reference architecture covering the full cluster size a 10T/200B run needs. The rack/SU/compute-fabric layer is exhaustively documented; the 10K→100K+ scale-out layer must be assembled in-house or licensed from whichever cloud hosts the build.

Three production papers anchor what "assembled in-house" looks like at hyperscale, and all three converge independently on the same handful of design moves.

**Meta's RoCE fabric** (Gangidi et al., SIGCOMM 2024) is the canonical scale-out reference: 24,000-GPU backend clusters at publication, a two-stage Clos "AI Zone" topology, and routing evolving through four stages — naive ECMP, path-pinning, Enhanced-ECMP with queue-pair (QP) scaling, centralized Traffic Engineering — because flow entropy in AI training traffic is pathologically low versus general cloud traffic. QP scaling to 16 gave up to 40% AllReduce improvement over QP=1; Traffic Engineering gave a further 5–10% but was not adopted at datacenter scale due to fragility under simultaneous link failures, leaving Enhanced-ECMP-with-QP-scaling as production default. The paper's most counterintuitive finding: Meta turned DCQCN off entirely for 400G deployments after firmware bugs, running over a year on PFC-only flow control with "stable performance" — contradicting the assumption that DCQCN is mandatory RoCE hygiene at scale. That fabric did not stop at 24K: a September 2025 @Scale disclosure states Meta's production RoCE backend scaled from 4,000 to 24,000 to 129,000 GPUs in under two years — evidence that Ethernet/RoCE does not cap out near 32K–64K GPUs before something like MRC becomes mandatory.

**Alibaba's HPN 7.0** (Qian et al., SIGCOMM 2024) bets on a two-tier dual-plane Clos instead of three tiers, fitting 15,360 GPUs in one non-oversubscribed Pod, purpose-built to avoid hash polarization. Its failure telemetry is the most granular production reliability data anywhere public: 0.057%/month NIC-to-ToR link failure rate, 0.051%/month ToR critical-failure rate, 5,000–60,000 link-flapping events per day cluster-wide, translating to only 1–2 crashed jobs per month at 15K-GPU-Pod scale thanks to non-stacked dual-ToR (each NIC connects to two independent ToRs with no sync link, avoiding the >40% of Alibaba's critical failures traced to stacked-dual-ToR sync bugs). A single-ToR failure costs only 6.25% throughput degradation rather than a full halt. On switch reliability, HPN's Table 3 is routinely misread: the 32.6× figure is a *population* ratio (single-chip switches outnumber chassis switches 32.6:1 in Alibaba's fleet), and combining it with the raw incident counts gives the real per-unit result — chassis switches suffered roughly 3.77× the critical hardware failures across a 32.6× smaller population, i.e., a per-unit failure ratio on the order of 120×, a considerably stronger case than the misquoted one for the industry's convergence on single-ASIC switches for AI fabrics.

**xAI's Colossus 2** is the counter-example on disclosure: confirmed live at gigawatt scale as of January 2026, two roughly 110,000-GPU clusters (one GB200, one GB300) on NVIDIA Spectrum-X Ethernet, targeting ~2 GW / ~555K GPUs across Memphis — with zero peer-reviewed or arXiv paper behind it, unlike Meta, Alibaba, and OpenAI. Its numbers should be read as trade-press triangulation, not verified disclosure.

Three further cross-checks matter for a build of this size. Azure's Fairwater fabric collapses a three-tier Clos into two by splitting each NIC's 800 Gbps port into 8×100 Gbps sub-ports fanned across parallel planes (worked example: 512 Tier-0 switches × 256 NICs = 131,072 NICs on two tiers); Fairwater 2 Atlanta goes further, splitting an entire compute tray's 3,200G into 32 planes to reach 524,288 GPUs on a two-layer network — at the cost that GPUs on the same tray can no longer talk over the scale-out network and must route through PXN over NVLink, with MRC handling the scale-out layer (Azure's Fairwater disclosures, Nov 2025). OCI's 131,072-GPU Supercluster publishes an exact tiered latency budget — Tier-0 ≤256 GPUs at ≤2 µs, Tier-1 ≤2,048 at ≤5 µs, Tier-2 ≤131,072 at ≤8 µs (OCI's blog, Oct 2025). And Meta's DSF, disclosed at Hot Chips 2025, publishes an exact Layer-2 non-blocking ceiling of 18,432 GPUs at 800G per zone, four zones composing one building-scale domain.

The condensed cross-architecture picture:

The through-line across these designs is a shared bet: static/deterministic source routing over dynamic control-plane routing, multi-plane topologies trading a third Clos tier for parallel lower-radix planes, and endpoint-driven congestion and failure handling — no two hyperscalers arrived here by sharing a document; each reinvented the same ideas independently. The RA paper trail also encodes an assumption a 10T-parameter MoE run will violate: every topology here is rail-optimized against a DP/PP-dominant traffic pattern, corroborated by Alibaba's table showing PP Send/Recv (6 MB) is the lowest-volume collective in their GPT-3-175B config, versus 5.5 GB for DP AllReduce. A 10T-MoE run with EP spanning multiple SUs pushes proportionally more spine/core-tier traffic than these switch-count ratios (32 leaf : 24 spine per SU) were sized to assume. The RA tables are correct topology inventories; their implicit sizing rationale needs re-deriving for an EP-heavy workload, not inheriting.

## The Transport Layer: MRC, UEC, Falcon, and the RDMA Fork

The primary source for OpenAI's MRC (Multipath Reliable Connection) is not a conference talk — it is a joint arXiv paper, "Resilient AI Supercomputer Networking using MRC and SRv6" (arXiv:2605.04333, submitted May 5, 2026), with 50 co-authors spanning OpenAI, Microsoft, AMD, Broadcom, and NVIDIA, plus a formal OCP 1.0 specification and reference implementation repository. The disclosure venue matters for citation hygiene: MRC's real public debut post-dates OCP Global Summit 2025 (which instead saw ESUN, a related but distinct scale-up-Ethernet workstream, launch) — no OCP Summit 2025 talk on MRC specifically exists.

MRC is a minimal extension to RoCEv2 Reliable Connection semantics, not a clean-slate hardware transport like Falcon. It restricts the verb surface to WRITE and WRITE-IMM only — no READ, SEND, or ATOMIC, RNR explicitly unsupported — trading generality for cross-vendor implementation simplicity. Out-of-order placement is native: every packet carries the RDMA virtual address and rkey so the NIC places arriving packets directly into memory regardless of order, making spraying-without-resequencing tractable at wire speed. PFC is disabled entirely; MRC runs Ethernet lossy/best-effort, pushing all reliability onto the endpoint NIC — a break from both Meta's PFC-based deployment and Alibaba's deep-buffer-switch approach. Spraying works via 32-bit entropy values (EVs) striped across the UDP source port and IPv6 flow label; each queue pair maintains 128–256 EVs, round-robins per packet, and balances them across network planes, with each EV deterministically mapped via SRv6 micro-segment IDs (uSIDs) to one static path — genuinely static source routing, not adaptive/hash-based ECMP. Reliability offload — SACK retransmission, "packet trimming" to distinguish congestion-drops from failure-drops, background path probing — lives entirely on the NIC; failure detection runs in tens of microseconds, full EV-set remapping completes in a few seconds.

MRC is already in production on OpenAI's largest GB200 clusters, including OCI's Abilene, TX site and Microsoft's Fairwater supercomputers, across ConnectX-8 (800 Gb/s), AMD Pollara/Vulcano (400 Gb/s), and Broadcom Thor Ultra NICs simultaneously. NCCL testing at 42,000-GPU scale measured 92 GB/s send/recv and peak link bandwidth of roughly 770 Gb/s (96% of theoretical), with a stated theoretical target of 131,072 GPUs; two named production clusters give latency of 5.09 µs same-rack and 6.54 µs cross-tier. The strongest argument for why MRC needed to exist as a new open spec rather than adopting UEC 1.0 wholesale is heterogeneity: Meta, Alibaba, and xAI each run single-vendor NIC fleets, while OpenAI/Microsoft need ConnectX-8, two distinct AMD NIC ASICs, and Broadcom Thor Ultra to interoperate on one wire protocol. NVIDIA's own framing positions MRC as an alternative transport alongside Spectrum-X's native "Adaptive RDMA," hedging by supporting both on the same ConnectX SuperNIC silicon.

MRC's relationship to the wider standardization effort is partial convergence, not adoption. UEC 1.0 (released June 11, 2025, 560+ pages) sprays packets using real-time congestion telemetry rather than hop-by-hop hashing, moving away from PFC toward receiver-driven congestion management — architecturally close to MRC's endpoint-driven, lossy-tolerant philosophy, but dynamic/telemetry-driven where MRC is static/SRv6-source-routed; MRC's paper cites UET work as prior art it partially diverges from, trading sophistication for determinism and simpler cross-vendor implementation. Google's Falcon is the opposite design point: a genuine clean-slate hardware transport, opened at OCP Summit October 2023 (older than MRC), formalized in a SIGCOMM 2025 paper — 200 Gbps/120 Mops/sec first implementation, up to 8× lower completion time and 65% higher goodput than ConnectX-7 RoCE under congestion, ~3 µs one-way latency, only 5–6% die area / 3–4% power to integrate, drawing on a decade of internal Google transport work (Carousel, Snap, Swift, PLB). It is the closest thing to a true UEC-native hardware transport shipping today, but has no public confirmation at 100K+-GPU training scale the way MRC does.

AWS's EFA/SRD completes the picture at Anthropic's Project Rainier — roughly 500,000 Trainium2 chips, Indiana campus, 455 MW across seven buildings (AWS disclosures plus industry reporting, late 2025). EFAv3 delivers up to 800 Gb/s/chip on Trn2 (deliberately reduced on Trn2-Ultra to prioritize NeuronLink), packet latency improved from 9 µs to 6.5 µs. AWS's internal codename for its EFA Gen3 fabric, surfaced only through a named-engineer interview (Ron Diamant, via SDxCentral, Nov 2025), is "10P10U": tens of petabytes/second of UltraCluster bisection bandwidth plus 10 µs worst-case any-to-any latency, with active rerouting rather than ring-based multipathing. One unresolved inconsistency: Diamant states NeuronLink delivers "two terabytes per second at approximately one microsecond per hop," while AWS's own Neuron docs state 1.28 TB/s per chip — a discrepancy we could not reconcile.

## Collectives: NCCL's 2.23–2.28 Arc and the SHARP Reality Check

NCCL's evolution across five point releases through mid-2026 tracks the transition to NVL72-scale domains directly. Version 2.23 introduced PAT (Parallel Aggregated Trees), giving AllGather/ReduceScatter logarithmic-latency scaling versus Ring's linear scaling and working on arbitrary rank counts. Version 2.26 optimized PAT so up to 16 warps run 16 parallel trees concurrently. Version 2.27 is most relevant to NVL72: symmetric memory, up to 9× lower latency for small messages but scoped to a single NVLink domain (up to NVL72 on GB200/GB300) — a scale-up optimization correlating positively with EP traffic already being NVL72-bounded. Secondary sources disagree on the exact multiple (7.6× vs 9×); both circulate, so both are preserved here rather than resolved. The same release extended SHARP to AllGather/ReduceScatter (cutting SM consumption from 16 to 6) and added communicator-shrink to exclude failed GPUs mid-training without a full restart. Version 2.28 added GIN (GPU-Initiated Networking), letting CUDA kernels directly trigger network transfers, matching NVSHMEM's device-initiated performance within roughly 1–2% (arXiv:2606.05951, June 2026).

Meta's NCCLX/CTran paper (arXiv:2510.20171, Oct 2025, built for Llama4) is the richest primary source on production 100K+-GPU collectives engineering. Its central break from stock NCCL is a zero-copy, SM-free transport: instead of routing data through a pre-registered FIFO buffer on both ends, CTran lets the NIC DMA engine RDMA directly between user buffers with zero device-to-device copy and no kernel involvement. The countervailing risk — zero-copy hands the entire message to the network at once, poorly matched to Meta's non-DCQCN strategy — is addressed with Dynamic Queue Pair Load Balancing tuned higher for cross-DC links, cutting switch buffer buildup by an order of magnitude. Measured gains: up to 12% steady-step latency reduction, up to 11× faster cluster init at 96K-GPU scale, and — the single most concrete number for modeling straggler/overlap budgets — cross-hop latency multipliers of 7× cross-AI-Zone and 30× cross-DC-building relative to same-rack. Microsoft's MSCCL++ (ASPLOS, Mar 2026; arXiv:2504.09014) sits below NCCL as a portable primitive/DSL, claiming up to 3.8× speedup over NCCL/RCCL/MSCCL and already adopted by AMD's RCCL — a de facto cross-vendor standard.

SHARP's actual production footprint is thinner than marketing suggests. NVIDIA's Quantum-X800 delivers 14.4 TFLOPS of in-network SHARP compute per switch, and NCCL 2.27's SHARP extension is real and shipped — but no primary source confirms SHARP in continuous use at a specific 100K+-GPU frontier run; available material is capability description, not a field report. There is also a measurement trap for the community's most widely-recommended benchmarking tool: Stas Bekman's `all_reduce_bench.py`, validated against `nccl-tests`, computes bandwidth using the same `busbw_coeff = 2*(ranks-1)/ranks` correction — a formula assuming a ring algorithm's data-movement ratio. When SHARP/NVLS is active, correct reduction is an N+1-sends model, not 2N; the ring-assuming formula systematically under-counts true bandwidth on any NVLS-active fabric — an open, unfixed `nccl-tests` issue (#153), so any NVLS-active number from this script should be read as a pessimistic lower bound. Separately, SHARP correctness cannot be assumed safe merely because health-check tooling passes — a documented incident found an NVSwitch ALU silently corrupting AllReduce results under NVLS while DCGMI reported healthy throughout. Independent numerical verification of in-network compute paths is a distinct discipline from link-failure health-checking.

## MoE Bandwidth Budgeting: EP All-to-All Against DP/PP AllReduce

The traffic pattern a 10T-total/200B-active MoE generates is structurally different from what every reference architecture above was sized against. NVIDIA's own GB200 RA rail-optimization principle — traffic per rail stays one hop from every other SU node, cross-rail traffic traverses the spine — assumes ring/tree gradient AllReduce and rail-aligned tensor/pipeline collectives, structurally predictable and mostly rail-local. EP dispatch and combine are not rail-local: every GPU in an EP group potentially exchanges tokens with every other GPU on every MoE layer, an inherently all-to-all pattern. The good news is that EP degree up to 72 stays entirely inside the non-blocking NVL72 domain — an all-to-all pattern that would be catastrophic on a rail-optimized scale-out fabric is comparatively cheap confined to a switched 130 TB/s domain. The risk is EP degree spanning multiple SUs, pushing more spine/core-tier traffic than the leaf:spine ratios (32:24 per SU) were provisioned to assume.

External validation for "all-to-all needs a switched fabric, not a torus" comes from AWS's own roadmap. Trainium2, deployed at Project Rainier, uses a pure 3D-torus mesh (4×4×4 = 64 chips, no switches) for NeuronLink scale-up; Trainium3 abandons this for an all-to-all switched fabric, with AWS stating explicitly that torus topologies "are not optimized for frontier MoE models which require all-to-all collectives," with oversubscription degrading badly as EP message sizes grow from 16 KB to 1 MB (per SemiAnalysis's Trainium3 coverage, Dec 2025). AWS discloses a concrete decision rule: its 64-chip switched topology suffices for MoE models with 2–3T total parameters, while models above roughly 4T benefit from a larger 144-chip domain — independent, cross-vendor confirmation that EP bandwidth-boundedness shapes hardware roadmaps directly, not a detail that falls out of generic provisioning.

The practical budgeting question is whether EP all-to-all traffic and DP/PP AllReduce genuinely overlap rather than compete for the same wires. Inside NVL72, both Meta's NCCLX paper and DeepSeek's DeepEP kernel answer yes by construction — EP rides NVLink, DP/PP AllReduce rides RDMA, independent unless a specific layout forces cross-traffic. NVIDIA's Megatron-Core MoE "Parallel Folding" technique matters directly: decoupling attention-layer and MoE-layer parallelism configs breaks the traditional EP≤DP constraint, freeing the layout to keep EP inside NVL72 while DP spans further out (arXiv:2603.07685, Mar 2026). The same report discloses per-GPU throughput for a real 685B/37B-active MoE — 1,233 TFLOPS/GPU on GB300, 1,048 on GB200 — a citable, framework-vendor MFU-class anchor (full derivation is Ch23's territory). ByteDance's MegaScale-MoE adds a caution: on a 352B-parameter MoE across 1,440 Hopper GPUs it achieves 1.65–1.88× speedup over Megatron-LM through per-layer-type overlap, but MFU still declines from 32.48% to 27.89% as GPU count scales (arXiv:2505.11432, May 2025) — even a purpose-built MoE communication system doesn't solve weak-scaling MFU decay, only shifts the curve up.

One more piece of arithmetic belongs here, because it determines whether the bandwidth-budgeting frame of this whole section even applies to this run. (The arithmetic is ours, from Ch18's Layout 2 and the Megatron measurements above.) Check the Megatron dispatch-latency table's own regime first: at its benchmark shape (4,096 tokens per GPU, hidden 7168, top-8, BF16), each GPU's dispatch payload is ~470 MB, whose pure wire time at 900 GB/s is ~520 µs — close to the measured 675 µs, so that benchmark is genuinely bandwidth-dominated and the "bandwidth budgeting" frame is honest *at fat microbatches*. Now substitute the microbatch sizes Ch18's Layout 2 actually produces under expert-batch starvation: a few thousand tokens per microbatch per *domain* — roughly 55 per GPU — collapses the per-GPU dispatch payload to single-digit megabytes (wire time: microseconds) and the per-source-destination message to **~56–113 KB (FP8/BF16)**, sitting at the bottom of the very 16 KB–1 MB range across which AWS documents all-to-all efficiency degrading badly. In that regime the collective's cost is fixed overhead — kernel launch, barrier synchronization, per-message and per-hop latency — not bytes on wires, and the measured "EP is ~20% of iteration time inside NVL72" figures, all taken at fat microbatches, do not extrapolate. The design consequence stacks on top of Ch17/Ch18's grouped-GEMM finding: the same thin microbatches that starve the expert GEMMs also push the dispatch collectives from the bandwidth-bound regime into the latency-bound one, so the two costs degrade *together*, and every remedy that fattens per-expert GEMM batches (larger global batch, fewer microbatches) simultaneously restores the dispatch collective to the regime the published measurements describe. Budgeting this network by bytes alone would miss the failure mode entirely; message-size accounting is where the actual risk lives.

Batching discipline generalizes directly to MoE communication design: Stas Bekman's `all_reduce_latency_comp.py` shows one 4 GB reduction dramatically outperforms 1,000× 4 MB reductions, arguing for batching expert-gradient or router-logit reductions rather than firing one collective per expert. `NCCL_DEBUG_SUBSYS=COLL` call-counting — grepping `NCCL INFO AllToAll`/`AllGather` counts per iteration — is an underused verification method for confirming actual all-to-all volume matches theoretical per-layer prediction.

## Multi-Datacenter Training: Where the Physics Bites

Synchronous, single-building-or-single-campus training remains the dominant frontier paradigm as of mid-2026 — OpenAI/Microsoft, Google, and Anthropic all report synchronous gradient descent for flagship runs. Google is the earliest confirmed instance of crossing a DC boundary at all: Gemini 1.0's technical report and Google Cloud CEO Thomas Kurian's statements confirm Gemini Ultra was trained "across multiple sites, and multiple clusters within those sites," though whether this means true multi-campus WAN training or multi-building-within-one-campus is undisclosed. SemiAnalysis's "Multi-Datacenter Training" analysis is the most detailed public treatment: roughly 5 Pbit/s of inter-campus bandwidth within a region and roughly 1 Pbit/s between regions for a 100T+-parameter-class model, at which line rate exchanging a 400 TB weight payload takes only 0.64 seconds. The hard physical floor is speed-of-light-in-fiber latency — a US east-west coast RTT floor of 43.2 ms that improves with neither better silicon nor cleverer software, only fewer syncs or shorter distances. DWDM capacity is not the bottleneck — 400ZR gives 25.5 Tbps per fiber pair, long-haul systems up to 121.6 Tbps. The proposed mitigation generalizes the NVL72→AI-Zone→cross-DC hop hierarchy one level further: hierarchical synchronous gradient descent, syncing every step within a building-scale domain and less often across regions.

Silicon and fiber infrastructure for this already exist. Broadcom's Jericho4 — a 51.2 Tbps fabric router, shipping since August 2025 — is explicitly positioned for distributed-AI-across-datacenters: "lossless RoCE across 100km+," UEC-compliant, HBM-based packet buffering claimed at 160× on-chip SRAM. Microsoft's AI WAN backbone connecting Atlanta and Wisconsin uses over 120,000 new fiber miles (Nov 2025), and Meta names deploying "some of the largest transoceanic fiber cable systems in the world" as AI-training infrastructure (Sept 2025). NVIDIA's InfiniBand MetroX (40 km extension) is essentially unused for this purpose — even InfiniBand-committed Microsoft uses Ethernet ZR/ZR+ optics between datacenters.

The asynchronous alternative is maturing but remains unproven at frontier quality bars for pretraining specifically. Streaming DiLoCo (arXiv:2501.18512, Jan 2025) claims data-parallel-comparable quality at roughly 400× less cross-island bandwidth through scheduled partial sync and 4-bit outer-gradient quantization. Decoupled DiLoCo (DeepMind, ~April 2026) combines DiLoCo with Pathways' heterogeneous-speed orchestration, reporting 88% goodput under high simulated hardware-failure rates (secondary-sourced). The cleanest revealed-preference signal against fully decentralized pretraining comes from the team most invested in proving it works: Prime Intellect's INTELLECT-3 (106B MoE, ~90.8% AIME24) was trained on centralized GPUs despite the company's decentralized-training mission. Decentralization already works for RL/post-training, but synchronous, single-campus training with hierarchical sync as the multi-site extension remains dominant for pretraining, and no source confirms any 2026 lab has crossed true multi-campus synchronous pretraining.

## Failure Math: Optics, Flaps, and the Mitigation Stack

The most granular public disclosure of the actual failure chain behind a link flap comes from OCI: a 400G Ethernet link's FEC tolerates at most three sequential uncorrectable 5,140-bit code blocks before going down, and three uncorrectable errors inside a 20-nanosecond window trigger a link-down event. The optical layer recovers within milliseconds, but full link bring-up — PMD, PMA, FEC, PCS, MAC, Datalink, and IP layers each independently re-running link-training — stretches into a 10–15 second outage, and each flap triggers a full BGP/OSPF network-wide reconvergence with real risk of transient routing microloops (OCI's blog, Oct 2025). OCI's "link debounce" mitigation prevents a momentary PMA-layer blip from cascading into the full reconvergence tax. Alibaba's HPN telemetry gives the aggregate picture this has to survive: 5,000–60,000 link-flapping events per day cluster-wide at 15K-GPU-Pod scale, with non-stacked dual-ToR absorbing that down to only 1–2 crashed jobs per month.

Co-packaged optics reliability sits on contested ground between vendor claims and independent validation. Broadcom states TH5-Bailly (second-gen CPO) testing at Meta demonstrated 1 million link-hours without a single flap and a claimed 2.5M-hour MTBF, versus "significantly higher failure rates" for pluggable FR4 optics (vendor-sourced, Oct 2025). A related Meta/Broadcom dataset presented at ECOC 2025 frames the same testing differently: 1,049k 400G-port-device-hours across 15 Bailly switches with zero failures, extending to 15M port-device-hours with zero uncorrectable codewords through the first 4M, yielding an MTBF comparison of 2.6M device-hours for CPO versus 0.5–1M for pluggables. Both point the same direction but are not identical calculations, and both are explicitly caveated as 15 switches over roughly eleven months in a lab, not thousands over years in the field. No independent, non-Broadcom-commissioned link-flap study at true 100K+-link production scale exists for either technology. CPO economics are also more modest than marketing implies at the scale-out layer: for a 200,000-GPU GB300 NVL72 three-layer cluster drawing 435 MW, optical transceivers account for only 17 MW, and moving fully to CPO cuts transceiver power 84% but total cluster power only 2–4% and total cost only 3–7% — the real CPO opportunity is scale-up (removing NVLink copper's ~2-meter reach ceiling), not scale-out (per SemiAnalysis's CPO analysis, Jan 2026).

Synthesizing Alibaba's link-failure telemetry, Meta's transport choices, and MRC's detection/reroute timings yields a coherent industry-wide mitigation stack for 100K+-GPU training: multipath spraying at the transport layer so no single link failure blocks a flow (MRC's EVs, Falcon's multipath connections, UEC's telemetry-driven spraying); multi-plane physical topology so a switch or plane failure degrades rather than halts a job (MRC's multi-plane Clos, Alibaba's dual-plane); dual-ToR/dual-NIC-port at the host, the most rigorously quantified layer (Alibaba's 6.25% degradation versus full halt); receiver-driven congestion admission in the collective library rather than transport-layer control alone (Meta's DCQCN-off choice, MRC's SACK-plus-trimming); straggler detection as a discipline distinct from link-failure handling (silent, gradual, not caught by health-check tooling — Ch21's fuller territory, though flapping links are a documented contributor); and independent numerical verification of in-network compute paths, since SHARP/NVLS correctness cannot be assumed safe just because DCGMI passes.

## Implications for the 10T/200B Run

The fabric decision for a 100,000-GB200 build is a stack of choices, and the evidence here points toward specific defaults rather than open questions at every layer. Inside the rack, NVL72 is now a de facto industry standard the build should simply inherit — but backplane reliability should be treated as still-hardening rather than closed, and the firmware-driven six-to-seven-month NVLink sync saga of 2024–2025 argues for real schedule slack around any delivery ramp rather than assumed day-one stability. Above the rack, there is no single reference architecture to adopt wholesale: NVIDIA's own tables stop at 9,216 GPUs, and the 10K→100K+ layer must be built from the hyperscaler playbook synthesized here — static/deterministic source routing, multi-plane two-tier topologies, endpoint-driven failure handling, non-stacked dual-ToR redundancy — whether via MRC directly, an internal equivalent, or a cloud partner whose fabric already implements these patterns at scale.

On transport specifically, MRC's production deployment at 42K-GPU-tested/131,072-GPU-target scale, its multi-vendor NIC interoperability, and its explicit heterogeneous-fleet design make it the most de-risked open option today for a build that will not be single-vendor NIC-homogeneous by construction; UEC 1.0 is more architecturally ambitious but a year behind in confirmed production deployment at this scale. The EP/TP-inside-NVL72, DP/PP-outside-it boundary established here should feed directly into Ch18's parallelism math as a hard constraint — AWS's Trainium3 torus-to-switched-fabric pivot is independent confirmation that all-to-all EP traffic breaks any topology not built to carry it. NCCL 2.27+'s symmetric memory and SHARP extensions are worth adopting for their NVL72-scoped latency wins, but SHARP correctness should be independently, continuously verified rather than trusted on DCGMI's word alone, and any `all_reduce_bench.py`-style benchmarking pipeline needs its busbw formula corrected for NVLS before the numbers are trusted as anything but a lower bound. Multi-datacenter training is not a near-term requirement at 100,000 GPUs — that scale fits comfortably within a single gigawatt-class campus using hierarchical-sync patterns already proven at building scale — but Jericho4-class inter-campus infrastructure exists today should the program need to federate for a subsequent, larger run.
