# Chapter 20 — Operations: War Stories, Debugging Doctrine, and Running the Run

Every chapter before this one has been about what to build. This one is about what happens after you turn it on: the incident that starts at 3am, the throughput regression nobody can explain, the eval score that looks a little off three weeks later. The published record of how large training runs are actually operated — as opposed to how they are architected — is thin, unevenly distributed, and aging in a specific way: the genre of the day-by-day training diary has migrated almost entirely out of the frontier commercial labs and into the open/academic tier, which means the best operational ground truth available for a 10T/200B run on 100,000 GB200s comes from labs one to three orders of magnitude smaller. Read correctly, that ground truth is still the most useful thing in this book for the humans who will be paged.

## The Logbook Genre: From OPT's Chronicles to the 2025-2026 Open-Tier Diaries

The reference artifact for this entire chapter is Meta's OPT-175B logbook: 148 pages of raw notes plus four dated progress updates covering roughly 90 restarts over 33 days on 1,024 A100s [OPT-175B chronicles, facebookresearch/metaseq](https://github.com/facebookresearch/metaseq/tree/main/projects/OPT/chronicles) [LOGBOOK] (11/2021–1/2022). The team's own history splits cleanly into two eras separated by one decision. The "11.xx lineage" (through Nov 5–11, 2021) was numerics-driven firefighting — grad-clip tightened 2.5→1.5→1.0, weight decay and Adam β2 hot-swapped mid-run, GeLU swapped for ReLU on suspicion of its cubic term interacting badly with FP16, a discovery that learned positional embeddings had never actually trained because sinusoidal initialization froze them in place. None of it stabilized the run. On Nov 11 the team invoked a pre-declared "plan B," abandoned six months of from-scratch architectural choices, and copied Megatron/GPT-3's published 175B settings as closely as possible. From experiment 12.00 onward, "the only restarts we've had to make were all related to hardware issues" — the cleanest data point in the corpus for when to stop tuning and start copying a known-working recipe. The subsequent "40+ restarts in two weeks" period around Thanksgiving 2021 was pure hardware attrition against an inadequate buffer pool (12–18 spare nodes, hours-long manual cloud-console replacement, "~2 machines go down every day" as the team's own baseline), compounded by a 1.6TB checkpoint that hung nondeterministically on download. The "Omicron SEV" — a cloud provider accidentally deleting the entire cluster while replenishing the buffer pool — pushed the team to build automated health-check/recovery tooling that immediately paid off: eight subsequent hardware failures over the Christmas break were auto-recovered with reduced on-call burden, with "14 consecutive days with zero human intervention" explicitly set as the metric for retiring the on-call role.

BLOOM-176B's `chronicles.md` is the most granular public training diary that exists: 694 lines of minute-by-minute SLURM log entries plus a 1,394-line pre-launch "prequel" [BLOOM chronicles.md, bigscience-workshop/bigscience](https://github.com/bigscience-workshop/bigscience/blob/master/train/tr11-176B-ml/chronicles.md) [LOGBOOK] (2022). Three episodes are worth carrying forward as case studies in their own right. First, a grad-clip/tensor-parallel-sync bug: DeepSpeed's `BF16Optimizer` wasn't clipping gradients on TP ranks >1, so layer-norm weights that are supposed to be identical across ranks silently drifted apart — invisible in the training loss (each rank just learned its own private copy) but catastrophic at checkpoint-merge time. Tracing and fixing it took from March 24 to March 30, 2022, across at least three restarts and a temporary all-reduce sync band-aid that itself only recovered ~60% of the drifted parameters on the first pass. Second, a three-act eval-hang saga spanning April 30 to May 18: a DeepSpeed pipeline-engine race condition that hung training specifically at eval time, recurring three separate times (costing 18h, then 6h, then 11h) before Daniel Hesslow traced it, via a fleet-wide `py-spy` dump, to a known PyTorch DataLoader worker-thread lock bug (`pytorch/pytorch#25158`) — never fixed upstream, only worked around by setting `num_workers=0` on the validation loader. Third, a pre-launch scale-dependent hang: models under 100B parameters trained fine, but crossing that threshold caused random GPUs to stall in `all_reduce`; the empirical fix, `CUDA_LAUNCH_BLOCKING=1` (forcing fully synchronous CUDA), eliminated the hang with **zero measured throughput penalty**, and the team shipped the entire 176B production run with it permanently enabled, never finding the true root cause [BLOOM chronicles-prequel.md](https://github.com/bigscience-workshop/bigscience/blob/master/train/tr11-176B-ml/chronicles-prequel.md) [LOGBOOK] (undated, ~2/2022). That result directly contradicts the reflexive assumption that synchronous CUDA always costs performance, and Stas Bekman — who was on that team — has carried the same anecdote into his own current-day debugging doctrine (below).

PaLM's spike-handling protocol is the field's most-cited historical citation, and both OPT and BLOOM's own practices descend from it directly: roughly 20 spikes over the full 540B run, handled by restarting from a checkpoint ~100 steps before the spike and skipping 200–500 data batches [PaLM: Scaling Language Modeling with Pathways](https://arxiv.org/abs/2204.02311) [PAPER] (2022). The detail most secondary sources drop is PaLM's own ablation: replaying the exact spike-triggering batches from a *different* earlier checkpoint did not reproduce the spike — evidence that spikes are a joint function of optimizer state and data, not a property of either alone (cross-reference Ch7's optimizer-stability treatment for the mechanistic side of this).

No frontier commercial lab — DeepSeek, post-Llama-3 Meta, OpenAI, Google, Anthropic, xAI — has published anything resembling a day-by-day diary since roughly 2022, though the underlying research corpus flags this as a plausible-but-unconfirmed absence rather than a proven negative: no equivalent chronicle was located for any of these labs' recent model families, but that could reflect a search-coverage gap as easily as genuine non-disclosure. DeepSeek's entire public stability disclosure across V3/V3.2/V4 is a one-line "zero loss spikes" claim (see Ch7). The genre now survives almost exclusively in the open/academic tier, and its 2025–2026 instances show the same texture at radically different reliability baselines. HuggingFace's "Smol Training Playbook" documents SmolLM3 (3B params, 11T tokens, 384 H100s) restarting its entire main run after burning 1T tokens (46,080 GPU-hours) because every GPU in a tensor-parallel group had been initialized with the identical random seed rather than `seed + tp_rank` — a bug invisible in the loss curve, caught only via a downstream eval regression against the model's own smaller predecessor [Smol Training Playbook](https://huggingfacetb-smol-training-playbook.hf.space) [LOGBOOK] (10/2025). Total ablation/debugging compute for that project (161,280 GPU-hours) exceeded the main run's own 276,480 GPU-hours by more than half — a ratio worth internalizing when budgeting a 10T run's total compute envelope, not just its "final" FLOPs. Marin's 32B retrospective is 2025's closest analogue to the OPT/BLOOM chronicle style, and it is the best evidence in the corpus that architectural intervention, not more monitoring, is sometimes the only fix: three separate numerics-level interventions at ~80,000 steps (tighter grad-clip, a clip-update-norm filter, a 2σ skip-bad-steps filter) all failed to stop recurring spikes; the actual fix was a mid-flight swap of the attention backbone to a QK-Norm architecture, warm-started from the old checkpoint, which imposed a one-time loss penalty that recovered within ~10B tokens [Marin 32B retrospective](https://marin.readthedocs.io) [LOGBOOK] (2025). The same retrospective documents a failed mid-run Muon swap that produced *temporary* loss relief before the model began performing what the team describes as gradient ascent — a cautionary counter-example for anyone assuming DeepSeek's from-scratch AdamW→Muon architectural choice (Ch7) implies a live optimizer swap is equally safe. And Ai2's OLMo Hybrid run (512 B200s, 6.19 days, 3T tokens, Dec 25–31, 2025) reports 97% uptime (99% excluding scheduled development windows) with a median incident-recovery time of 3 minutes 42 seconds under a "fail fast" philosophy of automated GPU health-checking and quarantine-and-restart rather than degraded continuation [Open model, open metrics](https://lambda.ai/blog/open-model-open-metrics) [OFFICIAL-BLOG] (~12/2025–1/2026) — a striking, dated, production before/after against OPT's 2021 reality of hours-long manual node replacement and a 12–18-node buffer pool.

## The Incident Taxonomy: Five Classes, and What 2026 Automated Away

Reading ten-plus logbooks side by side — OPT, BLOOM, PaLM, GPT-NeoX-20B, Pythia, OLMo 2/Hybrid, SmolLM3, Marin, INTELLECT-1/3 — five incident classes recur, and they have aged in sharply different ways.

| Class | Present in | 2026 status | Evidence |
|---|---|---|---|
| Raw hardware failure (GPU crash, NCCL hang, node loss) | Every logbook without exception | **Solved end-to-end.** Sub-5-minute automated recovery, no human paged | ByteRobust: 30s/10s/2s detection latencies across 200K GPUs [ByteRobust](https://arxiv.org/abs/2509.16293) [PAPER] (9/2025); FT-HSDP: ~3-minute stall at 100K GPUs [FT-HSDP](https://arxiv.org/abs/2602.00277) [PAPER] (2/2026); OLMo Hybrid's production 3m42s median matches both |
| Numerics instability (spikes, NaN, loss-scale underflow) | OPT, BLOOM, PaLM, Pythia, OLMo 2, Marin | **Partially automated**, with a ceiling. Default mitigation should be cheap and automatic, but needs a pre-authorized escalation trigger | AdaGC drives spike score to zero at Llama2-7B/Mixtral-scale [AdaGC](https://arxiv.org/abs/2502.11034) [PAPER] (2/2025); DeepSeek V3/K2/V4 report zero-spike runs (Ch7); Marin's three numerics-level fixes all failed and needed an architecture swap |
| Data-pipeline bugs (contamination, shuffling pathologies, repeated n-grams) | OPT, OLMo 2, Marin | **Only partially automated**; terminal detection mechanism is still a human noticing an anomalous eval score | Marin's GSM8k contamination via a cached Dolmino bundle required a full second 32k-step cooldown re-run to fix |
| Silent software/framework correctness bugs (non-crashing) | BLOOM (×2), SmolLM3, OPT | **Unchanged since 2021.** No threshold to alert on by construction | BLOOM's TP-grad-clip bug, SmolLM3's TP-seed bug, OPT's unexplained Megatron-dependency-bump — all caught only by a human noticing something subtly off, days later |
| Human/process/organizational | OPT, BLOOM, OLMo | **Not a tooling problem.** Requires explicit organizational design | Independently reinvented sentinel-file kill-switches (OPT and BLOOM, unrelated infrastructure, identical no-`sudo` constraint) |

The delta on hardware fault-tolerance is the single largest reliability-engineering improvement visible across this whole corpus: OPT's 2021 reality was a 12–18-node buffer pool and hours of manual cloud-console replacement; a production run in December 2025–January 2026 achieves sub-4-minute median automated recovery (see Ch19 for the checkpoint and replication mechanics underneath this). The class that has **not** moved is (d) — silent, non-crashing correctness bugs. None of BLOOM's grad-clip bug, SmolLM3's seed bug, or OPT's unexplained dependency-bump crashed training or crossed any monitored threshold; all were caught by a human noticing a downstream eval regression, a checkpoint-merge-time concern, or plain curiosity about an unexplained throughput bump, and all required multi-day forensic work — checkpoint tensor diffing, cross-node `py-spy` comparison, dependency bisection. There is no evidence anywhere in this corpus that 2026 tooling has compressed the detection-to-diagnosis time for this class at all, and INTELLECT-3's newly emerging training/inference policy-mismatch crashes in async RL — where inference and training policies silently diverge in token probabilities and only manifest as a crash "multiple days into the experiments" [INTELLECT-3 Technical Report](https://arxiv.org/abs/2512.16144) [PAPER] (12/2025) — suggest the class is growing, not shrinking, as systems get more complex. The organizational lesson for class (b): build automated spike suppression as the default cheap first response, but pre-agree an explicit escalation trigger ("if spikes recur after N automated interventions across M checkpoints, convene an architecture review") before the run starts, because a team without that pre-agreement will burn calendar time re-litigating the decision live, exactly as Marin's saga did across three numbered phases.

## Bare-Metal War Stories: Bring-Up Gauntlets and Bandwidth-Constrained Labs

Imbue's public bring-up of a 4,088-H100/511-node cluster is the fullest documented "how do you actually stand up a non-blocking fabric" gauntlet available [From bare metal to a 70B model](https://imbue.com/blog/70b-infrastructure) [OFFICIAL-BLOG] (6/2024). The quantified failure taxonomy is directly transferable: ~10% of machines failed to boot on the first attempt (mostly physical — miswired ethernet, dead PSUs, missing cabling); a baseline ~3%/week breakage rate once running, but explicitly **not uniform** — a small population of "malcontent" machines break repeatedly, which reframes fleet-reliability planning away from a Poisson-failure model and toward "identify and quarantine the bad 5%"; ~25% of hosts initially ran PCIe at x4 instead of x16; the initial InfiniBand fabric was misdesigned as eight disjoint networks with no common routing paths and had to be completely rewired. Imbue's most operationally useful artifact is a lookup table mapping MFU signature to root cause: immediate <10% of expected throughput means a dead switch or a host stuck at PCIe x4; stable ~30% MFU means GDR misconfigured on a single host; stable 60–80% MFU means degraded IB links forcing NVLink fallback or CPU throttling; periodic ~10x dips every ~15 minutes mean other CPU-heavy workloads sharing a host or a dataloader bottleneck; gradual throughput decay across a run means unsynchronized Python garbage collection stalling a synchronous FSDP step across all workers. This table is worth reproducing wholesale as a diagnostic reference for any 100k-GPU on-call rotation, since it converts a throughput number alone into a short list of hypotheses before anyone touches `py-spy`.

Character.AI's story is the inverse of every other lab surveyed in this beat: rather than assume adequate bandwidth and worry about failures, their largest pretraining cluster ran at "only one-quarter the bandwidth of state-of-the-art systems" [blog.character.ai/squinch](https://blog.character.ai/squinch) [OFFICIAL-BLOG] (2024), which directly motivated Squinch, a custom 6-bit block-wise gradient-compression scheme built specifically to make FSDP viable on a quarter-bandwidth fabric. For a 100k-GB200 build this is not the binding constraint (see Ch18), but the underlying discipline — profile the actual constraint you have, not the one the literature assumes — is the transferable lesson. Zyphra's public "training cookbook" is candid that it discloses no GPU counts, no cluster topology, and no MFU numbers; its real content is a reusable annealing/LR-schedule playbook (Ch12 covers this in the curriculum context) built by a roughly seven-person team running Zamba/Zamba2 on 128 H100s over 30–50 days [Zyphra Training Cookbook](https://www.zyphra.com/post/the-zyphra-training-cookbook) [OFFICIAL-BLOG/CODE] (2024) — a reminder that headline infrastructure disclosure and operationally useful disclosure are frequently two different documents from the same lab.

Yi Tay's account of building Reka with fewer than five people on pretraining is the sharpest cross-cloud reliability claim in the corpus: a UL2-20B run at Google once completed a full month with zero infrastructure failures purely by accident, whereas "if this were in GPU land, it would have failed within the first few days for sure" — a claim he attributes explicitly to Google's hardware-operations maturity rather than any fundamental TPU-vs-GPU silicon advantage [yitay.net](https://www.yitay.net/blog/training-great-llms-entirely-from-ground-zero-in-the-wilderness) [OFFICIAL-BLOG] (3/2024). Databricks' one disclosed DBRX war story reinforces the same lesson from the opposite direction: a misconfigured network driver caused a 5x slowdown on a 3,072-H100 cluster, caught only via monitoring granular enough to flag a non-crashing regression rather than a node death [Introducing DBRX](https://www.databricks.com/blog/introducing-dbrx-new-state-art-open-llm) [OFFICIAL-BLOG] (3/2024) — the same MFU-signature-not-crash-signature diagnostic problem Imbue's table is built to solve. Snowflake's Arctic Cookbook quantifies a related, easy-to-underestimate cost center at MoE scale: before kernel-level optimization (RoPE fusion +5–8%, SwiGLU fusion +8%, a fused MoE-gating kernel 2.2x faster for top-2 routing), the baseline all-to-all communication overhead consumed **more than 50% of step time** on their 480B-total/17B-active model [Snowflake Arctic Cookbook Series](https://medium.com/snowflake/snowflake-arctic-cookbook-series-building-an-efficient-training-system-for-arctic-6658b9bdfcae) [OFFICIAL-BLOG] (2024) — a number worth holding up against Ch16's parallelism math for the actual 10T/200B EP design. Apple's AXLearn repository, finally, is the one place in this entire research corpus with a directly measured, apples-to-apples cross-silicon step-time table sitting in a code comment above a 7B model's `mesh_rules` block — TPU v4, TPU v5e, TPU v5p, and H100 (AWS p5) timings for one fixed config [github.com/apple/axlearn](https://github.com/apple/axlearn/blob/main/axlearn/experiments/text/gpt/fuji.py) [CODE] (accessed 7/2026) — a level of disclosure no tech report or conference talk anywhere else in this fleet's research matches, and a reminder that some of the most load-bearing operational facts live in engineering repos, not papers.

## The Debugging Doctrine: Bekman's Toolbox at 100k-GPU Scale

Stas Bekman's two open books — `the-art-of-debugging` and `ml-engineering`, both actively maintained through the July 2026 commit window this research was gathered in — are the closest thing to a codified operational doctrine that this corpus contains, drawn from first-hand experience leading BLOOM-176B and IDEFICS-80B and current work at Snowflake AI Research on ArcticTraining. The core methodology is a five-step loop — reproduce reliably, shrink the payload, localize, get a real signal, change one thing — built on two compounding needs: debug cycles must be fast and the payload must be small, because slow cycles over large payloads cause an engineer to lose track of which hypothesis was actually tested [the-art-of-debugging](https://github.com/stas00/the-art-of-debugging) [CODE] (2023–2026). A companion instruction aimed explicitly at AI debugging agents — "observe before guessing": obtain a stack dump, traceback, syscall trace, or tensor stat before proposing a root cause, never speculate from an error string alone — is worth adopting as literal on-call policy independent of who or what is doing the debugging.

The fleet-scale hang-forensics tool is `py-spy`, scaled through four explicit transports: single-process attach, multi-process fan-out on one node via `pgrep` chains, multi-node via SLURM `srun` (`--gres=gpu:0` is load-bearing — omit it and the monitoring `srun` deadlocks waiting for the GPU allocation held by the job it's trying to inspect), and `pdsh`/`ds_ssh` as SLURM-independent alternatives [ml-engineering](https://github.com/stas00/ml-engineering) [CODE] (07/2026). Bekman's own largest documented worked example caps at 40 nodes; there is no evidence in either repo of testing beyond that. This is a genuine, acknowledged blind spot relative to a 100k-GPU/12,500-node fleet. The `srun --nodes=N --tasks-per-node=1` fan-out pattern is architecturally O(N) and SLURM is designed to handle multi-thousand-node launch trees, so the mechanism itself is unlikely to be the bottleneck — but aggregation and triage become the real problem: 12,500 `trace-%N.out` files are not human-`grep`-able the way 40-node examples are, and no first-party tooling exists anywhere in this corpus for automatically clustering stack traces by "same top frame" to spot the diagnostic signature of a genuine collective-comm deadlock (N-1 ranks stuck in the same NCCL call, one rank elsewhere). Building that aggregation layer — a `py-spy`-diff/histogram tool operating on tens of thousands of per-node dumps, not a human running `grep` — is squarely build-in-house territory for a 10T-MoE operation and belongs on the Ch22 gap list. A second, subtler trap sits inside the tool itself: two ranks stuck at different iterations of the same `for` loop report the *identical* line number in a `py-spy` dump, since it's literally the same source line, making a genuine desync look like a uniform hang unless the loop is manually unrolled first (`next(d_iter)` called explicitly per iteration) so each step gets a distinct line. This is a real, non-obvious false negative in the exact "dump every rank, diff by line" diagnostic the doctrine itself prescribes as primary.

`ml-engineering`'s `debug/pytorch.md` (3,849 lines, now merged with `the-art-of-debugging`'s equivalent content as of a July 2026 consolidation commit) documents a catalog of silent, non-obvious performance and correctness bugs worth treating as a pre-flight checklist rather than tribal knowledge rediscovered under fire. Since SLURM 22.05, `srun` no longer inherits `--cpus-per-task` from `sbatch`/`salloc` — it must be re-passed explicitly or via `SRUN_CPUS_PER_TASK`, or DataLoader workers silently starve down to a single CPU core per task, a regression invisible to any GPU-side monitoring and testable with a one-line `len(os.sched_getaffinity(0))` check. Separately, and orchestrator-agnostic: enabling Hyper-Threading on AWS p4 nodes made NCCL all-reduce throughput **4x slower**, the opposite of Bekman's experience on other clusters — the lesson generalizes to "test HT on vs. off empirically per instance-type/NIC-driver combination, never assume a default is correct" [ml-engineering, orchestration/slurm/performance.md](https://github.com/stas00/ml-engineering) [CODE] (07/2026). A third catalog entry with real 10T-MoE relevance: `all_gather_object` (Python-pickle-based, CPU-mediated) is both a documented PyTorch memory leak and 23x slower than `all_reduce` for the common "is everyone done" flag-synchronization pattern used in fault-tolerance signaling — a double reason to ban it from any hot-path status-sync code (see Ch19). A fourth, purely empirical finding worth flagging for a coherent-memory GB200/GB300 fleet: `nvidia-smi`/NVML cannot report GPU memory usage at all on DGX Spark-class coherent-memory hardware, and by default Linux silently lets CPU allocations borrow unused GPU HBM in one direction only — undocumented behavior Bekman discovered empirically and reported to NVIDIA without resolution as of this writing, fixable only by forcing CDMM mode via a modprobe parameter and reboot.

Three purpose-built pre-flight test scripts recur across Bekman's SLURM chapters and are worth lifting verbatim into any 100k-GPU admission-control gate rather than treating as reactive debug tools: a CUDA-availability check (`torch.cuda.is_available()` fanned out and reporting only failures), a "Broken NCCL" test (a minimal two-node NCCL `barrier()` wrapped in try/except with serialized per-rank output), and a GPU memory allocation check (attempt to allocate just under the card's full capacity, catching allocation failures per-GPU before a real job starts). The explicit process note attached to all three — "report the faulty nodes so they get replaced," since excluding a bad node from your own job is a workaround, not a fix — is a cheap organizational discipline worth codifying, especially given the SemiAnalysis-sourced burn-in data both repos cite: new accelerator batches show a 3–10% failure rate at delivery absent adequate burn-in, and accelerators account for roughly 95% of Bekman's own observed hardware failures.

## Scheduler and Ops Layer: SLURM, Kubernetes, and the FairShare Gap

Bekman's stated opinion is unambiguous: "IMHO kubernetes is the wrong environment for doing training work," citing loss of per-user Unix accountability, missing POSIX primitives, and higher operational headcount cost, while acknowledging SLURM-on-Kubernetes wrapper projects (SchedMD's Slinky, Nebius's Soperator, CoreWeave's unreleased SUNK) as the emerging compromise for orgs forced onto K8s-based fleets [ml-engineering](https://github.com/stas00/ml-engineering) [CODE] (07/2026). Most of the SLURM-specific operational patterns transfer conceptually even where the implementation does not: kill-switch/save-switch file-polling maps onto a K8s-native distributed KV-store flag; SIGUSR1-based graceful-preemption exit (with the crucial nuance that a `B:`-prefixed signal must route to the `sbatch` shell and be forwarded to the launcher's children, since `torchrun`/`accelerate`/`deepspeed` don't propagate `SIGUSR1` by default) maps onto Kubernetes' `preStop` hook plus `terminationGracePeriodSeconds`; automated benign-drain recovery and rolling maintenance reboots that respect running-job boundaries map onto a node-health-controller pattern. One SLURM pattern is strictly obsolete on K8s in a good way — SLURM's crontab emulation (`sbatch --begin=now+1hour` self-resubmission, chosen specifically because neither SLURM nor most training clusters ship native cron) is fully subsumed by K8s's first-class `CronJob` resource.

The one genuine capability gap is FairShare. SLURM's `sshare`-reported score — above 0.5 meaning under-allocation-usage (higher scheduling priority, lower preemption risk), below 0.5 meaning the opposite, decaying over time, with relative rank mattering more than the absolute number — has no drop-in Kubernetes equivalent. Kueue and Volcano offer static priority classes, not a decaying-usage-based scheduling-priority algorithm; a multi-team 100k-GPU K8s fleet needs to source or build this capability explicitly, not assume it comes free with the orchestrator switch. This is worth flagging directly for Ch22, since it is one of the few items in this entire operational survey that is a genuine build-vs-buy gap rather than a reimplementation exercise [ml-engineering, orchestration/slurm/users.md](https://github.com/stas00/ml-engineering) [CODE] (07/2026). On the hardware-diagnostic layer beneath either scheduler, nothing changes: Xid-error/DCGM RMA playbooks (`dcgmi diag -r 2/3/4` to catch silent data corruption, GPU-UUID tracking to detect "same broken node again") are vendor-specific, not orchestrator-specific, and Kubernetes' own `memory.oom.group=1` default (≥1.28) — which kills an entire pod's process group on a single CPU-OOM'd process rather than just the offending rank, catastrophic for interactive debugging where the goal is recovering one crashed rank without losing the whole job's logs — needs the `singleProcessOOMKill: true` kubelet flag (1.32+) set explicitly on any K8s-orchestrated 10T-MoE deployment [ml-engineering, compute/accelerator/nvidia/debug.md + orchestration/kubernetes/README.md](https://github.com/stas00/ml-engineering) [CODE] (07/2026).

## War-Room Design, On-Call, and the Six-Month Operational Readiness Checklist

Two independent teams working on completely unrelated infrastructure — Meta's OPT team on cloud-provider hardware, BigScience's BLOOM team on the Jean Zay HPC — converged on the identical solution to an identical constraint: neither SLURM cluster gave any concept of Unix-group job ownership, so a colleague's hung, unattended job could only be killed by its owner or a sysadmin holding `sudo`, and both teams independently built a sentinel-file-triggered graceful-exit kill switch as the fix. That convergence is itself the strongest evidence in this corpus that this is a predictable, generalizable organizational failure mode for any large shared cluster — one to design for on day one rather than rediscover mid-incident. Both teams also made the cost of downtime an explicit, stated operational motivator rather than an implicit assumption: OPT's on-call rotation of named volunteers paired with shadowing engineers was framed around "each hour the experiment stagnates or goes down costs us $$" [OPT-175B chronicles, 56_percent_update.md](https://github.com/facebookresearch/metaseq/tree/main/projects/OPT/chronicles) [LOGBOOK] (12/16/2021). And a specific, recurring pattern across both logbooks — major incidents clustering around holidays and weekends when senior engineers are hardest to reach (OPT's Thanksgiving and Christmas incidents, BLOOM's weekend throughput hunt and overnight eval hang) — argues for treating holiday-period coverage as a first-class rotation-design input, not an afterthought, a lesson OLMo Hybrid's own Dec 25–31, 2025 production run structurally echoed three years later.

Synthesizing the logbook practices that demonstrably worked, the debugging doctrine's pre-flight discipline, and the ops-layer transfer analysis above into a readiness checklist for a six-month, 100,000-GPU run:

- **Indexed, queryable per-step telemetry from step 1** — BLOOM's raw per-iteration scalar set (tokens consumed, LR, batch size, loss, grad norm, TFLOPs, skipped/NaN iteration counts) is still the right minimum schema; at 10T-token scale it must live in a time-series store, not a text log a human tails, because BLOOM's own workflow of manually running `perl` one-liners over dumped stack traces is infeasible past a few hundred nodes.
- **Archive before/after checkpoints around every detected spike**, not just the rolling checkpoint — BLOOM's practice of preserving `global_step31200`/`global_step31259` around its one spike cost negligible storage and would be the first artifact any root-cause investigation needs.
- **Automated hardware-fault recovery as a hard SLA (sub-5-minute), not a research target** — the technology exists and is production-validated (ByteRobust, FT-HSDP, OLMo Hybrid); re-creating OPT's or BLOOM's manual-heroics era for this incident class is not acceptable at this scale.
- **A pre-authorized, bounded escalation path for numerics instability** — automated spike suppression as the default first response, with an explicit, pre-agreed trigger ("N automated interventions across M checkpoints without convergence → architecture review") so the org doesn't re-litigate the decision live under a live incident, as Marin's team was forced to.
- **A standing, cheap "does this look right" eval cadence faster than the full benchmark suite**, specifically to shorten the detection lag on data-pipeline and silent-correctness-bug classes, which this entire corpus shows are still caught by a human noticing an anomaly, sometimes weeks later.
- **A byte-level checkpoint-diffing tool built and tested before it's needed**, not reinvented under fire — BLOOM's custom weight-dump-and-diff instrumentation is the template.
- **The three orchestrator-agnostic pre-flight scripts (CUDA-available, NCCL-barrier, GPU-memory-headroom) wired into node admission control**, gating every job allocation, not run reactively after a suspected bad node.
- **Imbue's MFU-signature-to-root-cause table posted in the war room**, since it converts a throughput number alone into a short, ranked hypothesis list before anyone opens a debugger.
- **A purpose-built aggregated stack-dump collector for hang forensics beyond ~100 nodes**, since no lab in this corpus has open-sourced one and Bekman's own tooling caps at 40 — build this before the first unexplained 12,500-node hang, not during it.
- **A kill-switch mechanism that works independent of who holds cluster-admin privilege**, and a named on-call rotation with real cross-timezone and holiday-period backup coverage, with cost-of-downtime stated explicitly as the motivating metric.
- **A dated internal incident diary maintained as a living artifact from day one** — every team in this corpus that kept one used it as its own best debugging tool during the run and its best postmortem material afterward; a 10T-parameter run producing no equivalent artifact discards this genre's single highest-leverage lesson.

## Implications for the 10T/200B Run

The operational floor this chapter establishes is that hardware fault-tolerance — the incident class every 2021–2022 logbook treats as the dominant daily burden — is now a solved engineering problem at sub-5-minute recovery, and a 100k-GPU program that fails to hit that bar has under-invested relative to 2025–2026 production baselines (Ch19 carries the checkpoint and replication mechanics that make this possible). The unsolved floor is everywhere else: silent correctness bugs still require a human, days later, noticing something subtly off, at exactly the rate they did in 2021; numerics instability has an automated first line of defense but a hard ceiling that Marin's 2025 experience shows can only be crossed by a pre-authorized architecture review, not more clipping; and fleet-scale hang forensics has no public precedent past 40 nodes, meaning the aggregation tooling a 100,000-GPU on-call rotation actually needs does not exist off the shelf and belongs on the build list in Ch22 alongside the FairShare-equivalent scheduling gap. None of this is exotic engineering — every individual piece (kill switches, pre-flight gates, spike-escalation triggers, an incident diary) is cheap and well-understood in isolation. What the logbook record shows, repeatedly and across labs with no contact with one another, is that the organizations who did this work before the run started paid a fraction of the cost of the organizations who improvised it during an active incident.
