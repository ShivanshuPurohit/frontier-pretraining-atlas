# Chapter 2 — What the Closed Labs Let Slip

The six labs that matter for this book's purposes — OpenAI, Anthropic, Google DeepMind, Meta, xAI, and Microsoft AI — have collectively disclosed more about their pretraining infrastructure across 2024-2026 than at any point since GPT-3, but almost none of it through the channel a reader would expect. Model cards stayed thin; the substance arrived through OCP networking papers, AWS product-launch blogs, sworn court testimony, a podcast where a chief scientist forgot to be careful, and one extraordinarily well-sourced paid newsletter that got a single Meta post-mortem section right when everyone else missed it. This chapter assembles that scattered record lab by lab, dates every figure, and flags where the evidentiary floor gives way to hearsay.

## OpenAI

OpenAI's richest disclosure of its actual production fabric came not from a model announcement but from a joint networking-industry publication. In May 2026, OpenAI co-developed **MRC (Multipath Reliable Connection)** with AMD, Broadcom, Intel, Microsoft, and Nvidia, publishing it as an Open Compute Project contribution, "Resilient AI Supercomputer Networking using MRC and SRv6" [OpenAI, "Supercomputer networking to accelerate large scale AI training"](https://openai.com/index/mrc-supercomputer-networking/) [OFFICIAL-BLOG], May 2026 (direct fetch 403'd; corroborated via [MarkTechPost](https://www.marktechpost.com/2026/05/07/openai-introduces-mrc-multipath-reliable-connection-a-new-open-networking-protocol-for-large-scale-ai-supercomputer-training-clusters/) [ANALYSIS] and a Towards Data Science deep-dive). MRC extends RoCE with SRv6 source routing developed alongside Ultra Ethernet Consortium techniques: each 800 Gb/s NIC splits into eight independent 100 Gb/s "planes," spraying a single flow's packets across all eight rather than relying on ECMP hashing. Routing state lives at the NIC — 128-256 Entropy Values per Queue Pair — not in switches, which hold **zero forwarding state** versus 100,000+ FIB entries conventionally; health-assessment cycles run at 1-2ms, giving convergence "1,000 to 30,000x faster" than traditional routing. OpenAI deliberately disabled switch-layer adaptive routing "because [they] didn't want two adaptive routing mechanisms interacting," and dropped PFC for lossy Ethernet with selective ACKs and packet trimming. The topology is two-tier, supporting "131,072 GPUs at full bisection bandwidth" on roughly 2/3 the optics and 3/5 the switches of a conventional 3-tier design, with a worst-case path length of 3 hops versus 5-7. It runs across Oracle's Stargate Abilene site and Microsoft's Fairwater Atlanta/Wisconsin sites, with production evidence of jobs surviving simultaneous transceiver failures and core-switch reboots without interruption — the closest thing the closed frontier has produced to a systems paper (Ch18).

The GPT-4.5 "Orion" retrospective is the other major seam. In "Pre-Training GPT-4.5" [OFFICIAL-VIDEO, YouTube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=6nJZopACRuQ), April 11, 2025, Sam Altman interviewed Chief Systems Architect Amin Tootoonchian, pretraining lead Alex Paino, and data-efficiency lead Daniel Selsam. No direct transcript was recoverable; the following triangulates three independent secondary write-ups that substantially agree. The run scaled from roughly 10,000 GPUs (GPT-4-class) to **over 100,000 GPUs**, introducing qualitatively new failure modes and forcing a shift to multi-cluster state management — the closest corroboration for cross-datacenter framing at OpenAI, though no source gives an inter-cluster bandwidth/latency figure, so treat the multi-cluster claim as [CREDIBLE-HEARSAY, paraphrase]. The now-famous bug: a PyTorch `torch.sum` edge case surfaced as "illegal memory access" errors roughly once per 100-1,000 steps, reproducible only via a specific Triton-kernel/torch-fallback interaction, generating a spray of seemingly unrelated symptoms; the team ranked hypotheses by vote and the least-favored one turned out correct — once patched, "all varied error symptoms disappeared simultaneously." The most substantively important line, repeated near-verbatim across sources: **"we're no longer compute constrained on the best models we can produce."** One source frames the human-vs-model data-efficiency gap at "100,000x" — [CREDIBLE-HEARSAY, imprecise]; a more careful paraphrase states the underlying hypothesis: "the volume of high-quality training data isn't growing as fast as compute... meaningful concepts in data are sparse and power-law distributed." GPT-4.5 is framed as roughly 10x GPT-4's pretraining compute, requiring "hundreds of people" over roughly two years, against a claim that a GPT-4-class model could now be reproduced by "5-10 people."

Independent reporting sharpens the Orion picture. WSJ-sourced coverage (via Fortune, Feb 25, 2025) [REPORTED-BY:WSJ] establishes OpenAI ran **at least two large training attempts** for what became GPT-4.5, each spanning several months, with language-task gains landing but coding/math gains falling short; Sutskever, same coverage: "The data is not growing because we have but one internet." A six-month run of this class is estimated at roughly **half a billion dollars in compute alone**, with a "GPT-5-scale" cloud-compute figure floated near **$1 billion**. Epoch AI's tracker, pulled July 12, 2026, still ranks **GPT-4.5 at 3.8e26 FLOP ("Likely")** as OpenAI's best-evidenced run, with **GPT-5 at only 6.6e25 FLOP ("Speculative")** — a 5-6x reduction, the opposite of the historical ~100x-per-generation pattern [Epoch AI, `notable_ai_models.csv`](https://epoch.ai) [EPOCH-ESTIMATE]. Epoch attributes this to a September 2024 RL/reasoning breakthrough letting cheap post-training substitute for expensive pretraining, pricing GPT-4.5 at "$200M pretrain + $2M post-train" against GPT-5's "~$20M pretrain" [Epoch AI, "Why GPT-5 used less training compute than GPT-4.5"](https://epoch.ai/gradient-updates/why-gpt5-used-less-training-compute-than-gpt45-but-gpt6-probably-wont) [EPOCH-ESTIMATE/ANALYSIS]. No OpenAI model since GPT-4.5 has an Epoch FLOP estimate at all as of the July 2026 pull. A persistent single-hop rumor sourced to SemiAnalysis via Hacker News claims OpenAI "has not completed a successful full-scale pre-training run... since GPT-4o in May 2024" [RUMOR, single-hop-sourced] — undercut by GPT-4.5's undisputed status as a real new pretrain; treat with skepticism. Compelled testimony offers a rare non-PR-controlled data point: OpenAI President Greg Brockman testified under oath in *Musk v. OpenAI* (Bloomberg, May 5, 2026) that OpenAI **plans to spend $50 billion on computing power in 2026**, up from "tens of billions of dollars" recently and "roughly $30 million in 2017" [REPORTED-BY:Bloomberg, sourced to sworn testimony].

Stargate is a $500B program targeting **>9 GW by 2029** across seven US sites, framed against "20 million H100-equivalents" of global 2025 AI compute [Epoch AI, "OpenAI Stargate: where the US sites stand"](https://epoch.ai/publications/openai-stargate-where-the-us-sites-stand) [ANALYSIS], April 2026. Abilene sat at roughly 0.3 GW / ~250,000 H100-equivalents as of April 2026, targeting 1.2 GW across 8 buildings by Q4 2026 [REPORTED-BY:DCD/Epoch aggregation]. Larry Ellison stated Abilene will "eventually house more than 450,000 Nvidia GB200 GPUs" at full buildout — an Oracle claim, not OpenAI's [OFFICIAL-STATEMENT, Oracle exec via DCD], June 2026. OpenAI **declined a planned 600 MW/2.1 GW Abilene expansion** in early-to-mid 2026, redirecting capacity to avoid mixing Blackwell with incoming Vera Rubin hardware on one site [Tom's Hardware, citing DCD/industry reporting] — a disclosed engineering decision worth noting for multi-year buildout staging.

## Anthropic

Anthropic's compute disclosure lives entirely in partnership press releases, never in model/system cards — a deliberate, consistent policy across every Claude generation. Project Rainier, AWS's Trainium2 buildout, is disclosed at **"nearly half a million" Trainium2 chips**, "70% larger than any other AI computing platform in AWS history," organized into EC2 UltraClusters of 64-chip UltraServers (4 servers × 16 chips, NeuronLink intra-server, EFA inter-server), giving Anthropic "more than five times the compute power" of its prior setup [AWS, "AWS activates Project Rainier"](https://www.aboutamazon.com/news/aws/aws-project-rainier-ai-trainium-chips-compute-cluster) [OFFICIAL-BLOG], ~Oct 2025. Amazon's later corroboration clarifies "~500K" describes Rainier specifically; Anthropic was tracking toward **"more than 1 million" Trainium2 chips for training and inference combined** across Rainier plus other AWS datacenters by end of 2026 [OFFICIAL-BLOG:Amazon, REPORTED-BY:Yahoo Finance]. Trainium3, unveiled at AWS re:Invent (Dec 2025), specs at **2.52 PFLOPS MXFP8/chip**, 144GB HBM3e, 144-chip UltraServers at 362 PFLOPS, and a stated path to "up to 1 million Trainium3 chips" in one application — 40% more energy-efficient than Trainium2 [AWS re:Invent 2025](https://www.aboutamazon.com/news/aws/aws-re-invent-2025-ai-news-updates) [OFFICIAL-BLOG]; Anthropic is named an early adopter. Anthropic's own framing commits "more than $100 billion over the next ten years" to AWS, securing "up to 5GW," with "nearly 1GW total of Trainium2 and Trainium3" targeted online by end of 2026 [Anthropic, official blog](https://www.anthropic.com/news/anthropic-amazon-compute) [OFFICIAL-BLOG].

The parallel Google relationship, announced Oct 23, 2025, discloses "up to one million TPUs," "well over a gigawatt" online in 2026, at a deal value of "tens of billions of dollars" [Anthropic, "Expanding our use of Google Cloud TPUs and Services"](https://www.anthropic.com/news/expanding-our-use-of-google-cloud-tpus-and-services) [OFFICIAL-BLOG]. A more granular reconstruction surfaces in the SemiAnalysis TPUv7 corpus: Phase 1 = 400k TPUv7 Ironwoods bought outright (~$10B, Broadcom-brokered), Phase 2 = 600k rented via GCP (~$42B of RPO); Google's equity stake in Anthropic is capped at 15% with no voting rights to preserve arm's-length hardware dealing; Anthropic's effective cost is estimated at **~$1.60/TPU-hour** [SemiAnalysis, "Google TPUv7: The 900lb Gorilla In the Room"] [CREDIBLE-HEARSAY, SA-PAID], Nov 28, 2025. The same source computes the economics justifying Anthropic's multi-vendor bet: a ~30% Nvidia training-MFU rule of thumb means Google/Anthropic need only ~15-19% TPU MFU to match GB300's cost-per-effective-FLOP; Anthropic's realized ~40% MFU implies roughly 52% lower TCO per effective PFLOP than GB300 NVL72 once GCP's margin is included. By mid-2026, at least three non-reconciling dollar/GW figures circulate for the cumulative relationship — the Oct 2025 official framing, an April 7, 2026 Google-Broadcom follow-on adding "3.5 GW starting 2027" [REPORTED-BY:TechCrunch, 2026-04-07], and Bloomberg's May 18, 2026 cumulative framing of "up to $40 billion... 5 gigawatts... up to one million Ironwood chips" [REPORTED-BY:Bloomberg] — illustrating that even official disclosures are tranche-by-tranche snapshots, not a ledger.

Anthropic's system cards (Claude Sonnet 4.5, Sept 2025, and earlier) consistently state customer conversations are not used for training by default, while pretraining pipelines draw on web crawl, licensed third-party data, Anthropic-generated synthetic data, and — recently — opted-in user data [Anthropic system cards, secondary summarization via Simon Willison's blog and Stanford CRFM's Foundation Model Transparency Index] [OFFICIAL]. No GPU/TPU/Trainium counts, FLOPs, or energy figures appear in any Claude system card; that information lives exclusively in the partnership-announcement channel.

Dario Amodei's Dwarkesh interview, "We are near the end of the exponential" [Dwarkesh Podcast](https://www.dwarkesh.com/p/dario-amodei-2) [OFFICIAL-INTERVIEW], gives the clearest CEO-level macro numbers: "The amount of compute the industry is building this year is probably, call it, 10-15 gigawatts. It goes up by roughly 3x a year." On training-inference split: "Let's say half of your compute is for training and half of your compute is for inference" — an industry generalization, not Anthropic-specific. On cost: "Each gigawatt costs maybe $10 billion, on the order of $10-15 billion a year." Amodei votes for "raw compute, data quantity, data quality/distribution, training duration, scalable objective functions, and numerical stability" over "novel algorithmic techniques" — a bitter-lesson stance in direct tension with Sutskever's "age of research" framing below — while declining to give Anthropic's exact spend beyond "comparable to what the biggest players in the game are buying." Separately, Sholto Douglas (Anthropic, ex-DeepMind) told Dwarkesh that Anthropic's RL compute spend was still only **~$1 million** and the team was deliberately not compute-limited on RL — a "tech tree" strategy of buying algorithmic certainty before committing large compute [SPOKEN-CLAIM] — a striking contrast to 2026's RL-dominant allocations.

## Google DeepMind

The single largest recent infrastructure disclosure any lab has made is Google's Virgo Network and TPU 8t/8i announcement at Cloud Next, April 22, 2026 [Google Cloud Blog, "Introducing Virgo megascale data center fabric"](https://cloud.google.com/blog/products/networking/introducing-virgo-megascale-data-center-fabric) and ["TPU 8t and TPU 8i technical deep dive"](https://cloud.google.com/blog/products/compute/tpu-8t-and-tpu-8i-technical-deep-dive) [OFFICIAL-BLOG]. TPU 8t (training-optimized, Broadcom-codesigned) delivers 12.6 PFLOPs peak FP4/chip, 216GB HBM3e at 6,528 GB/s, native FP4 support doubling MXU throughput versus FP8. A superpod packs **9,600 liquid-cooled chips** for 121 FP4 ExaFLOPs peak — roughly 3x the prior-generation (Ironwood) pod. TPU 8i (inference-optimized, MediaTek-codesigned) uses a "Boardfly" ICI topology cutting network diameter from 16 hops to 7 at 1,024-chip scale. The Virgo fabric — the scale-out network, not a chip — links **134,000 TPU 8t chips in one non-blocking fabric per site** at 47 Pb/s bisectional bandwidth on a flat two-layer topology. Google's stated rationale: **"Training demands now exceed the power and space of a single data center, requiring unified, multi-data-center domains"** — a dated (April 2026) acknowledgment that single-DC training no longer suffices at the frontier, echoing Microsoft's Russinovich (Ch18). The headline claim: with JAX + Pathways, Google states it can orchestrate **"more than 1 million TPU chips in a single training cluster"** — exceeding the Gemini-era ceiling by roughly two to three orders of magnitude in stated ambition.

That earlier ceiling is documented in the Gemini 1.5 Technical Report, still the field's most quantified goodput/reliability disclosure as of mid-2026 [Gemini Team, Google, arXiv:2403.05530](https://arxiv.org/abs/2403.05530) [PAPER], March 2024. TPUv4 SuperPods of **4,096 chips** carried a dedicated optical circuit switch reconfiguring 4×4×4 cubes into arbitrary 3D torus topologies in **~10 seconds**; Gemini Ultra trained "across multiple sites, and multiple clusters within those sites" (Thomas Kurian, on record), combining SuperPods via Google's Jupiter OCS, model-parallel within superpods and data-parallel across them, with Google stating "network latencies and bandwidths are sufficient to support the commonly used synchronous training paradigm" even across sites. The number every subsequent industry goodput claim gets benchmarked against: **goodput** — time computing useful steps over elapsed job time — "for the largest-scale training job increased from **85 to 97 percent**." Google explicitly expected **SDC events "every week or two"** at this scale, mitigated via deterministic replay plus proactive SDC scanners on idle machines, and moved recovery away from periodic disk checkpointing toward **redundant in-memory copies of model state** — an early explicit disclosure of the pattern Ch19 treats as a design default.

"How to Scale Your Model," the JAX-ML/DeepMind scaling textbook (2025, Jacob Austin, Sholto Douglas, and colleagues) [jax-ml.github.io/scaling-book](https://jax-ml.github.io/scaling-book/) [OFFICIAL/PAPER-adjacent], functions as disclosure-by-proxy: Jeff Dean endorsed it on X — **"Training our most capable Gemini models relies heavily on our JAX software stack + Google's TPU hardware platforms"** — confirming JAX+TPU remains the production Gemini path. Dean and Noam Shazeer, on Dwarkesh (Feb 12, 2025), gave the plainest confirmation anywhere that cross-datacenter *synchronous* pretraining is already routine at Google: Dean — "We're already doing it... in the Gemini 1.5 tech report, we said we used multiple metro areas and trained with some of the compute in each place," the constraint being purely a timing budget: "as long as the gradient accumulation and communication of the parameters across metro areas happens fast enough relative to the step time, you're golden." Shazeer: "As long as you can sync all of the parameters... in the time it takes to do one step, you're pretty good" [SPOKEN-CLAIM, Dwarkesh Podcast](https://www.dwarkesh.com/p/jeff-dean-and-noam-shazeer) — a materially stronger claim than "async is possible," gated by bandwidth relative to step time, not algorithm. Dean also volunteered a candid MoE critique: "current problem... it's still a very regular structure where each of the experts is the same size. The paths merge back together very fast" — relevant to the granularity choices in Ch5 and Ch7.

Dean pushed back on data-scarcity framing separately: **"I think everyone has this view that we're running out of training data... I'm not too worried about that as an impediment to making progress"** — citing untapped video, synthetic generation, and multi-epoch passes as substitutes for unique tokens [OFFICIAL-INTERVIEW]. This is Google's explicit counter to Sutskever's framing — not disputing finiteness, but disputing that it binds. That disagreement went fully on the record: Oriol Vinyals posted that Gemini 3's pretraining delta over 2.5 was the largest jump the team has seen, "contra the popular belief that scaling is over," explicitly referencing a NeurIPS '25 talk shared with Sutskever and Quoc Le [OFFICIAL X POST], ~Nov 2025 (exact wording unfetched; treat as [CREDIBLE-HEARSAY] pending verification) — a rare dated public disagreement between two frontier-lab principals, from the same panel, worth returning to in Ch6.

## Meta

Meta's Llama 3 paper remains the richest single frontier infrastructure disclosure as of mid-2026; treat its infra chapter as a methods appendix rather than a marketing document [Meta, "The Llama 3 Herd of Models," arXiv:2407.21783](https://arxiv.org/abs/2407.21783) [PAPER], July 2024. The 405B model trained on up to **16,384 of the 24,576 H100 GPUs** available in Meta's RoCE cluster, on 400 Gbps per-GPU interconnect across a 3-tier Clos fabric (Arista 7800 + Minipack2, 1:7 oversubscription between pods). Storage ran on Tectonic — 240PB across 7,500 SSD servers, 2TB/s sustained / 7TB/s peak. Meta discloses an actual **MFU range: 38-43% BF16**, dropping to ~41% at the 16K-GPU configuration from reduced per-GPU batch size — one of the only frontier labs to publish a real MFU figure for a named model. Parallelism was explicit 4D: TP + PP + CP (context parallelism) + DP, e.g. TP=8, CP=1, PP=16, DP=64-128. Over a 54-day window the run logged **419 unexpected component-failure interruptions** (~1 every 3 hours), 78% confirmed hardware issues, GPU/NVLink failures the largest category — yet maintained **>90% effective training time**, with a rare concrete finding of **diurnal 1-2% throughput variation** from ambient temperature effects on GPU voltage/frequency scaling (Ch19). NCCLX, Meta's internal NCCL fork, is credited as central to failure localization.

Llama 4 Behemoth's public footprint is thin by design: **~2T total parameters, 288B active, 16 experts**; Scout and Maverick shipped, Behemoth never did [Meta AI blog](https://ai.meta.com/blog/llama-4-multimodal-intelligence/) [OFFICIAL-BLOG], April 2025. The only hard efficiency number ever released: **"while pre-training our Llama 4 Behemoth model using FP8 and 32K GPUs, we achieved 390 TFLOPs/GPU"** — only 2x Llama-3-405B's 16K-GPU cluster despite ~5x the active parameters, implying FP8 + MoE sparsity doing substantial efficiency work. The family-wide mixture is stated at "more than 30 trillion tokens," spanning text/image/video and 200 languages.

What actually happened to Behemoth is the standout finding of this research pass, and it did not come from Meta. SemiAnalysis's "Meta Superintelligence – Leadership, Compute, Talent, and Data" (Dylan Patel, Jeremie Eliahou Ontiveros, Wei Zhou, July 11, 2025) [newsletter.semianalysis.com](https://newsletter.semianalysis.com/p/meta-superintelligence-leadership-compute-talent-and-data) [SA-PAID] names four concrete root causes in its "Llama Behemoth 4 Failure Post-Mortem" section — the most specific public attribution of blame for any frontier MoE pretrain failure found in this research. First, **chunked attention**: Behemoth used Meta's chunked local-attention scheme (independently confirmed via HuggingFace's `transformers` Llama4 config: `attention_chunk_size=8192`, a full-context NoPE layer only every 4th layer, a 3:1 local:global ratio) [CODE/OFFICIAL-DOCS]. SemiAnalysis: "Behemoth's implementation of chunked attention chasing efficiency created blind spots, especially at block boundaries. This impacts the model's ability to develop reasoning abilities as chain of thought exceeds one chunk in length" [SA-PAID], and claims Meta "didn't even have the proper long context evaluations or testing infrastructure set up to determine that chunked attention would not work" — an evals-before-scale-up governance gap, not just an architecture bet gone wrong.

Second, and the single most load-bearing claim in the post-mortem for a 10T-MoE planner: **Meta reportedly switched Behemoth's router from expert-choice (EC) to token-choice (TC) mid-run**, and "the performance drop from the switch resulted in a model that was meaningfully worse than a model fully trained on TC" [SA-PAID]. The switch itself — not merely the initial choice of EC — is blamed for the regression; SemiAnalysis calls EC→TC transitions "not unusual for EC models" generally, but asserts Meta's specific execution produced an unusually large, unquantified loss. No independent source corroborates this or its magnitude — single-sourced — but it is architecturally coherent (experts specialized under EC's balanced distribution must re-adapt to TC's skewed, popularity-driven assignment) and is the most specific "here is exactly what not to do" MoE-routing data point in this whole corpus (Ch5, Ch7). Third, **data quality**: Meta reportedly switched its crawl source mid-run, from Common Crawl-based corpora to a newly built internal crawler, and "struggled to clean and deduplicate the new data stream" because "the processes hadn't been stress-tested at scale" [SA-PAID] — swapping the ingestion pipeline itself mid-pretrain with immature dedup infrastructure (Ch10-Ch11). SemiAnalysis separately claims Meta uses no YouTube data at all, unlike OpenAI and DeepSeek by its account [CREDIBLE-HEARSAY, unquantified, unsourced within the piece itself]. Fourth, **scaling-strategy/coordination failure**: "competing research directions and a lack of leadership to decide which direction was the most productive path forward. Certain model architecture choices did not have proper ablations but were thrown into the model. This led to poorly managed scaling ladders" [SA-PAID] — substantiating at a process level the WSJ/Axios narrative of executives blaming the Llama 4 team and the non-public LMArena-optimized submission controversy [REPORTED-BY:WSJ via Axios/Computerworld/SiliconANGLE/Fortune, 2025-05-15]. As a bonus aside, SemiAnalysis reports OpenAI's GPT-4.5 team discovered mid-run that its "uncontaminated" code-monorepo validation set had itself been partly copy-pasted from public data, so apparent generalization was partly memorization [SA-PAID, unverified elsewhere].

Maverick and Scout were distilled from Behemoth's logits — "bypassing some of the flawed architectural choices of the larger model... [but] they aren't best-in-class for their size" [SA-PAID] — explaining their underwhelming reception as downstream of Behemoth's problems. The $14.3B Scale AI transaction is framed explicitly as fixing the data root cause, "especially the SEAL lab which specializes in evals" [SA-PAID]. Behemoth was never resumed; converging reporting has Meta Superintelligence Labs, under Alexandr Wang, "starting anew," with MSL's first ship being **Muse Spark** (April 8, 2026) and a single-source forecast of **May 2027** for its next true flagship [REPORTED-BY: aggregated via FutureSearch citing Reuters]. Zuckerberg confirmed on Dwarkesh (Aug 2025) that Behemoth exceeded 2T parameters and "we've had to build a bunch of infrastructure just to be able to post-train it ourselves" [SPOKEN-CLAIM]. Meta's post-Behemoth compute posture remains substantial: Prometheus (Ohio) expanded from ~1GW to **>3GW within two years** via a rapid "Tent" datacenter design, 27 datacenters across 6 campuses up to 80km apart, linked by a 22 Pb/s "AI-Backbone" network; Hyperion (Louisiana) targets 5GW [CREDIBLE-HEARSAY, SA-PAID, "The Future of Meta Superintelligence: A 1 Year Progress Update"]. Meta discloses explicit latency tiers driving its sync-vs-async choice — **1-10µs within a region, 500µs+ across ~100km+**, the latter "requiring asynchronous training strategies" [CREDIBLE-HEARSAY, SemiAnalysis].

## xAI

Colossus 1 (Memphis) is documented at roughly 200,000 H100-class GPUs, built in a widely reported ~122-day timeline. Colossus 2, per SemiAnalysis's deep-read buildout account, went from a bare 1M-sq-ft warehouse (acquired March 2025) to **200MW of cooling capacity within six months** (119 air-cooled chillers on site by Aug 22, 2025, enough for roughly 110,000 GB200 NVL72-equivalent GPUs) — "xAI built in six months what took 15 months for Oracle, Crusoe and OpenAI" [CREDIBLE-HEARSAY, SA-PAID, "xAI's Colossus 2 — First Gigawatt Datacenter In The World"], Sep 16, 2025. Power sourcing runs partly through an offsite Southaven, Mississippi gas-turbine hub (a former Duke Energy site) under a temporary 12-month no-permit approval, plus Tesla Megapacks bridging the two sites; Solaris Energy Infrastructure supplies rental gas turbines (xAI is 67% of its 1,700MW orderbook, 1,140MW, with a 50.1%/49.9% Solaris/xAI joint venture owning 900MW of that), guiding toward **>1.1GW fully operating by Q2 2027** — the "First Gigawatt Datacenter" of the piece's title is a target the source itself frames as roughly 21 months out from publication, not an already-reached January 2026 milestone some secondary aggregation implied. Financing for the ~555,000-GPU, ~$18-20B buildout runs through an unusual off-balance-sheet structure — ~$7.5B equity (including up to $2B from Nvidia) plus $12-12.5B of GPU-collateralized debt via a Valor Equity Partners-run SPV that buys chips and leases them back to xAI — which The Information frames as compensating for xAI's weaker balance sheet relative to OpenAI/Anthropic, with lenders reportedly pushing for 3-year repayment caps [REPORTED-BY:WSJ/The Information, 2025-07-22 onward].

Networking runs on Nvidia Spectrum-X Ethernet, not InfiniBand, claiming 95% data throughput with near-zero packet loss while syncing ~200,000 GPUs [Nvidia Newsroom](https://nvidianews.nvidia.com/news/spectrum-x-ethernet-networking-xai-colossus) [OFFICIAL-BLOG]. xAI's RL strategy is disclosed as genuinely distinct: rather than concentrating on verifiable code/math domains like OpenAI and Anthropic, xAI bets on Ani (an anime-persona companion product) as "one of the most diverse RL environments," treating human engagement itself as the reward signal — "It's debatable whether this leads to intelligence gains any time soon," SemiAnalysis notes [SA-PAID], but it is a genuinely different midtraining/RL-data strategy worth tracking. Grok compute claims deserve real skepticism: Musk states Grok 3 used "10x" Grok 2's compute and has floated a ~10T-parameter Grok 5 with xAI simultaneously training "seven models ranging 1T-10T params" — all [RUMOR, self-reported, unverified], with Grok 5's own slip from Q1→Q2→Q3 2026 evidencing the unreliability.

## Microsoft MAI

Microsoft's in-house model effort is deliberately small-scale relative to the other five labs in this chapter. MAI-1-preview, an in-house mixture-of-experts model, was pre-trained and post-trained on **~15,000 Nvidia H100 GPUs** — two orders of magnitude below Stargate/Colossus/Rainier scale, comparable to Mistral Large 3's disclosed 3,000 H200s [Microsoft AI, "Two in-house models in support of our mission"](https://microsoft.ai/news/two-new-in-house-models/) [OFFICIAL-BLOG], Aug/Sept 2025. No parameter, token, or MFU figures were disclosed. SemiAnalysis reporting scores it at ~38 on LMArena and confirms Microsoft's internal AI training compute spend is scaling toward **~$16B annualized** [CREDIBLE-HEARSAY, SA-PAID, "Microsoft & OpenAI, 2023-25: From All-In to the Big Pause"], Nov 2025. Microsoft's custom silicon has been a string of setbacks: Maia 100 (2023) never reached high-volume production; Maia 200's tape-out slipped to late 2024 and is "assessed a failure internally," with software development stopped entirely; Maia 300 (2nm) is the earliest credible next attempt, "late 2027" at best. Microsoft's fallback is IP access (excluding consumer hardware) to OpenAI's own Broadcom-designed "Titan" ASIC line [CREDIBLE-HEARSAY, SA-PAID].

Microsoft's real disclosure strength is Fairwater, covered in depth in Ch14 and Ch18: the "Azure AI superfactory" blog links Fairwater Wisconsin and Atlanta into one logical training fabric via a dedicated "AI WAN" built on 120,000+ new fiber miles in a year, with Azure CTO Mark Russinovich on record: **"The amount of infrastructure required now to train these models is not just one datacenter, not two, but multiples of that... Nobody's really run into that problem yet because they haven't gotten to the scale we're at now"** [Microsoft, "Infinite scale: The architecture behind the Azure AI superfactory"](https://blogs.microsoft.com/blog/2025/11/12/infinite-scale-the-architecture-behind-the-azure-ai-superfactory/) [OFFICIAL-BLOG], Nov 2025. Each Fairwater campus pairs a ~300MW GPU building (>150k GB200 GPUs) with a 48MW CPU/storage building — a ~1:6 CPU:GPU power ratio SemiAnalysis expects to worsen in the Rubin generation [CREDIBLE-HEARSAY, SA-PAID]. Fairwater 2 Atlanta's rail-only topology — splitting each compute tray's 3,200G into 32 planes rather than per-GPU — reaches **524,288 GPUs on a 2-layer network** using 24,576 switches (same 21.3 GPU/switch ratio as Stargate's 131,072-GPU network, at 4x the GPU count), at the cost that same-tray GPUs can no longer talk over the scale-out network and must route through NVLink, handled by Microsoft's own MRC implementation (Ch18) [CREDIBLE-HEARSAY, SA-PAID, "Microsoft's AI Strategy Deconstructed"], Nov 2025. Satya Nadella, on Dwarkesh (Nov 2025), summarized the incremental-buildout philosophy: "you want to be scaling in time as opposed to scale once and then be stuck with it" [SPOKEN-CLAIM].

Table 2.1 collects the load-bearing disclosed numbers; note how unevenly they cluster — hardware topology is disclosed far more freely than data, routing, or failure attribution.

Three patterns fall out of assembling this record end to end. First, **networking is the disclosure of choice**: five of six labs have put real, quantified fabric detail into the public record (MRC, Virgo, Fairwater, Llama 3's Clos topology, Colossus's Spectrum-X), because a fabric paper reads as an industry-collaboration story rather than a competitive-secrets leak. Second, **failure attribution is almost entirely absent from official channels**: where it exists at all, it comes from a single paid-newsletter analyst team working from partial access, not from any lab's own retrospective — Meta's Behemoth account is the only detailed technical postmortem for a frontier MoE pretrain failure in this corpus, and it is second-hand, single-sourced, and unquantified on its most load-bearing claim. Third, **the compute-scale numbers that dominate headlines are increasingly unreliable as a measure of current frontier position**: Epoch's tracker has no FLOP estimate for Gemini 3 Pro, any Claude Opus 4.x model, Claude Sonnet 4/4.5, or GPT-5.1 as of its July 2026 pull, and its "Confident"-rated large-scale estimates belong not to any lab in this chapter but to DeepSeek, GLM, and Kimi (Ch3) — precisely because they publish real technical reports. The closed frontier is not only closed on data and algorithms; as of mid-2026 it is functionally closed on the compute-scale question too.

## Implications for the 10T/200B run

Four disclosures here should shape planning assumptions elsewhere in this book. First, MRC's design choices — NIC-side routing state, avoidance of a second adaptive-routing layer, static SRv6 paths — are the most concrete production answer yet to the fabric-design question the book's own strawman (Ch16, Ch18) must resolve independently, since no framework ships this out of the box. Second, the Gemini 1.5 report's 85%→97% goodput improvement via in-memory state replication is the cleanest existence proof that a 10T/200B run's fault-tolerance layer (Ch19) should treat in-memory redundancy as the default, not a fallback. Third, Meta's Behemoth postmortem is the most actionable cautionary data point this book surfaces for MoE design: do not switch routing paradigms mid-run, do not swap data-ingestion pipelines mid-run without stress-testing dedup at target scale, and build long-context/reasoning evals before locking in a local-attention-majority architecture, not after (Ch5, Ch10). Fourth, every lab here treats compute-scale disclosure as a marketing lever, not a transparency obligation — the ratio of hardware-topology disclosure to failure-mode disclosure is roughly ten to one. A team building toward 10T/200B on 100k GB200s should expect to generate its own failure taxonomy from scratch: the public record from six labs that have done this at scale amounts to one paper (Llama 3), one paid-newsletter postmortem (Behemoth), and a scattering of podcast asides — the argument for Ch4's DeepSeek deep-dive and Ch20's operations chapter, where the open stack and the war-story genre are what is left to learn from.
