# Chapter 13 — Curation SOTA and What Lies Beyond Essential-Web

Ch12 priced the token *stock* and the legal terrain for acquiring it. This chapter asks the narrower, more technical question: once the raw tokens are on disk, what is the state of the art for turning them into a curated corpus, and where — precisely — does the published literature say the remaining headroom lives? The answer as of July 2026 has a settled center of gravity (taxonomy-annotate everything, filter with SQL) and a genuinely unsettled frontier (reject-pile recovery, dynamic influence selection, per-language bespoke curation) that a 10T-parameter build should be positioned to exploit rather than rediscover.

## Essential-Web v1.0, dissected

Essential-Web v1.0 ([arXiv:2506.14111](https://arxiv.org/abs/2506.14111), Jun 2025) is the reference architecture for 2025-26 curation: 24 trillion tokens across 23.6 billion documents, built from Common Crawl, with **every document** tagged by a 12-field taxonomy rather than a single quality scalar. The taxonomy groups into five families, documented in the project's own repository ([Essential-AI/eai-taxonomy](https://github.com/Essential-AI/eai-taxonomy), checked Jul 2026): a Free Decimal Correspondence field (a public-domain Dewey-Decimal analogue, three hierarchy levels); Bloom's Knowledge Domain and Bloom's Cognitive Process (the 2001 Anderson/Krathwohl revision of Bloom's Taxonomy, applied literally to web documents, both primary+secondary); Document Type v1 (17 categories) and Document Type v2 (25 refined categories, built directly on WebOrganizer's format taxonomy — see below); Extraction Artifacts and Missing Content flags; Reasoning Depth, Technical Correctness, and Education Level scales; 30+ RedPajama-v2-style continuous quality metrics; and six fastText classification scores (DCLM, English, Educational, Math, Web Code). The combinatorics are the point: **14.1 million unique primary-label combinations, 1.2 billion including secondary labels** — every document sits at a coordinate in a 12-dimensional space that downstream users slice with SQL rather than retraining a classifier per use case.

The labels are produced cheaply. **EAI-Distill-0.5b** distills a Qwen2.5-32B-Instruct annotator down to 0.5B parameters, with agreement reported within 3% of the 32B teacher at roughly 50x the inference speed, and infrastructure optimization (compression, prompt trimming, output condensation) raising throughput from 1.4 to 70 requests/second per GPU. Labeling the full corpus cost **~90,000 AMD MI300X GPU-hours**. At a wholesale rate of roughly $2-2.50/GPU-hour — our own rate estimate, not a disclosed Essential-AI or AMD figure — that implies roughly $180k-225k, about $0.0000075-0.00001 per token, excluding upstream crawl/extract/dedup. The domain-subset wins are the paper's real claim to fame — simple SQL filters, zero domain-specific classifier training:

| Domain | Result vs. named SOTA baseline |
|---|---|
| Math | -8.0% relative to FineMath-3+ |
| Web code | +14.3% relative to OpenCoder's FineWeb-derived subset |
| STEM | +24.5% relative to FineWeb-Edu |
| Medical | +8.6% relative to TheBlueScrubs-v1 |

A single generic taxonomy matches or beats three of four bespoke domain pipelines and comes within single digits on the fourth (math). That is the strongest published evidence that "annotate once, filter many ways" beats building N domain-specific classifiers — and it is the reason Essential-Web functions as this chapter's reference point rather than merely one entry in the dataset table below.

## The lineage question: WebOrganizer as the direct comparison

The instinct to compare Essential-Web against WebOrganizer resolves as lineage, not rivalry. WebOrganizer ([arXiv:2502.10341](https://arxiv.org/abs/2502.10341), Feb 2025), from Princeton/Ai2/UC Berkeley/UW, predates Essential-Web by four months and defines two complementary, near-independent 24-category taxonomies — **Topic** (subject matter) and **Format** (venue/style) — with normalized mutual information ≈0.10 between them, empirically confirming the two axes are close to orthogonal. Its 140M-parameter classifiers (built on `gte-base-en-v1.5`, two-stage distillation from Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct then Llama-3.1-405B-Instruct) drive a RegMix-style mixture search across 512 small (50M-param, 1B-token) proxy models; combined Topic×Format mixing improves average downstream accuracy from 51.6% to 54.6%, and layered on a FineWeb-Edu quality filter, mixing alone recovers 84% of FineWeb-Edu's own gain without any quality classifier at all. Essential-Web's own Document Type v2 (25 categories) is explicitly built on WebOrganizer's format taxonomy — so "Essential-Web vs. WebOrganizer" is really "incorporates a component, but does not adopt the second axis (Topic) or the mixture-weighting methodology." The lineage is directly sourced, not inferred: Essential AI's own eai-taxonomy repository states the Document Type v2 taxonomy is "based on the WebOrganizer taxonomy." That leaves an open combination for a 10T-scale build: SQL-filter subsetting and training-time mixture weighting are complementary techniques nothing in the published literature has combined in one pipeline.

Three of these deserve comment beyond the table. **FinePDFs** is the sharpest 2025-26 evidence that document *source format*, not just filtering quality, is a first-order lever: PDF-derived documents run roughly 2x the median character length of HTML-derived web text with a much heavier tail (95th percentile ~68k characters vs. ~11-13k), and a 1.67B-parameter/36B-token ablation shows a FinePDFs/FineWeb-Edu/DCLM blend at just a 0.25 mixing ratio beating the current public-SOTA baseline, Nemotron-CC-v2 ([huggingfacefw-finepdfsblog.hf.space](https://huggingfacefw-finepdfsblog.hf.space), Sept 2025). **Common Pile v0.1** has a real, mechanistically-explained quality gap rather than a generic one: at 7B/1T tokens Comma beats budget-matched Pile/OSCAR/FineWeb baselines on knowledge and code tasks but is specifically worse on HellaSwag, PIQA, and CommonSenseQA — the paper's own diagnosis is that openly-licensed sources systematically under-represent personal blogs, tutorials, hobby content, and sports writing, exactly the register those benchmarks draw from, and the gap persists as a structural signature of the source mix rather than closing with scale ([arXiv:2506.05209](https://arxiv.org/abs/2506.05209), Jun 2025). **Institutional Books 1.0** is architecturally unlike everything else in the table — pre-2006 institutional library-scan OCR rather than crawl-derived text — but its non-commercial initial license, driven by unresolved uncertainty around non-Harvard re-digitizations of the same public-domain works, means a commercial 10T-parameter run cannot ingest it without a separate licensing conversation ([arXiv:2506.08300](https://arxiv.org/abs/2506.08300), Jun 2025).

One upstream lever sits underneath every entry in this table and is easy to under-weight: text extraction itself is not a solved commodity. February 2026 work found HTML extractors (resiliparse/trafilatura/jusText) keep substantially different pages from the same WARC pool — only 39% of surviving pages are retained by more than one extractor — and taking the union increases DCLM-Baseline token yield by up to 71% at fixed benchmark quality (arXiv:2602.19548); a November 2025 Shanghai AI Lab paper (AICC, arXiv:2511.16397) replaces heuristic extraction with a 0.6B sequence-labeling model and lifts ROUGE-F1 content-match from trafilatura's 63.6% to 81.8%. This is Ch12's territory in full, but it bears directly on curation SOTA: every classifier and dedup number in this chapter is computed downstream of an extraction step that the 2025-26 literature has only just started treating as its own model-training problem rather than a regex.

## Technique deltas: three generations of selection

The field has moved through three generations of data-selection methodology in roughly eighteen months. Generation one is static, single-axis classifiers — DCLM's fastText scorer, FineWeb-Edu's Llama-3-70B-derived regression head. Generation two is static, multi-axis taxonomies — Essential-Web, GneissWeb, WebOrganizer, Meta-rater. Generation three is dynamic, model-state-aware, group-level influence selection, and it is the one with genuine unexploited headroom for a 10T-scale build, because nobody has published it validated past 400M-7B-parameter proxy scale.

Ensembles reliably beat single-axis classifiers where the comparison has been run directly. GneissWeb's ensemble (not single-annotator) quality filters beat FineWeb 1.1.0 by +2.73pp averaged over 11 benchmarks at 7B/350B tokens ([arXiv:2502.14907](https://arxiv.org/abs/2502.14907), Feb 2025), and a GneissWeb-adjacent survey found only 368 of the top 1,000 domains overlap between the DCLM fastText classifier and the FineWeb-Edu classifier — the two widely-used single-axis scorers agree on fewer than 37% of their own top-ranked domains, meaning a single classifier's "top tier" is closer to one opinion than a ground truth. Meta-rater ([arXiv:2504.14194](https://arxiv.org/abs/2504.14194), ACL 2025 Best Theme Paper) replaces a single quality scalar with four learned dimensions — professionalism, readability, reasoning, cleanliness — combined via a regression model trained to predict validation loss from proxy-model runs, doubling convergence speed at 1.3B and delivering +3.23% downstream, with gains holding to 7.2B. Perplexity-correlation methods sidestep training a new classifier entirely: correlating ~90 existing open models' per-domain perplexity against downstream benchmark performance beats DSIR on all 8 tested benchmarks and matches DCLM's hand-tuned bigram classifier at 160M scale, with the advantage growing with scale in a 2025 preregistered follow-up to 1.4B ([arXiv:2409.05816](https://arxiv.org/abs/2409.05816), ICLR 2025); PreSelect ([arXiv:2503.00808](https://arxiv.org/abs/2503.00808), ICML 2025) packages the same compression-implies-transfer intuition into a lightweight fastText scorer.

Generation three replaces the static score with a signal that updates as the model trains. MATES ([arXiv:2406.06046](https://arxiv.org/abs/2406.06046), NeurIPS 2024) trains a small influence model that continuously re-fits to the pretraining model's *current* preferences, halving FLOPs-to-target and doubling the prior static-reference-model SOTA's gains at 410M/1B scale on C4. Group-MATES ([arXiv:2502.14709](https://arxiv.org/abs/2502.14709), Feb 2025) extends this to relational, group-level influence — individual-document scoring cannot capture documents cancelling or amplifying each other's marginal contribution — reporting +3.5-9.4% relative gains over random selection across 22 tasks. TiKMiX ([arXiv:2508.17677](https://arxiv.org/abs/2508.17677), Aug 2025) reformulates mixture selection as a *Group Influence* metric re-solved as training progresses rather than fixed at the start (unlike DoReMi/RegMix); TiKMiX-D matches RegMix at 20% of its compute, and the regression variant TiKMiX-M delivers +2% average across 9 benchmarks. All of these are proxy-model experiments at 400M-7B parameters — the load-bearing caveat is that dynamic influence selection has never been validated anywhere near frontier scale, let alone the scale of the actual training run.

## Reject-pile recovery and agentic pipeline search

The most structurally interesting 2026 finding is that taxonomy annotation is not the ceiling, because it makes an irreversible per-document keep/discard decision — and two independent 2026 papers show the discard pile is recoverable signal rather than noise. **REWIRE** ("Recycling the Web," Meta FAIR/UW; [arXiv:2506.04689](https://arxiv.org/abs/2506.04689), COLM 2025) takes documents a standard pipeline would discard — filtering typically removes up to ~99% of raw crawl — and guided-rewrites them into usable text instead of dropping them; 82% of REWIRE's final synthetic mix comes from transforming lower-quality documents, and the recycled mix outperforms simply crawling 2x as much raw web data, with gains of +1.0/+1.3/+2.5pp at 1B/3B/7B across 22 tasks. A June 2026 paper ([arXiv:2606.07778](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.07778)) builds directly on Essential-Web's taxonomy to make the same point more explicitly: documents scored low often fail only on extraction-artifact or missing-content flags rather than genuinely poor content, and a targeted recovery pass pulls performant training examples back out of the reject pile. Both papers converge on the same cheap, unglamorous insight: don't throw away your low scores without asking *why* they're low.

The most literal "data agents" answer published so far is **DataEvolve** (GAIR-NLP; [arXiv:2603.14420](https://arxiv.org/abs/2603.14420), Mar 2026), a closed-loop agentic system that searches over curation *strategies* rather than document scores — a data observer (GPT-4o-mini) inspects sample documents per category, a strategy designer (o4-mini) proposes cleaning/rewriting prompts informed by an experience pool from prior generations, a data cleaner (gpt-oss-120b) executes the strategy, and a quality judge (gpt-5-mini) scores before/after pairs and seeds the next generation. Thirty generations across 8 academic content categories produced **Darwin-CC**: 504B tokens, a 25% cut from 672B raw input. At 3B/500B tokens, Darwin-CC beats DCLM by +1.71pp, FineWeb-Edu by +7.61pp, and Ultra-FineWeb by +7.84pp on average score, with knowledge-intensive tasks seeing the largest jumps (MMLU +18.64pp, MedQA +13.48pp) versus raw unfiltered data; a suboptimal-strategy ablation shows a 2.93pp gap versus the fully-evolved strategy set, meaning the evolutionary search itself — not just "use an LLM to clean data" — accounts for roughly 40% of the total gain. No total compute-cost figure for the 240-run search is disclosed in the DataEvolve paper, which is the real limitation for budgeting this at Essential-Web's 24T-token scale rather than DataEvolve's own 672B-token, 8-category scope.

## Deduplication: commodity today, unproven past 10T

Deduplication at trillion-token scale has moved from research problem to infrastructure line item. SemDeDup ([arXiv:2303.09540](https://arxiv.org/abs/2303.09540), 2023), still the reference method, removes ~50% of a dataset via embedding-based semantic similarity at near-zero downstream loss, with the dedup pass itself costing only ~1% of total training compute. NVIDIA's NeMo Curator, rewritten from Dask/RAPIDS to a Ray-based backend spanning text/image/video/audio in the Feb 2026 26.02 release ([docs.nvidia.com/nemo/curator/26.02](https://docs.nvidia.com/nemo/curator/26.02)), deduplicates 1.96T tokens (RedPajama-V2 scale) in 30 minutes on 32 H100s, and 10T tokens in 4-8 hours on a single 8xH100 node — two numbers consistent with the all-to-all LSH bucket-collision shuffle, not raw signature computation, being the actual bottleneck. Zyda-2's build specifically credits NeMo Curator with cutting build time from 3 weeks to 2 days and total cost of ownership 2x ([arXiv:2411.06068](https://arxiv.org/abs/2411.06068), Nov 2024).

Different dedup architectures catch structurally different duplication — a nuance most 2024-era pipelines treat as interchangeable, and it is not. MinHash-LSH catches whole-document near-duplication; suffix-array exact-substring dedup catches repeated boilerplate and headers *within* otherwise-unique documents that document-level Jaccard similarity misses — the BigScience ROOTS corpus found 21.67% of bytes duplicated at the substring level, well above what MinHash typically flags ([arXiv:2303.03915](https://arxiv.org/abs/2303.03915), 2023). Olmo 3's Dolma 3 pipeline is the most recent frontier-adjacent open pipeline to explicitly re-adopt suffix-array dedup at production scale — a 56-way sharded fuzzy suffix-array pass (`duplodocus`) layered on top of, not instead of, its fuzzy MinHash-style methods ([arXiv:2512.13961](https://arxiv.org/abs/2512.13961), Nov/Dec 2025). Dolma's original three-stage cascade (exact URL dedup removing ~53.2% of documents, exact document dedup a further ~14.9%, paragraph-level Bloom-filter dedup ~18.7-19.1%) is a coarse-to-fine architecture distinct from both MinHash-only and suffix-array approaches ([arXiv:2402.00159](https://arxiv.org/abs/2402.00159), 2024). For a 10T-MoE build the practical read is: dedup infrastructure is solved through roughly 10T tokens on today's GPU-native tooling, and the two dedup families (fuzzy near-duplicate, exact substring) should be run as separate, complementary stages rather than one being treated as a substitute for the other.

## Synthetic rephrasing at trillion-token scale

Synthetic rephrasing is now table stakes, not a research curiosity. Nemotron-CC generates 2T tokens of synthetic diverse-QA/distill/extract-knowledge/knowledge-list text via 4 LLMs; Nemotron-CC-v2 adds 879.1B tokens (Qwen3-30B-A3B) plus 1,629.1B tokens (Mistral-Nemo-12B) more. Kimi K2 uses 10x knowledge-domain rephrasing to lift SimpleQA from 23.8% (10-epoch literal repetition) to 28.9% (10x rephrase, no repeat), plus SwallowMath-style step-by-step math rewriting ([arXiv:2507.20534](https://arxiv.org/abs/2507.20534), Jul 2025) — a clean, directly actionable number for anyone planning multi-epoch strategy on a >20T-token budget: rephrase scarce high-value data rather than repeating it. OLMo 2's own microannealing ablations corroborate the mechanism at a finer grain (arXiv:2501.00656, Table 12): TinyGSM in raw code-answer form actually *hurts* (GSM*=25.0, below the 28.5 pre-anneal baseline) while the MIND-rewritten natural-language version scores 65.5, and doubling that rewritten data reaches 70.0 — rewriting code-formatted synthetic math into natural language is worth roughly +40 points on its own held-out metric (see Ch14 for the full midtraining-stage treatment).

**BeyondWeb** (DatologyAI) is the most-cited commercial synthetic-data result and deserves the scrutiny its baseline choice invites. DatologyAI's original blog claim — 86.9% compute savings / 7.7x speedup, a "10x compute-equivalent" framing — is benchmarked against RedPajama v1, a 2023-era dataset already obsolete by FineWeb-Edu/DCLM standards when the claim was published ([datologyai.com](https://datologyai.com), 2024-25). BeyondWeb itself uses harder baselines: +5.1pp vs. Cosmopedia, +2.6pp vs. Nemotron-Synth across 14 benchmarks, 7.7x/2.7x speedups respectively, and a 3B model on 180B BeyondWeb tokens outperforms an 8B model on the same token budget of Cosmopedia ([arXiv:2508.10975](https://arxiv.org/abs/2508.10975), Aug 2025). The paper's own explicit negative result is worth carrying forward: larger generator models do not necessarily produce better synthetic pretraining data than ~8B-parameter generators. "Demystifying Synthetic Data in LLM Pre-training" ([arXiv:2510.01631](https://arxiv.org/abs/2510.01631), Oct 2025) corroborates independently: mixing ~30% rephrased-synthetic with 70% natural web gives a 5-10x speedup to matched validation loss with no degradation at studied scales; purely textbook-style, heavily-abstracted synthetic (Cosmopedia-like) does show collapse-like patterns at high concentration; and this single-round rephrasing regime is distinct from the iterative recursive self-training regime where "Strong Model Collapse" ([arXiv:2410.04840](https://arxiv.org/abs/2410.04840), Oct 2024) documents real degradation within ~5 generations. **ProX** ([arXiv:2409.17115](https://arxiv.org/abs/2409.17115), ICML 2025) is a distinct, cheaper, more auditable alternative to free-text rewriting: a 0.3-1.7B model generates and *executes* fine-grained programmatic cleaning operations per document rather than rewriting prose, beating both raw and selection-only-curated data by >2% across 10 benchmarks, with domain-specific standout results on OpenWebMath refinement of +7.6% (Mistral-7B), +14.6% (Llama-2-7B), and +20.3% (CodeLlama-7B) over human-crafted rule-based cleaning.

Not every frontier lab agrees synthetic rephrasing belongs in the base model at all. Microsoft's MAI-Thinking-1 report (microsoft.ai, Jun 2026) explicitly avoided all synthetic/AI-generated data in its 30T-token base pretraining run (MAI-Base-1, on 8,000 GB200s), with active filtering to strip AI-generated text from the crawl — a live, unresolved strategic disagreement between "synthetic data is essential for data-efficiency" (Ai2, DatologyAI, Nemotron) and "avoid synthetic/AI-contaminated data at all costs in the base model" (Microsoft MAI), from a lab training at genuinely frontier scale. NVIDIA's Nemotron Nano 2 disclosed practice sits closer to the pro-synthetic camp but at the midtraining stage specifically: taking a 14.5T-token checkpoint, continuing pretraining for 100B more tokens, and replacing 5% of those 100B tokens with synthetic "Fundamental Reasoning" SFT-style data (in place of Common Crawl) lifted MMLU-Pro from 44.24 to 56.36 (+12.1pp) and average Math from 37.92 to 39.70 (arXiv:2508.14444, Aug-Sep 2025) — a midtraining-stage result properly owned by Ch14, but directly relevant here as evidence that even a small synthetic-data injection late in training moves benchmarks a base-pretraining-stage rephrase does not obviously match.

## Domain pipelines: code and math

Code and math curation have converged on the same "rules vs. model" tension visible in the general-web literature. The Stack v2 (~900B tokens, 4x v1, repo-grouped) was found insufficiently high-quality for top-tier code LLMs by OpenCoder's own team, motivating RefineCode: 960B raw tokens across 607 languages, 130+ language-specific filter rules, two-stage dedup (file-level SHA256+LSH, then repo-level), downsampled to ~730B final tokens ([arXiv:2411.04905](https://arxiv.org/abs/2411.04905), Nov 2024). Seed-Coder (ByteDance Seed; [arXiv:2506.03524](https://arxiv.org/abs/2506.03524), Jun 2025) inverts the approach entirely: rather than hand-crafted rules, LLMs score and filter code data end-to-end, producing a 6T-token dataset with dramatically less human rule-engineering effort at a comparable or better quality bar per ByteDance's own benchmarks — the same rules-vs.-model-judgment split visible in Essential-Web (taxonomy rules) vs. DataEvolve (agentic judgment) above, replayed in the code domain specifically.

Math data has a documented pipeline-brittleness problem: OpenWebMath's Resiliparse-based HTML rendering is prone to stripping or corrupting formulas and code, a weakness FineMath (54B tokens, 5.5x more tokens than prior comparable sets) inherits by building on the same pipeline. MegaMath (371.6B tokens: 279B web + 28.1B code + 64.5B synthetic; [arXiv:2504.02807](https://arxiv.org/abs/2504.02807), COLM 2025) is the largest fully-open math corpus at release, and its own build reused FineMath's classifier as an input before further LLM-based refinement — classifiers becoming reusable, composable components across dataset projects rather than one-off artifacts is a recurring 2025-26 pattern (visible also in Ultra-FineWeb and ProX). Nemotron-CC-Math is a 133B-token dataset built via a Lynx+LLM pipeline explicitly claimed to surpass MegaMath, FineMath, and OpenWebMath ([arXiv:2508.15096](https://arxiv.org/abs/2508.15096), Aug 2025) — the two figures circulating for it are both correct and name different tiers: 133B tokens is the Math-3+ quality tier, 52B the stricter Math-4+ subset, both stated in the paper's own abstract.

| Kimi K2 | 15.5T tokens | 4 domains (Web/Code/Math/Knowledge); rephrasing-over-repetition pipeline | [arXiv:2507.20534](https://arxiv.org/abs/2507.20534), Jul 2025 |
| Seed-OSS-36B | 12T tokens | Released both synthetic-augmented and synthetic-free checkpoints for research transparency | ByteDance Seed, Aug 2025 |
| Hunyuan-Large | 7T tokens | High-quality synthetic-data emphasis | Tencent |
| Nemotron-H-56B | 20T tokens, FP8 | Hybrid Mamba-Transformer | NVIDIA ADLR |

No frontier from-scratch MoE report (DeepSeek-V3, Kimi K2, Qwen3-MoE, Hunyuan-Large, Nemotron-H) claims a data mixture that differs in kind from what a compute-matched dense model would use — every disclosed mixture looks like a standard general/math/code/multilingual split regardless of architecture. The clearest MoE-data interaction documented anywhere in this literature is in *upcycling*, not from-scratch pretraining: BTS ([arXiv:2502.00075](https://arxiv.org/abs/2502.00075), Feb 2025) trains dense experts on specialized 200B-token continued-pretraining mixtures (a code expert at >85% code tokens, a math expert on OpenWebMath) before merging into a generalist MoE — architecturally a sparse-upcycling setup, not evidence about optimal from-scratch MoE data composition. The honest position for a 10T/200B build: no controlled, compute-matched dense-vs-MoE mixture ablation exists in the published literature, so the strongest prior is that the same curation pipeline that works for a compute-matched dense model works for the MoE — flagged explicitly as an unfilled research gap rather than a settled result either way.

## Multilingual: a distinct curation axis, not a mixing afterthought

ÜberWeb ([arXiv:2602.15210](https://arxiv.org/abs/2602.15210), Feb 2026) is the newest and most actionable multilingual result for a 10T-scale build: across 13 languages, most "curse of multilinguality" regressions are not inherent to scaling multiple languages together but are correctable data-quality/composition deficiencies — curating English data improves non-English performance in 12 of 13 tested languages, and bespoke per-language curation (rather than one uniform multilingual pipeline) lets a 3B/8B model match uniform-mixture baselines at 4-10x lower compute on a 20T-token corpus. This is a harder-baseline, more actionable descendant of the same DatologyAI methodology behind BeyondWeb, and it inverts the intuitive assumption that multilingual curation requires uniform per-language effort throughout. FineWeb2 (20TB/5B docs, per-language adaptive pipeline, beats prior multilingual sets on 11/14 languages; [arXiv:2506.20920](https://arxiv.org/abs/2506.20920), Jun 2025) and HPLT v2/v3 (8T tokens/193 languages confirmed for v2 per [arXiv:2503.10267](https://arxiv.org/abs/2503.10267), Mar 2025; growing to a secondary-sourced ~30T tokens for v3 — a figure not independently confirmed against a primary HPLT paper or project page, so treat it as unverified pending direct confirmation — CC0-licensed, more permissive than FineWeb2's ODC-By) are largely-independent, differently-sourced pipelines (CC-focused vs. CC+Internet-Archive) that a 10T-run's multilingual mixture should plausibly draw from both rather than treating either as the sole reference. CCI4.0 is the clearest evidence that Chinese-language curation and English-language curation are increasingly not independent efforts even outside DatologyAI's framework: its 22.5TB English subset is drawn directly from Nemotron-CC rather than built from scratch, with a from-scratch 5.2TB Chinese pipeline layered on top and 4.5B extracted chain-of-thought "thinking templates" targeting reasoning benchmarks specifically ([arXiv:2506.07463](https://arxiv.org/abs/2506.07463), Jun 2025).

## The labeling-FLOP economics: is classifying 100T tokens affordable

The question a 10T-MoE build actually needs answered is whether taxonomy-labeling a 100T-token raw pool is a real budget line item or a rounding error. Our own arithmetic, using the standard FLOP conventions (≈2N inference FLOPs/token, ≈6·N·D training FLOPs): a 0.5B labeler over 100T tokens costs **1×10²³ FLOPs**, while training a 200B-active-parameter MoE comes to 2.4×10²⁵ FLOPs at D=20T tokens and 6×10²⁵ FLOPs at D=50T tokens — a labeling-to-training ratio of **0.42% at 20T tokens and 0.17% at 50T tokens**. Two independent cross-checks land in the same range. Essential-Web's own disclosed 90,000 MI300X-GPU-hours to label 24T tokens scales linearly to ~375,000 GPU-hours for 100T tokens — at the same ~$2-2.50/GPU-hour wholesale-rate estimate used above (again ours, not a disclosed AMD or Essential-AI figure), roughly $750k-$938k, negligible against a 100,000-GPU cluster's own hourly burn rate. And a separate academic filtering-pipeline study reports classifier-filtering FLOP overhead at under 1% of training compute — secondhand sourcing, but consistent with the arithmetic.

The practical verdict: "can we afford to label everything" is not a real planning question for a 10T-scale build — the answer is trivially yes, and the same math shows a 30B-parameter labeler over 100T tokens still costs only ~10-25% of training FLOPs, still affordable, just no longer a rounding error. The actual bottleneck, per Essential-Web's own disclosed experience, is **taxonomy design and distillation-quality validation** — engineering and judgment time, not GPU-hours. A 10T-scale program has genuine headroom to spend a larger labeler (7B-32B rather than 0.5B) if taxonomy quality, not compute, turns out to be the limiting factor.

## Where the real headroom is: three directions past taxonomy annotation

Taxonomy annotation (Essential-Web, GneissWeb, WebOrganizer, Meta-rater) is a solved, table-stakes layer for a 10T-scale build in 2026 — the evidence above shows ensembles reliably beat single-axis scorers and the labeling budget is not a constraint. Three directions beyond it carry the strongest evidence of genuine, currently-underexploited value:

1. **Reject-pile recovery.** REWIRE and the taxonomy-guided low-tier recovery paper independently converge on the same structural insight — the discard pile is under-processed signal, not noise — with REWIRE's recycled mix beating 2x the raw crawl volume outright. This is the cheapest of the three directions: it requires no new classifier, only a second pass over data already scored and already discarded.
2. **Dynamic, group-level influence selection.** MATES → Group-MATES → TiKMiX represent a real methodological progression away from static classifier scores toward model-state-aware, group-level signal, with TiKMiX matching RegMix at 20% of its compute. The caveat is real: none of this has been validated past 7B-parameter proxy scale, so a 10T-scale build adopting it is extending published evidence, not applying it.
3. **Bespoke per-language/per-domain curation over uniform mixing.** ÜberWeb's 4-10x compute-matching result at 20T-token scale is the most directly transferable of the three, precisely because it is validated at the token scale closest to the run's own budget.

A fourth, more speculative candidate — agentic pipeline search (DataEvolve) — shows a genuinely large effect (Darwin-CC's evolved strategies account for roughly 40% of the total gain over raw data) but with no disclosed compute-cost figure for the 240-run search and validation confined to 672B tokens across 8 STEM/medicine categories. Our own best-guess read — moderate confidence, not a stated conclusion of the DataEvolve paper — is to treat it as a technique for curating specific high-value sub-domains, not a whole-corpus solution at Essential-Web's 24T-token scale. Metadata conditioning (MeCo's URL-prepending, delivering a 33%-less-data result at 1.6B/8B scale, [arXiv:2501.01956](https://arxiv.org/abs/2501.01956), ICML 2025; and its 2026 successor Introspective X Training's feedback-conditioning result at 7.5-12B/18T tokens, [arXiv:2605.20285](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.20285), May 2026) is the cheapest lever of all — string concatenation — but as of July 2026 no named frontier lab has disclosed adopting it, so it remains a high-upside, unconfirmed-at-scale bet rather than a direction with the same evidentiary weight as the three above.

## The 2026 reference data-factory blueprint

Layering the evidence above into a single pipeline for a 10T-total/200B-active build: an Essential-Web-style 12-field taxonomy backbone (SQL-filterable, not a single scalar), with Document Type built explicitly on WebOrganizer's Topic×Format axes so the pipeline gets both hard-filter subsetting and RegMix-style mixture weighting rather than one or the other; ensemble rather than single-axis classifiers at every quality-scoring step (GneissWeb's +2.73pp and the 368/1000-domain classifier-disagreement finding both argue against trusting one scorer); a reject-pile recovery pass (REWIRE-style guided rewrite) run on the taxonomy's low-tier bucket before final discard; multi-extractor union at the HTML-extraction stage rather than a single trafilatura/resiliparse pass; FinePDFs-style dense PDF ingestion layered on top of HTML-derived web text given its 2x-median-length advantage; bespoke per-language curation for the top-N languages by token share rather than uniform multilingual mixing, per ÜberWeb; synthetic rephrasing targeted at knowledge-dense subsets specifically, held near the ~30% mixing ratio the collapse literature supports, generated by an ~8B-class model rather than the largest available teacher; two complementary dedup stages — GPU-native fuzzy MinHash (NeMo Curator-class throughput) plus suffix-array exact-substring dedup layered on top, not instead; and a labeling budget sized for a 7B-32B classifier rather than 0.5B, since the FLOP math shows headroom to spend more on taxonomy quality once compute stops being the binding constraint. Dynamic influence reweighting (TiKMiX/Group-MATES-class) is the one component to stage as a research track rather than a production dependency, given its proxy-scale-only validation — a natural candidate for the de-risking ladder Ch9 and Ch24 describe for other unproven-at-scale techniques.

## Implications for the 10T/200B run

Three planning consequences follow directly. First, the curation-technology budget for a 10T-scale run should be sized around taxonomy-design and validation engineering time, not classifier compute — the labeling-FLOP math makes a larger, better labeler affordable, so the constraint is judgment, not GPU-hours. Second, the highest-confidence near-term wins (reject-pile recovery, ensemble classifiers, multi-extractor union, per-language bespoke curation) are all compatible with an Essential-Web-style taxonomy backbone and require no new training infrastructure beyond what Ch15's dataloading pipeline already assumes; dynamic influence selection is the one technique that should be treated as an active research bet, prototyped at proxy scale before being trusted at the full run's data-selection layer. Third, the MoE-specific data question remains genuinely open — nothing in the published literature offers a compute-matched, controlled dense-vs-MoE mixture ablation — so the safest default is to inherit the dense-model curation pipeline wholesale rather than construct a bespoke MoE mixture theory with no supporting evidence, while treating that inheritance as a hypothesis a 10T-scale program is well-positioned to test rather than a settled fact to build around.
