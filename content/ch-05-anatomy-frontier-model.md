# Chapter 5 — Anatomy of a 2026 Frontier Model

Read a July 2026 model card cold and it hardly parses as English: Kimi K3 ships "KDA with Gated MLA, Attention Residuals, SiTU, Per-Head Muon, Quantile Balancing, stable LatentMoE"; DeepSeek-V4 ships "hybrid CSA+HCA, mHC, first-k-hash routing, √softplus gating." Every one of those terms names a replacement for something that, in the 2017 transformer, was a single undifferentiated line of code. This chapter and the next are the decoder ring. This one walks the *block structure* — what a layer of a 2026 frontier MoE actually computes, component by component, and which parts of the original transformer survive. Chapter 6 does the same for attention, the block that has fractured furthest. Chapter 7 then argues about which variants to *choose*; these two chapters exist so that argument is legible. Readers who want a visual companion should keep Sebastian Raschka's [LLM Architecture Gallery](https://sebastianraschka.com/llm-architecture-gallery/) open in another tab — 85 dissected models from GPT-2 through DeepSeek-V4 and Gemma 4, with a side-by-side diff tool (2026); this chapter supplies the mechanism and the why, the gallery supplies the diagrams.

## The Skeleton That Hasn't Changed

Strip any 2026 frontier model — K3, V4, MiniMax-M3, Nemotron-3 Ultra — to its control flow and the 2017 skeleton is intact. A token id indexes an embedding table to produce a d_model-dimensional vector. That vector enters a stack of L residual blocks; each block computes *update = F(norm(stream))* and adds it back: two sublayers per block, first a **token mixer** (attention or a successor) that moves information *between* positions, then a **channel mixer** (an FFN, now almost always a mixture of experts) that transforms information *within* each position independently. A final norm and an unembedding projection to vocabulary logits close the model. Autoregressive next-token cross-entropy remains the objective (with an auxiliary multi-token term, below). Everything in this chapter is a substitution *inside* that skeleton — which is precisely why "transformer" remains the right noun for K3 even though its attention is mostly not attention, its FFN is 896 experts, and its residual connections are no longer additions. The skeleton is the API; 2026 is a fight over the implementations.

What has genuinely *left* the architecture is worth listing once, because absences are invisible in model cards: dropout (gone from every frontier pretraining recipe — data is too plentiful and each token is seen approximately once, so the regularizer solves a problem frontier pretraining doesn't have); learned absolute position embeddings (replaced by rotary embeddings or nothing at all); post-layer-norm (the 2017 placement, unstable at depth, universally replaced by pre-norm); bias terms on linear layers (dropped nearly everywhere for stability and simplicity); and weight tying between embedding and unembedding (dropped at frontier scale, where the two matrices' jobs — geometry-of-inputs versus logit-geometry — diverge; small models still tie to save parameters).

## The Residual Stream, and Why 2026 Is Re-Plumbing It

The most useful mental model for everything below is the **residual stream**: the d_model-wide vector highway running the depth of the network, which every sublayer reads from (through its norm) and writes to (through its output projection). Attention heads write retrieved context into it; experts write transformations into it; the unembedding reads the final state. Depth-L training stability is, mechanically, the problem of keeping this stream's statistics sane through 2L consecutive additive writes.

The 2019-2023 answers are now fixed infrastructure. **Pre-norm** (normalize before each sublayer, not after) keeps a clean identity path from layer 0 to the loss, which is why 61-to-108-layer models train at all. **RMSNorm** replaced LayerNorm — dropping mean-centering costs nothing measurable and saves a reduction. **QK-Norm** (an extra RMSNorm applied to queries and keys before their dot product, used by Qwen3, GLM-4.5, and V4) is the same medicine applied to a hotspot: attention logits are the network's most explosion-prone scalars, since they are dot products of two learned, unnormalized vector families whose scales can co-inflate. Ch9 covers the failure mode this prevents (MaxLogit blowups) and the alternative treatment Kimi K2 chose (QK-Clip, a weight-space intervention chosen precisely because MLA's absorbed-matrix inference trick makes materializing per-head Q/K — which QK-Norm needs — awkward; mechanism in Ch6). The router z-loss (penalizing large router logits) is the same idea a third time, applied to the MoE gate.

What's new in 2025-2026 is that the frontier stopped treating the *stream itself* as fixed plumbing. Three generations of one idea: ByteDance's **Hyper-Connections** ([arXiv:2409.19606](https://arxiv.org/abs/2409.19606), Sep 2024) widens the single stream into n parallel streams with learned, dynamic mixing matrices deciding how each sublayer reads from and writes to each stream — trading the fixed identity shortcut for a learned routing of residual signal, at the cost of raw stability. DeepSeek-V4's **mHC (Manifold-Constrained Hyper-Connections)** makes that safe at 1.6T scale by projecting the mixing matrices onto the Birkhoff polytope (the set of doubly-stochastic matrices — rows and columns each summing to 1) via 20 Sinkhorn-Knopp normalization iterations, so mixing can *redistribute* signal among streams but never amplify it; disclosed cost is 6.7% wall-clock overhead on an overlapped pipeline stage ([DeepSeek-V4](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.19348), 2026; kexue.fm walkthrough). And Moonshot's **Attention Residuals (AttnRes)**, per the GTC 2026 keynote and the pre-GTC paper release, replaces the fixed *add* entirely: instead of each layer receiving exactly the running sum of all previous writes, it computes a softmax-attention read-out over previous layers' outputs — "on-demand reading" of depth, in Yang Zhilin's framing, with launch coverage of K3 quoting roughly 25% training-efficiency gain at under 2% added cost (GTC keynote coverage, Mar 2026, and K3 launch coverage, Jul 2026 — both secondhand, pending the K3 report).

The pattern deserves stating because it rhymes across the whole architecture: **the 2026 frontier converts fixed uniform mechanisms into learned, sparse or normalized selection mechanisms** — FFN→routed experts (MoE), full attention→selected KV subsets (Ch6), additive residual→attended depth (AttnRes/mHC). Each conversion buys expressivity-per-FLOP and pays in a new balancing/stability problem, which is then solved by a constraint (doubly-stochastic mixing, quantile-balanced routing, clipped logits). That trade — selection for uniformity, then a constraint to re-tame the selection — is the signature move of this hardware generation, and once you see it, every opaque model-card term slots into place.

## Position: Rotary, Partial, and None

RoPE encodes position by rotating each query/key dimension-pair at a wavelength set by its channel index — relative position then falls out of the dot product automatically. Three refinements matter at the frontier. **Partial RoPE**: rotate only a fraction of head dimensions (V4 and Ling-1T rotate only the last 64), leaving the rest position-free; Su Jianlin's ablations find this *helps* loss independent of its main structural benefit, which Ch6 explains (RoPE is what breaks MLA's weight-absorption trick, so confining it to a small decoupled slice is load-bearing for cache-compressed attention) (kexue.fm #20, May 2025). **NoPE on global layers**: Kimi Linear's full-attention layers carry *no* position encoding at all — position information arrives implicitly from the interleaved recurrent layers, and NoPE globals extrapolate better to 1M contexts ([Kimi Linear](https://arxiv.org/abs/2510.26692), Oct 2025); Llama 4's iRoPE made the same bet. **Context-extension schedules** (YaRN-style base rescaling, staged length curricula) are training procedure, not architecture — Ch14's territory.

## The MoE Block, Taught Properly

Everything in a modern MoE layer descends from one observation: a transformer FFN is a key-value memory. Up-projection rows are "keys" matched against the hidden state; the activation gates how much each "value" (a down-projection column) is added back to the stream. Make that memory bigger and quality improves; make it bigger *densely* and every token pays for every parameter. MoE is the sparse dictionary version: partition the memory into n expert FFNs (each a standard SwiGLU block of width d_expert), consult only k of them per token, and let a learned router pick which k. Parameters scale with n; per-token FLOPs scale with k. That asymmetry is the entire economic engine of the 10T/200B design — and the "sparsity ratio" debates of Ch7 are about how far apart n and k can be pushed (DeepSeek-V3: 256+1 shared, top-8, 5.5% active; K3: 896, 16 active, ~1.8% per launch coverage).

**The router** is a single linear map producing n affinity scores per token. Three design decisions hide in that sentence. *Score activation*: softmax (original), sigmoid (DeepSeek-V3 onward — removing softmax's zero-sum coupling so several experts can score high at once), or V4's √softplus — unreplicated elsewhere as of this writing. *Selection and gate values*: take the top-k scores; the surviving scores (renormalized over the selected k, in DeepSeek's recipe) become multiplicative gate weights on their experts' outputs. Su Jianlin's geometric derivation is the cleanest first-principles account of *why* the gate weight must multiply the expert output rather than merely rank candidates: decompose each expert's output as magnitude × direction, and the router's learnable job is exactly the magnitude prediction — multiplying it in is what routes a usable gradient back to the router ([MoE环游记 #1](https://kexue.fm/archives/10699), Feb 2025). His follow-up derivation answers a question the field mostly cargo-cults: softmax-then-select beats select-then-renormalize ("Re-Norm") — the REINFORCE-style derivation lands specifically on "normalize, but do not Re-Norm" ([#9](https://kexue.fm/archives/11782), Jun 2026). *Shared experts*: k routed experts plus one always-on shared expert (DeepSeek, Kimi, Ling all ship exactly one; Qwen3's flagship ships zero) — the shared expert absorbs whatever computation every token needs, freeing routed experts to specialize; Ch7 covers the scaling law saying one is the right number.

**Load balancing** is the tax sparse selection pays, and its 2024→2026 evolution is a microcosm of the field. A router left alone collapses — a few experts win early, get more gradient, win harder — and even a *quality*-neutral imbalance is an *engineering* disaster, because expert parallelism (Ch18) shards experts across GPUs, so the slowest-loaded GPU sets the step time. The classical fix, an auxiliary loss pushing the batch's expert-load distribution toward uniform, provably distorts the main objective ("too large an auxiliary loss will impair model performance" — DeepSeek-V3). DeepSeek's **aux-loss-free** method routes around this: add a per-expert bias b_i to the scores *only for selection* (not the gate value), and update it each step outside backprop — b_i decremented if expert i is overloaded, incremented if starved (sign-SGD on load error, γ=0.001 for 14.3T tokens, then frozen). Su's re-derivation shows this is secretly the same aux-loss objective with its gradient surgically confined to the bias parameter — "loss-free" in implementation, not spirit ([#3](https://kexue.fm/archives/10757), Mar 2025). His own **Quantile Balancing (QB)** then does what the sign-update approximates, exactly: pose balanced top-k routing as a linear program (every token gets k experts, every expert gets its fair share), prove the relaxation tight, and read off the optimal bias as a *quantile* of the score distribution — β_j is exactly the (1−k/n)-quantile of expert j's adjusted scores ([#6](https://kexue.fm/archives/11619), Feb 2026). Five months later "Quantile Balancing" appears by name in K3's launch materials (July 2026 coverage) — the clearest instance yet of kexue.fm functioning as Moonshot's public lab notebook, and worth pausing on: the load-balancing method in the largest open model on earth shipped as a blog post with NumPy reference code before it shipped in a flagship. Sequence-level balance (a single document collapsing onto few experts, invisible to batch-level methods) gets Su's MQB — a histogram-EMA quantile that stays causally valid and parallelizable — at a measured cost curve: full sequence-level enforcement costs ~0.06 loss (unacceptable), λ≈0.3 is near-neutral ([#8](https://kexue.fm/archives/11760), May 2026).

The bluntest 2026 balancing datapoint, though, is DeepSeek abandoning learned routing entirely where it works worst. V3 kept its first 3 layers dense because early layers — processing near-raw embeddings — route badly. V4 instead makes the first k layers MoE but assigns experts by a **fixed token-id→expert-id lookup table** ("first-k-hash," hash routing): structurally guaranteed balance, zero learning, with router scores surviving only as gate weights. DeepSeek didn't publish the table's construction; Su reverse-engineers a greedy frequency-sorted assignment and identifies the failure boundary (a single token more frequent than k/n can't be balanced by any table; fall back to n-gram hashing) ([kexue.fm/archives/11750](https://kexue.fm/archives/11750), May 2026). The lesson generalizes and is worth carrying to Ch7's router row: **where signal is weak, structural balance beats learned balance** — and a 10T/200B design should treat "which layers get learned routing at all" as a real axis, not a given.

**Dispatch mechanics** close the loop between this math and the hardware chapters. Within a device, tokens routed to the same expert are gathered (permute), each expert's tokens run as one batched **grouped GEMM**, and outputs scatter back (unpermute) — "dropless" MoE, no capacity factor, no dropped tokens, settled since MegaBlocks (Ch7). Across devices, experts are sharded (expert parallelism) and the permute/unpermute becomes **all-to-all** network collectives — the communication pattern whose overlap-or-die economics drive Ch17's kernels, Ch18's parallelism math, and Ch20's network design. Every routing decision above is therefore also a *traffic-shaping* decision; the reason balancing gets frontier-lab-founder attention is that MaxVio (maximum per-expert load violation) is, at EP=64, a direct multiplier on step time.

## Multi-Token Prediction

MTP adds small auxiliary head(s) predicting token t+2 (and beyond) alongside the main t+1 head — in DeepSeek's design a cascaded mini-block sharing the embedding and unembedding, its loss weighted 0.3 early, 0.1 late. Three distinct benefits explain its universality (V3/V4, K2/K2.5, Qwen3-Next, Ling-1T, MiMo-V2-Flash, Nemotron-3): a denser, slightly-future-aware training signal; a free self-speculative decoder at inference (draft t+2, verify in the same forward pass — 80-90% acceptance when the head is properly self-distilled against the exact final checkpoint, and K2's initially-mismatched 39%-acceptance checkpoint release is the cautionary tale); and — the 2026 reframing, via MiMo-V2-Flash — an RL-rollout accelerator, scaling token-level parallelism instead of batch size during the post-training phase a 10T/200B program will spend much of its life in. Ch7 covers the design fork that matters (shared-weight-across-offsets heads, per Nemotron-3, versus fixed-offset heads).

## Three Dissections

**DeepSeek-V4-Pro** (1.6T total / 49B active, 33T tokens, 1M native context; [arXiv:2606.19348](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.19348)): token → embedding → first-k blocks route experts by hash table (learned gates, fixed assignment) → remaining blocks: attention alternates **CSA and HCA** — both attend over *compressed* KV (every m tokens summarized into one entry; CSA adds lightning-indexer top-k selection over those summaries, HCA compresses far harder and attends densely), each with a sliding-window branch for the recent past, partial RoPE (last 64 dims), QK-RMSNorm, and a learnable attention-sink logit (a per-head "attend to nothing" escape valve — StreamingLLM's fix for softmax's forced-allocation pathology, now a learnable scalar in the denominator, as in gpt-oss) → MoE with sigmoid→√softplus gating → residual writes mixed across streams by Sinkhorn-normalized mHC matrices → MTP head. Optimizer: Muon (8 fast + 2 stabilizing Newton-Schulz iterations) — Ch9's story. Expert weights in FP4 — Ch10's.

**Kimi K3** (2.8T / 16-of-896 experts, 1M context — all launch-coverage numbers, Jul 2026, pending the report): token → three of every four token-mixer slots are **KDA** (a gated-delta-rule recurrent state, not attention at all — Ch6 §Linear teaches it), every fourth is full **MLA** under an output gate ("Gated MLA"), globals presumably NoPE per Kimi Linear precedent → MoE at ~1.8% activation with **Quantile Balancing** (Su's QB, above) over a "stable LatentMoE" variant — routing in a down-projected latent space, the Nemotron-lineage mechanism Ch7 details, which cuts all-to-all bytes and expert weight-read bandwidth at matched quality → residual handled by **AttnRes** → SiTU activations (a sigmoid-tanh gated unit replacing SwiGLU, per launch coverage) → Per-Head Muon (plausibly MuonClip's per-head machinery promoted into the optimizer proper — a guess until the report lands). The reason this card was unreadable a chapter ago is that *every* slot in the skeleton — mixer, channel mixer, residual, activation, optimizer, balancer — carries a named substitution; the reason it's readable now is that each one answers to a mechanism in this chapter or the next.

**Nemotron-3 Ultra** (550B / 55B active; [arXiv:2606.15007](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.15007)): the divergent branch — most token-mixer slots are **Mamba-2** state-space layers (selective-decay linear recurrence; Ch6 explains why "SSM" and "linear attention" are one family) with a minority of full-attention "global anchors"; MoE dispatch runs through **LatentMoE** proper (α=4 down-projection before routing and expert compute, 512 experts at top-22, ℓ=2048); bulk linear layers train natively in NVFP4 (Ch10). Same skeleton, third set of implementations.

## Implications for the 10T/200B Run

Four. First, **the block diagram is now a portfolio of independent bets**, and this chapter's decomposition is the ablation checklist: mixer family, cache-compression scheme, residual plumbing, gate activation, balancing mechanism, activation function, MTP design — a 10T/200B program should hold *most* slots at the boring converged default and spend its novelty budget on at most two or three, because (Ch9, Ch21) every exotic slot is also a new divergence surface at 100k-GPU scale. Second, **balancing is a solved-enough problem that unsolved balance is now a choice**: between aux-loss-free bias, QB's exact quantiles, MQB's sequence-level control, and hash-routed early layers, a modern run has a principled mechanism at every level of the stack — budget MaxVio telemetry as a first-class metric and pick per-layer mechanisms deliberately. Third, **the residual stream is the cheapest live experiment**: mHC's 6.7% overhead and AttnRes's claimed <2% are small bets on the one component nobody touched for eight years, with two independent frontier labs now claiming wins — a natural candidate for the strawman's ablation ladder (Ch8) before the run freezes. Fourth, **read every new model card as slot-substitutions against this skeleton** — the taxonomy is stable even when the acronyms aren't, and the next K-series or V-series card should cost you minutes, not a literature search.
