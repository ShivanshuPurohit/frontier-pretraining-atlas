A 10T-total/200B-active MoE on 100,000 GB200-class GPUs will not run for six months without interruption; it will run through several interruptions per hour, and the run’s economic case rests on how cheaply those interruptions can be absorbed. This chapter builds the failure-rate ground truth from independent field datasets, works the checkpoint-state math for the strawman spec, and synthesizes the layered reliability architecture — hardware acceptance through algorithmic mitigation — a well-engineered version of this run would need.

The Failure-Rate Ground Truth

The single richest public dataset remains Meta’s Llama 3 405B run: 466 total job interruptions over 54 days on up to 16,384 H100 GPUs — 47 planned, 419 unexpected — yet the team still held >90% effective training time [PAPER, arXiv:2407.21783, Jul 2024]. The unexpected-interruption breakdown is the base rate everything below is measured against: GPU faults 30.1%, HBM3 memory 17.2%, software bugs 12.9%, network switch/cable 8.4%, unplanned host maintenance 7.6%, SRAM 4.5%, host/system processor 4.1%, NCCL watchdog timeouts 1.7%, NIC failures 1.7%, SDC 1.4% — roughly 78% hardware, only 3 of 419 requiring manual intervention [PAPER, arXiv:2407.21783, Jul 2024]. Checkpointing wrote 1MB–4GB per-GPU shards to Meta’s Tectonic filesystem, 7,500 SSD servers delivering 2TB/s sustained and 7TB/s peak aggregate throughput — a number that matters directly for the bandwidth analysis below [PAPER, arXiv:2407.21783, Jul 2024].

MegaScale (ByteDance/Peking, NSDI’24) is the next maturity-curve point: a multi-week 100B+-parameter run on 12,288 GPUs recorded over 100 failure-and-recovery events with the loss curve converging through all of them, >90% auto-diagnosed, mean detection+diagnosis under 10 minutes, recovery within 15 [PAPER, arXiv:2402.15627, Feb 2024]. Its successor ByteRobust reports at genuinely frontier scale: across three months, 778,135 jobs, a fleet exceeding 200,000 GPUs, 43,264 explicit failures (78.1%, dominated by CUDA/CPU errors) and 5,948 implicit failures (job hangs), checkpoint-blocking-time down to 0.01–0.04s, and an Effective Training Time Ratio (ETTR) of up to 97% for a three-month dense run on 9,600 GPUs versus 91% for a one-month MoE run [PAPER, arXiv:2509.16293, Sep 2025] — a dense-vs-MoE gap that recurs below.

The most directly relevant public source for this beat is “Training LLMs with Fault Tolerant HSDP on 100,000 GPUs” (FT-HSDP), which appears — from its “AI-Zone” building-level replica terminology — to be a Meta-style architecture, though the paper does not confirm authorship [PAPER, arXiv:2602.00277, Feb 2026]. It reports 12 replicas across 4 datacenter buildings, each ~8,192 GPUs, and gives this chapter’s single most load-bearing number: at 100,000-GPU scale, a failure occurs every 18 minutes. A naive synchronous-restart baseline spends 10 of every 18 minutes recovering, for only 44% effective training time; their peer-replica recovery scheme — HTTP fetch of optimizer state at ~60ms/GB from a peer’s CPU memory, a 60-second hang-detection timeout, root-cause analysis under 5 seconds at 97.8% accuracy across 1,500 injected-failure tests — cuts total stall to ~3 minutes and pushes effective training time to 80% running with 11 of 12 healthy replicas [PAPER, arXiv:2602.00277, Feb 2026]. Their 32K-GPU production reference (2.3 interruptions/1,000 servers/day; top causes GPU HBM3 22.9%, PCIe 18.0%, NCCL watchdog 9.0%) is structurally the same top-3 taxonomy as Llama 3’s, differently weighted.

A smaller academic study checks scale-dependence itself: a 63-node, 504-GPU B200 cluster over 55 days logged 17 failure events, found “no single Prometheus metric is consistently distinctive across failure types” across 751 monitored metrics, and documented “performance characteristics observed at 2–4-node scale do not predict behavior at 60 nodes” — a storage I/O bottleneck invisible below 60 nodes cost over 8 hours to fix [PAPER, arXiv:2605.09370, 2026], a warning against extrapolating small-cluster burn-in without dedicated mid-scale validation.

GB200 NVL72: A Step Change in Blast Radius, Not Just a Faster Chip

The GB200 generation changes the reliability calculus in a way a pure FLOPs/TCO comparison misses entirely. SemiAnalysis’s field-derived mean-time-between-interruption (MTBI) ranges put H100 clusters at 2,000–5,000 GPU-days and GB200 NVL72 clusters at 1,000–3,000 GPU-days — GB200 racks fail roughly 2x more often per GPU-day than H100 servers even at the most-advanced-operator end. Blast radius compounds this: an H100/H200/B200 HGX server failure takes down 8 GPUs, but a GB200 NVL72 rack’s 18 compute trays, 9 NVSwitch trays, and 4 copper backplane cartridges share a common NVLink domain, so a switch-tray or backplane-cartridge swap drains the entire 72-GPU rack — a 9x larger, structural blast radius. Root-cause diagnosis compounds further: identifying which component is faulting “can take upwards of a couple hours” with current tooling, and the swap sequence — compute tray (1–4h, no drain), switch tray (1–4h, full drain), backplane (8–24h) — is an order of magnitude more complicated than an 8-GPU Hopper HGX board swap. XID 149 is the near-universal early-warning signature of backplane signal-integrity problems, and the gold-plated Paladin HD connectors are rated for at most 200 mating cycles before discard. Whole-rack diagnosis also requires three-way coordination between the customer (compute-tray access only), the hyperscaler (SSH to the switch tray), and NVIDIA support — friction SemiAnalysis states is why no large-scale frontier run had completed on GB200 NVL72 as of mid-2025, with advanced labs confined to inference and small experiments precisely because wide-EP MoE inference pays off the NVLink domain fastest without needing the mega-training reliability problem solved first [CREDIBLE-HEARSAY, SemiAnalysis, “H100 vs GB200 NVL72 Training Benchmarks,” Aug 2025].

The planning number this report carries forward: expect 1.5–4 unplanned interruptions per hour (35–100/day) on a 100,000-GB200 fleet, strongly dependent on operator sophistication and whether the fleet has completed its first 3–6 months of tribal-knowledge accumulation.

Silent Data Corruption: From Ad Hoc Tooling to a Board-Level Problem

Silent data corruption is qualitatively different from the crash-and-restart failure modes above, because nothing crashes. The lineage starts with Google’s “Cores that don’t count” (HotOS’21), establishing that mercurial/defective cores produce silent corrupt execution errors at rates far above classical soft-error expectations, chip-specific rather than fleet-wide [PAPER, HotOS 2021]. Meta’s July 2025 disclosure is the clearest statement of what is deployed today: Fleetscanner (targeted micro-benchmarks during maintenance, full-fleet coverage every 45–60 days), Ripple (lighter-weight tests alongside live workloads), and Hardware Sentinel (test-agnostic, inspecting application exceptions in kernel space, claimed 41% better detection than testing-based approaches) [OFFICIAL-BLOG, engineering.fb.com, Jul 2025]. Meta states historical soft-error rates of ~1-per-million devices have been superseded by SDC rates of ~1-per-thousand devices — ~1000x higher — with over 66% of training interruptions tracing to hardware failures in SRAM/HBM/processing-grid/network-switch components; Google states SDC events occur weekly-to-biweekly during Gemini training, while Meta attributed 6 of 419 unexpected Llama-3-405B interruptions (1.4%) to SDC [OFFICIAL-BLOG, engineering.fb.com, Jul 2025; CREDIBLE-HEARSAY via arXiv:2502.12340, Feb 2025].

The methodologically strongest public study, “Understanding Silent Data Corruption in LLM Training” (ACL 2025), makes the case that loss-monitoring alone cannot be the whole defense: one consistently-corrupting node produced training loss curves indistinguishable from healthy nodes while its gradients diverged up to 5.1% (noise-to-signal ratio) from ground truth, weights measurably drifting despite flat loss. Checksummed-matmul ABFT caught corruption on only 1 of several known-bad nodes tested; the proposed viable mitigation — a shadow data-parallel replica comparing gradients during all-reduce, at ~2x compute cost — is the only method shown to reliably catch what loss-monitoring misses, and corrupted-node models degraded to near-0% accuracy on downstream tasks despite normal pretraining loss [PAPER, arXiv:2502.12340, Feb 2025].

A genuinely non-obvious result for anyone choosing precision on throughput/memory grounds alone: LLM-PRISM’s 7,664-run fault-injection study found SDC resilience ranks FP8 ≫ BF16 ≫ FP16, because FP8’s narrow dynamic range forces faults to either saturate harmlessly or trigger a detectable NaN — binarizing outcomes — while BF16/FP16 permit large-but-finite silent corruption NaN-checking never catches, with perplexity deviations up to 400–600% [PAPER, arXiv:2604.10390, 2026]. Going to FP8 (Ch8) is, incidentally, also an SDC mitigation. A companion gate-level fault-injection campaign (3M+ simulator-hours) finds corruption is dominated not by NaN/Inf (1.01%) but by silent nullification (50.68%) and non-special bit-flips (48.31%), with “warp-aligned spatial periodicity” — GPU SDC is structured, not uniform-random [PAPER, arXiv:2605.04213, 2026].

OCP’s “Silent Data Corruption in AI” whitepaper (Sept 2025), co-authored by NVIDIA, Meta, Google, AMD, Intel, ARM, and Microsoft under a new Server Component Resilience Workstream, is a rare case of cross-vendor coordination on training reliability specifically, signaling the problem crossed a severity threshold no single lab’s tooling could address alone [OFFICIAL-BLOG, opencompute.org, Sep 2025]. The most concrete hardware response is Google’s Ironwood (TPU v7), the first TPU generation to move SDC defense into silicon: a Functional Built-In Self-Test in the MXU for manufacturing/burn-in, plus a compiler-transparent Hardware Replay Unit in the VPU that re-executes vector-instruction bundles on idle SIMD lanes at “zero performance overhead,” reported to catch defects that evaded all prior screening (Ch14) [PAPER, arXiv:2606.15870, Jun 2026]. No equivalent hardware SDC mitigation has been disclosed for Blackwell/GB200 in the sources surveyed here — an absence-of-evidence observation, not a confirmed denial, but an open gap against Ironwood’s silicon-level answer regardless.

Worth naming the failure class none of the above catches: a correctness bug with no corruption signature. HuggingFace’s SmolLM3 team restarted an 11T-token run after burning 1T tokens (46,080 GPU-hours) because every GPU in a tensor-parallel group shared the same random seed — passed loss-curve inspection, caught only by downstream eval regression [LOGBOOK, HuggingFace Smol Training Playbook, Oct 2025]. Checkpointing protects against hardware failure and instrumented SDC; it does nothing for this class, Ch20’s territory.

Checkpoint Math for the 10T/200B Strawman

Base Adam-style optimizer state for the 10T-total-parameter design: BF16 parameters at 10×10¹² × 2 bytes = 20TB; FP32 master weights (mixed-precision training) at 10×10¹² × 4 bytes = 40TB; Adam’s first and second moments, both FP32, at 10×10¹² × 4 bytes × 2 = 80TB. Total: 140TB.

Muon (Ch7) changes this materially: it replaces Adam’s two per-parameter state tensors with a single momentum buffer and no second-moment estimate, applied to the >95% of parameters that are 2D matrices — FFN/expert/attention weights, excluding embeddings/LayerNorm/biases, which stay on Adam [PAPER-derived, multiple 2025-2026 Muon papers]. FP32 momentum replaces Adam’s 80TB with 40TB (new total ~100TB, 29% reduction); BF16 momentum drops it to 20TB (~80TB total, 43% reduction). Published 8-bit quantized Muon variants report 74–86% optimizer-state reduction versus Adam-32/Muon-32 in small-model ablations, extrapolating — with real caution about scale-dependent quantization error, explicitly unvalidated at 10T scale — toward an 11–21TB footprint and ~70–80TB total, roughly half the Adam baseline [PAPER, arXiv:2509.23106, Sep 2025].

[Derived from the 10T-total/200B-active spec; Muon reduction mechanics from multiple 2025-2026 papers; 8-bit figures from arXiv:2509.23106, Sep 2025]

The device→host “stage 1” write is not the bottleneck at any realistic scale. At 100,000 GPUs, 140TB divided across the fleet is a 1.4GB/GPU local shard; copying that over GB200’s NVLink-C2C (900GB/s Grace↔Blackwell, up to ~450GB/s per GPU) completes in ~3ms, and even over PCIe Gen5 x16 (~64GB/s) it takes ~22ms — matching ByteRobust’s 10–40ms blocking stall and FT-HSDP’s ~60ms peer-memory fetch per GB [PAPER, arXiv:2509.16293, Sep 2025; PAPER, arXiv:2602.00277, Feb 2026].

The real bottleneck is draining to a durable, off-node tier. A synchronous full-fleet drain of 140TB to object storage inside a 60-second budget requires ~2.33TB/s aggregate sustained write bandwidth (~1.67TB/s Muon-100TB, ~1.33TB/s Muon-80TB). Meta’s Tectonic fleet built for the much smaller Llama 3 405B checkpoint sustained 2TB/s and peaked at 7TB/s across 7,500 dedicated flash servers [PAPER, arXiv:2407.21783, Jul 2024] — a 10T-MoE, 100,000-GPU deployment draining in under 60s would need a comparably sized dedicated storage fleet just for checkpoint traffic, layered on top of whatever the dataloader needs (Ch13). This is precisely why every 2025-2026 production system surveyed here — Gemini, MaxText, ByteRobust, FT-HSDP — converges on the same architecture: local/peer DRAM replication absorbs the common-case single-to-few-node failure at sub-100ms recovery latency, and the slow durable tier refreshes at a much longer cadence (tens of minutes) purely as a correlated-failure backstop.

Young’s formula, modernized, confirms the implied cadence: t* = √(2δM), δ the checkpoint-write stall, M the MTBF. Plugging in δ≈1–5s (async local/peer snapshot) and M≈1,080s (FT-HSDP’s 18-minute failure interval) gives t* ≈ 80 seconds — recommending checkpointing every 1–2 minutes at 100K-GPU MTBF, exactly the cadence ByteRobust and FT-HSDP independently converged on empirically [PAPER, arXiv:2509.16293, Sep 2025; PAPER, arXiv:2602.00277, Feb 2026]. If δ is instead the synchronous full drain to durable storage — tens of minutes to an hour at this scale — the formula correctly recommends a much longer interval, exactly why nobody drains to object store every step.

The Hierarchical Checkpoint Architecture

Google’s in-memory-replica architecture from the Gemini SOSP’23 paper remains the conceptual root of every subsequent system: rather than periodic dumps to persistent storage, keep redundant in-memory copies of model state across replicas and recover directly from an intact peer on failure, because “conventional periodic checkpointing to persistent cluster storage” could not sustain goodput at Gemini’s scale [PAPER, SOSP 2023]. This architecture’s goodput claims remain the still-unbeaten reference point: Gemini 1.0 on TPU v4 went from 85% to 97% goodput on a single supercomputer, and Gemini 2.5 sustained 93% doing synchronous data-parallel training across multiple 8,960-chip TPU v5p pods in multiple datacenters — a materially harder problem, still comfortably above 90% [OFFICIAL-BLOG/PAPER, Gemini 1.0/2.5 tech reports, cited directly in arXiv:2606.15870, Jun 2026]. Every GPU-side system below is, in effect, re-deriving a GPU-cluster analogue of this design.

The concrete stack, layer by layer:

  • torch.distributed.checkpoint (DCP), PyTorch-native, async_save=True overlapping serialization with training; NeMo-reported single-checkpoint save times ~1s (Megatron Core distributed optimizer) or ~3s (Apex distributed fused Adam) [OFFICIAL-DOCS, docs.pytorch.org / docs.nvidia.com].
  • ByteCheckpoint, disaggregating checkpoint data/metadata storage from parallelism strategy for online resharding across Megatron-LM/DDP/FSDP; 54.20x average reduction in checkpoint-induced stalls, up to 9.96x faster saves, 8.80x faster loads vs prior open-source baselines [PAPER, arXiv:2407.20143, Jul 2024, rev. Apr 2025].
  • Orbax (JAX-native, Google), replica-parallel multi-slice saving, up to 2.39x speedup across 16 slices; an independent 2026 benchmark shows 405B-model saves at 173.0s vs 595.0s for PyTorch DCP (3.4x) [PAPER, arXiv:2605.23066, 2026]; its emergency-checkpointing feature restores from a single replica in chunks to manage HBM pressure — a direct descendant of Gemini’s design.
  • MaxText / Google Cloud: three explicit tiers — ramdisk local RAM, peer replica in a different slice/superblock, Cloud Storage — combining “speed of ramdisk, resilience of in-cluster replication, durability of GCS” [OFFICIAL-DOCS, cloud.google.com, MaxText docs, 2025-2026]; TierCheck generalizes the same idea academically to four tiers (HBM → host memory → NVMe → object store) [PAPER, arXiv:2605.17821, 2026].
  • ByteRobust’s “Hot-Update” in-place recovery, 11.04x faster than full requeue (454s→46s at 128×16 GPUs), 0.01–0.04s checkpoint-blocking via hierarchical host-memory-plus-SSD tiering, in production across 200,000+ GPUs [PAPER, arXiv:2509.16293, Sep 2025].

NVIDIA’s resiliency stack (nvidia-resiliency-ext, “NVRx”) is the open-source infrastructure most directly usable underneath a Megatron build: per-rank heartbeat monitored by a “rank monitor” launched by ft_launcher supporting in-job restart without SLURM reallocation, plus in-process restarting, async/local checkpointing, and straggler detection — plumbing, light on published overhead numbers [CODE, github.com/NVIDIA/nvidia-resiliency-ext].

At 140TB (Adam) or 80–100TB (Muon), the layering above is non-optional: the local/peer tier absorbs the 1.5–4-interruptions-per-hour common case at sub-100ms-to-low-seconds recovery, and the durable tier — comparable in scale to Meta’s Llama-3-era Tectonic buildout — exists purely as a correlated-failure backstop refreshed every 10–30 minutes, not the steady-state recovery path.

Recovery Engineering: Warm Pools, Stragglers, and Semi-Sync Fault Tolerance

Straggler detection. MegaScale’s CUDA Event Monitor — a heatmap of unsynchronized per-op timings — found roughly 0.5% of machines meaningfully slower than the fleet, worth ~0.7% MFU when removed [PAPER, arXiv:2402.15627, Feb 2024]; a 2026 follow-on, Guard, benchmarks against it and DCGM/SuperBench, though its quantitative improvement was not extractable in this research pass [PAPER, arXiv:2605.17879, 2026 — open question]. Mycroft traces dependency graphs through collective communication to localize the causally first faulty rank rather than the rank that merely timed out first [PAPER, arXiv:2509.03018]. NCCL Flight Recorder + fr_trace — a ring buffer of collective start/end/enqueue times and stack traces per rank, aligned across ranks by sequence ID — exists because collective desync, not raw slowness, is almost always the actual root cause behind a raw watchdog timeout [CODE/OFFICIAL-BLOG, pytorch.org/blog, PyTorch 2.5-2.6 era].

torchft implements per-step fault tolerance for semi-synchronous algorithms (LocalSGD, DiLoCo, Streaming DiLoCo) via a Lighthouse global coordinator and per-replica-group Manager, Gloo for cross-group control-plane traffic and NCCL within a group. On 300 L40S GPUs with TorchTitan and Llama-3-1B, it degrades gracefully but not indefinitely under chaos testing: step-commit efficiency 82.3% at 1 injected failure per 60 seconds, collapsing to 30.2% at 1 per ~15 seconds; dead-replica recovery is under 1 second via Gloo, and a 40-step DiLoCo sync interval sustains ~4,000 tok/s versus ~1,200 tok/s for an HSDP2 baseline under identical conditions [OFFICIAL-BLOG, pytorch.org/blog, mid-2025]. SemiAnalysis’s own hands-on testing corroborates the cost differently: cross-replica-group allreduce under torchft must fall back to Gloo-over-CPU/TCP instead of NCCL-over-RDMA, measured at over 10% throughput overhead [CREDIBLE-HEARSAY, SemiAnalysis, Apr 2026]. ElasWave, a 2026-era successor, targets torchft’s throughput ceiling via multi-dimensional scheduling across graph/dataflow/DVFS/RNG state; on a 96-NPU testbed it reports 1.35x throughput over ReCycle and 1.60x over torchft with sub-second communicator recovery [PAPER, arXiv:2510.00606, Oct 2025] — a research prototype, not near 100K-GPU scale.

Hot-spare ratios and elastic vs. fixed world size. The only concrete industry number is Lenovo’s recommended minimum 2% spare-to-production ratio for NVL72; NVIDIA’s rack design holds back 8 of 72 GPUs (~11%), conflating true idle spares with GPUs standing by on pre-emptible workloads, and SemiAnalysis separately notes top-tier providers reserve 2–6% of nodes as hot spares [CREDIBLE-HEARSAY, SemiAnalysis, Aug 2025 & Apr 2026; OFFICIAL-DOCS, Lenovo NVL72 documentation, 2025-2026]. No public source validates a spare ratio tuned to a 100K-GPU fleet’s own MTBI curve. Every production-scale system surveyed — Llama 3, MegaScale/ByteRobust, FT-HSDP, Gemini, Alibaba HPN — runs a fixed logical world size with hot-spare or warm-pool replacement, not elastic membership. torchft/DiLoCo and ElasWave demonstrate genuine elastic resharding, but the largest publicly documented run for either is 300 GPUs (torchft/Crusoe) or 96 NPUs (ElasWave) — three to four orders of magnitude below 100K-GPU. Warm-spare-pool-with-fixed-topology, not elastic membership, is the frontier-lab pattern as of mid-2026.

TPU/Pathways Resilience Culture: The Contrast Case

Google’s TPU resilience architecture is structurally different because it is built around Optical Circuit Switches (OCS), present from TPU v4 onward. TPUs are grouped into “cubes” of 4×4×4=64 chips, each cube’s 96 optical links routing out to OCSes rather than fixed copper, making the 3D-torus wraparound links software-reconfigurable — a failed chip or cube is routed around, not hardware-swapped, MEMS-mirror reconfiguration happening in milliseconds. This buys a scheduling benefit beyond fault tolerance: without OCS a scheduler must find contiguous idle chips; with it, any two cubes anywhere can be selected, which is why Ironwood is provisioned at 9,216 nodes rather than a clean power of 2, running four 2,048-chip slice jobs simultaneously with some nodes down, backed by 16 spare cubes. The paper is explicit about the real bottleneck: “without OCSes, host availability must be >99.9% to achieve high slice goodput” — OCS relaxes an otherwise punishing host-uptime requirement, Ironwood running 2,304 CPU hosts, each serving 4 TPUs. OCS also changes deployment economics: each 64-chip cube enters production as soon as it and its cables are tested, whereas pre-OCS (TPU v3) the entire 1,024-chip system had to be fully cabled and tested first [PAPER, arXiv:2606.15870, Jun 2026].

The paper frames Google’s dependable-computing philosophy as inherited from distributed-systems practice for long-running batch jobs, not classical HPC checkpoint-restart culture, resting on five pillars: enhanced node quality, error checking, checkpoint/restore, strict deterministic repeatability, and modular isolation. Headline goodput, from Google’s own tech reports: Gemini 1.0 on TPU v4 achieved 97% (up from 85% pre-optimization) on a single supercomputer; Gemini 2.5 achieved 93% at multi-pod, multi-datacenter scale — slightly lower, plausibly the harder synchronization problem of more pods/regions even as per-pod hardware improved. The paper’s summary: “O(10,000) supercomputer size in a single-tier network with enhanced resilience allows training of long-running giant models across multiple supercomputers in multiple regions at >90% goodput simply by using data parallelism” [PAPER, arXiv:2606.15870, Jun 2026].

Google has also productized goodput as a first-class metric via the open-sourced ml-goodput-measurement library, instrumenting “goodput” and “badput” with async export to Cloud Monitoring/TensorBoard [OFFICIAL-BLOG/CODE, github.com/google/cloud_tpu_goodput]. No equivalent cross-lab-adopted library exists on the GPU side — every GPU-cluster goodput number here comes from a paper’s self-reported ETTR, not a shared standard, a gap worth noting for Ch20.

Goodput Accounting: What the Number Actually Means

ByteRobust’s Effective Training Time Ratio is the closest GPU-side analogue to Google’s goodput metric, and its own numbers already show the structural penalty MoE pays relative to dense: 97% ETTR for a three-month dense run on 9,600 GPUs, versus 91% for a one-month MoE run [PAPER, arXiv:2509.16293, Sep 2025]. SemiAnalysis’s April 2026 “Grand Unifying Theory of Goodput” gives the most rigorous public decomposition of what drives that gap: Goodput Expense is modeled under three fault-recovery regimes — G_chkpt-cold (provider-managed cold spare, hours to days), G_chkpt-hot (the 2–6%-of-nodes hot-spare pool top-tier providers reserve), and G_tolerant (the job survives entirely, well understood for stateless inference, “less well understood in training”). Its central point: cluster-level MTBF shrinks as cluster size grows, so larger jobs suffer disproportionately more goodput loss than small ones at identical per-GPU reliability — the same dynamic behind the interruption-rate derivation above [CREDIBLE-HEARSAY, SemiAnalysis, Apr 2026].

Implications for the 10T/200B Run

Layered reliability spec.

  • Layer 0 — Hardware acceptance. Stricter-than-vendor-default ODM/OEM acceptance testing on every GB200 NVL72 rack before fleet entry; budget for a first-3-to-6-month tribal-knowledge tax on backplane/switch-tray diagnosis, and staff a dedicated NVL72 hardware-diagnostics team before the run starts.
  • Layer 1 — Fabric/topology. Dual-plane, multi-ToR-redundant Clos (Ch18); NVLink-domain-aware scheduling that never splits a job across a partial NVL72 rack; whole-spare-rack provisioning on top of the in-rack 8-of-72 hot-spare GPUs.
  • Layer 2 — Transport/collective layer. NCCL Flight Recorder plus fr_trace-style desync debugging as a standing capability; dependency-tracing fault localization (Mycroft-style); continuous straggler-detection heatmapping, not just at admission.
  • Layer 3 — Framework/checkpoint layer. Hierarchical, tiered checkpointing — HBM local write in single-digit milliseconds, peer/replica DRAM copy at sub-100ms recovery, periodic drain to a dedicated flash/object tier sized for 1–2TB/s+ sustained write, refreshed every 10–30 minutes as a correlated-failure backstop. At 140TB (Adam) / 80–100TB (Muon), this tier is a multi-thousand-SSD-server capex line comparable to Meta’s Llama-3-era Tectonic fleet.
  • Layer 4 — Fault-tolerance/recovery orchestration. Fixed-world-size warm-standby-pool architecture, the only pattern validated above 10K-GPU scale; peer-replica HTTP/RDMA fetch rather than always-from-persistent-storage; automated root-cause triage targeting FT-HSDP’s demonstrated bar of sub-5-second, >95%-accuracy attribution.
  • Layer 5 — Algorithmic/numerical layer. FP8 doubles as SDC mitigation by collapsing silent-degradation failure modes into binary recover/crash outcomes; a periodic shadow-replica gradient-comparison pass for SDC detection, since loss/NaN monitoring alone is insufficient; Muon or a quantized variant shrinks the checkpoint problem, its interaction with SDC-style gradient corruption an open question at this scale.

Expected goodput number. Triangulating Gemini 1.0/2.5 (93–97%, single-to-multi-pod TPU), ByteRobust production (91–97% dense vs MoE), the SemiAnalysis MTBI-derived range (77.8%–99.6% by operator sophistication and GPU generation), and FT-HSDP’s demonstrated 80% (versus 44% naive-synchronous) at 100K GPUs: a well-engineered 100K-GB200, 10T-MoE run should target 85–93% goodput, the wide range reflecting MoE’s heavier communication overhead versus dense, GB200’s larger blast radius and lower MTBI versus H100, and the fact that this exact scale/hardware combination has no direct precedent.

Top 5 residual risks, ranked:

  1. GB200 NVL72 backplane/switch-tray reliability may not mature fast enough. No frontier lab had completed a mega-training run on GB200 NVL72 as of the most recent primary data (Aug 2025); the 72-GPU blast radius and 200-mating-cycle connector wear-out are architectural, not firmware-fixable. If unimproved by procurement time, plan around HGX B200/B300 for portions that don’t need the scale-up domain, reserving NVL72 for wide-EP-MoE-communication-bound work where its 18x all-to-all advantage pays for the tax.
  2. SDC at 10T-parameter scale is unvalidated territory. Every quantitative SDC study is calibrated on ≤670B-parameter, ≤16K-GPU regimes; whether SDC’s rate and downstream gradient impact scale linearly, worse, or better at 10T parameters and 100K GPUs is unknown, and a shadow-replica detection scheme costs roughly 2x compute if deployed at full scale.
  3. Checkpoint-tier storage capex may be underestimated. The ~2.3TB/s-for-under-60s-drain calculation implies a storage fleet on the order of Meta’s entire Tectonic buildout, dedicated just to checkpoint traffic; if unbudgeted it becomes a late-discovered bottleneck — the 504-GPU study’s 8-plus-hour storage-I/O surprise at 60 nodes previews the same mistake at far higher cost.
  4. No validated elastic-pretraining path exists at frontier scale, so the fallback under sustained failure is a fixed-world-size warm-pool architecture that, per FT-HSDP’s own numbers, still costs ~3 minutes of stall per failure when well-engineered — at 1.5–4 interruptions/hour, potentially 4.5–12 minutes/hour lost to recovery alone, before detection overhead.
  5. Muon/quantized-optimizer memory-savings claims are extrapolated, not scale-validated. The 74–86% optimizer-state-reduction figures for 8-bit Muon come from ablations orders of magnitude below 10T total parameters; whether quantization error interacts badly with MoE routing sensitivity, or with gradient-comparison SDC-detection schemes it could mask or amplify, is unstudied.
