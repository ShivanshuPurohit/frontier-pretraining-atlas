Ten labs shipped trillion-class or near-trillion MoE reports between mid-2025 and mid-2026, and no two disclosed the same slice of their stack. One hands you a full MFU waterfall and a closed-form scaling law; the next publishes 36 trillion tokens of curriculum detail and omits every hardware fact down to the GPU vendor. Read as a set, these reports triangulate something none states outright: what training a multi-hundred-billion-active-parameter MoE on export-controlled Hopper silicon actually costs, and which architectural bets the field has already made and unmade in public.

This chapter covers every major Chinese lab except DeepSeek, detailed enough to warrant its own chapter (Ch4) and used here only as a comparison anchor. The throughline is disclosure asymmetry: the labs that publish infrastructure detail (Moonshot, Ant, Baidu) and the labs that publish almost none (Alibaba, ByteDance’s current generation, Tencent) are not the ones you’d predict from model quality alone, and the gaps are as informative as the numbers.

Moonshot AI: Kimi K2 and the MuonClip Disclosure

Kimi K2 is a 1.04T-total / 32.6B-active MoE — 384 routed experts with 8 active per token, a 48:1 sparsity ratio, Multi-head Latent Attention with 64 heads (half of DeepSeek-V3’s 128), and 128K context via YaRN extension — trained on 15.5T tokens with, per the paper’s own claim, zero loss spikes across the full run Kimi K2: Open Agentic Intelligence [PAPER, Jul 2025]. Of every report surveyed in this chapter, K2’s is the most infrastructurally complete: H800 GPUs, 8 per node with 2TB RAM, nodes linked by 8×400Gbps RoCE; a parallelism recipe of 16-way pipeline parallelism (with virtual stages) × 16-way expert parallelism × ZeRO-1 data parallelism, with the model-parallel group spanning 256 GPUs and per-device memory for parameters/gradients/optimizer states held to roughly 30GB; a 67M-token global batch Kimi K2 §2.4.2 [PAPER, Jul 2025]. And yet the report never states an aggregate cluster size or an MFU figure — the single most-cited “transparent” Chinese infra disclosure of the period still withholds the two numbers a capacity planner would want first. Treat this as a pattern, not an oversight: see the “conspicuously undisclosed” discussion below.

The headline algorithmic contribution is MuonClip: Muon (Newton-Schulz-orthogonalized updates with RMS rescaling) combined with a post-update weight clip on the attention query/key projections, applied per head as γ_h = min(1, τ/S_max^h) with τ=100, where S_max^h is that head’s maximum attention logit Kimi K2 §2.1 [PAPER, Jul 2025]. Moonshot’s own research-diary blog — kexue.fm, run by Jianlin Su, a Moonshot researcher writing in first person about “our newly released trillion-parameter model Kimi K2” — published the QK-Clip derivation ten days before the technical report itself, adding training telemetry the paper omits: roughly 220,000 total steps, the first head crossing τ=100 around step 7,000, and a roughly 70,000-step “tug of war” between Muon (pushing MaxLogit up) and QK-Clip (pulling it down) before every head settled below threshold on its own QK-Clip / MuonClip [BLOG, Jul 2025]. Su’s structural explanation: Muon’s orthogonalized update msign(M) is exactly full-rank, while Adam’s empirical updates are low-rank; when a full-rank update’s singular vectors align with a weight matrix’s existing ones, they add constructively and the spectral norm — hence the attention logit ceiling — inflates. He also states QK-Clip was directly inspired by DeepSeek’s own auxiliary-loss-free load-balancing strategy, a documented case of technique cross-pollination via public writing rather than personnel movement kexue.fm/archives/11126 [BLOG, Jul 2025]. (Ch7 covers the general Muon-at-scale stability story; this is K2’s specific disclosure.)

Three further Moonshot data points matter for a 10T/200B planner. Activation-memory engineering stacks selective recomputation (LayerNorm/SwiGLU/MLA up-projections, MoE down-projections), FP8-E4M3 compression (1×128 tiles, FP32 scales) for MoE up-projection inputs, and CPU offload of remaining activations with copy-engine-overlapped streaming Kimi K2 §2.4.3 [PAPER, Jul 2025]. A concrete number on synthetic rephrasing versus repetition: K2’s own ablation shows 10× single-epoch LLM-rephrased knowledge data beating 10-epoch repetition of the same raw data, 28.94% vs. 23.76% SimpleQA accuracy — one of the only quantified answers in the corpus to a question every lab gestures at (Ch11 covers this in the curation context) Kimi K2 Table 1 [PAPER, Jul 2025]. And checkpoint-engine, Moonshot’s open-sourced RL weight-broadcast middleware, updates the full 1T-parameter K2 model in place across thousands of GPUs in roughly 20 seconds and underpins both K1.5 and K2 production RL — a concrete number for a problem most labs mention only qualitatively checkpoint-engine [CODE, 2025].

Moonshot’s architecture line continued past K2 with Kimi Linear (Kimi Delta Attention, KDA) — a hybrid design at a 3:1 layerwise ratio of KDA to full MLA layers, which Moonshot claims “for the first time” outperforms full attention under a fair comparison, reporting up to 75% KV-cache reduction and 6x decode throughput at 1M context, since validated to scale into K2-class models Kimi Linear: An Expressive, Efficient Attention Architecture [PAPER, Oct 2025] — the direct counter-datapoint to MiniMax’s attention retreat below, and a live, unresolved architectural fork. K2 Thinking (~Nov 2025) applied quantization-aware training to MoE weights specifically, producing native INT4 checkpoints at roughly 2x generation speed and half the FP8 variant’s memory at near-FP16 quality, a technique LMSYS independently reverse-engineered for RL rollout within about two months Squeezing 1TB Model Rollout into a Single H200 [OFFICIAL-BLOG, Jan 2026]. Kimi K3 — reportedly targeting 2.5-4T parameters, Kimi Linear attention, ~1M native context, expected Q3 2026 — remains unreleased as of this writing and is [RUMOR] beyond that framing.

Alibaba Qwen: Efficiency Claims Without Infrastructure

Qwen3’s flagship report — 235B-total/22B-active, 36T tokens across 119 languages in a three-stage curriculum (general → reasoning-emphasis → long-context), 94 layers, 128 experts with 8 active and no shared expert (a departure from both DeepSeek-V3 and Kimi K2), QK-Norm added, a 151,669-token BBPE vocabulary — discloses architecture and data curriculum in real depth, then says nothing about hardware, cluster size, parallelism, MFU, throughput, precision, optimizer, LR schedule, or batch size anywhere in the document Qwen3 Technical Report [PAPER, May 2025]. Against Ling-1T’s and Kimi K2’s infra transparency, this is a total blackout from the single most commercially deployed open-weight Chinese model family.

Qwen3-Next-80B-A3B compensates with an efficiency claim rather than an infra disclosure: hybrid attention at a 3:1 layer ratio (75% Gated DeltaNet, 25% standard gated attention), 512 total experts with 10 routed + 1 shared active (~3.7% activation, sparser than the 235B flagship’s 6.25%), and the claim that Qwen3-Next-80B-A3B-Base needed under 10% of the GPU-hours of dense Qwen3-32B — 9.3% of its compute cost — while outperforming it downstream, trained on 15T tokens sampled from the 36T Qwen3 corpus Qwen3-Next: Towards Ultimate Training & Inference Efficiency [OFFICIAL-BLOG, Sep 2025]. This directly rebuts the assumption that once a lab has settled on “MoE at 5-8% activation,” architecture is a solved axis: Alibaba claims a further order-of-magnitude efficiency step from hybrid linear attention plus deeper sparsification, resting purely on relative GPU-hour accounting with no hardware disclosure behind it. Qwen3.5 (Feb 2026, 397B-A17B flagship) and Qwen3.5-Omni (Apr 2026, hundreds-of-billions-parameter omni-modal line trained on 100M+ hours of audio-visual content) continue the pattern — parameter counts and benchmarks public, pretraining infrastructure absent — and beyond the Omni report itself Qwen3.5-Omni Technical Report [PAPER, Apr 2026] no primary source with training methodology exists for either release.

Zhipu/Z.ai: GLM-4.5’s Muon Recipe and the WSD Dissent

GLM-4.5 (355B-total/32B-active) and GLM-4.5-Air (106B/12B) trade width for depth relative to DeepSeek-V3 and Kimi K2 — a narrower 5,120 hidden dimension against 89 MoE layers and 96 attention heads, a choice the team states directly was because “deeper models exhibited better reasoning capacity” GLM-4.5: Agentic, Reasoning, and Coding (ARC) Foundation Models §2.1 [PAPER, Aug 2025]. The paper’s own comparison table places GLM-4.5 alongside DeepSeek-V3 and Kimi K2 on total params, active params, dense/MoE layer counts, and QK-Norm usage — one of the few Chinese-lab reports to formally cross-reference competitors’ architecture rather than only its own. Pretraining runs 23T tokens across four stages: 15T tokens general corpus at 4K sequence length, 7T tokens code-and-reasoning continual pretraining, then three mid-training phases (500B repo-level code at 32K, 500B synthetic reasoning at 32K, 100B long-context/agent data at 128K) GLM-4.5 §2.2-2.4 [PAPER, Aug 2025]. Notably the team explicitly did not use best-fit packing during core pretraining — finding random truncation a useful data-augmentation effect at that stage — but did apply it during mid-training to avoid truncating reasoning chains or repo-scale code, a curriculum-staged packing decision Ch12/Ch13 should weigh against.

GLM-4.5’s optimizer section is one of the most complete Muon hyperparameter recipes published by any lab: Newton-Schulz iterations N=5, momentum μ=0.95, update RMS scaled to 0.2, batch-size warmup from 16M to 64M tokens over the first 500B tokens, weight decay 0.1, no dropout, aux-loss-free routing-bias update rate 0.001 for the first 15T tokens then 0.0 GLM-4.5 §2.4 [PAPER, Aug 2025]. The team explicitly chose cosine LR decay over WSD after early experiments showed WSD-scheduled models underfit on SimpleQA and MMLU — a named data point against a scheduling trend otherwise gaining adoption in the same window (Ant’s Ling 2.0 sidesteps the question with checkpoint-merging “Warmup-Stable-Merge” instead of LR decay; MiniMax-M1 uses neither cosine nor WSD but a two-phase constant-then-decay schedule) — worth weighing against Ch6’s treatment of WSD-law practice. Separately, Su’s optimizer-adoption-guide post confirms the Moonlight-variant of Muon is now used in both Kimi K2 and GLM-4.5 — cross-lab technique adoption from Moonshot to Zhipu via public blog rather than shared codebase Muon优化器指南 [BLOG, Nov 2025]. GLM-4.5’s report gives essentially none of the hardware, cluster-size, parallelism, or MFU detail Kimi K2 and Ling-1T do; the RL infrastructure behind the GLM-4.x/5.x line, slime (Megatron-LM for training, SGLang for rollout), is separately open-sourced by THUDM but documents RL-phase engineering, not pretraining parallelism slime [CODE, 2025-2026]. GLM-4.6 extended context 128K→200K without a fresh infra disclosure, and per third-party coverage was the first Chinese flagship reported running inference on Cambricon chips at FP8+Int4 — a domestic-silicon deployment datapoint, not a training-hardware one IntuitionLabs secondary coverage [CREDIBLE-HEARSAY, ~Sep 2025].

MiniMax: The Full-Attention Retreat

MiniMax-M1, shipped June 2025, was a 456B-total/45.9B-active hybrid-attention model: one softmax (transformer) block following every seven lightning-attention (transnormer) blocks, native 1M context, built via continual pretraining of MiniMax-Text-01 on 7.5T additional tokens MiniMax-M1: Scaling Test-Time Compute Efficiently with Lightning Attention [PAPER, Jun 2025]. Long-context extension was reported as genuinely dangerous for this architecture: aggressive extension caused sudden gradient explosions, attributed to earlier transformer layers lagging later layers in parameter optimization because lightning-attention layers at different depths carry different decay rates — mitigated only by a smoother four-stage extension from 32K up to 1M. On the RL side, M1 introduced CISPO (Clipped Importance-Sampling Policy Optimization), which clips the importance-sampling ratio itself rather than token-level updates, motivated by the observation that reflective tokens (“However,” “Recheck,” “Wait”) are rare and low-probability and were getting clipped out under GRPO/DAPO in the hybrid-attention setting — a 2x step-count speedup over DAPO was reported on a controlled Qwen2.5-32B-base study, and the full RL training run (512 H800 GPUs, three weeks) cost roughly $534,700 MiniMax-M1 §3 [PAPER, Jun 2025].

Roughly a year later, MiniMax-M2 reversed course entirely: 229.9B-total/9.8B-active, full multi-head attention (GQA, 48 query/8 KV heads) across all 62 layers, no hybrid or linear component anywhere, pretrained on 29.2T tokens The MiniMax-M2 Series §2 [PAPER, ~2026]. This is the load-bearing datapoint of this chapter, and MiniMax’s own accompanying blog post is unusually candid about why. On saturated benchmarks — MMLU, BBH, MATH, LongBench — the M1-era hybrid lightning attention “looked just as good as pure full attention,” but at larger scale “clear deficits in complex, multi-hop reasoning tasks” emerged, meaning the very benchmarks used to validate the architecture decision were the blind spot. M1’s RL training separately exposed “serious precision issues”: linear attention is far more sensitive to numerical precision than full attention, a direct problem for low-precision KV-cache and state-storage schemes at inference. Performance degraded noticeably as context length grew in hybrid sliding-window-attention variants, unacceptable for agentic use. Infrastructurally, linear-attention variants were frequently memory-bound even during training, leaving GPU FLOPs on the table, and the theoretical compute-favorable crossover point (a few thousand tokens) never materialized without simultaneously solving prefix caching, speculative decoding, and low-precision state storage. Finally — and this is the part with the most direct 10T/200B-planning consequence — global attention patterns (retrieval heads, induction heads) are established early in pretraining and “resist mid-training architectural changes,” making a late-stage swap to selective full attention impractical once you’ve committed Why did M2 end up as a full attention model? [OFFICIAL-BLOG, Nov 2025].

MiniMax quantified the regression rather than just asserting it, rare enough to flag on its own: on RULER-128K’s “CWE” metric, hybrid sliding-window attention scored 72.0 against full attention’s 90.0; on a translation in-context-learning benchmark (MTOB), scores roughly halved, from 60.0/44.8 to 45.0/27.2 MiniMax-M2 Series Table 2 [PAPER, ~2026]. Within 32K context the gap was small or reversed on short-horizon tasks — the failure mode is specifically long-context, multi-hop, agentic degradation, invisible on the benchmark suites that dominated 2024-2025 architecture validation. M2’s own MoE uses sigmoid gating rather than softmax top-k, dropping the zero-sum constraint across experts so several can activate confidently at once, reducing reliance on auxiliary balancing loss MiniMax-M2 Series §2 [PAPER, ~2026]. MiniMax’s own framing is worth carrying forward: “efficient” attention isn’t dead, it’s just not mature enough to be the default for a system that has to do code, math, agents, multimodality, long-chain reasoning, and RL simultaneously — direct counter-evidence to Kimi Linear’s claim of clean superiority and to Qwen3-Next’s efficiency framing (Ch5 owns the design-space synthesis; this is the empirical retreat that constrains it).

StepFun: Step-3 and the Economics of Decode

Step-3 is a 321B-parameter VLM (316B LLM + 5B vision encoder), 38B activated per token, whose real contribution is not a pretraining report but a model-system co-design paper for cheap inference, relevant here because the design choices feed back into how a future trillion-scale MoE should be architected from the start Step-3 is Large yet Affordable [PAPER, Jul 2025]. Its Multi-matrix Factorization Attention (MFA) uses 64 query heads sharing a single K/V head at dimension 256 with a low-rank-factorized query path, reaching an “effective rank” of 16,384 — matching DeepSeek-V3’s MLA and doubling Qwen3-MoE-235B’s GQA. Attention-FFN Disaggregation (AFD) physically splits attention and FFN layers onto separate GPU pools run as a three-stage pipeline targeting a 50ms time-per-output-token SLA; the headline result is 4,039 tokens/s/GPU versus DeepSeek-V3’s 2,324 under an identical setup, with DeepSeek-V3 needing 320 GPUs per decoding instance against Step-3’s 32.

The paper’s real value here is its cross-lab decode-cost comparison table — DeepSeek-V3, Kimi K2, Qwen3-MoE-235B, Qwen3-32B, Llama-4-Maverick, MiniMax-M1, ERNIE-4.5, Huawei’s Pangu Pro MoE, and Step-3 itself, normalized onto a single $/1M-decoded-tokens axis across H800/H20/A800/Ascend-910B — one of the only places this comparison exists at all Step-3 [PAPER, Jul 2025]. Two conclusions carry forward: neither total nor active parameter count reliably predicts decode cost (Qwen3-32B, smaller than DeepSeek-V3 or Step-3, has the highest decode cost among the compared models on most hardware), and attention design dominates decode cost more than FFN/MoE design, the gap widening sharply with context length. A February 2026 follow-up complicates the AFD story: extending a communication-aware roofline model, it finds a bandwidth-limited “dead zone” where scaling out FFN instances stops improving hardware utilization because throughput becomes capped by scale-out interconnect bandwidth rather than compute — AFD’s benefits are more cluster-topology-contingent than Step-3’s framing suggests Revealing the Challenges of Attention-FFN Disaggregation [PAPER, Feb 2026].

ByteDance Seed: From MegaScale to MegaScale-MoE

ByteDance’s most-cited infra number is still MegaScale — 55.2% MFU training a 175B dense model on 12,288 GPUs, a 1.34x improvement over a comparable Megatron-LM baseline (~41%) — and it is from February 2024, describing a dense architecture, not the current MoE line MegaScale: Scaling LLM Training to More Than 10,000 GPUs [PAPER, Feb 2024]. For 2025-2026’s Doubao Seed 2.0/2.1 generation, ByteDance has published essentially nothing at that level of detail — no cluster scale, no GPU generation, no MFU, no parallelism layout — despite operating one of the largest GPU fleets in China, a meaningful gap relative to Ant Group and Moonshot. Seed 2.0 Pro is reported in secondary coverage as a roughly 1T-parameter Gemini-style natively multimodal MoE (98.3 AIME 2025, 88.9 GPQA-Diamond, 76.5 SWE-bench Verified), but treat the exact architecture as [CREDIBLE-HEARSAY], since no technical report with an architecture table was located technode.com Doubao Seed 2.0 coverage [CREDIBLE-HEARSAY, Feb 2026].

What partially closes this gap is a MoE-specific training-systems paper from the same lineage, published outside the Doubao release cadence: MegaScale-MoE, accepted at EuroSys ’26 MegaScale-MoE [PAPER, May 2025]. It measures a 352B MoE model at 1,440 H800 GPUs reaching 1.41M tokens/s at 27.89-32.48% MFU, 1.88x over Megatron-LM, and separately confirms production use training a proprietary 200B-total/20B-active MoE across 10,000+ GPUs for months with stable convergence, “saving millions of GPU-hours” — the closest thing to a current-generation ByteDance MoE infra disclosure in the public record, even though it isn’t badged as a Doubao/Seed release. Its two headline mechanisms: sequence parallelism (rather than tensor parallelism) for the attention/dense path, cutting communication to roughly a quarter of TP’s latency within an 8-GPU NVLink domain; and, for the FFN/expert path when top-k exceeds EP world size, an allgather + local-scatter + reduce-scatter sequence replacing standard all-to-all dispatch, matching or beating it while improving GroupedGEMM utilization — a different mechanism from both DeepSeek’s DeepEP and NVIDIA’s HybridEP (Ch15/Ch17 own the kernel-level mechanics; this is production-scale evidence it works at frontier scale). FP8 forward/backward uses per-token quantization because SwiGLU’s dynamic range breaks naive FP8; gradient reduce-scatter downcasts FP32→BF16 with FP32 local accumulation preserved.

Two smaller Seed-line points round this out. Seed-OSS-36B, a dense 36B model, trained on only 12T tokens — a third to half the 15.5-36T-token norm elsewhere in this chapter — with ByteDance framing the resulting competitive benchmark performance as a data-quality argument rather than a scale argument, worth treating skeptically absent a data-composition disclosure Seed-OSS-36B [CODE, Aug 2025]. And ByteDance’s HybridFlow RLHF framework, open-sourced as verl, has become a broad multi-organization project — adopted by the Qwen team and Shanghai AI Lab among others — reporting 1.5-20x throughput improvements over prior RLHF baselines HybridFlow [CODE, 2024].

Ant Group: Ling-1T and the Efficiency Leverage Law

Ling 2.0 is Ant Group/InclusionAI’s three-model family sharing one design — Ling-mini-2.0 (16B/1.4B), Ling-flash-2.0 (103B/6.1B), and Ling-1T (1T-total/51B-active, 80 layers, hidden 8,192) — all using 256 routed experts plus 1 shared expert with 8 active (roughly 3.5% activation), GQA, QK-Norm, and partial RoPE on only the first 64 dimensions Ling 2.0: Every Activation Boosted Table 1 [PAPER, Oct 2025]. This is, by a clear margin, the richest infrastructure disclosure surveyed in this chapter. Ant fit power-law relationships between compute budget and both optimal batch size and learning rate separately for MoE and dense architectures from roughly 1,000 experiments up to 3e20 FLOPs, finding MoE systematically prefers larger batch sizes and lower LR than dense at matched compute, attributed to sparse per-expert gradients needing larger batches for stable statistics. The “Ling Wind Tunnel” runs five standardized experiments spanning 500M-8B parameters (a 100x range), fully determined by the fitted laws, then extrapolates 100x further to predict Ling-1T’s behavior — reportedly predicting final training loss to within 0.01 at 35% of the compute cost of a naive single-scale ablation Ling 2.0 §2.3 [PAPER, Oct 2025]. This simulate-before-you-burn-GPU-hours methodology is directly reusable for a 10T/200B build’s own scaling-law program (Ch6 covers the general practice).

The centerpiece is Efficiency Leverage (EL), a closed-form scaling law defined as the compute-cost ratio for a dense model to match a given MoE configuration’s validation loss, derived from a 300+-model empirical study up to 28B parameters: EL(A, G, C) = Â^(α + γ(log G)² + β log G), where A is a saturating transform of activation ratio, G is expert granularity, and α = a + d·log(C) — meaning EL itself grows with training compute, so sparsity’s efficiency advantage compounds at larger scale rather than staying fixed Ling 2.0 §2.3.2 [PAPER, Oct 2025]; companion paper Towards Greater Leverage [PAPER, Jul 2025]. Activation ratio is the dominant driver, following a robust power law down to 1/128 activation; expert granularity has a nonlinear, largely compute-independent effect with an empirically identified optimal range of 8-12 activated experts (Ling 2.0’s choice of 8 sits inside this band); shared-expert count and layer placement are secondary. All three Ling 2.0 models’ 256-expert/8-active/3.5%-activation configuration delivers over 7x measured efficiency leverage versus a dense equivalent (Ch5 should weigh this directly against DeepSeek’s and Kimi’s own activation-ratio choices when deriving the 10T/200B strawman’s sparsity).

Infrastructure: a modified Megatron 0.11, FP8 training with native MTP support, 2,016 Hopper GPUs, TP1/EP8/PP21/VPP2, sequence length 4K, full activation recomputation. The MFU waterfall is the rarest artifact in this entire corpus — most labs report a single final number, if any:

Ling 2.0 §5, Fig. 14 [PAPER, Oct 2025]

FP8 alone contributes +15% MFU over BF16 and holds within 0.25% relative loss deviation from a BF16 baseline over a 900B-token run, tracked in real time by a “FP8 Training Safeguard System” monitoring per-layer quantization underflow and distortion as a first-class training metric (Ch8 owns precision recipes in full; this is Ling’s specific numbers). Operationally, distributed checkpoint save time was cut from 269s to 30s via a metadata cache avoiding rank-0 recomputation, and checkpoint interval is set to 48 minutes as a calculated failover-loss/overhead tradeoff (Ch19’s territory). A “Warmup-Stable-Merge” scheduler replaces LR decay with checkpoint merging entirely, reportedly delivering 1-2% average benchmark gains that persist through post-training — a third distinct answer, alongside GLM’s cosine-over-WSD and MiniMax’s two-phase schedule, to the same “how do you end training” question.

Tencent Hunyuan and the Mamba Detour

Hunyuan-Large (389B-total/52B-active, 64 layers, 16 specialized experts with top-1 routing plus 1 shared expert) trained on 7T tokens plus roughly 1.5T tokens of synthetic data, combining GQA with Cross-Layer Attention (KV cache shared every 2 layers) for a claimed ~95% KV-cache reduction versus standard MHA Hunyuan-Large [PAPER, Nov 2024]. Its one genuinely novel disclosure is expert-specific learning-rate scaling: the shared expert uses the standard optimal rate, while each specialized expert scales to roughly 0.31x that rate, justified by unequal per-iteration token load — a detail no other lab in this chapter discloses. The report gives no hardware, parallelism, or MFU figures anywhere.

Hunyuan-TurboS (560B-total/56B-active, 128 layers in an “AMF/MF” interleaved Attention-Mamba-FFN pattern, 16T-token corpus) is Tencent’s hybrid Transformer-Mamba line, motivated explicitly by pure-transformer models’ struggles with long-text O(N²) costs Hunyuan-TurboS: Mamba-Transformer Synergy [PAPER, May 2025]. Its infra opacity is unusually well-confirmed: MiniMax’s own M1 paper independently calls out Hunyuan-T1/TurboS as the one exception among reasoning models using a non-Transformer backbone, noting “this model is not open-sourced and few details are disclosed” — a competitor’s citation graph corroborating a disclosure gap MiniMax-M1 §1 [PAPER, Jun 2025]. Beyond the architecture concept, no MFU, hardware, or parallelism detail exists for TurboS anywhere.

Baidu ERNIE and PaddlePaddle’s Parallel Universe

ERNIE 4.5 is architecturally unremarkable relative to its peers — plain GQA (64/8 query/KV heads for the 300B-A47B flagship, no MLA-style latent compression), 54 layers, a heterogeneous modality-isolated MoE where text and vision tokens route through structurally distinct expert pools — but its infrastructure disclosure is the second-richest here after Ling-1T, once gap-fill research resolved an initial fetch failure against Baidu’s self-hosted PDF ERNIE 4.5 Technical Report [PAPER, Jun 2025]. ERNIE-4.5-300B-A47B was pretrained on 2,016 NVIDIA H800 GPUs with RoCE interconnect, sequence length 4,096, global batch size 15,120, reaching 47% MFU — higher than any other Chinese lab here discloses. The parallelism is 8-way expert parallelism × 12-way pipeline parallelism × ZeRO-1 data parallelism at sharding degree 168, tensor parallelism held at 1 for the text-only model, cross-verified against the open-sourced training YAML — EP=8 is deliberately sized to fit inside one 8-GPU H800 node, so the MoE all-to-all never crosses a node boundary, using plain NCCL primitives yet claimed to reach throughput “comparable to DeepEP-based MoE implementations” github.com/PaddlePaddle/ERNIE [CODE, verified Jul 2026].

Baidu trains on PaddlePaddle, not Megatron or PyTorch — the one frontier Chinese lab here not building on the Megatron lineage — but the disclosed 47%-MFU run used PaddlePaddle’s older, manual “Fleet” explicit-parallelism path, functionally equivalent to Megatron’s mpu, rather than the framework’s own GSPMD-descended Auto Parallel system. Auto Parallel is real, documented, and shipped (paddle.distributed.shard_tensor/shard_layer/process_mesh), with its own working reference implementation of the identical 300B-A47B model requiring only 56 H800 GPUs — but that implementation is explicitly annotated “more advanced optimizations are on the way,” i.e. not yet the production path at full scale github.com/PaddlePaddle/ERNIE/examples/auto_parallel/README.md [CODE, checked Jul 2026]. FP8 is explicitly modeled on DeepSeek-V3’s recipe: E4M3, block-wise weight quantization plus tile-wise activation quantization, retained FP8 activations across backward, FP8-precision first all-to-all ERNIE 4.5 Technical Report §5.3 [PAPER, Jun 2025]. Fault tolerance is framework-native: automatic recovery under 8 minutes, and a projected >98% effective training time at a hypothetical 10,000-GPU scale assuming linear failure-rate scaling (Ch19’s territory; a claim, not a measured result). Training hardware is confirmed 100% NVIDIA H800 — no Kunlun, no Ascend — despite a separate, unconnected 30,000-card Kunlunxin P800 cluster Baidu lit up two months earlier, apparently serving its inference/fine-tuning cloud business rather than pretraining ERNIE 4.5 Technical Report [PAPER, Jun 2025]. ERNIE 5.0 (Feb 2026) scaled EP from 8-way to 64-way, switched from intra-node-only NCCL EP to DeepEP for necessarily cross-node communication, and added context parallelism — but disclosed zero MFU, hardware, or cluster-size figures, a regression in infra transparency worth flagging for anyone treating 5.0 as a clean successor data point ERNIE 5.0 Technical Report [PAPER, Feb 2026].

What the Aggregate Reveals

Read across all these disclosures, three points of convergence and one genuine, unresolved divergence stand out. FP8 training is now the default wherever precision is disclosed at all — Ling-1T (full, fine-grained blockwise), ERNIE 4.5 (full, DeepSeek-modeled), MegaScale-MoE (full, per-token quantized) — with Kimi K2 as the chapter’s explicit counter-example rather than a partial-adoption data point: K2’s GEMM compute ran in BF16 with FP32 gradient accumulation throughout pretraining (params never touched FP8), and its one disclosed FP8-E4M3 usage is a memory-engineering technique — compressing recomputed MoE up-projection activation inputs to cut activation memory, not a compute-precision choice — layered on an explicit, disclosed decision to decline FP8 pretraining compute after a preliminary study flagged degradation risk (Ch4 covers the full recipe and its cross-lab implications). Every lab reporting an FP8-vs-BF16 validation gives the same order-of-magnitude tolerance, well under 0.5% relative loss deviation over hundreds of billions of tokens. Where MFU is disclosed at all, it clusters in a wide 17-47% band with no obvious correlation to model size; the single highest number (ERNIE’s 47%) comes from the lab using the least MLA-like architecture and the least Megatron-conventional framework, a reminder that MFU is as much a function of engineering effort and cluster topology as of architecture. Activation ratios span a wide range even among labs converged on “sparse MoE”: Kimi K2 at 3.1%, Ling-1T at 3.5%, MiniMax-M2 at roughly 4.3%, DeepSeek-V3 at 5.5%, GLM-4.5 at 9%, Hunyuan-Large at 13.4%, ERNIE-4.5 at 15.7% — Ling-1T’s ~3.5% is the one figure stated as a percentage in its own report; the rest are this chapter’s own division of each paper’s disclosed active/total parameter counts, not verbatim percentages from the sources. Ant’s Efficiency Leverage law argues the sparser end should keep winning as compute scales further, but no lab here has published a controlled ablation isolating activation ratio at trillion-parameter scale the way Ling’s sub-28B study does.

The genuine divergence is attention architecture. MiniMax’s retreat to full attention, backed by quantified long-context degradation, sits directly against Kimi Linear’s claim of a fair-comparison win for hybrid linear attention, Qwen3-Next’s order-of-magnitude efficiency claim from a 3:1 linear-hybrid ratio, and Hunyuan-TurboS’s undisclosed-but-shipped Mamba hybrid. No two labs that tried a non-full-attention architecture at scale report the same verdict, and MiniMax is the only one that quantifies a negative result rather than only reporting positive ones — worth weighting accordingly.

What is conspicuously undisclosed forms its own pattern:

  • Aggregate cluster size and MFU for Kimi K2 — the single most detailed parallelism and memory-budget disclosure in the chapter never states either number.
  • Any hardware, parallelism, or precision detail for Qwen3, the most commercially deployed open-weight family here — a complete blackout against otherwise thorough architecture and data disclosure.
  • ByteDance’s current-generation (Seed 2.0/2.1) training infrastructure — despite likely operating one of the largest GPU fleets in China, the newest matched infra detail (MegaScale-MoE) documents a 352B benchmark model and a separately cited 200B/20B production model, neither badged as the actual Doubao flagship.
  • MiniMax-M2’s pretraining infrastructure — the M1→M2 reversal is extensively documented; the hardware and parallelism used to actually train M2’s 29.2T tokens are not.
  • Hunyuan’s hardware entirely, across both Hunyuan-Large and TurboS — confirmed opaque even by a competitor’s own paper.
  • iFlytek’s Spark line — no technical report or credible engineering post with any pretraining-relevant detail exists at all, a genuine gap rather than a search failure.
  • GB200/Blackwell training anywhere in this corpus. Every hardware figure here — H800, H20, A800, Hopper generically — is pre-Blackwell, mostly export-controlled-tier Hopper. None of these MFU numbers, parallelism layouts, or FP8 recipes transfer directly to a GB200 NVL72 build without translation through Ch14 and Ch16; they are evidence about MoE methodology at scale, not about Blackwell utilization.

Implications for the 10T/200B Run

The aggregate argues for three concrete positions. First, the Efficiency Leverage law’s compute-dependence term (EL grows with log compute) is the strongest available evidence that a 10T/200B design sitting an order of magnitude above Ling-1T’s training compute should push sparser than Ling’s own 3.5% activation, not merely match it — this should anchor Ch5’s activation-ratio decision, with Kimi K2’s 3.1% as a second, independently-arrived-at data point in the same direction. Second, MuonClip and the broader Muon-adoption pattern (confirmed independently at Moonshot and Zhipu, and — per Ch4 — adopted by DeepSeek itself in V4) means any 10T/200B optimizer plan should treat Muon-class orthogonalized updates plus a QK-clip-style safety valve as the field’s converged answer to trillion-parameter attention-logit stability, not a Moonshot curiosity; budget per-head MaxLogit monitoring as a first-class training metric regardless of whose exact recipe you follow. Third, and most consequentially: MiniMax’s full-attention retreat warns against locking in a hybrid or linear-attention architecture based on saturated 2024-2025-era benchmarks, because the specific failure mode — long-context, multi-hop, agentic degradation — is exactly the capability profile a 10T/200B model is presumably being built to have, and MiniMax’s own account states plainly that the relevant architectural commitment (global attention patterns fixed early in pretraining) cannot be cheaply undone mid-run. A strawman that bets on hybrid attention needs a validation suite built to catch this failure mode before the architecture is frozen, not after.
