A text-only frontier pretraining run is now the exception a lab has to justify, not the default it can assume. Every architecture family in the closed-lab and China-frontier surveys (Ch2, Ch3) ships some form of native vision and, increasingly, audio ingestion from step one — and the labs that have published enough detail to compare (Baidu, Meta, Moonshot, Alibaba, Ant, Google) disagree sharply on how modality should touch the MoE router, the tokenizer, and the parallelism layout. This chapter treats that disagreement as data: it is not a solved design space, one lab has already reversed its own flagship choice within eight months, and a 10T/200B host planning a primarily-text run still has to make load-bearing infrastructure decisions now to avoid an expensive retrofit later.

The 2026 baseline: why multimodal-native is no longer optional

The evidence for “multimodal-native is the default” is not a single number but a convergence: Baidu’s ERNIE 4.5 and 5.0, Meta’s Llama 4/Behemoth, Moonshot’s Kimi-VL, Alibaba’s Qwen3-VL and Qwen3-Omni/Qwen3.5-Omni, Google’s Gemini 2.5, and ByteDance’s Doubao Seed 2.0/2.1 are all disclosed or credibly reported as natively multimodal from pretraining, not vision-bolted-on-after (Ch3 and Ch8 carry the lab-by-lab architecture tables). Meta’s own framing of Llama 4 is explicit: “more than 30 trillion tokens… text, image, and video,” with “10x more multilingual tokens than Llama 3” (The Llama 4 herd, Apr 2025). Google states plainly that “the Gemini 2.5 models are sparse mixture-of-experts (MoE)… transformers with native multimodal support for text, vision, and audio inputs” (Gemini 2.5 technical report, 2025). Even labs whose primary published artifact is a text benchmark leader — Kimi K2, GLM-4.5, MiniMax-M2 — sit inside product families (Kimi-VL, GLM’s broader Zhipu multimodal line, MiniMax’s own omni efforts) that ship vision and audio variants close behind the text flagship.

The practical consequence is not that a 10T/200B run must be multimodal — it is that the design decisions this chapter covers (routing, tokenizer, dataloader, parallelism topology) are being made by every peer lab regardless, and several are cheap to provision for now and expensive to retrofit later. The closing section turns that observation into a checklist.

Three architectures for wiring modality into MoE

There is no single answer to “how does a vision (or audio) token interact with an MoE router.” The public record supports three genuinely distinct, coexisting design families rather than one converged pattern.

Pattern A — modality-isolated expert pools within a single MoE backbone. Baidu’s ERNIE 4.5 (technical report, Jun 2025) is the most fully specified public example. Text and vision tokens route to structurally distinct expert pools (text experts, vision experts, shared experts) inside every MoE layer, while both modalities pass through all self-attention layers, which stay dense. Vision experts are quantitatively cheaper: their FFN intermediate dimension is one-third that of text experts, yielding roughly a 66% reduction in per-token FFN FLOPs for a routed vision token relative to a routed text token (report §2.1). Vision experts are excluded entirely from the final Transformer layer, “as their weights do not contribute to the cross-entropy loss,” and are introduced only in later stages of the training curriculum specifically to avoid routing collapse — ERNIE’s own ablations show naively extending a text-only router to multimodal inputs degrades textual capability. The stability machinery behind the split is a router-orthogonalization loss applied directly to router weight vectors with an AdamW-style decoupled update (coefficient 1e-3, not LR-scaled), plus a token-balanced loss that normalizes cross-entropy by total sequence length rather than valid-token count — in multimodal sequences only text tokens contribute to loss and their proportion varies wildly sample-to-sample, which otherwise “induces substantial gradient variance” (report §3.4).

Modality-isolated MoE did not originate at Baidu. Meta FAIR’s MoMa (Jul 2024) publishes it first, with FLOPs-accounting rigor ERNIE’s paper doesn’t reproduce: a 1.4B model with 4 text + 4 image experts hits 3.7× overall FLOPs savings (2.6× text, 5.2× image) at a 1T-token budget, versus 3× for a standard 8-expert mixed-modal MoE (3× text, 2.8× image); adding mixture-of-depths pushes the isolated design to 4.2× overall. Image tokens benefit disproportionately — the paper’s interpretation is that they are structurally more redundant, so a narrower expert suffices without quality loss. MoMa’s efficiency section (§3.1–3.2) also surfaces an operational cost ERNIE’s paper glosses over: under FSDP, the text:image token ratio varies across GPUs and iterations, creating a “cascading straggler effect” that bounds step latency by the slowest GPU’s worst-case modality mix. The fix is dataloading-level, not routing-level — pre-align the text:image data-mix ratio to the per-GPU expert ratio, plus sequential expert execution by modality to overlap compute with weight prefetch.

The load-bearing update to this whole line is that ERNIE 5.0 (technical report, Feb 2026) explicitly reversed it. Its own text: “modality-agnostic expert routing, where routing decisions are conditioned on unified token representations rather than modality identifiers… In contrast to modality-isolated routing strategies in our previous models (ERNIE 4.5).” ERNIE 5.0 pairs this with an ultra-sparse design (activation rate below 3%) and a novel elastic training paradigm — within one pretraining run the model simultaneously learns sub-networks varying in depth (75% full-depth / 25% reduced-depth), expert width (80% full-expert / 20% sampled-subset), and routing sparsity (80% standard top-k / 20% varied top-k), reportedly preserving near-full performance at 53.7% activated parameters and 35.8% total parameters. Its disclosed parallelism is 4-way TP, 12-way PP with virtual stages, 64-way EP, ZeRO-1 DP, plus context parallelism, and it disaggregates vision/audio tokenizers onto separate GPU nodes from the MoE backbone — a disaggregation choice absent from ERNIE 4.5. No GPU count, MFU, or per-modality token budget was disclosed for 5.0. This is a rare, citable case of a frontier lab publicly walking back its own architectural bet on multimodal MoE routing within eight months of shipping it — and it reframes modality-isolated MoE as a contested design point being abandoned by its inventor’s successor, not a converged answer.

Pattern B — continuous encoder feeding a modality-unaware shared MoE. This is the majority pattern in practice, despite “native multimodal” marketing language suggesting something architecturally novel. Llama 4, Kimi-VL, and Qwen3-VL all do this: a vision encoder (MetaCLIP for Llama 4; MoonViT, a NaViT-style native-resolution packer, for Kimi-VL; continuously-trained SigLIP-2 for Qwen3-VL) produces continuous embeddings, an adapter projects them into the LLM’s hidden dimension, and the resulting tokens flow through the same expert pool as text with no router modification. Meta’s own description of Llama 4’s MoE layer — “128 routed experts and a shared expert. Each token is sent to the shared expert and also to one of the 128 routed experts” — makes no distinction for image tokens (The Llama 4 herd, Apr 2025). Kimi-VL’s MoE decoder (Moonlight, 16B total / 2.8B active) uses standard 4D parallelism (DP + EP + PP + CP) with no modality-conditioned routing logic disclosed (Kimi-VL technical report, Jun 2025 revision). Qwen3-VL frames its novelty as DeepStack — injecting visual features from three distinct ViT depths into the first three LLM layers via dedicated mergers — and interleaved MRoPE, not routing; its MoE variant’s expert pool is otherwise unmodified from the text-only Qwen3 MoE (Qwen3-VL technical report, Nov/Dec 2025).

Pattern C — dual independent MoE stacks split by function, not modality. Qwen3-Omni and its April 2026 successor Qwen3.5-Omni use a “Thinker-Talker” architecture where both halves are independently sparse MoE (Qwen3-Omni, Sep 2025; Qwen3.5-Omni, Apr 2026). The Thinker handles multimodal understanding and text generation; the Talker consumes only audio/visual features (not the Thinker’s text hidden states) and autoregressively predicts streaming multi-codebook speech tokens via an MTP-style multi-track codec, with waveform synthesis via a lightweight causal ConvNet (“Code2Wav”) rather than a diffusion model, running audio codes at 12.5 Hz for low-latency synthesis. This is architecturally distinct from A and B — the modality split happens at the macro level, two independent transformer/MoE stacks with different objectives, rather than at the expert level within one stack.

Gemini 2.5 is confirmed sparse-MoE native-multimodal with no further routing-mechanism disclosure, trained on TPUv5p pods of 8,960 chips each with synchronous data-parallel training across multiple datacenters (Gemini 2.5 technical report, 2025). Amazon’s Nova report (The Amazon Nova Family of Models, Mar 2025) is essentially silent on architecture beyond confirming a Transformer base and multilingual/multimodal pretraining data, with Nova Canvas/Reel disclosed as architecturally separate latent-diffusion models with a VAE. Both are genuine disclosure gaps, not search failures.

Two very recent papers, both positioned explicitly as responses to ERNIE 4.5’s limitations, suggest the field is converging on a middle ground. SMoES (“Soft Modality-Guided Expert Specialization in MoE-VLMs,” Apr 2026) argues hard modality routing causes “sharp specialization at the cost of rigid boundaries that hinder adaptation to cross-modal features,” and proposes continuous, layer-adaptive soft modality scores plus an “expert binning” mechanism for EP-friendly device placement — reporting +4.2% average on language-only tasks, +0.9% on multimodal tasks, a 56.1% reduction in EP communication overhead, and 12.3% throughput improvement across four MoE-VLM backbones. MoIIE (“Mixture of Intra- and Inter-Modality Experts,” Aug 2025) pursues a related hybrid where some experts are modality-specific and others shared cross-modal. Net read: treat “hard isolation vs. full sharing” as a tunable spectrum with a real, now-quantified EP-communication cost at the isolated end, not a binary commitment.

Tokenization: discrete codebooks, continuous embeddings, and vision-as-compression

Two tokenizer families coexist and interact with MoE routing very differently. Chameleon (Meta, May 2024) represents the discrete, unified-vocabulary approach: a 512×512 image becomes 1,024 discrete tokens from an 8,192-entry codebook, and those 8,192 codes live inside a single 65,536-token vocabulary shared with text, so image and text tokens are literally interchangeable positions in the same embedding table and the same next-token objective applies uniformly. This genuinely early fusion — image and text tokens flow through identical weights from layer 1 — required two non-obvious stability interventions: QK-norm (LayerNorm on query/key vectors pre-softmax, to control attention-logit growth, worse under mixed-modal training) and a z-loss term (1e-5 · log²Z penalizing softmax-partition-function growth), plus, for the 34B variant, reordered norm placement to bound feedforward-block norm growth. ERNIE 5.0 uses a related but more sophisticated discrete scheme — a “causal 2D multi-scale tokenizer” for images, “inflated into 3D for video,” with bit-wise quantization and progressive tokenizer switching from low-bit to higher-bit variants (a curriculum over discretization granularity, not a fixed codebook), while audio uses hierarchical Residual Vector Quantization codec tokens with distilled semantic tokens from Whisper and “Next-Codec Prediction” across codec dimensions (ERNIE 5.0 report, Feb 2026).

The majority pattern — ERNIE 4.5, Kimi-VL, Qwen3-VL, Llama 4 — does not discretize images into a shared vocabulary at all; continuous ViT features are projected into the LLM’s embedding space, so there is no “image vocab size,” and the interaction with the 200K+ text vocabulary is purely at the embedding-dimension level. ERNIE 4.5’s vision encoder is an adaptive-resolution ViT (it independently resizes height/width to the nearest patch multiple rather than forcing square resizing) using 2D RoPE for spatial position and an image-packing technique to batch variable-length images; its adapter does spatial compression (2×2 patches → 4× token reduction) and temporal compression (2×) via pixel-shuffle + MLP, treating static images as synthetic two-frame video to unify handling. For the fused sequence it uses a genuinely novel 3D RoPE — the temporal axis gets the lowest (slowest-varying) frequencies, the spatial height/width axes get the interleaved remaining frequencies, and text tokens default to standard 1D RoPE (report §2.2–2.4). It also encodes video time via literal timestamp text rendered onto frames rather than a position-embedding or token-based scheme, claiming this is “flexible to any frame rate, consumes no extra tokens.” Qwen3-VL converged on a related but token-based solution — explicit timestamp tokens (e.g. <3.0 seconds>) prefixed to each temporal patch — because tying temporal position IDs to absolute time, as Qwen2.5-VL did via MRoPE, “produces excessively large and sparse temporal position ids for long videos” (Qwen3-VL report §2.3, Nov 2025). Kimi-VL’s MoonViT instead relies purely on interpolated + 2D-RoPE positional embeddings with no timestamp mechanism, its NaViT-style patch-packing flattening variable-resolution images into 1D sequences that “share the same core computation operators and optimization as a language model,” explicitly to preserve FlashAttention-compatible variable-length training throughput (Kimi-VL report §2.1, Jun 2025).

The most consequential idea here for a text-centric host is DeepSeek-OCR’s reframing of vision tokens as a context-compression primitive rather than a modality-understanding mechanism (Oct 2025). Its encoder is a serial SAM-base (80M, window attention) → 16× convolutional token compressor → CLIP-large (300M, dense global attention) pipeline: a 1024×1024 image becomes 4,096 SAM patch tokens, compressed to 256 before the CLIP stage. The headline compression curve: within 10× compression, roughly 97% OCR decode accuracy; at roughly 15× compression (64 vision tokens per ~1,000-token document), 79–96.5% depending on document; at roughly 20× compression, accuracy drops to about 60%. Training data mix was 70% OCR (30M PDF pages + 20M scene-text images), 20% general vision, 10% text-only. The paper poses vision-token compression as a general answer to “for a document containing 1,000 words, how many vision tokens are at least needed for decoding,” and floats progressive optical downsampling of older conversational or document history as a token-economics technique — a pretraining- and serving-time context-budget lever, not an OCR trick, directly relevant to a 200B-active host’s long-document token-budget planning even absent any product need for OCR (see Ch14).

Data mixing at scale: what the disclosed curricula actually show

Clean, comparable percentage-of-tokens-by-modality tables are the scarcest resource in this literature — most 2025–2026 reports disclose stage structure far more consistently than stage token-count percentages, a genuine and consistent reporting gap relative to, e.g., Llama 3’s clean 50/25/17/8 text-domain split. We checked ERNIE 4.5/5.0, Qwen3-VL, Kimi-VL, Chameleon, and Llama 4’s own materials directly; the absence is real, not a search failure. Two patterns cross all four. First, every non-Chameleon lab stages modality introduction — text-only or text-heavy pretraining first, vision/multimodal data introduced or ramped later — rather than mixing from token zero the way Chameleon did; ERNIE 4.5’s paper is explicit that vision experts are added late “to avoid routing collapse,” and Kimi-VL’s multimodal proportion is described as “gradually increasing” from a pure-text start toward a ~40% ceiling. Second, Qwen3-VL’s total disclosed pretraining budget (~2.17T tokens) is far below the 15T+ text-only budgets typical of contemporaneous pure-LLM training (see Ch8 and Ch10’s ladder-recipe discussion), suggesting that VL pretraining built on top of a strong existing text base is token-cheap relative to training multimodal capability from scratch — consistent with Qwen3-VL’s own square-root-normalized per-token loss, adopted specifically to prevent multimodal and text losses from imbalancing each other during joint training on a comparatively small joint budget (report §3.1, Nov 2025).

Kimi K2.5 is the anchor this table was missing at flagship scale, and — reading the Ch3 disclosure through this chapter’s lens — it complicates the “retrofit is token-cheap” conclusion rather than confirming it. K2.5 took the fully-trained 1.04T/32.6B K2-Base — the largest model on this table by an order of magnitude — and made it natively multimodal via a single continual-pretraining pass of roughly 15T mixed visual-and-text tokens, essentially doubling the model’s cumulative pretraining budget, versus Kimi-VL’s 4.4T staged budget at 16B scale and Qwen3-VL’s ~2.17T. Three readings follow. First, the staging pattern survives at 1T scale unchanged: text flagship first, modality after — the strongest confirmation yet that no frontier lab mixes from token zero at scale (Chameleon remains the lone counterexample, at 7B/34B). Second, the cost of the retrofit path scales with ambition, not model size per se: Qwen3-VL’s ~2T buys VL understanding bolted onto a text model, while K2.5’s ~15T buys a natively multimodal flagship at full capability parity — the difference between adding a capability and re-founding the model’s distribution. A 10T/200B host planning “we’ll add vision later” should budget the later at K2.5’s ratio (roughly 1x the original pretraining budget for parity-grade integration), not Qwen3-VL’s (~15% for capability-grade bolt-on). Third, K2.5 validates that the retrofit is safe at flagship scale — no disclosed instability, MuonClip retained unchanged, context extended to 256K in the same pass — which, combined with ERNIE 5.0’s routing walk-back, strengthens the Pattern-B-and-defer recommendation below: the option value of deferring modality is real, and the eventual bill, while large, is now a known quantity at the relevant scale.

MoMa’s efficiency section (§3.1) adds an operational point orthogonal to curation: under modality-isolated routing, the data-mix ratio isn’t purely a curation decision — it is a distributed-systems constraint that must be co-designed with the per-GPU expert ratio to avoid stragglers. That coupling is easy to miss if data mixing and MoE architecture are organizationally owned by different teams, which they usually are.

The input pipeline: packing, load balance, and deterministic replay

Chapter 15 owns dataloading requirements in full; this section covers only what multimodal data specifically breaks. Megatron’s dedicated multimodal loader, Megatron-Energon, is WebDataset-lineage — a standardized on-disk format optimized for high-speed multi-rank reads, dataset mixing, and, critically, savable/restorable iteration state via get_savable_loader(), so a long multimodal run can checkpoint which shard, which sample within shard, and worker RNG state, then resume deterministically after preemption. This state-save design is architecturally closer to MosaicML StreamingDataset’s or Grain’s continuous-index approach than to Megatron-Core’s precompute-then-stream GPTDataset model, and it becomes load-bearing the moment any multimodal or agentic-data component enters a 10T host’s mix even at low weight.

The core breakage is that fixed-length sequence packing assumes roughly uniform per-sample compute cost — an assumption that holds for text and fails hard under variable-resolution images and, worse, variable-duration video. ERNIE 4.5’s paper documents this directly: naive packing causes “extreme computational and memory imbalances between different data parallel ranks of the ViT encoder,” because “video data presents more severe imbalance due to temporal length variations” than image resolution does. Their fix is hierarchical. A coarse level sorts all packed sequences by token count within the ViT encoder’s DP group and round-robin-distributes them for balanced total token count per device. A fine level, “packed sequence parallelism” (PSP, distinct from Megatron’s TP-based sequence parallelism), concatenates and evenly partitions packed sequences along the sequence-length dimension outside attention operators, then performs an all-to-all to exchange sequence-length and attention-head dimensions before and after attention, which needs the full sequence-length dimension per packed sequence. Measured result: up to 32% overall end-to-end multimodal training throughput improvement versus no load balancing (ERNIE 4.5 §5.1.2, Jun 2025).

ByteDance’s MegaScale-Data (EuroSys 2026) quantifies the same failure mode independently and more starkly, in a pure dataloading context rather than an encoder-specific one: because attention FLOPs scale with sequence length squared, non-uniform per-rank sample-length distributions from multi-source data create compute imbalance even when sample counts are balanced across ranks. In an 8-GPU VLM trial, max microbatch FLOPs were 3.2× higher (image) and 6.9× higher (full sequence) than the minimum — pure dataloader-composition-induced bubbles, invisible to naive samples/sec metrics. Their actor-model redesign, which decouples preprocessing scheduling from the standard iterator model, delivers 4.5× end-to-end training throughput and 13.5× CPU memory reduction versus PyTorch DataLoader, tf.data, Ray Data, Cachew, and Pecan baselines at up to 4,096 GPUs, with context-length-dependent gains (1.71× at 4K, 3.09× at 16K) and fault tolerance via shadow-loader hot failover with differential checkpointing plus elastic resharding for mid-run parallelism changes. This is the closest thing in the public literature to “what a dataloader built for a 10T-MoE-scale run with any multimodal component should look like” — and notably it is not Megatron-Core, Energon, StreamingDataset, or Grain; it’s a bespoke, unreleased system.

torchtitan’s own maintainers flag a smaller but concrete version of the same problem: multimodal loading — image download, decode, resize, pad — is CPU-intensive enough to bottleneck a PyTorch-native reference trainer, with “more DataLoader workers” as the first-line mitigation (torchtitan issue #650). NVIDIA DALI exists as a GPU-accelerated answer to exactly this class of problem, but its reader/decoder library is overwhelmingly vision/audio-centric (JPEG/video decode, augmentation) with no text-pretraining-specific readers — relevant mainly if interleaved data is tokenized to image patches rather than for the text stream itself.

ERNIE 4.5 solves a second, dataloading-adjacent problem worth flagging independent of its multimodal motivation: reproducible, non-duplicating data consumption under elastic or interrupted training. REEAO (“Record Everything Everywhere All at Once”) is a bitwise-deterministic data-flow manager guaranteeing the exact token sequence is fixed before training starts and stays reproducible “even under complex scenarios such as changes in the number of training nodes, distribution strategies, global batch size, or context length” (ERNIE 4.5 §3.2, Jun 2025). ERNIE’s own framing is that multimodal data’s heterogeneity specifically motivated building this, but the guarantee is valuable for any 10T-scale run with mid-run elasticity, text-only or not (Ch21 covers fault tolerance and checkpoint replay).

Parallelism topology: decoupling encoder and backbone

Does a vision encoder change EP/TP/PP composition? Yes — and a very recent NVIDIA paper is the first to quantify it systematically rather than gesture at it. Heterogeneous Parallelism for Multimodal Large Language Model Training (released May 28, 2026) identifies the exact failure mode a 10T/200B host would hit forcing one shared TP/CP/PP/DP/EP layout onto both encoder and LLM. LLM-scale tensor-parallel degrees introduce all-reduce overhead that “can outweigh compute and memory savings” for a small encoder. LLM context parallelism shards the fused sequence, but encoder sequence length is bounded by image/video resolution rather than context window, so inheriting LLM-CP wastes shards that could instead be encoder-DP replicas processing more samples per step. And pipeline parallelism confines the encoder to the LLM’s first PP stage, forcing encoder-heavy workloads to inherit that stage’s TP degree and overload it.

Their solution is a Megatron-LM extension in which each module — encoder, LLM — independently picks TP/CP/PP/DP/EP, connected via autograd-aware “boundary communicators” that materialize forward activations in the destination layout and route backward gradients back to the source layout. Measured on an H100 80GB cluster (900 GB/s NVLink intra-node, 8×400 Gb/s ConnectX-7 IB inter-node): for a 1B encoder + 14B LLM at 8K context on 72 GPUs, the best homogeneous configuration is a shared 4/1/1/18 TP/CP/PP/DP layout, while the best heterogeneous configuration splits to encoder 2/1/1/36 and LLM 2/1/2/18 at identical GPU count and peak memory. Across the full sweep, colocated heterogeneous execution improves TFLOPS/GPU by up to 49.3% over the homogeneous shared-layout baseline. For a 120B LLM, where encoder memory residency starts competing with LLM pipeline layout, non-colocated execution — encoder and LLM on physically disjoint GPU islands — becomes the better operating point, improving aggregate token throughput by up to 13.0% and TFLOPS/GPU by up to 9.6%. The crossover from colocated-optimal to non-colocated-optimal happens starting around a 6B-parameter encoder paired against a 120B LLM. Convergence parity (FP32 step-level numerical tolerance plus longer BF16 LLaVA-style runs) is validated, and the system ships open-source as a Megatron-LM extension (the paper’s Appendix A has the configs).

This paper directly extends Ch18’s parallelism-composition math, which is developed for the text backbone alone — an EP/TP/PP/DP layout tuned for a 200B-active MoE at 100K GPUs was never going to be encoder-optimal even for a small encoder, and the crossover point (~6B encoder parameters) gives a concrete threshold for when to stop trying to force colocation.

Compute accounting: what vision tokens actually cost

Three independent quantifications converge on the same nuanced answer: a vision token is cheaper per-token in FFN FLOPs than a text token under modality-isolated routing, but that advantage can reverse once encoder overhead, EP communication tax, and architecture-driven (not modality-driven) throughput effects are counted.

  • ERNIE 4.5: vision-expert FFN intermediate dimension is one-third of text-expert dimension, giving roughly 66% lower per-token FFN FLOPs for a routed vision token versus a routed text token, since FFN FLOPs scale with the product of input and intermediate dimension (report §2.1).
  • MoMa: under FLOPs-matched training, image tokens gain more from modality-specific parameter allocation than text tokens do — 5.2× FLOPs savings for image versus 2.6× for text in the 4-text/4-image configuration — interpreted as image tokens being structurally more redundant, so a narrower expert suffices without quality loss (arXiv:2407.21770, Jul 2024).
  • Kimi-VL: the vision encoder itself (MoonViT, 400M params) is a rounding error relative to the 16B-total MoE backbone, and Kimi-VL’s own comparison states overall training throughput is “around 60% higher” than a 7B dense VLM at comparable capability — but this delta is architecture-driven (MoE sparsity), not modality-driven, and shouldn’t be read as a vision-specific efficiency number (Kimi-VL report, Jun 2025).
  • DeepSeek-OCR inverts the framing entirely, asking not “what does a vision token cost” but “how few vision tokens can replace N text tokens” — landing on a 10–20× token-count compression ratio at 60–97% fidelity depending on target compression. This is a token-count reduction lever, orthogonal to per-token FLOPs cost, and arguably the more consequential number for a 200B-active host doing long-context or long-document pretraining (arXiv:2510.18234, Oct 2025).
  • SMoES supplies the missing cost on the other side of the ledger: its expert-binning fix for modality-isolated-style routing reduces EP communication overhead by 56.1% and improves throughput by 12.3% relative to unbinned modality-aware routing under expert-parallel deployment — direct evidence that Pattern A carries a nontrivial, now-quantified EP communication tax versus Pattern B that a 10T-host planner should budget for if choosing hard isolation (arXiv:2604.23996, Apr 2026).

Two production-scale throughput datapoints round out the picture. Meta’s only disclosed Behemoth efficiency number is 390 TFLOPs/GPU using FP8 on 32,000 GPUs (The Llama 4 herd, Apr 2025) — notable here mainly because Meta discloses no vision-specific FLOPs breakdown, consistent with Pattern B’s “no modality-aware anything” design. ByteDance Seed’s VeOmni (Aug 2025, feeding AAAI 2026’s “OmniScale”) decouples parallel-strategy choice (FSDP/HSDP/SP/EP) from model definition via a “parallel plan” API and reports a 30B omni-modal MoE training at over 2,800 tokens/sec/GPU scaling to 160K context on 128 GPUs, a 7B dense VLM hitting 61.5% MFU at 192K context on 8 GPUs (FSDP+SP4), and a 72B dense VLM hitting 54.82% MFU at 96K context on 128 GPUs — the most complete public MFU accounting for omni-modal training infrastructure anywhere in this literature, though its link to any specific lab’s frontier production run, including ByteDance’s own Doubao Seed 2.x line, is unconfirmed.

Implications for the 10T/200B run

A primarily-text 10T/200B host is unlikely to ship without some multimodal capability eventually, given every disclosed or credibly-reported peer architecture in Ch2/Ch3 already has one. The infrastructure decisions here split cleanly into cheap-now/expensive-later and can-defer categories:

  • Decouple encoder and backbone parallelism from day one, even with zero vision data in the launch mix. Building the LLM’s TP/CP/PP/DP/EP composition (Ch18) as a module-level choice rather than a single fixed layout is the single highest-leverage provisioning move this chapter surfaces — NVIDIA’s heterogeneous-parallelism paper shows the retrofit cost (forcing a shared layout, then re-deriving a decoupled one later) is exactly the 49.3% TFLOPS/GPU gap between homogeneous and colocated-heterogeneous execution.
  • Default to Pattern B (continuous encoder into a modality-unaware shared MoE) if and when multimodal data is added, not Pattern A. It is the majority pattern among 2025–2026 shipped systems, and ERNIE 5.0’s own reversal plus SMoES’s quantified EP tax both argue against hard isolation as a durable choice. Treat modality-isolated routing as a research-frontier option requiring its own EP-communication budget, not a safe default.
  • Provision Energon-class dataloader state-save semantics and hierarchical packing/load-balance logic even for a text-only launch. The measured cost of not having this — up to 32% end-to-end throughput lost to naive packing under ERNIE’s regime, and the 3.2×/6.9× per-rank FLOP imbalance MegaScale-Data measures from dataloader composition alone — recurs the moment any variable-length, variable-cost data source (video, long documents, agent trajectories) enters the mix, which is a near-certainty for a run planning midtraining and agentic data curricula (Ch14, Ch15).
  • Build bitwise-deterministic data replay (REEAO-style) as a fault-tolerance primitive, not a multimodal one. It composes directly with Ch21’s checkpoint and elastic-restart architecture and pays for itself independent of any modality decision.
  • Treat vision-token compression (DeepSeek-OCR’s framing) as a context-budget lever worth reserving data and encoder-path headroom for, even in a text-centric model. A 10–20× token-count reduction at 60–97% fidelity is a lever on effective context length and long-document training cost that has nothing to do with shipping OCR as a product feature.
  • Budget FLOPs and EP-communication accounting separately if any modality-isolated routing is considered. The ~66% per-token FFN saving ERNIE 4.5 reports is real but is not free — SMoES’s 56.1% EP-communication-overhead reduction from expert binning implies the unbinned baseline tax is substantial, and that tax must be modeled against Ch18’s EP topology before committing to Pattern A over Pattern B.

None of this requires shipping a multimodal model on day one of a 10T/200B run. It requires not architecting the parallelism stack, the dataloader, and the checkpoint/replay layer as if text is the only modality that will ever exist — because on the evidence of every peer lab in Ch2 and Ch3, it will not be for long.
