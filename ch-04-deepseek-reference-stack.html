No other near-frontier lab has published a training stack in the granularity DeepSeek has: a full FP8 numerics section with the exact hardware bug it works around, a pipeline schedule with a bubble-vs-memory table, an all-to-all comm budget quantified down to the SM, and — almost uniquely — the raw PyTorch Profiler traces from the actual production run. So the V3/V3.2/V4 lineage serves as the reference implementation here, the stack every design choice in Chapters 5-21 gets checked against — not because DeepSeek is the biggest lab, but because it is the only one that shows its work. Four primary sources carry most of this chapter and are cited by section throughout: the V3 technical report (Dec 2024), the hardware-reflections paper “Insights into DeepSeek-V3” (May 2025), the Fire-Flyer AI-HPC paper (Aug 2024), and the V4 report (April 2026).

The V3 cluster: parallelism without tensor parallelism

DeepSeek-V3 trained on 2,048 NVIDIA H800 GPUs, 8 per node, NVLink+NVSwitch intra-node and InfiniBand inter-node (V3 report §3.1). The production parallelism recipe is 16-way pipeline parallelism, 64-way expert parallelism spanning 8 nodes (EP64 = 8 nodes × 8 GPUs, routed experts spread uniformly across those 64 GPUs), and ZeRO-1 data parallelism — with no tensor parallelism anywhere in training (§3.2). That is a real departure from 2024-era Megatron defaults, which lean on TP as the standard intra-node lever. DeepSeek’s argument: once EP is already saturating the interconnect, adding TP’s all-reduce/all-gather traffic on top is pure overhead, and the memory pressure TP normally relieves can be absorbed instead by recomputation and CPU-offload tricks (below). The training framework is HAI-LLM, the direct successor to HaiScale from the pre-V3 Fire-Flyer cluster (§3.2). For a 10T/200B design that will almost certainly need TP somewhere (Ch18), this is a useful existence proof that TP is not structurally required even at 64-way EP. The open question is whether the same recompute/offload substitutions scale to 100k GPUs and 200B active parameters — or whether TP’s absence is itself an artifact of 2,048-GPU scale.

DualPipe: bidirectional scheduling for near-zero all-to-all overhead

DeepSeek’s own framing of the problem: cross-node EP communication and compute run at roughly a 1:1 ratio without overlap, so naive execution would nearly double wall-clock step time (§3.2.1). DualPipe’s answer splits each micro-batch chunk into four components — attention, all-to-all dispatch, MLP, all-to-all combine — with backward chunks further split into “backward-for-input” and “backward-for-weights” in the style of ZeroBubble/ZB1P. It then feeds micro-batches from both ends of the pipeline simultaneously, manually tuning the SM split between comm and compute so both all-to-all and PP communication hide fully behind compute (§3.2.1).

(DualPipe repo README; V3 report Table 2.)

The price is explicit: DualPipe carries two copies of the model’s parameters per device — tolerable specifically because a large EP degree already keeps the effective per-GPU parameter count small. DualPipeV, a V-shaped derivative credited to Sea AI Lab’s “cut-in-half” blog post rather than DeepSeek itself, halves device count for the same bubble bound — a genuine third-party contribution folded back into the official repo, and an early data point on how quickly the open Chinese ecosystem iterates on DeepSeek’s releases (Ch3 has the broader picture).

The genuinely underused artifact is profile-data: DeepSeek open-sourced the actual PyTorch Profiler traces — chrome://tracing-loadable JSON — for training (EP64, TP1, 4K sequence length, 4 MoE layers per chunk, PP comm excluded for clarity), prefill (EP32), and decode (EP128). This is the literal DualPipe overlap pattern from the real V3 pretraining run, not a synthetic reproduction, and almost no third-party analysis engages with the raw trace files directly. For anyone doing overlap-schedule engineering at 10T/200B scale, it is a high-value resource sitting in plain sight.

The 20-SM comms budget: routing topology as the binding constraint

The network asymmetry on H800 is NVLink at 160 GB/s versus InfiniBand at 50 GB/s, roughly 3.2:1 (§3.2.2). The routing rule caps each token at dispatch to at most M=4 nodes (“node-limited routing”), selected by summing top-K_r/M affinity scores per node. A token crosses IB once, to the GPU with matching in-node index on the target node, then NVLink-forwards to the GPU(s) actually hosting its target experts, with the IB and NVLink phases fully overlapped. The paper states this scheme lets each token reach an average of 3.2 experts per node — meaning the same comm-cost envelope that supports V3’s actual 8-of-256 expert selection could support up to 4 nodes × 3.2 experts/node = 13 activated experts with zero added communication cost (§3.2.2). That is one of the more consequential numbers in the whole record for the Ch7 strawman: DeepSeek is explicit that routing topology, not per-token compute, is the binding constraint on activation count at their scale — and they chose to leave that slack on the table, presumably trading against quality-per-FLOP rather than comms.

The mechanism itself: 20 of 132 SMs on H800, split into 10 communication channels via warp specialization. During dispatch, separate warps handle IB sending, IB→NVLink forwarding, and NVLink receiving, with warp counts dynamically rebalanced by load; combine mirrors this in reverse with NVLink sending, NVLink→IB forwarding+accumulation, and IB receiving+accumulation, using custom PTX and auto-tuned chunk sizes to avoid L2 pollution from comm kernels sharing SMs with compute (§3.2.2). The hardware-reflections paper calls this SM allocation a tax outright — “using SMs for communication results in significant inefficiencies, as tensor cores remain entirely under-utilized” — and asks for a GPU/network co-processor analogous to NVIDIA SHARP to absorb IB↔NVLink forwarding, RDMA buffer transport, all-to-all-combine reduction, and chunked memory-layout management directly, unifying scale-up and scale-out into one addressable domain (Insights §3.5.1).

FP8: the recipe, precisely, and why it isn’t naive

The mixed-precision framework runs Fprop, Dgrad, and Wgrad for every Linear operator in FP8, while embedding, output head, MoE gate, normalization ops, and attention itself stay in BF16/FP32 (§3.3.1). Two design choices separate this from a naive cast.

Granularity and format. Activations are scaled 1×128 (per-token, per-128-channel tile); weights are scaled 128×128 (per-128-input-channel × per-128-output-channel block), both computed online per step rather than from a delayed/historical amax (§3.3.2). Format is E4M3 uniformly — a deliberate break from the common E4M3-forward/E5M2-backward convention, viable because fine-grained block scaling already compensates for E4M3’s narrower dynamic range by sharing exponent budget within small groups (“Mantissa over Exponents,” §3.3.2). NVIDIA’s own later choice for Nemotron-H-56B — the simplest Transformer Engine recipe, per-tensor current scaling with the conservative E4M3/E5M2 split and the first/last four GEMMs held in BF16 — shows “the FP8 recipe” is not yet a converged industry answer (Ch10).

The accumulation fix. DeepSeek found H800’s tensor-core FP8 accumulation empirically retains only about 14 mantissa bits, not full FP32, producing up to ~2% relative error on a K=4096 GEMM without correction. The fix promotes partial WGMMA results to FP32 CUDA-core registers every NC=128 accumulated elements (4 WGMMAs) — “the minimal accumulation interval that can significantly improve precision without introducing substantial overhead” — and because H800 can run two WGMMAs concurrently, the promotion of one overlaps the MMA of the other, hiding the extra CUDA-core work (§3.3.2). Colfax Research’s independent analysis confirms the same ~14-bit accumulation limit on Hopper tensor cores, notes the CUDA-core promotion pattern is now standard in CUTLASS ≥3.2, and flags that DeepSeek found activation-only blockwise scaling (without matching weight-block scaling) insufficient — with documented instability in the V3 paper’s Appendix B.2.

Two sensitive spots stay off the FP8 path even in the forward-cached direction. Inputs to the post-attention Linear are stored in a custom E5M6 format with power-of-2 scale factors, re-tiled from 1×128 to 128×1 for backward; SwiGLU-in-MoE inputs are recomputed rather than cached. Dispatch-direction MoE activations are FP8 with power-of-2 scales; the reverse combine direction stays BF16 to protect accuracy (§3.3.3). The recipe was validated at DeepSeek-V2-Lite/V2 scale (~1T tokens) at <0.25% relative loss error versus BF16 before being trusted at full V3 scale (§3.3). It has since become production-standard well beyond DeepSeek: NVIDIA’s Transformer Engine ships it as --fp8-recipe blockwise in Megatron-Core, citing validation “at DeepSeek-V3 class” scale plus MiniMax-M2 and Ant Ling-2.0, and recommends it as the standard for Hopper-generation training, with MXFP8 for Blackwell (TE docs, “FP8 Blockwise Scaling”, 2026). Nebius independently reproduced DeepSeek-V3-671B (and a 16B MoE variant) on a 256×B200 TorchTitan cluster using native MXFP8 rather than the original blockwise scheme, claiming ~41% faster tokens/sec versus BF16 combined with DeepEP (ml-cookbook, 2026) — rare independent confirmation that the precision philosophy transfers across a hardware generation even where the exact format changes. And one counter-example keeps the claim honest: Kimi K2, a comparably-sized MoE trained months after V3, explicitly declined FP8 for pretraining compute after a preliminary study suggested degradation risk, casting to blockwise FP8 only post-hoc for inference weights (Kimi-K2 HF discussion, referencing the K2 report, July 2025). “DeepSeek’s FP8 recipe works” is not the same claim as “FP8 pretraining is a solved default.”

MLA, MTP, and aux-loss-free balancing: the mechanisms behind the headline architecture

MLA’s usual framing is parameter efficiency; the hardware-reflections paper’s framing is less common and matters more for a systems reader. LLM decode is GEMV-bound, not GEMM-bound, because KV-cache reads dominate — “the computation shifts from GEMM to GEMV, which has a much lower compute-to-memory ratio” — and MLA’s low-rank joint KV compression cuts per-token cache footprint from 516 KB (LLaMA-3.1-style MHA) to 70.272 KB for V3, a 7.28× reduction. MLA is primarily a memory-bandwidth optimization. Su Jianlin’s controlled ablations at Moonshot sharpen this further (Transformer升级之路 #20, May 2025; #21, July 2025): matching MHA’s loss at 1/7th the KV cache decomposes into three separable effects. Larger effective head dimension (128+64 → effective ~192) is the single biggest lever — bigger than increasing head count or MLP width at matched parameters. Partial RoPE helps independently. KV-sharing helps but is the smallest and least-validated-at-scale of the three. His theoretical argument: under NoPE, any of MHA/GQA/MQA is expressible as some head-dims-K, KV-shared MQA via matrix absorption, so for a fixed KV-cache budget the best decode-time model is head-dims-equals-cache-size KV-shared MQA, while for fixed compute the best model is head-dims-K unshared MHA. MLA is precisely the LoRA-style bridge between those two optima — behaving like each depending on whether you’re compute-bound (train/prefill) or memory-bound (decode), with the same weights. If you are not copying MLA verbatim, the free variables that matter are head dimension and Partial RoPE fraction, not the MLA formulation per se.

Multi-Token Prediction in V3 runs at depth D=1 (one extra token predicted beyond next-token), implemented as a complete causal chain — sequential MTP modules each combining the prior depth’s hidden state with the next token’s embedding via a projection matrix, unlike Gloeckle et al.’s parallel-independent-head design. The loss weight λ follows an explicit, rarely-reproduced schedule: 0.3 for the first 10T tokens, 0.1 for the remaining 4.8T (§2.2, §3.2.3). Embedding and output head are physically shared with the main model by co-locating the shallowest and deepest layers on the same PP rank under DualPipe, saving both parameter and gradient memory (§3.2.3). At inference — not training, but the number that retroactively justifies the training-time investment — second-token acceptance runs 80-90%, yielding 1.8× TPS via speculative-decode-style use of the MTP head (Insights, May 2025).

Aux-loss-free load balancing uses sigmoid affinity scores with a per-expert bias term b_i added only to the top-K routing decision, never to the gating value itself. Bias update speed γ is 0.001 for the first 14.3T tokens, frozen at 0.0 for the final 500B; a small complementary sequence-wise balance loss (α=0.0001) supplements it, deliberately tiny per the paper’s own note that “too large an auxiliary loss will impair model performance” (§2.1.2, §4.2). Result: zero token drops across all of V3 training. Su Jianlin’s MoE环游记 series re-derives the update rule from an Aux-Loss-plus-straight-through-estimator starting point, showing it is not actually loss-free in spirit — it is the same objective with gradient flow surgically routed to touch only the bias parameter — and flags a causality requirement the paper doesn’t spell out: model parameters must update on LM loss first, then b updates on the resulting batch statistics, since updating b first risks future-information leakage (MoE环游记：3, March 2025). His successor method, Quantile Balancing, reframes balanced top-k routing as a linear program and claims to beat Loss-Free by dropping BIP’s inequality-constraint clipping — “can only rob the rich, not save the poor” (MoE环游记：6, Feb 2026). This is one of several places where the Chinese technical blogosphere runs ahead of, not just behind, the papers it comments on.

The remaining memory-saving techniques that let V3 dispense with TP: recomputing RMSNorm and MLA up-projections in the backward pass rather than persisting their activations, a CPU-resident EMA of parameters updated asynchronously at no extra GPU cost, and the shared embedding/output-head placement noted above (§3.2.3).

Training cost, and the MFU-denominator trap

(V3 report Table 1.) At $2/GPU-hour rental this is DeepSeek’s headline $5.576M figure (§1) — a rental-market spot-price number for the training run only, excluding R&D salaries, failed/ablation runs, and CapEx. SemiAnalysis’s January 2025 rebuttal citing ~$1.3B total server CapEx is not actually in conflict: that figure is total CapEx across DeepSeek’s full H800/H100/H20 fleet, not a V3-run-specific number, and the two answer different questions (via secondary reporting; the original is paywalled).

The MFU calculation is worth reconstructing explicitly, because the “DeepSeek trains at ~40% MFU” claim circulating publicly is, on the numbers in DeepSeek’s own companion paper, misleading by a factor of two. Take the paper’s 250 GFLOPs/token training-compute figure (Insights, Table 2) × 14.8T tokens = 3.7×10²⁴ total pretraining FLOPs. Divide by 2.664M GPU-hours × 3600 s/hr = 9.59×10⁹ GPU-seconds: 385.8 TFLOPS/GPU average achieved. H800’s dense (no-sparsity) peaks are 1979 TFLOPS FP8 and 989.5 TFLOPS BF16 — H800 has identical compute to H100; only NVLink bandwidth is cut for export control. So: FP8-normalized MFU ≈ 19.5%, BF16-normalized MFU ≈ 39.0% (our own arithmetic on the paper’s numbers, July 2026). A third-party recompute using profiler-measured forward FLOPs (×3 for fwd+bwd+update) instead of the 250 GFLOPs/token approximation lands at 424.3 TFLOPS/GPU achieved — 21.4% FP8-MFU / 42.9% BF16-normalized — closely bracketing the same range (dlrover/ATorch team on Medium, “What is the MFU for DeepSeek-V3 training,” undated ~2025; methodology described in the post). Because H800/H100 FP8 dense peak is exactly 2× the BF16 dense peak, any FP8-trained model’s MFU swings by 2× depending on which peak you normalize against — and public claims almost always use the BF16 peak despite the GEMMs actually executing in FP8. Check every FP8-MFU number in circulation (and in Ch18, Ch23) against this denominator trap before taking it at face value.

Fire-Flyer AI-HPC: the cluster DeepSeek built to afford this

Before V3, DeepSeek operated Fire-Flyer 2, a roughly 10,000-GPU cluster whose central architectural bet was that PCIe A100s beat SXM DGX-A100 nodes on cost-performance for LLM-scale workloads: node cost at 60% of DGX-A100, TF32/FP16 GEMM at 83% of DGX-A100’s per-GPU throughput, power at 40% less — a cost-performance ratio of 1.38 versus 1.0 for the DGX design (Fire-Flyer paper, Aug 2024). A two-layer fat-tree with 800-port zones ×2, integrating compute and storage traffic onto the same fabric, cut switch count to 122 versus 1,320 for an equivalent 3-layer DGX-style topology, and network CapEx to $350 relative units versus $4,000 (§Network Topology). The centerpiece is HFReduce, a custom allreduce doing intra-node CPU-side reduction (GDRCopy async D2H/H2D plus SIMD reduction across FP32/FP16/BF16/FP8) before an inter-node RDMA double-binary-tree allreduce — entirely bypassing NCCL’s GPU-kernel reduction path and its PCIe P2P limitations. Measured: 6.3-8.1 GB/s inter-node bandwidth versus NCCL’s 1.6-4.8 GB/s under identical conditions, against a theoretical ceiling of ~13.3 GB/s bottlenecked by EPYC Rome’s shared PCIe host-bridge bandwidth (§HFReduce). HaiScale, HAI-LLM’s predecessor, hit 88% parallel scalability from 32→512 GPUs on VGG16 with the HFReduce backend (~50% faster than PyTorch-NCCL DDP), 91% efficiency on LLaMA-13B, and 76.14%/92.92% on DeepSeekMoE-16B at 640/320 GPUs (§HaiScale).

Two more Fire-Flyer data points earn a place in any 100k-GPU reliability plan (Ch21). First, a one-year hardware-failure taxonomy — NVLink errors (Xid74) at 42.57% of all GPU failures, software/memory errors (Xid13/31/43/45) at 33.48%, ECC memory errors at ~2%, IB link failures at 30% of the non-Xid74 remainder (§Fault Tolerance) — a rare public large-cluster reliability dataset with Xid-code granularity that essentially no other lab has published. Second, the original 3FS deployment (180 nodes, 2,880 SSDs, 20+ PiB mirrored) sustained 9 TB/s outbound IB capacity and 8 TB/s achieved read, checkpointing every 5 minutes via a batch API (§3FS). The paper’s stated 2024 roadmap — 1:1 GPU:NIC ratio, multi-plane fat-trees, 400Gbps RoCE, 32,768 GPUs — is DeepSeek pre-announcing the design direction that shows up a year later as the Multi-Plane Fat-Tree in the hardware-reflections paper.

V3.2-Exp and DSA: sparse attention as a cheap retrofit

DeepSeek Sparse Attention, shipped in V3.2-Exp, pairs a lightning indexer with a top-k selector inside MLA’s MQA mode (V3.2 report, Dec 2025). The indexer score is I_{t,s} = Σ_j w^I_{t,j}·ReLU(q^I_{t,j}·k^I_s), computed in FP8 with H^I indexer heads and learnable per-head weights — O(L²) but cheap per pair (a ReLU-plus-dot-product versus full attention math) — feeding a top-k=2048 selection into the real MLA attention, which then runs O(Lk). The continued-pretraining curriculum from V3.1-Terminus is deliberately tiny: 1,000 dense-warmup steps on 2.1B tokens (indexer-only KL-divergence loss aligning the indexer to the main attention distribution), then 15,000 sparse-training steps on 943.7B tokens (LM loss on the main model, indexer gradients detached). Roughly 946B tokens total — about 6.4% of V3’s original 14.8T-token budget. Kernel support shipped same-day: FlashMLA PR #98 for sparse attention, DeepGEMM PR #200 for indexer MQA-logit scoring, and a TileLang reference implementation (FlashMLA, DeepGEMM, Sep 2025).

FlashMLA’s own FP8 sparse-decode deep-dive found the kernel is dequantization-bound, not MMA-bound: MMA for 64 query heads costs ~34 cycles/token versus ~50 cycles/token for FP8→BF16 dequantization of the KV cache. The fix pairs CTAs in clusters of two sharing dequantized K/V via Distributed Shared Memory, so each CTA dequantizes only half the cache — reaching 410 TFLOPS at batch=128/topk=2048 on H800 SXM5, up to 460 TFLOPS at topk=32768, against a dense BF16 decode baseline of 640 TFLOPS. Sparse overtakes dense past a sequence length of roughly 3,000 tokens (FlashMLA deep-dive docs, Sep 2025). The per-token FP8 KV-cache byte layout: 512 bytes quantized “NoPE” content (e4m3) + 16 bytes scale factors + 128 bytes unquantized RoPE part (bf16) = 656 bytes/token. DSA is a case study in how cheaply an architectural retrofit can land relative to pretraining from scratch — worth weighing against building sparse attention in from step zero for a 10T/200B design (Ch7, Ch11).

V4: the deltas that matter for infra

V4 (preview April 24, 2026; full report April 27, 2026) ships two variants — V4-Pro (1.6T total / 49B active, 33T tokens) and V4-Flash (284B total / 13B active, 32T tokens), both natively 1M-token context (V4 report). Four deltas are load-bearing for a systems reader; three others are worth flagging as design signal.

Attention: hybrid CSA+HCA replaces MLA+DSA-only. Compressed Sparse Attention compresses every m tokens into one entry (softmax-weighted over 2m overlapping source tokens), then applies DSA-style lightning-indexer top-k selection over the compressed entries, using shared-KV MQA and grouped output projection to cut projection cost. Heavily Compressed Attention uses the same compression machinery at a much larger ratio with no sparse selection at all, applying dense attention directly over the heavily-compressed cache. Layers interleave CSA and HCA, plus a sliding-window branch, partial RoPE on only the last 64 dims, Q/K RMSNorm pre-attention (replacing V3’s need for QK-clip), and a learnable attention-sink logit. At 1M context, V4-Pro uses 27% of V3.2’s per-token inference FLOPs and 10% of its KV cache; V4-Flash uses 10% FLOPs / 7% KV cache (§Hybrid Attention).

Optimizer: Muon replaces AdamW for most weights. Momentum accumulation followed by hybrid Newton-Schulz orthogonalization — 8 fast iterations at coefficients (3.4445, −4.7750, 2.0315) plus 2 stabilizing iterations at (2, −1.5, 0.5) — with RMS-rescaled updates, Nesterov momentum, and weight decay applied directly to Muon params. AdamW is retained only for embeddings, prediction heads, mHC’s static biases/gates, and RMSNorm weights. MoE gradient sync for the all-to-all is quantized to BF16 with stochastic rounding, halving communication, with a two-phase all-to-all plus local FP32 summation replacing tree reduce-scatter for robustness. No QK-Clip is needed because Q/K RMSNorm handles logit blowup directly (§Muon). This continues the public Muon lineage Su Jianlin has built at Moonshot since late 2024 (Ch9); DeepSeek adopting it for V4 is one data point in a broader pattern of Chinese frontier labs converging on spectral-norm-flavored optimizers over AdamW.

mHC: residual mixing constrained to the Birkhoff polytope. Manifold-Constrained Hyper-Connections generalizes the residual stream with three dynamically-generated linear maps (A_l, B_l, C_l) per layer; the residual-mixing matrix B_l is projected onto the doubly-stochastic-matrix manifold via 20 Sinkhorn-Knopp iterations, guaranteeing spectral norm ≤1 and stabilizing backprop through very deep stacks, at a measured 6.7% wall-time overhead on an already-overlapped 1F1B stage via fused kernels and selective checkpointing (§mHC). Su Jianlin’s March 2026 posts frame this as a design space DeepSeek got to first and validated at scale — Moonshot’s internal rival, Attention Residuals (AttnRes), replaces residual streams with cross-layer attention entirely, reporting ~5% extra compute for ~25% of the full benefit in its “Block” variant, not yet shipped as of the source date (Attention Residuals 回忆录, March 2026). Two frontier Chinese labs racing the same architectural problem from different angles, in public.

EP overlap goes wave-based. V3’s DualPipe targets full-pipeline-level comm hiding; V4 instead splits Dispatch→Linear1→Linear2→Combine per MoE layer into waves, where each wave’s compute starts only once all that wave’s expert comms finish — targeting comm-time < compute-time within a single layer rather than across the whole pipeline. The compute/comm boundedness threshold for V4-Pro is stated explicitly: 6hd FLOPs per token-expert pair against 3h bytes of communication, requiring compute/byte ≤ 2d = 6144 FLOPs/byte to stay comm-bound-safe. Claimed speedups are 1.50-1.73× for general inference, up to 1.96× for RL rollouts, shipped as the open-sourced “Mega MoE” fused kernel in DeepGEMM (fusing dispatch+L1+SwiGLU+L2+combine via symmetric memory, requiring PyTorch ≥2.9) (V4 report + DeepGEMM PR #304/#316, April 2026). The paper’s own language leans toward this being primarily a post-training/RL-serving optimization rather than core pretraining infra, though it does not state that definitively either way.

Three more V4 deltas, a sentence each. Precision defaults to BF16 for training, with the KV cache mixing BF16 (RoPE dims) and FP8 (rest), the indexer running FP4 at extreme context lengths, and routed-expert weights using FP4 — framed explicitly as “equivalent to FP8×FP8 on current hardware, theoretically 1/3 more efficient on future hardware,” a forward-compatibility bet rather than a speed win today, with FP4 QAT applied post-training (§Precision). The TileLang kernel/determinism infrastructure moves host-side contract checks into codegen (per-kernel overhead to <1μs), adds Z3 SMT-solver integer-range analysis, and disables fast-math by default — bitwise-reproducible training runs by default, a deliberate departure from the speed-over-reproducibility norm, with dedicated deterministic kernels for attention backward, MoE backward, and mHC matmul (§Training Infra). And Su Jianlin separately reports V4 replaces V3’s “first_k_dense” strategy with “first_k_hash” — early MoE layers routed by a fixed, precomputed token-id-to-expert-id lookup table rather than the learned router. That detail is undocumented in the V4 report itself, reverse-engineered by Su as a plausible greedy frequency-based construction, and corroborated externally by a Hugging Face discussion referencing an all-zeros tid2eid tensor in a leaked V4-Flash checkpoint (DeepSeek V4的tid2eid是怎么来的？, May 2026). It is the strongest evidence yet that at extreme expert counts, structurally-guaranteed deterministic balance for early layers is displacing learned routing, not just supplementing it.

V4’s training hardware is genuinely unresolved, and worth not papering over. Reporting ranges from all-Huawei-Ascend-950 claims to an alleged H800+Ascend-910C mix to a U.S. government allegation of smuggled Blackwell chips, timed suspiciously close to launch. NIST’s CAISI evaluation of V4-Pro (May 2026) discloses no training-hardware information — only that CAISI itself served the model from cloud H200/B200 for evaluation — and the official V4 model card is a data-sourcing/licensing document, not a hardware disclosure (CAISI evaluation, May 2026; model card at fe-static.deepseek.com, April 2026). Treat any specific-chip claim about V4’s training hardware as rumor.

The Open Infra Index: production kernels, not reference code

The repo family is worth treating as a shared vocabulary rather than a set of one-off tools, because each piece has been revised at least once against real production feedback.

DeepGEMM is JIT-compiled (NVRTC-optional, up to 10× faster compile) and CUTLASS/CuTe-inspired but template-light, hitting 1550 TFLOPS on H800 (April 2025). SM90/SM100 support landed in a July 2025 refactor; SM120 (RTX 5090/Pro 6000) is explicitly unsupported as of mid-2026, with an official warning against enabling it. Scaling-factor format differs by architecture — SM90 wants FP32 scales, SM100 wants packed UE8M0 (four packed per torch.int), a direct consequence of Blackwell’s native microscaling tensor-core support — and the grouped-GEMM API groups only the M-axis (N, K fixed), deliberately tailored to same-shaped MoE experts rather than general grouped GEMM (DeepGEMM).

EPLB ships two placement policies: hierarchical (pack expert groups to nodes evenly, replicate within-node, then pack to GPUs — used for prefill with smaller EP) and global (replicate regardless of groups, pack directly to GPUs — used for decode with larger EP). It is the open-sourced version of the redundant-experts strategy from V3’s inference-deployment section: 32 redundant experts for prefill, one extra per GPU beyond its native 8; decode treats the shared expert as a 9th always-selected routed expert (EPLB; V3 report §3.4).

DeepEP went through a full v1→v2 rewrite: the NVSHMEM backend was replaced by a lighter NCCL “Gin” backend (header-only, reuses existing NCCL communicators), separate high-throughput/low-latency APIs were unified into a single ElasticBuffer interface, analytical SM/QP-count calculation replaced v1’s auto-tuning, and the EP domain scaled to EP2048. SM usage for “V3-like legacy training” dropped from 24 SMs to 4-6 in v2. On SM100 (Blackwell) with pure NVLink, it hits 726 GB/s dispatch / 740 GB/s combine at 64 SMs, or 643/675 GB/s at the minimum 24-SM configuration (DeepEP README, 2026). The fork ecosystem is more active than commonly assumed: uccl-ep adds heterogeneous NVIDIA/AMD and EFA/Broadcom/CX7 NIC support, Mori-EP is an AMD ROCm backend, AntGroup’s “Normal-SMFree” branch removes SMs from the RDMA path entirely (a stronger claim than DeepSeek’s own v2 reduction), and Hybrid-EP uses TMA instructions for even lower SM usage plus NVFP4 support — real infra convergence across the Chinese frontier-adjacent ecosystem contributing back into the repo, not just consuming it. Read alongside ByteDance’s independently-engineered COMET/Flux/Triton-distributed overlap stack and MegaScale-MoE’s SP-for-attention/EP-for-FFN split — a materially different philosophy (thread-block-level fusion versus DeepEP’s dedicated dispatch/combine kernels) achieving comparable ends — DeepEP is clearly not the only viable answer to this problem.

FlashMLA’s performance progression moved from an early-2025 kernel to an April 2025 rewrite hitting 660 TFLOPS dense decode (5-15% gain, H800 SXM5) via a “seesaw” schedule distinct from FlashAttention-3’s ping-pong. It splits the output matrix into two 64×256 halves (32,768 of 65,536 total registers needed per output matrix) and alternates two warpgroups on different KV blocks with a single shared running max — ~2% slower than ping-pong in memory-bound regimes, a deliberate trade for the compute-bound-dominant real workload. Dense decode reaches up to 3000 GB/s memory-bound or 660 TFLOPS compute-bound on H800 SXM5; NVIDIA-reported dense MHA prefill on SM100/B200 reaches 1460 TFLOPS forward / 1000 TFLOPS backward; sparse MLA prefill reaches 640 TFLOPS (H800) / 1450 TFLOPS (B200, CUDA 12.9) (FlashMLA + deep-dive docs).

3FS (Fire-Flyer File System) sustains 6.6 TiB/s aggregate read on a 180-node cluster (16×14TiB NVMe + 2×200Gbps IB per node) even with background training traffic running concurrently — a number that has become the industry reference point for AI-workload parallel filesystems. It also completed a GraySort of 110.5 TiB in 30m14s (3.66 TiB/min) on 25 storage + 50 compute nodes via smallpond, a DuckDB-on-3FS data-processing framework, with KVCache-over-3FS peaking at 40 GiB/s across clients (3FS README, Feb 2025). Ch15 covers dataloading requirements in full; 3FS is the concrete existence proof that a single filesystem can serve bulk training I/O and background checkpoint/data traffic at this throughput without a dedicated separate fabric.

Scaling-law lineage

DeepSeek’s own hyperparameter scaling law — η_opt = 0.3118·C^(-0.1250), B_opt = 0.2920·C^(0.3271) — predates V3 by nearly a year (DeepSeek LLM, Jan 2024), and the batch-size ramp and LR schedule in V3’s hyperparameter table above are visibly downstream of it. Ch8 covers the broader scaling-law landscape (muP, Step Law, Farseer, WSD laws) in depth. The point worth flagging here: DeepSeek’s practice of publishing the compute-optimal LR/batch-size fit alongside the model that used it is itself unusual — most labs disclose the resulting hyperparameters without the law that produced them, which is part of why this stack functions as a reference rather than just a data point.

Implications for the 10T/200B run

What borrows close to verbatim: the FP8 blockwise recipe (1×128/128×128, uniform E4M3, NC=128 FP32 promotion) is now productionized in Megatron-Core’s Transformer Engine and validated at DeepSeek-V3 scale plus at least two other frontier-adjacent MoE labs — there is little reason to reinvent it for Hopper-class hardware, and Ch10 extends its logic onto Blackwell’s native MXFP8/NVFP4 paths rather than replacing it. The aux-loss-free balancing mechanism, with its Loss-Free/Quantile-Balancing lineage as refined in the open Chinese blogosphere, is similarly close to a solved problem worth adopting rather than re-deriving — budgeting Su’s causality requirement (update model params on LM loss before updating the bias) as a correctness constraint, not an optimization. EPLB’s hierarchical/global placement split and the 3FS throughput target (6.6 TiB/s-class aggregate read under live training load) are directly reusable specs for Ch15 and Ch21.

What needs redesign, not adoption: DualPipe’s 2× parameter-memory cost is a bet that only pays off at DeepSeek’s specific EP-heavy, TP-free 2,048-GPU configuration. A 100k-GPU/200B-active build sits at a materially different point in the parallelism space (Ch18) and needs to re-derive whether the same bubble/memory tradeoff holds — or whether TP re-enters once EP degree, node count, and NVLink domain size (NVL72 versus H800’s 8-GPU domain) all change simultaneously. The 20-SM comms budget and its 3.2:1 NVLink:IB asymmetry are H800-specific artifacts of export-control-driven NVLink throttling; GB200 NVL72’s 900 GB/s-class unidirectional NVLink changes the ratio enough that the entire warp-specialization design — and the “13 experts at zero extra cost” slack calculation — needs rerunning from scratch, not porting (Ch18, Ch20). And V4’s wave-based EP overlap, Muon adoption, and mHC are recent enough (April 2026) that none has the multi-generation validation history the V3 stack has. They are the most promising near-term upgrades to evaluate — not yet default choices to inherit.
