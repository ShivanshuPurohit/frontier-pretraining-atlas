Chapter 05 · The MoE layer is the run

MoE systems engineering: expert parallelism, all-to-all, and the fused mega-kernel

At a 10T-total / 200B-active scale spread across 100,000 GB200 GPUs, attention is a fixed cost — the MoE FFN is where the run lives or dies. This is the systems field manual for that layer: the all-to-all substrate, the overlap schedule, the routing and balancing machinery, and the grouped-GEMM kernel zoo.

The MoE layer is where a 10T-total/200B-active run lives or dies. Attention is a fixed, well-understood cost; the FFN, sharded into experts and scattered across 100,000 GB200 GPUs, is where you pay the all-to-all tax, where load imbalance silently wastes a third of your FLOPs, and where the difference between a naive dense-dispatch kernel and a fused symmetric-memory mega-kernel is a 1.5–1.96× throughput swing on the critical path (DeepSeek-V4). This chapter is the systems field manual for that layer: the communication substrate (DeepEP and its V2/hybrid-ep rewrites), the compute/comm overlap schedule (DualPipe and its successor the fused MegaMoE kernel), the routing and balancing machinery (node-limited gating, auxiliary-loss-free bias, EPLB/LPLB), the grouped-GEMM kernel zoo (MegaBlocks dropless, CUTLASS, Triton, DeepGEMM, Tokamax ragged_dot), and the dropless-vs-capacity tradeoff — grounded throughout in the user's actual codebase (MaxText src/maxtext/layers/moe.py), which implements every one of these primitives in JAX/Pallas and is the most legible open reference for the JAX/XLA path.

On a flat NVL72 domain with DeepEP V2 and MegaMoE the overlap acrobatics shrink — but the architectural bets (sparser routing, node-limiting, dropless ragged buffers, FP8/FP4 dispatch, bias balancing) transfer directly and are the load-bearing decisions for your run.

The governing arithmetic, established up front so the rest of the chapter can refer back to it: for a DeepSeek-V3-shaped config (256 routed experts, top-8, d_model≈7168, expert intermediate ≈2048), dispatch moves top_k · tokens activation rows of width d_model to the experts and combine brings them back. Per token per MoE layer that is 2 · top_k · d_model · bytes of bidirectional all-to-all traffic — roughly 115 KB/token/layer in BF16, ~86 KB with FP8 dispatch + BF16 combine (DeepEP). The expert GEMM does 6 · h · d FLOPs per token-expert against 3 · h bytes of weight traffic, so the ratio you must beat to hide communication under compute is ≈6144 FLOPs/Byte (DeepSeek-V4). Everything below — node-limiting, FP8 dispatch, kernel fusion, overlap scheduling — is in service of staying above that line at EP spanning hundreds of NVL72 domains.

01Expert parallelism and the all-to-all volume problem

At your scale, EP is not optional and it is not small. A 256-expert layer with experts large enough to matter cannot be replicated; it must be sharded, and the shard count (the EP degree) climbs into the hundreds. The MaxText mesh exposes expert as a first-class parallelism axis alongside fsdp, tensor, sequence, pipeline, and context (in utils/sharding.py and configs/base.yml's ici_*/dcn_* axis lists), and the logical-axis rules route expert-sharded tensors onto it. The decisive systems fact is that EP turns the FFN into an all-to-all-bound operation, and the all-to-all volume is fixed by the model (2·top_k·d_model·bytes/token/layer) while the bandwidth available per token degrades as EP grows, because each token's top_k experts land on more distinct devices and more distinct NVLink domains.

DeepEP's own measurements make this concrete and they are the load-bearing numbers for capacity planning. The V1 low-latency (pure-RDMA, IBGDA) path does dispatch at 77µs / 98 GB/s at 8 experts, degrading monotonically to 194µs / 39 GB/s at 256 experts; combine runs 1.5–1.8× the dispatch latency (DeepEP legacy). The internode "normal" (high-throughput) RDMA path peaks near 58 GB/s at EP32 and sags to ~50 GB/s at EP64. The lesson is brutal and counterintuitive: per-token RDMA bandwidth falls as expert count rises, and the low-latency floor of 39 GB/s is well under a single CX7's ~50 GB/s line rate once you exceed ~128 experts. You do not get to assume your NICs run at line rate; the EP fan-out structurally prevents it.

This is the entire motivation for node-limited (group-limited) routing, covered in §04, and for the asymmetric-domain forwarding that DeepEP's high-throughput kernels implement: NVLink→RDMA→NVLink, where intra-NVL72 traffic rides the 130 TB/s flat NVLink fabric and only the genuinely cross-domain fraction of top_k touches the NIC. At EP=N spanning many NVL72 domains, per-GPU RDMA demand scales with the internode share of top_k, not all of it (DeepEP). On GB200 NVL72 you get a 72-GPU flat NVLink domain, which is the single biggest architectural lever you have: keep an expert group inside one NVL72 and the RDMA leg carries only cross-group traffic.

The one lever to remember

The GB200 NVL72 72-GPU flat NVLink domain is your biggest architectural lever. Keep an expert group inside one NVL72 and the RDMA leg carries only cross-group traffic — everything else in this chapter is downstream of that decision.

MaxText's EP all-to-all, in code

MaxText implements EP dispatch/combine not with a dense one-hot all-to-all but with jax.lax.ragged_all_to_all, which moves only the valid (variable-length) per-expert token runs. The flow in sparse_matmul is: get_topk → permute (argsort by flattened selected experts, then _sort_activations with a custom-VJP gather) → get_all_to_all_params (computes input_offsets/send_sizes/output_offsets/recv_sizes from per-shard group sizes via cumsum for the batch-sharded case) → ragged_all_to_all into a sized buffer → local_permute to regroup by local expert → grouped GEMM → ragged_all_to_all back → unpermute. The permute/unpermute/local_permute trio plus get_all_to_all_params is the EP communication layer, and reading it is the fastest way to internalize what dispatch/combine actually does at the tensor level. The custom-VJP sort (use_custom_sort_vjp=true, default) matters for throughput: its backward is itself a sort by argsort(sort_indices), avoiding a materialized scatter.

    recv_sizes   = base[:, shard_id]                      # rows we receive

    output_offsets = jnp.concatenate([z, jnp.cumsum(recv_sizes)[:-1]])  # [2]
    return input_offsets, send_sizes, output_offsets, recv_sizes

1Offsets, not one-hot. The metadata is computed from per-shard group sizes — no dense BSEC dispatch mask is ever materialized.

2Cumsum bookkeeping. input_offsets/output_offsets are exclusive cumsums so each expert's variable-length run lands contiguously in the receive buffer.

3Custom-VJP sort. permute argsorts by flattened expert id; its backward is a sort by argsort(sort_idx), avoiding a materialized scatter.

4Ragged, not dense. ragged_all_to_all moves only valid per-expert runs into a sized buffer — the volume the §05 ragged-buffer math bounds.

5Symmetric return. Combine is the same primitive reversed; unpermute scatters expert outputs back and applies the un-biased gating weights.

For the EP-in-the-hundreds regime MaxText adds ring-of-experts (use_ring_of_experts) with custom Pallas ragged-sort kernels (kernels/ragged/ragged_sort: a2a_ragged_sort, ring_ragged_sort, ring_ragged_unsort, gated by use_ragged_sort). The point of these is precise: a naive sort over the padded ragged buffer is pure waste when the buffer is sized for worst-case imbalance and the true occupancy is a small prefix. The ragged-sort kernels gather only the valid prefix, which is exactly the optimization that pays off when EP is large and the worst-case buffer dwarfs the real token count. The ring schedule additionally overlaps the all-to-all with the expert GEMM by streaming experts in a ring rather than doing one monolithic dispatch — the JAX/Pallas analog of DeepEP's overlapped channels.

02DeepEP V1 → V2 → hybrid-ep: the substrate to target on GB200

DeepEP V1 split into two kernel families: normal/high-throughput (NVLink intranode + RDMA internode, NVSHMEM-based, asymmetric-domain forwarding aligned to V3's group-limited gating, measured 153/158 GB/s dispatch/combine over NVLink at EP8) and low-latency (pure RDMA via IBGDA, hook-based comm/compute overlap consuming zero SMs). For a GB200 build neither V1 number is what you ship against — DeepEP V2 is.

The V2 rewrite (2025–2026) is the more important development, and three changes matter for the 10T run:

The branch to actually track for GB200 is hybrid-ep, NVIDIA-authored, which re-architects the NVLink+RDMA path using TMA copies and warp-level pipelining to minimize SM occupancy while saturating both NVLink and RDMA, with experimental NIXL/UCX transport integration (hybrid-ep). Caveat to verify before committing verify

As of the surveyed docs, hybrid-ep's stated GPU support still lists SM80/SM90 — confirm SM100 status before you build against it, because the whole point is Blackwell.

Two operational landmines. First, DeepEP issue #427: low-latency dispatch can silently corrupt under heavily imbalanced expert load (#427). This is not a tail risk for you — it is exactly the regime in the first few thousand steps, before your auxiliary-loss-free bias balancer has converged, when expert load is maximally skewed. If you run low-latency dispatch from step 0 you can corrupt activations precisely when the model is most fragile.

Step-0 hazard — #427 imbalance corruption

Mitigation: use the high-throughput path early, or warm up the bias balancer aggressively, or pin a correctness check on dispatch output during the first N steps. Second landmine — the 39 GB/s low-latency RDMA floor at ≥128 experts is a hard ceiling on decode-style latency-bound dispatch; design your serving EP topology to keep the internode fan-out small enough that you live on the high-throughput curve, not the low-latency floor.

03DualPipe and the move to fused mega-kernels

V3's compute/comm overlap rests on DualPipe, which decomposes each forward and backward chunk into four phases — attention, all-to-all dispatch, MLP, all-to-all combine — with backward further split into backward-for-input and backward-for-weights, so a hand-tuned schedule overlaps one micro-batch's all-to-all with another's GEMMs. The bubble count is (PP/2−1)(F&B + B − 3W) versus 1F1B's (PP−1)(F+B), a meaningful reduction. The cost is two resident copies of the parameters (the bidirectional schedule runs from both ends), which V3 argues is cheap precisely because EP already shards the experts, so the duplicated tensors are the comparatively small dense/attention params. The communication side reserves 20 SMs (10 warp-specialized channels, custom PTX, auto-tuned chunk size to limit L2 interference) (DeepSeek-V3).

MaxText's layers/pipeline.py provides the analogous machinery for the JAX path: a circular (looping) pipeline with num_pipeline_microbatches, num_layers_per_pipeline_stage, and pipeline_delay_activation_forwarding to overlap the activation send with the next stage's compute. It is not DualPipe's bidirectional schedule, but it is the same family of bubble-reduction techniques, and combined with the ring-of-experts EP overlap it covers the two axes (PP bubble and EP all-to-all) that DualPipe addresses jointly.

Treat DualPipe as the fallback, not the target

That 20-SM tax is exactly what the field is trying to eliminate, and it is being eliminated from two directions: DeepEP V2/hybrid-ep drops the comm-kernel SM cost to 4–6 (or near-zero with TMA pipelining), and DeepSeek V4 deletes the two-piece overlap entirely by fusing dispatch, GEMM, and combine into one kernel (§06). For a greenfield GB200 build in mid-2026, DualPipe-style manual overlap was a workaround for the H800's throttled interconnect and a non-fused kernel stack. On a flat NVL72 domain with DeepEP V2 the overlap problem is dramatically smaller, and with MegaMoE it largely disappears.

04Routing: node-limited gating and auxiliary-loss-free balancing

Two routing decisions dominate MoE systems behavior: how many distinct nodes a token can touch (which sets your RDMA bill) and how you keep experts balanced without an auxiliary loss that distorts the gradient.

Node-limited (group-limited) routing

Each token is restricted to at most M=4 nodes, selected by summing the top K_r/M expert affinities per node and keeping the M best nodes; experts are only eligible within those nodes (DeepSeek-V3). This directly caps the internode fan-out that drives the RDMA-bandwidth decay of §01. MaxText implements the symmetric construct in expert_group_mask and deepseek_routing: it reshapes the gate logits to (batch·seq, n_routing_groups, experts_per_group), takes a per-group score (sum of top-2 in-group affinities), selects topk_routing_group groups via jax.lax.top_k, one-hots them into a group_mask, and broadcasts that mask back over experts so out-of-group experts are masked out before the final top-k. The config knobs are n_routing_groups and topk_routing_group; setting n_routing_groups == -1 disables grouping.

jax — MaxText deepseek_routing group-mask (illustrative shape flow)
# gate_logits: [batch*seq, n_experts]  ->  group-limit fan-out before top-k
g = gate_logits.reshape(tokens, n_routing_groups, experts_per_group)
group_score = jax.lax.top_k(g, 2)[0].sum(axis=-1)            # sum of top-2 in-group
_, grp_idx = jax.lax.top_k(group_score, topk_routing_group)  # keep M best groups
group_mask = jax.nn.one_hot(grp_idx, n_routing_groups).sum(1)
mask = jnp.repeat(group_mask, experts_per_group, axis=-1)     # broadcast over experts
masked_logits = jnp.where(mask > 0, gate_logits, -jnp.inf)    # out-of-group -> -inf
top_w, top_i = jax.lax.top_k(masked_logits, top_k)           # final routing
Map one routing group to one NVL72 domain

For your 10T build, set the group structure so one routing group maps to one NVL72 domain — then node-limited routing with M chosen to match your domain count keeps the dominant traffic on NVLink and the RDMA leg sees only cross-group tokens.

Auxiliary-loss-free balancing (the bias trick)

Rather than an auxiliary load-balance loss (which fights the language-modeling gradient and degrades quality), V3/V4 add a per-expert bias b_i to the affinity scores for top-K selection only — the gating weight still comes from the original, un-biased affinity — and nudge b_i by ±γ per step based on whether expert i is over- or under-loaded (DeepSeek-V3). V3's schedule was γ=0.001 for the first 14.3T tokens, then γ=0.0 for the final 500B to let the distribution settle. This is the correct default for you: it decouples balancing from the loss landscape and costs nothing but a per-expert counter.

MaxText implements exactly this. calculate_load_balance_updates(top_k_indices, num_experts, rate) computes the per-expert over/under-load and the bias delta; should_update_load_balance gates whether it fires (it requires routed_bias and routed_bias_update_rate > 0.0 and not is_hash_routing); the bias is applied to pre_bias_logits only (preserving the un-biased gating weight) for the deepseek3/deepseek4 model names. The update rate is the config field routed_bias_update_rate (default 0.0) — note that the default is off, so you must set it (e.g. 0.001) and supply a decay schedule to get V3's behavior.

src/maxtext/layers/moe.pycalculate_load_balance_updatesaux-loss-free biastrimmed
def calculate_load_balance_updates(top_k_indices, num_experts, rate):  # [1]
    # count tokens routed to each expert this step
    counts = jnp.bincount(top_k_indices.reshape(-1), length=num_experts)
    mean   = counts.mean()
    over   = counts > mean                                   # [2]
    delta  = jnp.where(over, -rate, rate)                    # +/- gamma per expert
    return delta                                             # bias update, NOT a grad

def should_update_load_balance(cfg):                          # [3]
    return (cfg.routed_bias
            and cfg.routed_bias_update_rate > 0.0
            and not cfg.is_hash_routing)

# selection uses biased logits; gating weight uses the un-biased affinity:
sel_logits = pre_bias_logits + routed_bias                   # [4]
top_w, top_i = jax.lax.top_k(jax.nn.sigmoid(pre_bias_logits) , top_k)  # weight un-biased

1A counter, not a loss. The update is computed from routed-token counts each step — there is no gradient term fighting the LM objective.

2±γ nudge. Over-loaded experts get −rate, under-loaded get +rate. V3 used γ=0.001 for 14.3T tokens, then 0 for the final 500B.

3Default is off. routed_bias_update_rate defaults to 0.0 — you must set it and supply a decay schedule yourself.

4Bias on selection only. The bias shifts which experts win the top-k; the gating weight comes from pre_bias_logits, so quality is untouched.

A classic bug — bias is a control signal, not a parameter

Critically, the bias update is applied post-optimizer in the train loop (trainers/pre_train/train.py applies moe_bias_updates via update_state_param after the optimizer step), not folded into the gradient — it is a control signal, not a learned parameter, and conflating the two is a classic bug.

Hash routing for early layers (V4)

V4 uses deterministic hash routing in its earliest blocks: a token's expert is a fixed function of its id via a lookup table, with no gating network and therefore no load-balance loss and no balancing dynamics at all (DeepSeek-V4). MaxText already supports this end to end: is_hash_routing gates the routing in get_topk (a tid2eid lookup table via Tid2EidVar, applied to first_num_hash_layers), and — importantly — it gates the load-balance loss off (load_balance_loss is skipped when is_hash_routing, and should_update_load_balance returns false). The deepseek4-284b.yml config wires in the hash-routing layers. Hash routing's systems appeal is that it is perfectly balanced by construction and deterministic, so dispatch buffers can be sized exactly and there is no imbalance-correctness hazard (the #427 regime cannot occur for hash-routed layers).

EPLB and LPLB: absorbing residual imbalance at deployment

The bias balancer keeps the training-time distribution roughly flat but cannot make it perfectly flat, and at inference you face a fixed deployment with hot experts. EPLB (Expert-Parallel Load Balancer) replicates the hottest experts redundantly and does hierarchical node→intra-node→GPU packing (used when #nodes divides #expert-groups, else global replication). For a 100k-GPU deployment, EPLB-style replication of the hottest experts is essentially mandatory — a single hot expert on one GPU bottlenecks the entire EP group.

The frontier successor is LPLB (Linear-Programming Load Balancer), which formulates per-batch dynamic token redistribution as an LP over a redundant-expert topology and solves it with a single-SM Interior Point Method on cuSolverDx/cuBLASDx in ~100µs/batch, fed by live statistics from DeepEP's buffers (LPLB). EPLB handles static imbalance (the long-run hot-expert distribution); LPLB handles the per-batch imbalance that EPLB's static replication cannot. For your run, ship EPLB redundant replication as the baseline and evaluate LPLB as the online refinement — it consumes the DeepEP buffer stats you already have and adds ~100µs/batch, which is negligible against a multi-millisecond decode step.

A genuine edge underwatched

Almost nobody outside DeepSeek's repo watchers knows LPLB exists; it is a genuine edge — an online LP refinement that piggybacks on the buffer statistics you are already collecting.

05Grouped-GEMM: dropless vs capacity-factor, and the kernel zoo

The expert FFN is a grouped (ragged) GEMM over variable-sized token groups — one matmul per expert, with group sizes that depend on routing. There are two regimes, and the choice between them is one of the highest-leverage decisions in the whole stack.

Capacity-factor (dropping/padding). Fix expert_capacity = ceil(tokens_per_batch / num_experts) · capacity_factor, then dispatch and combine via dense one-hot masks; tokens beyond capacity are dropped, under-capacity experts are zero-padded. MaxText's dense_matmul path implements exactly this — generate_masks builds the dispatch/combine masks and the einsums BSM,BSEC->EBCM … EBCM,BSEC->BSM do the masked routing (the code paths gate on capacity_factor > 0). The pathology, documented by MegaBlocks, is decisive: matching dropless quality with padding required capacity factors as high as 11, and dropless improved validation-loss reduction 1.73× over CF=1 (MegaBlocks). A capacity factor of 11 means you compute 11× the necessary FLOPs to avoid dropping tokens — economically absurd at 100k GPUs.

Dropless block-sparse (MegaBlocks dMoE). Reformulate the block-diagonal expert matmul as a block-sparse operation with 128×128 blocks, encoded in a hybrid blocked-CSR-COO format with transpose indices so the column-iteration (DSD / backward) needs no materialized transpose. Each expert's token batch is padded only to a multiple of 128 (negligible). Speedups: 1.38×–4.35× over Tutel (padding-based) and 1.8–2.4× over dense Megatron-LM (MegaBlocks). This is the dropless quality win without the capacity-factor FLOP blowup, and it is the recommended Hopper path via the megablocks[gg] grouped-GEMM backend.

The recommendation is unambiguous: dropless

At your scale, throwing away tokens degrades quality and wasting FLOPs on padding is unaffordable; the ragged-buffer machinery (§5.1 below) gives you bounded-memory dropless without the dense-mask cost.

5.1 · The MaxText ragged buffer: bounded-memory dropless

The single most useful piece of engineering in MaxText moe.py for your purposes is get_ragged_buffer_size, which encodes the worst-case dropless buffer math exactly. The function:

  • With ragged_buffer_factor > 0 it returns balanced_size · factor — the ragged analog of capacity factor. This can drop tokens if the true distribution exceeds the buffer (the comment is explicit that factor=1.0 is dropless only in the perfectly balanced case).
  • With factor ≤ 0 (the default, ragged_buffer_factor: -1.0) it computes the worst-case dropless size as worst_case_factor = min(ep_degree, global_experts / top_k) times balanced_size.
moe worst_case_factor = min( |EP| , global_exp / topk ) Once |EP| > global_exp/topk the bound stops growing — for 256/8 it is 32×, independent of EP.

The inequality encoded in the comment is worth internalizing because it is the capacity-planning formula for your activation memory. The worst case is "all of an EP replica group's tokens route to a single shard," which is |EP|× over balanced — but only while local experts ≥ top_k. Once EP > global_exp/top_k there are fewer than top_k local experts per shard, so at most local_exp/top_k of the tokens can land on the hottest shard, and the imbalance factor becomes EP · local_exp / top_k = EP · (global_exp/EP) / top_k = global_exp / top_k, independent of EP. For DeepSeek's 256/8 config that bound is 256/8 = 32×, and it does not get worse no matter how far you scale EP. The buffer never needs to be more than 32× the balanced size.

Set ragged_buffer_factor ≈ 3.0

The engineering recommendation, which MaxText's own comment endorses: the 32× worst-case bound is far larger than real imbalance ever is, so set ragged_buffer_factor ≈ 3.0 as a near-dropless, memory-bounded compromise. You accept a vanishingly small drop probability (un-guaranteed) in exchange for ~10× less activation memory than the worst-case buffer. For a config that widens experts or changes top_k, recompute global_exp/top_k — that ratio, not EP, is your buffer bound. And pair the buffer with the ragged-sort Pallas kernels (use_ragged_sort) so you sort only the valid prefix, not the padded buffer.

5.2 · The MaxText grouped-GEMM dispatch, in code

sparse_matmul's inner gmm() dispatches the grouped GEMM three ways:

  1. jax.lax.ragged_dot — the XLA-native ragged matmul, with set_xla_metadata(ragged_dot_tiling=...) to pass tile hints. The tiling knobs are exposed as config (wi_tile_fwd_* / wo_tile_*, 6 forward configs).
  2. Forked megablox mblx.gmm — the quantized path, supporting lhs_quantize_dtype/rhs_quantize_dtype, weight_gather_axes, and a gmm-v2 variant (use_gmm_v2, currently incompatible with quantization). This is the path you take when running FP8/INT8 expert weights.
  3. tokamax.ragged_dot — implementation="mosaic", gated by use_tokamax_gmm. The Mosaic/Pallas ragged-GEMM, and the most actively tuned of the three for TPU-style tiling.

06DeepSeek V4's fused MoE mega-kernel: the GB200 endgame

V4 (arXiv 2606.19348, April 2026; V4-Pro 1.6T total / 49B active, V4-Flash 284B / 13B active) replaces the DualPipe + DeepEP two-piece overlap with a single fused MoE mega-kernel — "MegaMoE," open-sourced in DeepGEMM (DeepSeek-V4). It fuses the entire MoE forward — EP-dispatch → first FP8×FP4 expert GEMM → SwiGLU (+requant to FP8) → second FP8×FP4 GEMM → EP-combine — into one symmetric-memory (SymmBuffer) kernel that overlaps NVLink transfers with tensor-core MMA inside a single launch.

The mechanism is expert "waves": the current wave computes while the next wave's tokens transfer and finished experts' results are sent back, all concurrently — finer-grained overlap than Comet, and far finer than DualPipe's chunk-level schedule. On SM100 the kernel uses three warp roles (dispatch / UMMA non-epilogue / epilogue), Tensor Memory (TMEM), UE8M0 2D-TMA scaling descriptors, and a MegaMoEScheduler that prioritizes NVLink-active tiles so the comm-bound work always has compute to hide behind (DeepGEMM MegaMoE). Measured speedup: 1.50–1.73× general, up to 1.96× latency-sensitive over the non-fused baseline (DeepSeek-V4). DeepSeek validated the kernel on both NVIDIA GPUs and Huawei Ascend NPUs — the strongest public signal that a frontier lab is engineering MoE serving for a dual-stack future under export pressure.

Other V4 systems facts relevant to your kernel choices:

  • Sqrt(Softplus(·)) affinity replaces V3's sigmoid gating — a small change with quality motivation, trivial to implement.
  • Hash routing for the first layers (deterministic, no aux loss), which MaxText already gates correctly (is_hash_routing turns off lb-loss; see §04).
  • FP4 QAT on expert weights plus FP4 on the indexer QK path — the first trillion-scale model trained with FP4 in the loop, directly relevant to GB200's native NVFP4 microscaling.
  • TileLang DSL for the kernels (sub-µs host launchers, Z3-checked indexing, bitwise-reproducible IEEE-754 intrinsics).
  • A pull-based all-to-all (each GPU reads remote activations rather than pushing) with FP32 local-sum reduce-scatter for gradients.
The FP4 caveat you must not gloss over stated in V4

V4 explicitly notes that FP4×FP8 currently executes at FP8×FP8 speed on existing silicon and only realizes the ~1/3 efficiency win on future hardware (DeepSeek-V4). On GB200 today, the FP4 expert GEMM is a memory-bandwidth and footprint win (half the weight bytes, so you fit more experts per GPU and move fewer bytes in dispatch/combine), not yet a compute win. Budget accordingly: FP4 buys you capacity and bandwidth on Blackwell, and the compute speedup lands on the next generation.

07Synthesis: the recommended MoE stack for 10T/200B on 100k GB200

Pulling the chapter together into a concrete build, anchored to your real cluster economics. A 100k-GB200 cluster delivers roughly 0.45 EFLOP/s dense FP8 peak (100,000 × ~4.5 PFLOP/s per B200 dense; the headline 720 PFLOP/s-per-rack figures are with-sparsity numbers, dense is one-half per NVIDIA's own footnote) (NVIDIA GB200 NVL72), and realistic large-MoE FP8 MFU sits in the 35–40% band — heavy routing pushes it lower. Every percent of that is gated by the MoE layer's communication and balancing, which is why the choices below matter.

Communication substrate

Target DeepEP V2 (ElasticBuffer, NCCL Gin backend, 4–6 SMs, SM100 support at 726/740 GB/s NVLink at EP8) and track the hybrid-ep branch for the TMA-pipelined near-zero-SM path — but verify its SM100 status before committing. Treat the 39 GB/s low-latency RDMA floor at ≥128 experts as a hard design constraint and the #427 imbalance-corruption bug as a step-0 hazard: run the high-throughput dispatch path until the bias balancer converges, or use hash-routed early layers (which cannot hit #427) as a clean warm-up.

Kernel

Ship the DeepGEMM MegaMoE fused kernel as the target — it deletes DualPipe's 20-SM overlap tax and the two-piece dispatch/GEMM/combine handoff in one move, for 1.5–1.96×. Bake it off against SonicMoE (GB200-benchmarked, IO/tile-aware) for the standalone grouped GEMM. If any part of the run is JAX/TPU, MaxText's sparse_matmul + ragged_all_to_all + Tokamax ragged_dot is the production-credible non-CUDA equivalent.

compute ratio to beat = (6 · h · d) FLOPs / (3 · h) bytes ≈ 6144 FLOP/byte Stay above this and the expert GEMM hides the all-to-all under compute. Node-limiting + FP8 dispatch + the fused kernel are all in service of this line.

Dropless and buffers

Commit to dropless. Size ragged buffers at the global_exp/top_k bound (≤32× for a V3-style 256/8 config, recomputed if you widen experts) for guaranteed no-drop, or set ragged_buffer_factor ≈ 3.0 for the near-dropless, ~10×-smaller-memory compromise MaxText recommends. Pair with the ragged-sort Pallas kernels so you sort only the valid prefix at high EP. Never run a capacity factor of 11 to chase dropless quality with padding — that is 11× wasted expert FLOPs.

Routing and balancing

Run auxiliary-loss-free bias balancing with routed_bias_update_rate ≈ 0.001 on a decay-to-zero schedule (V3 decayed at ~96% of training; remember MaxText's default is off and applies the update post-optimizer). Use node-limited M=4 routing with one routing group mapped to one NVL72 domain so the RDMA leg carries only cross-group traffic. Consider hash routing for the first layers (perfectly balanced, deterministic, immune to #427). At deployment, EPLB redundant-expert replication is mandatory for the hottest experts; evaluate LPLB for online per-batch redistribution (~100µs/batch, fed by DeepEP buffer stats).

Precision

Use FP8 dispatch / BF16 combine to halve the dispatch leg (~86 KB vs ~115 KB per token/layer). Plan for FP4 expert weights as a footprint/bandwidth win on GB200 — fitting more experts per GPU and moving fewer dispatch bytes — while remembering it is not a compute win until next-gen silicon. Keep the controlling ratio in view: you need ≈6144 FLOPs/Byte to fully hide MoE communication under compute, and node-limiting + FP8 dispatch + the fused kernel are all in service of staying above that line.

The meta-lesson

The deepest meta-lesson from the open reports is that most of the DualPipe/DeepEP overlap acrobatics were workarounds for the H800's throttled interconnect. On a flat NVL72 domain with DeepEP V2 and MegaMoE, the overlap problem shrinks dramatically — but the architectural bets (sparser routing, node-limiting, dropless ragged buffers, FP8/FP4 dispatch, bias balancing) transfer directly and are the load-bearing decisions for your run.
