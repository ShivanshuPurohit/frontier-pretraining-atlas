Most writing about frontier models stops at the architecture diagram. This one keeps going — into the GB200 NVLink domains, the all-to-all that gates MoE throughput, the FP4 grouped GEMMs, the optimizer that finally broke the AdamW monoculture, the data taxonomies nobody photographs, and the fault-tolerance math that decides whether a 100,000-GPU run survives the month. The worked example throughout is concrete: a 10T-total / 200B-active Mixture-of-Experts model on 100,000 GB200 GPUs.

The reference bar for this book is the worklog-style technical post — the JAX Scaling Book, the DeepSeek-V3 report, Jianlin Su's kexue.fm — where every figure answers one question and every number names its denominator. Frontier labs disclose almost nothing, so claims here carry an explicit epistemic tier: confirmed (in a paper, datasheet, or source tree), leaked (credible reconstruction — SemiAnalysis, Epoch AI), and rumored (single-source, directional). Where a number is a marketing or sparse-peak figure, it is flagged.

How to read this

The chapters are self-contained and cross-referenced. Systems engineers building the run: 2 → 3 → 4 → 5 → 6 → 14 → 15, then the punch-list (20), with Appendices A and C at your elbow. Pretraining scientists: 8 → 9 → 10 → 11 → 12 → 13 → 16 → 18. Sizing the bet: the argument below, then 1 → 6 → 20 → Appendix B. Completists: front to back — it is designed to take days. Every figure states its regime and its evidence, because a number without a denominator is just a vibe.

▸The argument in brief

Ten claims, each expanded, sourced, and quantified inside:

  • The target sits at the frontier of the announced envelope, not beyond it — the bottleneck is whether your software extracts 35% MFU instead of 18%, and whether the run survives months of failure. (Ch 1)
  • At this scale you are communication-bound on the MoE all-to-all — keep expert parallelism inside the NVLink domain and overlap the dispatch/combine with compute. (Ch 5)
  • Networking became a protocol problem and the protocol changed: OpenAI's MRC — packet spraying, SRv6, microsecond failover — is the systems disclosure of the year. (Ch 3)
  • The MFU you actually get is 20–40%; anyone quoting 50%+ is using sparse peak or a dense Llama-style transformer. (Ch 6)
  • FP8 is the floor; FP4 QAT on the expert weights is the 2026 frontier, and it is real. (Ch 9)
  • The optimizer monoculture broke: Muon / MuonClip trained a trillion-parameter MoE with zero loss spikes. (Ch 10)
  • "Scaling laws" now means your own laws, fit on your own data, with quality-dependent exponents — not Chinchilla. (Ch 12)
  • Data curation is the highest-leverage, least-photographed work — taxonomies are a template, not a ceiling. (Ch 13)
  • The dataloader is a distributed storage system with a hard real-time SLA, and goodput — not MFU — is the real metric. (Ch 14)
  • The payoff is a build-order, not a parts list. (Ch 20)
At 100k GB200 you are communication-bound on the MoE all-to-all, not compute- or memory-bound. Almost every other decision follows from that one fact.
