Nobody at a frontier lab in 2026 opens a whiteboard and fits N^a·D^b to a Kaplan-style loss surface and calls it done. What actually happens is closer to an industrial instrumentation program: hundreds to thousands of small runs feeding a stack of coupled power laws — learning rate, batch size, the loss surface itself, MoE sparsity, precision, data repetition — cross-checked against a handful of expensive mid-scale confirmation runs before a single proprietary token touches the target-scale cluster. This chapter is about that stack: what functional forms the field has converged on, what actually transfers across scale and what doesn’t, what it costs, and what a lab building a 10T-total/200B-active MoE on its own data should commission before committing the run.

From a single exponent to a multi-axis ladder

The live practice partitions into two loosely coupled sub-problems. The first is compute allocation and hyperparameter selection — how big should N and D be, what learning rate η and batch size B does that point need — fit from many cheap small/mid-scale runs. The second is the bridge from pretraining loss to anything a stakeholder actually cares about (downstream task accuracy), fit from fewer but larger runs because, as later sections document, bridge laws are precisely the component that breaks under careless extrapolation. Almost every methodological argument below is really an argument about which of these two sub-problems a technique addresses, and how far it can be trusted away from the scale where it was fit.

The single most important shift from the Chinchilla era (covered as history elsewhere in the field’s canon, not reproduced here) is that N and D are no longer the only axes worth power-law treatment. A lab now expects separate laws for η(N,D), B(N,D or D-only), sparsity/granularity, precision P, and data-repetition count, each interacting with the others in ways a single Chinchilla-style fit cannot capture. DeepSeek’s own foundational HP-scaling paper made this multi-axis structure explicit as early as 2024: rather than adopting muP, DeepSeek-AI fit direct power laws for optimal learning rate and batch size against compute budget C from small-scale grid search, arriving at η_opt = 0.3118·C^-0.1250 and B_opt = 0.2920·C^0.3271, with model/data compute allocation M_opt = 0.1715·C^0.5243 and D_opt = 5.8316·C^0.4757 (DeepSeek LLM [PAPER], 1/2024). The detail that matters most for a lab about to curate its own 30-40T-token corpus is buried past the headline formulas: the exponents shift with data quality. DeepSeek’s higher-quality “current in-house” dataset gave a=0.524/b=0.476 (favoring model size), while their earlier, lower-quality “early in-house” dataset gave a=0.450/b=0.550 (favoring data volume) — the compute-optimal N/D split is not a universal constant, it is a property of the specific data distribution being trained on. That single finding is the strongest argument in the whole literature against importing anyone else’s published compute-optimal ratio wholesale.

muP/muTransfer: what actually transfers in 2025-2026

The original muP/muTransfer result (Tensor Programs V, Yang et al. [PAPER], 2022) promises hyperparameters tuned on a small proxy model transfer zero-shot to arbitrary width via per-layer init/LR scaling rules. By 2026 the question worth asking is not whether muP works — it does, cleanly, for width — but whether it covers everything a frontier lab needs scaled simultaneously, and the accumulated 2025-2026 literature answers no.

Adoption evidence is real but narrower than the marketing implies. Cerebras-GPT and EleutherAI’s muTransfer port were the first clean open demonstrations that HP search on a ~40M model transfers to 2.7B+ (EleutherAI blog, “The Practitioner’s Guide to the Maximal Update Parameterization” [OFFICIAL-BLOG]). MiniCPM took a related but distinct path — WSD-schedule checkpoint reuse rather than muP proper — to derive a 192× data-to-model compute ratio against Chinchilla’s ~20×, i.e., a massively-overtrained point is compute-optimal once inference and small-model economics enter the objective (MiniCPM [PAPER], 4/2024). Moonlight (MoonshotAI’s 3B-active/16B-total MoE, 5.7T tokens, Muon optimizer) needed a muP-style “consistent update RMS across matrix and non-matrix parameters” correction before Muon would scale at all — vanilla Muon does not muTransfer out of the box because orthogonalized-update norms mismatch between routed and shared experts (Moonlight [PAPER + CODE], 2/2025), a correction that became MuonClip in Kimi K2 after attention-logit explosions past 1000 surfaced in a 9B-active/53B-total mid-scale ablation (Kimi K2 [PAPER], 7/2025) — QK-Clip mechanics belong to Ch7, but the scaling-law point is that muP-for-Muon required its own bespoke patch.

Five specific failure modes are now documented well enough to plan around rather than discover the hard way:

  1. Depth transfer. Standard muP transfers width but not depth. “Don’t be lazy: CompleteP” shows simultaneous width+depth transfer needs α=1 residual-branch scaling — stronger than the initialization-only stability condition — plus rescaled LayerNorm/bias LR and AdamW ε (CompleteP, Cerebras [PAPER], 5/2025).
  2. Attention/QK scale. Attention logits don’t obey the network’s fan-in law, so QK-normalization needs its own scaling rule under muP; omitting this is a common source of “transferred fine to 1B, blew up at 30B” reports. GQA-µP extends the fix to grouped-query attention (GQA-µP [PAPER], 2026).
  3. Batch size and duration. Vanilla muP says nothing about how LR should co-move with batch size or training horizon — that requires the CBS machinery below. “Complete^d” unifies width+depth+batch+duration transfer into one parameterization and names this gap unresolved by muP alone (Complete^d, Apple [PAPER], 12/2025).
  4. MoE fan-in ambiguity. Is the effective width for muP purposes total parameter count or activated (top-k) count? µ-Parametrization for MoE shows LR transfer holds across expert count but breaks across top-k and granularity — a lab changing granularity between ladder scale and target scale cannot trust muP alone (µ-Parametrization for MoE [PAPER], 8/2025). Complete-muE proposes a two-bridge fix — dense→dense-MoE via active-width µP plus normalized router scale, then dense-MoE→sparse-MoE via activated-expert scaling — with the practical recipe “tune dense once, transfer to all MoE configs” (Complete-muE, Adobe [PAPER], 5/2026).
  5. Init-std sensitivity. MoE training is measurably more sensitive to initialization std than dense equivalents; naive FFN-LR-reduction (tried in Switch Transformer, Skywork-MoE) underperforms because it breaks the maximal-update condition (survey synthesis, “How To Scale” [CREDIBLE-HEARSAY], 12/2025).

Su Jianlin’s ongoing “MuP之上” (Beyond MuP) series, from Moonshot’s own research diary, reframes muP around three Θ(1) stability conditions and argues the linear-layer special case reproduces Muon from first principles — theoretical grounding rather than a new empirical law, but evidence the muP-vs-Muon debate has a rigorous Chinese-language counterpart running months ahead of any English survey (kexue.fm archives/11340 et seq. [BLOG], 10/2025-4/2026; see Ch7, Ch19).

No credible source claims zero-shot muP transfer from a 100M proxy straight to a 200B-active/10T-total MoE across width, depth, batch, duration, and sparsity simultaneously. The pattern that has emerged is layered: muP-style width scaling as one component, empirical HP power laws (Step Law/DeepSeek-style, below) layered on top for batch/LR/duration, and MoE-specific bridge laws layered on top of that.

The HP-law family: Step Law, Farseer, and full-curve fitting

Three lineages dominate the public post-Chinchilla HP-scaling literature, and all three are more empirically grounded — and more expensive to produce — than anything Kaplan-era scaling work attempted.

Step Law (2503.04715, StepFun, “Predictable Scale Part I” [PAPER], 3/2025) is the largest public HP-scaling sweep documented anywhere (scale in the table below) — comparable to roughly 38% of DeepSeek-V3’s entire 2.664M H800-GPU-hour pretraining budget, spent purely on instrumentation rather than a deployable model (see Ch4 for DeepSeek-V3’s own infra). Its B(D) formula carries a strong, independently testable claim: optimal batch size depends on total tokens D alone, not on model size N. For MoE, Step Law validates active parameter count N_a over total N as the correct HP-scaling variable, cutting relative prediction error to 0.5% versus >4× larger error using a DeepSeek-style total-N formula, and its 0.94‰ deviation from exhaustive grid search dominates OpenAI-style (9.51‰), Microsoft-style (9.25‰), DeepSeek-style (9.26‰), and Porian-style (3.71‰) laws. The loss landscape under fixed (N,D) is convex but broad near the optimum — the practical payoff of a precise HP law is mostly about avoiding gross misallocation, not squeezing out the last 0.1%.

Farseer (2506.10972, StepFun, “Predictable Scale Part II” [PAPER], 6/2025) attacks the loss surface itself rather than HP selection, replacing Chinchilla’s separable L(N,D) with a form where the data-scaling exponent is itself a function of N (full form in the table below), fit via a three-stage “Differential Piecewise + Multi-round Iterative” procedure across ~1,000 models and ~3M H100-hours.

LR-annealing full-curve fitting (2408.11029, Tencent [PAPER], 8/2024) is the mechanism that makes the whole ladder economically tractable: L(s) = L₀ + A·S₁^-α - C·S₂, where S₁ is an “area under the LR curve” generalization of token count and S₂ the extra loss reduction attributable to LR decay. Fitting this from just 1-2 actual runs predicts the entire loss curve at every step under any scheduler — a stable-phase WSD checkpoint plus a short decay run stands in for a from-scratch cosine run at that budget. This is the mechanism underneath MiniCPM’s cheap ladder and the reason WSD (not cosine) has become the ladder-default schedule field-wide; the recipe below returns to it as the single highest-leverage cost lever available.

Gemstones (2502.06857, “A Model Suite for Multi-Faceted Scaling Laws” [PAPER, NeurIPS 2025], 2/2025) is the field’s built-in caution against over-trusting any of the above: 4,000+ open checkpoints, transformers to 2B params, 10T+ cumulative tokens, deliberately varying LR, cooldown schedule, and width/depth ratio within the ladder. Its central finding is that scaling-law prescriptions — the compute-optimal N/D split, in particular — are highly sensitive to which checkpoint subset and which LR/cooldown regime the fit is run on, a direct warning against copying a published law’s numeric constants onto a differently-shaped architecture.

Critical batch size: not what gradient-noise-scale predicts

The classical McCandlish et al. (2018) framework gives η*(B) = η_max/(1+B_noise/B) for SGD, with the noise scale estimated cheaply from per-device gradient variance. Su Jianlin’s kexue.fm derivation extends this to Adam via a SignSGD mean-field approximation and corrects a mid-2024 paper’s claim about the Adam-specific “Surge phenomenon” (2405.14578 [PAPER]): past a second, higher critical batch size β_noise, the optimal learning rate for Adam-family optimizers can decrease as batch size increases further, rather than merely saturating as SGD’s η* does. The original paper’s per-coordinate approximation forces the conclusion Surge “almost always” occurs; Su’s mean-field treatment shows the reversal is conditional on β_noise < 1, and derives a second, Hessian-free noise-scale estimator from measured (steps, examples) pairs (kexue.fm/archives/10542 [BLOG, high-SNR independent technical blog], 11/2024).

Ai2’s “Critical Batch Size Revisited” (2505.23971, NeurIPS 2025 Spotlight [PAPER], 5/2025) tests directly whether gradient-noise-scale is a usable CBS proxy at LLM scale and finds it is not: for OLMo 7B the noise-scale estimate does not even track the qualitative trend of the empirically-measured CBS, underestimating true CBS by orders of magnitude at both 1B and 7B. Their branched-training protocol — train at batch k·B and LR f(k)·η over a fixed 2B-token window, find the max k with ≤ε loss degradation — measures CBS directly instead, finding it starts near zero at initialization, rises rapidly over the first ~50K tokens, then plateaus (≈4096 documents / ~16M tokens in their setup). This motivates batch-size warmup as standard practice: start small, double batch size (with √k LR rescaling for Adam) whenever measured CBS exceeds 2× the current batch size. Applied to OLMo-1B, this produced a run reaching slightly better final loss in 43% fewer gradient steps than a fixed-small-batch baseline, while a naive fixed-large-batch run used 75% fewer steps but landed at a measurably worse loss — a concrete quantification of the cost of getting CBS scheduling wrong in either direction.

Optimizer choice materially shifts CBS: multiple 2025-2026 sources converge on Muon roughly doubling to tripling usable CBS versus AdamW at matched validation loss (survey synthesis citing Moonlight-lineage results, howtoscalenn.github.io [CREDIBLE-HEARSAY], 12/2025 — not independently verified against primary sources; theoretical treatment in “Convergence Bound and Critical Batch Size of Muon Optimizer” [PAPER]). AdamW’s CBS tends to plateau around ~10M tokens for many setups, a soft ceiling on data-parallel scale-out before switching optimizer families becomes the higher-leverage move — relevant to the DP/EP composition math in Ch16.

Batch-size schedule, not just the CBS point estimate, has its own recent literature. “How to Allocate Your Tokens?” proposes a three-term law L(N,M,K) = E + A/N^α + B/M^β + C/K^γ (M = batch tokens, K = steps, D = MK) whose optimum M* = [βB/γC]^(1/(β+γ)) · D^(γ/(β+γ)) depends only on total token budget D, not on N — empirically M* = 0.67·D^0.566, closely matching Step Law’s independently-derived B(D) = 0.58·D^0.571 despite a different methodology, needing only 2 batch sizes per (N,D) point (28% of a full grid) to recover the same law (2607.01487 [PAPER], 2026). “Fast Catch-Up, Late Switching” finds the optimal within-run batch-ramp shape is task-dependent: easy objectives favor continuously increasing batch size, hard/late-saturating objectives favor staying small then switching late (2602.14208 [PAPER], 2026).

MoE-specific scaling laws: efficiency leverage and the joint form

MoE scaling in 2025-2026 is treated as its own joint law over (N or N_active, D, granularity/sparsity, shared-expert ratio) rather than a dense law with a correction bolted on — the choices quantified here feed directly into the architecture decisions in Ch5. Three independent efforts converge on compatible pictures.

Ant Group’s Ling team defines Efficiency Leverage (EL) — the compute ratio between a dense model and its loss-equivalent MoE counterpart, so EL=5 means the MoE needs 1/5 the FLOPs for equal loss — from 300+ models trained to 28B params. EL is driven by activation ratio and total compute budget via clean power laws, while granularity is a non-linear modulator with a genuine interior optimum rather than “more granular is better,” predicting a 3.1%-activation, granularity-12 MoE achieves >7× EL at 1e22 FLOPs, validated by training Ling-mini-beta (0.85B active) to match a 6.1B dense model on 1T identical tokens using >7× less compute (Ling EL, 2507.17702 [PAPER], 7/2025) — a result that directly informed the production Ling-2.0/Ling-1T configuration: 256 routed experts, 8 active plus 1 shared (~3.5% activation), up to 7× active-compute efficiency versus dense at full-scale FP8 (Ling-1T, 2510.22115 [PAPER/OFFICIAL], 10/2025).

“Joint MoE Scaling Laws” fits a joint L(N_active, D, Ê) power-law form, where Ê is a monotonic transform of expert count, across 280+ experiments up to 2.7B active/5B total params, and lands on a genuinely counter-intuitive headline: MoE can be more memory-efficient than dense at matched loss, contradicting the usual framing that MoE trades memory for compute savings (2502.05172, ICML 2025 [PAPER], 2/2025).

The “Comprehensive MoE Scaling Law” runs 446 controlled experiments across five factors — D, N, N_active, active-expert-count G, and shared-expert-ratio S — and finds optimal G and S are independent of model architecture and data size, i.e., a granularity/shared-ratio choice tuned once transfers across scale, a rare genuinely scale-invariant MoE knob. But optimal N_active/N shrinks (gets sparser) as N grows: a 10T-param model’s compute-optimal active fraction is not the same fraction a lab would pick at a 100B-param ladder rung, and must be swept as its own function of scale (2509.23678 [PAPER], 9/2025).

Cross-checked against Step Law’s finding that active-parameter-count HP formulas cut relative error 4× versus total-parameter-count formulas, the field is converging hard on N_active — not N_total — as the correct primary MoE scaling variable, with granularity and shared-ratio as secondary, largely scale-invariant knobs, and top-level activation ratio as the variable that must itself follow its own power law in N. This is a materially different picture from “fit Chinchilla on active params and call it done.”

Data-constrained scaling at 30T+ token budgets

Muennighoff et al.’s foundational data-constrained law established the reference points every later paper measures against: up to 4 epochs of repetition is roughly equivalent to unique data, and the value of additional compute decays toward zero beyond roughly 16 epochs, tested to 900B tokens/9B params (2305.16264 [PAPER], 5/2023). “Practical Scaling Laws: Converting Compute into Performance in a Data-Constrained World” extends this with a three-term decomposition L(N,D,T) = E + (L₀-E)·h/(1+h) separating undercapacity, undertraining, and overfitting, distinguishing unique examples D from total-examples-seen T, and recommending WSD schedules with log-spaced compute checkpoints — the same checkpoint-reuse trick underlying MiniCPM and Tencent’s LR-annealing law, applied to the data-constrained regime — so a single long run yields many (N,D,T) loss measurements cheaply. In its extreme-scarcity regime (ρ_D/ρ_C = 10^13), the fit implies optimal model size shrinks ~20× versus the data-unconstrained optimum, optimal epoch count climbs to ~1,250, and ~86% of compute budget should go to data repetition (2605.09189 [PAPER], 5/2026) — an extreme-regime number, but directionally the lesson frontier labs training 15-18T+ token corpora with multiple passes over higher-quality subsets already lean on. For a 30-40T-token target corpus, unique high-quality web/code/book supply is the binding constraint before parameter count is — see Ch10, Ch12.

Precision-aware scaling laws: the 7-8 bit claim and its pushback

Kumar et al.’s “Scaling Laws for Precision” introduces an effective parameter count N_eff(P) ≈ N·(1-e^(-P/γ))³ that lets loss degradation from low-precision training or post-training quantization be predicted as if the model simply had fewer parameters. Its two headline claims: jointly optimizing N, D, P under fixed compute gives P* ≈ 7-8 bits as compute-optimal, quantifying that BF16 is somewhat wasteful and full INT4 somewhat too aggressive; and a PTQ degradation formula that increases with D — meaning past some token budget, additional pretraining data actively harms a model destined for low-precision deployment (Kumar et al., 2411.04330, ICLR 2025 [PAPER], 11/2024).

A competing Chinese-lab law, self-named “Capybara,” independently derives a cost-optimal FP precision range of 4-8 bits — wider and lower than Kumar’s — and confirms the qualitative critical-data-size finding, while decomposing the loss dependence into exponent-bit versus mantissa-bit contributions, finding exponent bits matter slightly more (2501.02423, ICML 2025 [PAPER], 1/2025). Neither paper anticipated native FP4 training working well: “Quartet” reports FP4-native recipes with proper stochastic rounding and scaling reaching parity with higher precision, meaning the practical precision floor moved below both papers’ stated optimum within about a year (Quartet, 2505.14669 [PAPER], 5/2025) — the field’s clearest illustration of a durable functional form (effective-parameter framework, data-size-dependent PTQ degradation) attached to a numeric constant with a shelf life measured in single-digit months.

In practice, frontier labs mostly bypass the “pick one global precision” framing both laws assume: DeepSeek-V3 and Ling-2.0 both run full-scale FP8 mixed-precision training with fine-grained block/tile-wise scaling factors rather than a single uniform P. DeepSeek’s own hardware-co-design retrospective flags FP8’s constrained Tensor Core accumulation precision on Hopper as a live stability issue requiring architecture-level workarounds — the precision-scaling-law numbers are necessary but not sufficient; engineering reality is dominated by which fine-grained quantization scheme a given accelerator generation supports well (DeepSeek-V3 hardware retrospective, 2505.09343 [PAPER/OFFICIAL], 5/2025). Ch8 covers the full FP8→MXFP8→NVFP4 recipe landscape; the point to carry forward here is that a lab should fit its own effective-parameter-count curve on its own hardware/recipe rather than import Kumar’s or Capybara’s numeric optimum.

Inference-aware and distillation scaling laws

Two adjacent law families matter for a lab that will also serve the model it trains. Sardana et al.’s “Beyond Chinchilla-Optimal” modifies compute-optimal N,D selection to account for lifetime inference volume rather than training FLOPs alone, finding loss keeps improving as tokens-per-parameter is pushed to extreme ranges — up to 10,000 — i.e., overtraining relative to Chinchilla is often globally optimal once serving cost is counted; a lab expecting ~2T inference tokens over a model’s lifetime could cut total train+serve compute 17% by training a 7B model instead of Chinchilla-optimal 13B (Sardana et al., 2401.00448 [PAPER], 1/2024). MoE-specific inference-aware laws remain immature in the public literature — a gap worth flagging. Busbridge et al.’s Distillation Scaling Laws give a compute-optimal teacher/student FLOPs allocation and resolve the “capacity gap” (student quality degrading as teacher grows) as an addressable learning-capacity gap, not a fixed penalty: with many students to distill or an existing teacher, distillation beats from-scratch training up to a threshold scaling predictably with student size; with exactly one student and a teacher that must also be trained, direct pretraining is usually preferable (Busbridge et al., 2502.08606, Apple [PAPER], 2/2025) — relevant to a 10T-param flagship alongside a family of smaller served models.

How labs actually build ladders, and what it costs

Three open-lab programs document the ladder-construction process in enough detail to serve as templates (numbers in the table below).

Ai2/OLMo runs a two-step prediction methodology — fit N,D → intermediate loss, then intermediate loss → downstream task accuracy, because direct N,D → task-accuracy fits are noisier (Bhagia et al., 2412.04403, COLM 2025 [PAPER], 12/2024), using the OLMES eval framework to stabilize the second-stage fit target.

Marin (Stanford CRFM) makes the entire ladder process public, including a standing GitHub issue tracking scaling-law experiments across extraction method, mixture ratio, and quality-filter choice. Its Speedrun leaderboard ranks submissions by “Projected C4-EN BPB at 1e22 FLOPs” — the slope of a method’s scaling curve extrapolated to a fixed reference budget, not raw score at whatever scale was trained (marin.community [OFFICIAL-BLOG + CODE], 5/2025 ongoing) — a rigor upgrade worth adopting for internal ablation programs generally.

HuggingFace’s SmolLM3 / “Smol Training Playbook” gives the most transparent public accounting of ladder cost as a fraction of final-run cost: the primary ablation setup trains the full 3B target architecture on 100B tokens instead of the final 11T, shrinking the token budget rather than the model — a different ladder-axis choice than Ai2’s, costing “more than half the cost of our main training run” by the playbook’s own accounting. Its methodological caution is worth carrying forward: small-scale ablations are trustworthy for negative results, much less trustworthy for positive ones, which need confirmation at a reasonable token count before being trusted (HF Smol Training Playbook [OFFICIAL-BLOG], 10/2025).

Step Law’s own ladder is the most extreme documented ratio: ~1M H800-GPU-hours on the law-fitting sweep alone against DeepSeek-V3’s entire 2.664M H800-GPU-hour pretraining budget for a comparable-era, comparable-hardware MoE effort — instrumentation-only spend producing no deployable model at all. This is the strongest evidence available that serious HP-law fitting for a frontier-scale run is a double-digit-percentage line item against the final training budget, not a small ablation, and should be staffed accordingly.

Why ladders fail: extrapolation breakdown and downstream unreliability

Multiple independent 2025-2026 results converge on the same warning from different directions. A meta-analysis of published downstream scaling-law fits finds predictable scaling holds only about 39% of the time, with failure modes including emergence-like jumps, inverse scaling, and — most operationally relevant — high sensitivity to seemingly benign choices like prompt format, eval-set choice, and checkpoint selection (2507.00885, EMNLP 2025 Findings [PAPER], 7/2025). Gemstones, discussed above, delivers a companion warning purely about methodology: the same training-run distribution, refit on different LR-schedule/checkpoint subsets, yields materially different compute-optimal prescriptions. Related work frames the problem as a two-stage compounding-error process — N,D→loss, then loss→accuracy — where models with near-identical loss can differ substantially in downstream performance, making loss necessary but insufficient as a ladder target (2512.08894 [PAPER], 12/2025).

Two cross-check methodologies are worth knowing. Observational scaling laws exploit the roughly 100 existing public model families as an implicit, free scaling sweep, fitting cross-family correlations to predict downstream behavior as capability scales; emergent-seeming benchmark jumps turn out to follow smooth sigmoidal curves once plotted against a suitable capability axis rather than raw compute, reconciling with the earlier “Are Emergent Abilities a Mirage?” argument that apparent discontinuities are measurement artifacts of non-smooth metrics (Ruan et al., 2405.10938, COLM 2024 [PAPER], 5/2024). Loss-to-loss prediction fits shifted power-law relationships directly between the losses of different models or datasets rather than fitting N,D→loss separately per dataset; these laws extrapolate well to 20× the largest fit FLOP budget, sometimes beating a single-dataset N,D law directly (Brandfonbrener et al., 2411.12925, TMLR 2025 [PAPER], 11/2024).

The composite lesson: loss-based ladders are comparatively trustworthy; anything downstream of loss needs its own validated bridge law, validated per-lab and per-eval-suite, and re-validated whenever checkpoint or schedule choices change. No lab should assume a bridge law fit at 1B tokens-per-param survives a schedule change, let alone a 100×+ scale jump.

The recipe: commissioning a laws program for a 10T/200B run

Synthesizing the above into a concrete ladder design — the “how to build your own laws” deliverable this chapter promised, not any single paper’s prescription:

1. Split the ladder into two decoupled sub-problems. (a) The N,D compute-allocation law and HP power laws (η, B) — cheap-ish, many small runs. (b) The loss→downstream-task bridge law — fewer but larger runs, since bridge laws are unreliable at small scale.

2. Sweep axes, in the order a 10T/200B lab should budget for them:

  • N × D grid at 5-7 log-spaced model sizes (Step Law used 60M→1B; a lab targeting 10T total should extend one or two rungs higher, e.g. to 3-8B, given Gemstones’ warning that shape/schedule sensitivity grows with extrapolation distance) crossed with 5+ data scales.
  • Active-parameter ratio and expert granularity as their own axis at fixed N and D, not inferred from a dense law. Expect granularity’s optimum to be roughly scale-invariant (tune once) but N_active/N’s optimum to be a function of N and swept across the ladder’s full range.
  • Batch size and LR jointly, via direct grid search at each (N,D) point (Step Law’s approach) or the cheaper branched CBS-measurement protocol (Ai2’s 2B-token windows) plus the token-budget-only three-term law as a cross-check — the latter needs only ~2 batch sizes per (N,D) cell instead of a full grid.
  • Precision as a genuine sweep axis at 2-3 model sizes if the run is FP8/FP4-bound: fit an in-house effective-parameter-count curve rather than importing Kumar’s or Capybara’s numeric P*, since the published constants are already stale relative to native FP4.
  • Data mixture and epoch count via the data-constrained scaling framework, since a 10T-param model on tens of trillions of tokens is likely to hit the unique-high-quality-token ceiling before a parameter ceiling — fit the multi-epoch decay law on the corpus’s own quality tiers rather than assuming Muennighoff’s public-web constants transfer (see Ch10, Ch12).

3. Run the entire ladder on WSD (or another checkpoint-reuse-compatible schedule), not cosine. Each run yields many (N,D) loss observations along its stable-phase trajectory plus a cheap decay-phase re-measurement via the Tencent LR-annealing law, instead of a fresh cosine run per grid point — the single highest-leverage cost-reduction lever in the entire practice.

4. Budget the ladder at 30-60% of final-run compute, not a rounding error. SmolLM3’s own accounting (58%) and Step Law’s absolute scale (~38% of a DeepSeek-V3-scale run) both land in this band; plan cluster time accordingly.

5. Fit at least 2-3 decades of compute before trusting extrapolation past the largest ladder rung, and treat any extrapolated number with Gemstones/2507.00885-grade skepticism — refit on a different schedule or checkpoint subset as an internal consistency check before committing the full run’s HPs.

6. Never assume a loss→downstream bridge law survives from ladder scale to target scale unchanged. Use the two-step Ai2-style prediction validated at 2+ intermediate scales, or loss-to-loss prediction as a lower-variance intermediate signal, or accept downstream-metric extrapolation is only ~39% likely to hold — let the loss-based law drive N/D/sparsity allocation, treat downstream predictions as directional.

7. Documented failure modes to plan around, not rediscover:

  • Fitting muP transfer on width alone and assuming it covers depth, batch, duration, or MoE granularity.
  • Trusting gradient-noise-scale as a CBS proxy — off by orders of magnitude at LLM scale.
  • Importing a published precision-scaling constant (7-8 bit, or 4-8 bit) instead of refitting on the run’s own hardware and quantization recipe.
  • Fixing MoE granularity or activation ratio at the value optimal at ladder scale and never re-deriving it at target N.
  • Trusting a positive small-scale ablation result without a large-enough token budget — SmolLM3’s most common ablation failure mode.
  • Fitting the loss→downstream bridge once and reusing it across eval-set or prompt-format changes.

Implications for the 10T/200B run

A laws program for this scale is not optional pre-work squeezed into idle cluster capacity; on the evidence above it is a 30-60%-of-final-run line item needing its own budget, cluster allocation, and schedule milestone before the target run is greenlit. The highest-leverage single design decision is running the entire ladder on WSD rather than cosine, since the LR-annealing full-curve law converts every stable-phase checkpoint into a free additional (N,D) observation — closer to Step Law’s economics than to a naive from-scratch grid, and worth adopting regardless of which other lever is chosen. Muon (or any spectral-optimizer choice, see Ch7) changes the CBS and muP picture enough that a lab cannot simply reuse an AdamW-fit ladder; both the batch-size ceiling and the width-transfer rules shift, and QK-Clip-style stabilization needs budgeting into the ladder’s mid-scale confirmation runs, not discovered at target scale as Kimi K2’s team was. The MoE-specific axes — N_active as the primary scaling variable, granularity/shared-ratio as a scale-invariant knob tunable once, activation ratio requiring its own scale-dependent sweep — should be fit on proprietary data explicitly, since Step Law and the comprehensive-MoE-law both show total-parameter-count formulas carry multiple-times-larger error than active-parameter-count formulas for the architecture class (see Ch5) this run targets. Finally, given DeepSeek’s own compute-optimal N/D split moved by roughly 7 percentage points between two in-house datasets of different quality (Δa = Δb ≈ 0.074, from a=0.450/b=0.550 on the lower-quality corpus to a=0.524/b=0.476 on the higher-quality one — arithmetic on the raw exponents from [DeepSeek LLM, 2401.02954], not a percentage-point figure the paper itself states), no published constant here — Step Law’s exponents, Kumar’s 7-8 bit optimum, Muennighoff’s epoch knee — should be treated as more than a starting prior; the corpus this run trains on is proprietary precisely where it matters most.
