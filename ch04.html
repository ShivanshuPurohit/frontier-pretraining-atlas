Chapter 04 · Laying the model across 100k GPUs

Parallelism strategy for a 10T/200B-active MoE: the five axes and the fabric that decides everything

The parallelism plan is the single decision that most determines whether your 100,000-GB200 cluster runs at 40% MFU or 18%. It is not one choice but a composition of five-to-six orthogonal axes mapped onto a strictly hierarchical fabric — and getting the mapping wrong doesn't cost a few points, it halves your throughput.

~28 min read6 figuresgrounded in MaxText configs/base.yml + layers/pipeline.py

The parallelism plan is the single decision that most determines whether your 100,000-GB200 cluster runs at 40% MFU or 18%. It is not one choice but a composition of five-to-six orthogonal axes — data/FSDP, tensor (with sequence-parallel variant), pipeline, expert, and context — each of which must shard a different part of the model state, mapped onto a strictly hierarchical fabric where intra-rack NVLink runs at 1.8 TB/s/GPU and inter-rack InfiniBand at roughly one-twentieth of that. Get the mapping wrong — put a high-volume collective on the wrong tier of the fabric — and you do not lose a few points of MFU, you lose half your throughput. This chapter does the memory math that rules out the naive plans, derives why each axis exists, and proposes a concrete layout with numbers, then grounds it in the user's actual codebase (MaxText) ici_/dcn_ axes and pipeline.py.

The parallelism plan is not one choice but the disciplined assignment of each axis to the one fabric tier and one slice of model state it was built for: EP×TP on NVLink, PP on InfiniBand, DP/FSDP across islands, CP folded onto attention. That single composition rule is the entire layout — everything else is justification.

01Memory math: the optimizer state, not the weights, dictates the layout

The first thing to internalize is that a 10T-parameter model is not a capacity problem on this cluster. It is a sharding-and-bandwidth problem dressed up as a capacity problem. Walk the per-parameter byte budget for the steady state.

For a Muon-primary optimizer — which is the right call in mid-2026, since DeepSeek-V4 and Kimi-K2 both adopted Muon as the main optimizer and keep AdamW only on embeddings, output heads, and RMSNorm parameters (Muon-state quantization paper) — the resident per-parameter footprint is:

ComponentBytes/param (Muon)Bytes/param (AdamW)
Compute weights (bf16)22
Master weights (fp32)44
Gradient (bf16)22
Optimizer state4 (one fp32 momentum)8 (two fp32 moments m,v)
Total~12 B~16 B
 Muon's single momentum buffer halves the dominant optimizer-state term against AdamW's two moments.

For 10T parameters that is 120 TB (Muon) to 160 TB (Adam) of resident, non-replication-shardable state, plus an FP8 (E4M3) compute copy of the weights — the V3/V4 training format — adding 10 TB. The bf16 weights alone are 20 TB; in FP8, 10 TB. The decisive observation: the optimizer-and-master state is 60–75% of the resident footprint, and Muon's single momentum buffer (4 B/param vs AdamW's 8 B) is the cheapest 40-TB saving available — it halves the dominant term. The Scaling Book's own capacity note generalizes here: pure data parallelism with Adam's 10 bytes/param caps a model at ~9B parameters on a 96 GB chip (JAX Scaling Book, training chapter). The 10T model holds 1,100× that.

mem resident bytes ≈ N · (2bf16 + 4fp32 master + 2grad + opt)  →  12 B (Muon) · 10T = 120 TB opt = 4 B (Muon, one momentum) or 8 B (Adam, m+v). Plus a 10 TB FP8 compute copy. None of this is replication-shardable — it must be split across devices.

Now the aggregate picture. A GB200 carries 192 GB HBM3e per GPU at 8 TB/s (NVIDIA GB200 NVL72). 100,000 GPUs → ~19.2 PB aggregate HBM. The 120–160 TB of model+optimizer state is 0.6–0.8% of total HBM. There is no capacity wall. This is precisely why the problem is sharding and bandwidth: the binding constraints are (a) you must shard optimizer state so no device holds a meaningful fraction, (b) activation memory, not parameter memory, is what actually fills HBM per device, and (c) every collective must land on a fabric tier fast enough to hide under that layer's compute.

The one number to remember

The 120–160 TB of model+optimizer state is 0.6–0.8% of 19.2 PB aggregate HBM. There is no capacity wall — this is a sharding-and-bandwidth problem in disguise. The optimizer-and-master state is 60–75% of the resident footprint, so Muon's single momentum buffer is the cheapest 40-TB win on the table.

Activation memory is the real per-device pressure

Per the transformer accounting, a transformer block saves ~20 tensors/layer without rematerialization, which for a 4M-token batch at L=64, D=8192 is ~84 TB of activation memory (Scaling Book transformers). "Big-matmuls-only" remat saves ~7×; full-block remat saves only layer inputs but raises training FLOPs from 6Nθ to ~8Nθ (same) — a 33% compute tax you pay grudgingly and selectively. For a 10T MoE the activation footprint also carries the dispatched expert tokens (top_k copies of the routed activations) and the all-to-all staging buffers. This is why the layout must shard activations along sequence (SP-TP, CP) and pipeline-stage boundaries, not merely shard weights. A plan that shards 120 TB of optimizer state perfectly but leaves activations replicated will OOM on the first long-context microbatch.

Compute-time sanity check

Training FLOPs ≈ 6 × active-params × tokens (Scaling Book transformers). For 200B active over a 30T-token run: 6 · 2e11 · 3e13 = 3.6e25 FLOP. At 100,000 GB200, dense FP8 peak is ~4.5 PFLOP/s/GPU — note that NVIDIA's headline 720 PFLOP/s FP8 per NVL72 rack is the with-sparsity (2:4) figure; the page footnotes "Dense is one-half sparse spec shown," so dense rack FP8 is 360 PFLOP/s and the cluster dense FP8 peak is ~0.45 EFLOP/s, not ~1.0 (NVIDIA GB200 NVL72). At a realistic 40% MoE FP8 MFU: 1e5 · 4.5e15 · 0.40 = 1.8e20 FLOP/s → 3.6e25 / 1.8e20 ≈ 2.0e5 s ≈ 2.3 days of ideal compute, which in practice becomes weeks once all-to-all, pipeline bubble, checkpointing, and restart overheads land. The point of the parallelism plan is to keep that MFU near 40% rather than letting MoE communication drag it to 18%.

02Why pure FSDP (ZeRO-3) fails at this scale

FSDP/ZeRO-3 shards weights, gradients, and optimizer state across the DP axis, all-gathers the full weight shard just-in-time per layer in the forward pass, and reduce-scatters gradients in the backward. Its memory story is perfect — state ÷ X for FSDP degree X. Its communication story is what kills it at 100k.

The roofline is unambiguous: DP and FSDP are compute-bound only when per-device batch exceeds the network's arithmetic intensity C/W (Scaling Book training). On TPU v5p that threshold is ~2,550 tokens/chip over ICI and ~73,440 tokens/pod over the slower DCN. Recompute it for GB200: with NVLink W ≈ 1.8e12 B/s bidirectional and FP8 compute C ≈ 4.5e15 FLOP/s, C/W ≈ ~2,500 tokens/GPU — essentially the same regime as v5p (Scaling Book GPU chapter). FSDP's communication volume is batch-independent: it moves the full weight set every step regardless of how many tokens ride along. That is the fatal property. As you grow FSDP degree toward 100,000, the all-gather of the 10–20 TB weight set must complete inside each layer's compute window, and the per-device shard shrinks to tens of MB while the collective still moves the whole model.

Worse, FSDP at 100k crosses the fabric hierarchy. NVLink intranode is 1.8 TB/s/GPU; inter-rack RDMA is ~90 GB/s/GPU (DeepEP) — a ~20:1 cliff. A 100k-way all-gather necessarily spans thousands of NVL72 racks over InfiniBand, and its latency cannot hide behind one layer's compute. And FSDP cannot exploit MoE structure at all: it replicates the all-to-all-bound expert FFNs across the entire mesh and pays the weight-gather cost on all 10T parameters when only 200B are active per token. You would be all-gathering 50× more expert weight than any token touches.

FSDP's fatal property at 100k

FSDP communication volume is batch-independent — it moves the entire 10–20 TB weight set every step no matter how many tokens ride along. Pushed to 100k-way it spans thousands of racks over the 20:1 IB cliff and all-gathers 50× more expert weight than any token touches. The memory story is perfect; the comms story is fatal.

The fix is structural: confine FSDP/ZeRO to a fast island and offload the "shard the 10T" job to EP + PP. FSDP becomes the outer, elastic optimizer-sharding axis (ZeRO-1, gradients/optimizer only) across DP replicas — not the mechanism that shards the model.

03The five axes and what each must shard

Each axis exists because it shards a different state and rides a different fabric tier. The discipline is to assign each its natural job and no more.

  • EP (expert parallel) — the load-bearing axis for a 10T MoE. Experts sit on disjoint device groups; routing becomes all-to-all dispatch → grouped-GEMM → all-to-all combine. EP shards the bulk of the 10T weights without replication and without per-step weight all-gather. All-to-all volume per layer is T·K·h·(EP−1)/EP (T local tokens, K top-k, h hidden) (Megatron-Core MoE). This is the only axis that natively matches MoE sparsity, and it must live on NVLink.
  • TP + sequence-parallel TP — shards attention and the dense parts (MLA up-projection, router, shared expert) along the model dimension; SP-TP additionally shards LayerNorm and activations along the sequence dimension to kill replicated-activation memory. TP becomes communication-bound past Y > F/(C/W), i.e. ~8–16 way in practice and only ever inside an NVLink domain (Scaling Book training). For a DeepSeek-class small expert F (~2,048), the GPU-chapter figure caps useful TP-on-experts at ~1–2 way (Scaling Book GPU chapter) — which is the whole argument for expert-sharding the FFN instead.
  • PP (pipeline) — partitions layers into stages; the cheapest way to shard 10T across slow links because it moves only stage-boundary activations (≈1.5·2BD/(W·L) per layer, negligible), not weights (Scaling Book training). The cost is the bubble.
  • CP (context parallel) — shards the sequence and KV for long context (V4 targets 1M tokens). Ring/striped attention. For an MLA/DSA model the KV is already compressed, so CP need only engage at the 64K→1M stages.
  • DP/FSDP outer + ZeRO-1 — replicates the (EP×TP×PP)-sharded unit and shards optimizer state across replicas. This is the elastic, fault-isolation axis. DP all-reduce of gradients is batch-amortized, so it tolerates the slow top-of-fabric.

Token-sharding vs expert-sharding: the central MoE choice

The FFN can be sharded two ways. Expert-sharding (EP) keeps each expert whole on a device and shuffles tokens to it (all-to-all dispatch/combine). Token-sharding (TP-on-experts) splits each expert's matrices and keeps tokens local (all-gather/reduce-scatter). DeepSeek and Megatron-Core both favor expert-sharding for the FFN because high TP "fragments small experts into inefficient shards" while low TP "underutilizes attention" (Megatron-Core MoE). The expert intermediate dim is small — 2,048 for DeepSeek-V3 (V3 report) — so a TP=8 split of a 2,048-wide GEMM leaves 256-wide tiles that under-fill the tensor cores. Expert-sharding keeps the GEMM full-width and pays only an all-to-all, which on NVLink is cheap (the all-to-all is one-quarter of an all-gather in the bandwidth-bound regime, since shards travel only part-way round the ring (Scaling Book sharding)).

Expert-shard the FFN — its F≈2,048 is too small to TP-split

A TP=8 split of a 2,048-wide expert GEMM leaves 256-wide tiles that starve the tensor cores. Keep each expert whole, shuffle tokens to it, and pay only an all-to-all — one-quarter the cost of an all-gather in the bandwidth-bound regime, and cheap on NVLink.

Megatron-Core's Parallel Folding resolves the attention-vs-expert TP conflict cleanly: it gives attention layers and MoE layers different TP/DP/EP meshes via a dual DeviceMesh — e.g. TP=8 with high CP on attention, TP=1 with high EP on experts — and does a zero-copy reshard at the boundary (Megatron-Core MoE). This is the production answer to "attention wants TP, experts want EP." MaxText expresses the same intent more coarsely through its separate tensor and expert logical axes plus the CP-as-EP custom_mesh_and_rule path (more in §07).

04Pipeline schedule: DualPipe vs zero-bubble vs 1F1B

With PP=16–32 stages on a 10T model, the bubble is real money. The schedules, ranked by sophistication:

ScheduleBubbleWeight copiesActivation stagesComm overlap
1F1B(PP−1)(F+B)1×PPnone
ZB-H1 (zero-bubble)~⅓ of 1F1B at equal peak mem1×PPpartial
ZB-H2≈0 (true zero bubble)1×higherpartial
DualPipe(PP/2−1)(F&B+B−3W)2×PP+1native
DualPipeVsame as DualPipe2×PP/2native
 Bubble, weight-duplication, activation-stage and overlap trade across the pipeline schedule zoo.

Zero-bubble (ZB paper) splits the backward into input-gradient (∂x) and weight-gradient (∂W) passes. ZB-H1 cuts the 1F1B bubble to ~1/3 at the same peak memory; ZB-H2 reaches a true zero bubble but demands more activation memory and enough microbatches to fill the schedule. Its virtue is no weight duplication; its weakness is that overlapping the EP all-to-all under compute is harder because the schedule wasn't designed around it.

DualPipe (DeepSeek DualPipe) is the V3 recipe: each forward and backward chunk is decomposed into four phases — attention, all-to-all dispatch, MLP, all-to-all combine — and a bidirectional schedule overlaps the all-to-all of one micro-batch with the GEMMs of another (V3 report). Bubble drops to (PP/2−1)(F&B+B−3W). The cost is 2× resident parameter copies (the bidirectional schedule), which at 10T is +20 TB resident — non-trivial but absorbable against 19 PB aggregate HBM, and DeepSeek argues it is cheap precisely because EP already shards experts. The communication side reserves 20 SMs across 10 warp-specialized channels with custom PTX (V3 report) — a tax that DeepEP-V2 and the V4 mega-kernel are explicitly trying to eliminate.

DualPipeV (DualPipe repo) is the under-discussed memory-saving variant: the "cut-in-half" V-shape keeps the same bubble with PP/2 devices and PP/2 activation stages (versus PP+1 for DualPipe), halving per-device activation memory. For a GB200 build where the EP all-to-all crosses NVL72 boundaries, DualPipe-family overlap is worth the weight duplication because it hides the InfiniBand and cross-rack traffic — DualPipeV is the practical choice.

Two adjuncts to hold in reserve

PipeOffload (arXiv 2503.01328) offloads activations to host memory, making deep PP at large microbatch counts memory-feasible without DualPipe's weight duplication — a real alternative if the +20 TB resident is unwelcome. And vocabulary parallelism (arXiv 2411.05288) addresses the timing imbalance the large embedding/output head injects into PP stages — material for a 10T model where the vocab projection (DV, counted once) is large enough to unbalance a zero-bubble or DualPipe schedule unless explicitly sharded.

05The fabric hierarchy and the composition rule

Treat the fabric as a strict three-tier hierarchy and assign each collective to exactly one tier:

TierBandwidthSpansCarry here
NVLink 5 (intra-NVL72)1.8 TB/s/GPU bidir, 130 TB/s rack bisection72 GPUsTP, EP all-to-all, SP-TP AG/RS
InfiniBand leaf/spine (intra-island)~90 GB/s/GPU RDMA (~800 Gb/s scale-out)racks in an islandPP stage-boundary activations
Top-of-fabric (inter-island)slowest tierislandsDP/FSDP gradient all-reduce (batch-amortized)
 One collective per fabric tier — the assignment that makes or breaks MFU.

The ~20:1 NVLink:IB cliff (DeepEP; NVIDIA GB200) is more extreme than the 4:1 cliff DeepSeek navigated on H800 (DeepSeek-V3 hardware retrospective), so the H800 lessons sharpen rather than soften: node-limited routing (cap fan-out), IB-then-NVLink forwarding to dedupe inter-node traffic, and overlapping IB with NVLink. The Scaling Book GPU chapter is blunt about the failure mode: cross-node all-to-all degrades to ~50 GB/s when it spans 2+ nodes (Scaling Book GPU chapter) — the single biggest GB200 MoE risk.

The composition rule that falls out:

EP × TP ≤ 72 (NVLink-resident). PP across racks within an island (InfiniBand). DP/FSDP across islands (top-of-fabric). CP folded into the attention mesh only.

Every O(activations·model) collective stays on NVLink; InfiniBand carries only O(activations) PP sends and batch-amortized DP all-reduce. This is non-negotiable — it is the difference between hiding the all-to-all and watching it serialize at 50 GB/s.

The single biggest way to lose half your MFU

Cross-node all-to-all degrades to ~50 GB/s once it spans 2+ nodes. Put TP, SP, or EP all-to-all over InfiniBand and you do not lose a few points — you serialize the dominant collective at one-twentieth of NVLink and watch your MFU collapse from 40% toward 18%.

06Proposed device layout for 100,000 GB200

100,000 GB200 ≈ 1,389 NVL72 racks. The concrete assignment:

Inside one NVL72 (72 GPUs): EP × TP

Use TP=8 for attention/MLA/shared-expert/router (the dense, model-dim-sharded parts), and EP up to 64 spanning the rest of the rack for the routed experts — both ride 1.8 TB/s NVLink, so the expert all-to-all and the attention AG/RS never touch InfiniBand. This mirrors DeepSeek's "256 experts over 8 H800 nodes, EP=64" (V3 report) but collapses it into a single rack, because the NVL72's 72-GPU NVLink domain is what 8 H800 nodes used to be — node egress jumps from ~400 GB/s (H100 8-GPU node) to ~3,600 GB/s (Scaling Book GPU chapter). For 10T/200B-active at ~6–8 experts/token, group experts so routing is rack-limited (the GB200 analog of DeepSeek's node-limited M=4 routing, where each token reaches ≤4 nodes chosen by per-group affinity sums (V3 report, hardware retrospective)): cap each token to ≤2–4 racks, send once over RDMA then fan out over NVLink to dedupe IB traffic. Keep an entire expert group inside one NVL72 domain so the RDMA leg carries only cross-group traffic.

The one architectural lever

The NVL72's 72-GPU flat NVLink domain is what 8 H800 nodes used to be — node egress jumps from ~400 GB/s to ~3,600 GB/s. Keep EP×TP ≤ 72 so the entire expert all-to-all and attention AG/RS ride NVLink and never touch InfiniBand. Everything else in the layout is downstream of this single decision.

A subtlety: TP=8 and EP=64 do not multiply to 72 on the same 72 GPUs — they are different meshes over the same rack (Parallel-Folding style). Attention runs TP=8 over 8-GPU NVLink subgroups with the experts collapsed; the MoE layer runs EP=64 with TP=1, resharding at the boundary. In MaxText's coarser model you instead set ici_tensor_parallelism=8 and ici_expert_parallelism=8 so ICI EP×TP = 64 ≤ 72, letting ici_fsdp_parallelism=-1 auto-fill the remainder of the rack (see §07).

Across racks within an island: PP

Put PP=16–32 stages across racks connected by one InfiniBand leaf/spine island. PP moves only stage-boundary activations over IB — the cheapest cross-rack traffic. Run DualPipeV so the EP all-to-all and PP sends overlap with compute; bubble ≈ (PP/2−1)(F&B+B−3W). With 61 decoder layers (DeepSeek-V3 scale) or more for a 10T model, PP=16–32 gives 2–4 layers/stage — comfortable for circular/interleaved scheduling.

Across islands: DP/FSDP outer + ZeRO-1

With EP·TP filling ~72 GPUs/rack and PP=16–32 racks/replica, one replica ≈ 1,150–2,300 GPUs; 100k GPUs → ~43–87 DP replicas. Shard optimizer state (ZeRO-1) across DP so each replica holds 1/N of the 120 TB Muon state (~1.4–2.8 TB/replica, trivially distributed within the replica's GPUs). DP all-reduce of gradients is the only full-model collective crossing the top of fabric, and it is batch-amortized — keep per-replica token batch above the DCN arithmetic-intensity threshold (the GB200 analog of ~73k tokens/replica (Scaling Book training)) to stay compute-bound. With ~43 replicas and a ~60M-token global batch you get ~1.4M tokens/replica — comfortably DP-safe.

CP for long context

Engage CP=2–8 only at the 64K→1M sequence stages, folded onto the attention mesh (Parallel-Folding style) so the MoE EP layout is untouched. MLA/DSA's compressed KV keeps CP all-gather volume low — and DSA's FP8 lightning indexer selecting top-2048 KV tokens per query reduces attention from O(L²) to O(Lk) (DeepSeek-V3.2 / DSA), so the CP traffic is on a compressed, sparse KV. This mirrors Llama-3 405B's 4D layout (TP=8, CP=16, PP=16, DP=4 on 16,384 H100 at 128K context (Llama-3)), but with EP added as the load-bearing fifth axis and the rack as the new "node."

07Mapping to MaxText ici_/dcn_ axes and pipeline.py

The user's actual codebase (MaxText) expresses this directly. Its logical mesh, from src/MaxText/configs/base.yml, is:

yaml — MaxText logical mesh axes (configs/base.yml)
mesh_axes: ['diloco', 'data', 'stage', 'fsdp', 'fsdp_transpose',
            'context', 'context_autoregressive', 'tensor',
            'tensor_transpose', 'tensor_sequence', 'expert', 'autoregressive']

with the hard ordering rule that DCN axes must appear earlier in the list than ICI axes, and the product of all ici_* degrees equals chips-per-slice (MaxText base.yml). On a GB200 build the mapping is ICI → NVLink rack domain, DCN → InfiniBand. The literal config:

src/MaxText/configs/base.ymlici_/dcn_ parallelismGB200 layout

ici_fsdp_parallelism: -1         # auto-fill the rack remainder          [3]
ici_pipeline_parallelism: 1

dcn_pipeline_parallelism: 16     # PP across racks (the 'stage' axis)    [4]
dcn_data_parallelism: -1         # auto-shard DP across islands
dcn_expert_parallelism: 1        # never put EP all-to-all over IB       [5]
dcn_tensor_parallelism: 1        # 'never recommended' in base.yml
dcn_sequence_parallelism: 1      # 'never recommended'
dcn_tensor_sequence_parallelism: 1

1TP on NVLink. Tensor parallelism shards attention/MLA/router/shared-expert along the model dim — kept inside the rack so AG/RS never hits IB.

2EP×TP ≤ 72. ICI EP×TP = 8×8 = 64, comfortably under the 72-GPU NVL72 domain — the composition rule in literal form.

3Auto-fill. ici_fsdp_parallelism: -1 auto-shards the recommended ICI axis over the remainder of the rack.

4PP on the slow tier. The stage axis carries only stage-boundary activations over IB — the cheapest cross-rack traffic.

5Never EP/TP over DCN. The explicit # never recommended comments encode the ~50 GB/s cross-node all-to-all cliff.

This is the literal expression of "EP×TP on ICI, PP×DP on DCN." The base.yml defaults confirm the philosophy: ici_fsdp_parallelism: -1 (auto-shard the recommended ICI axis), dcn_data_parallelism: -1 (auto-shard the recommended DCN axis), and the explicit # never recommended comments on dcn_tensor_parallelism, dcn_sequence_parallelism, and dcn_tensor_sequence_parallelism (MaxText base.yml) — TP/SP over DCN is fatal precisely because of the ~50 GB/s cross-node all-to-all cliff. Note that EP is named via the expert axis (and TP via tensor, SP via tensor_sequence); the stage axis is pipeline. Because EP sits late in the mesh-axis list and PP (stage) sits early, the ordering rule naturally places PP on DCN and EP on ICI when you set the degrees as above.

Context-as-expert

MaxText exposes a CP_AS_EP path via custom_mesh_and_rule (utils/sharding.py) that folds context parallelism onto the expert mesh — useful when you want long-context CP to reuse the EP all-to-all machinery rather than carve out separate devices. For the long-context phase this is how you raise effective CP without disturbing the routed-expert layout.

Pipeline

src/MaxText/layers/pipeline.py implements a circular pipeline. The number of decoder layers equals num_stages × num_layers_per_pipeline_stage × num_pipeline_repeats. The relevant knobs from base.yml:

  • num_layers_per_pipeline_stage: 1 (default) — more layers/stage means easier comm overlap but fewer repeats;
  • num_pipeline_repeats: -1 — auto-set to num_decoder_layers / (num_pipeline_stages × num_layers_per_pipeline_stage);
  • num_pipeline_microbatches: -1 — auto-set to the number of stages; must be a multiple of the stage count; more microbatches → smaller bubble;
  • pipeline_delay_activation_forwarding: false — when true, delays activation forwarding one loop iteration to simplify XLA's overlap of the circular (last-stage→first-stage) transfer, doing it asynchronously.
data bubble frac = (num_stages − 1) / (num_repeats · num_microbatches + num_stages − 1) The circular-schedule arithmetic MaxText documents — the reason to push microbatch count up. Same family as 1F1B, not DualPipeV.

The bubble fraction MaxText documents is (num_stages − 1) / (num_pipeline_repeats × num_pipeline_microbatches + num_stages − 1) (MaxText base.yml) — the same circular-schedule arithmetic that motivates pushing microbatch count up. MaxText's circular pipeline is a 1F1B-family schedule, not DualPipeV; if you want native all-to-all/PP comm-compute overlap you either lean on pipeline_delay_activation_forwarding plus XLA's overlap, or you port a DualPipe-style bidirectional schedule. For the GB200 target, the pragmatic path inside MaxText is: maximize num_pipeline_microbatches, enable delayed activation forwarding, and rely on the EP all-to-all (jax.lax.ragged_all_to_all) overlapping with grouped-GEMM at the kernel level rather than at the schedule level.

Where the MoE sharding actually happens

MaxText's sparse_matmul path in layers/moe.py is the EP-sharded, dropless realization. EP cross-device routing uses jax.lax.ragged_all_to_all with offsets/sizes from get_all_to_all_params; the grouped GEMM dispatches across jax.lax.ragged_dot, the forked megablox mblx.gmm, or tokamax.ragged_dot. The dropless ragged buffer is sized at min(ep_degree, global_experts/top_k) × balanced_size for worst-case no-drop — never worse than 256/8 = 32× for a DeepSeek-style config, and once EP > 32 the bound is global_experts/top_k independent of EP (MaxText moe.py). The base.yml defaults are megablox: true, sparse_matmul: true, capacity_factor: -1.0 (dropless), ragged_buffer_factor: -1.0 (worst-case dropless buffer) (base.yml).

08Communication kernels and the FP8 dispatch path

The all-to-all is make-or-break, and the kernel substrate is where the layout's promises get cashed. DeepEP gives the measured numbers on Blackwell (SM100): NVLink dispatch/combine of 726/740 GB/s at EP8 (max-perf, 64 SMs) and 643/675 GB/s at the minimal-SM config; SM90/CX7 RDMA is 90/81 GB/s dispatch/combine (DeepEP). The ~20:1 NVLink:RDMA ratio in these numbers is exactly why EP must stay on NVLink. DeepEP supports FP8 dispatch with BF16 combine, halving the dispatch bytes, and the V2 rewrite drops SM consumption from 24 to 4–6 SMs while unifying the high-throughput and low-latency kernels behind a single ElasticBuffer and an NCCL "Gin" backend (DeepEP). Track the NVIDIA-authored hybrid-ep branch for GB200 — it uses TMA copies and warp-level pipelining to minimize SM occupancy while saturating both NVLink and RDMA (verify SM100 support before committing).

The endgame is the DeepGEMM MegaMoE fused kernel that V4 introduced: it fuses EP-dispatch → first FP8×FP4 expert GEMM → SwiGLU(+requant) → second GEMM → EP-combine into a single symmetric-memory kernel, overlapping NVLink transfers with tensor-core MMA via expert "waves," for 1.50–1.73× general / up to 1.96× latency-sensitive speedup over the non-fused baseline (DeepSeek-V4). In JAX/MaxText, the equivalent stack is sparse_matmul + ragged_all_to_all + tokamax/megablox ragged_dot.

Two systems landmines at this scale

DeepEP's low-latency RDMA path floors at ~39 GB/s once you exceed ~256 experts (each token fans out to more remote domains), and DeepEP issue #427 reports low-latency dispatch can corrupt under heavily imbalanced expert load — precisely the early-training regime before the auxiliary-loss-free bias balancer (V3's ±γ=0.001 schedule, MaxText's routed_bias_update_rate) has converged (DeepEP; V3 report). 09Reliability forces DP to be the outer, elastic axis

The last constraint is not a roofline, it is the MTTF. At ~131k GPUs a training job's projected MTTF is ~14 minutes; SDC affects ~1/1000 machines with corruption events every 1–2 weeks; at 100k accelerators the checkpoint cadence approaches ~967/day (~every 1.5 min) (reliability analysis). GB200 shifts the unit of repair from server to rack. This is the systems reason DP/FSDP must be the outer axis: it is the only axis you can elastically resize — drop a failed replica, reform the mesh — without re-laying-out the EP/TP/PP sharded unit. The proposed layout deliberately confines a failure's blast radius to one PP-stage rack inside one DP replica.

10The opinionated bottom line

For 10T/200B on 100,000 GB200:

  1. Muon as primary optimizer (AdamW only on embeddings/heads/norms) to hold the resident state at 120 TB rather than 160 TB — the optimizer state is the binding memory term, not the weights.
  2. EP is the load-bearing axis. Shard the 10T weights with expert-parallelism, not FSDP. Keep EP×TP ≤ 72, entirely on NVLink.
  3. TP=8 for attention, EP up to 64 for experts, as separate Parallel-Folding meshes over the same rack; expert-shard the FFN (its F≈2,048 is too small to TP-split).
  4. PP=16–32 across racks on InfiniBand, scheduled with DualPipeV (or MaxText's circular pipeline with maximized microbatches + delayed activation forwarding) to overlap the all-to-all under compute.
  5. DP ~43–87 replicas across islands with ZeRO-1, as the elastic, fault-isolated outer axis — the only one you resize on failure.
  6. CP=2–8 folded onto attention only at the long-context (64K→1M) stages, riding MLA/DSA's compressed sparse KV.
  7. Never put TP, SP, or EP all-to-all over InfiniBand. The ~50 GB/s cross-node all-to-all cliff is the single biggest way to lose half your MFU.

In MaxText terms: ici_tensor_parallelism=8, ici_expert_parallelism=8, ici_fsdp_parallelism=-1, dcn_pipeline_parallelism=16-32, dcn_data_parallelism=-1, everything else dcn_*=1. That single config block is the entire layout, and the rest of this manual is the justification for why each number is what it is.
