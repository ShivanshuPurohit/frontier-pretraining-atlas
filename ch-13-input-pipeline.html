The reader who has watched training runs stall on things nobody warned them about will ask this chapter’s question before any other: is the dataloader ever actually the bottleneck at 100,000 GPUs, and if not, why does every practitioner’s gut still flinch at the phrase “data pipeline”? The honest answer, worked from first principles and from the loader-by-loader evidence below, is that raw token throughput was never in danger — the physics make that case overwhelmingly — but three adjacent failure modes (cold-start index construction, multi-source composition imbalance, and the absence of load-time metadata-conditional sampling) are real, documented, and specific enough to budget engineering time against.

The bandwidth math nobody needs to do, but should

Start with the ceiling. A 200B-active-parameter MoE at sequence length 8k, running on a 100,000-GPU cluster, is compute-bound by the model’s own forward/backward FLOPs long before it is I/O-bound by token ingestion. Cross-checked two independent ways — a FLOPs-budget derivation from public GB200/B200 FP8 dense FLOPS figures, and a scaling argument from DeepSeek-V3’s disclosed 14.8T tokens over 2.788M H800-GPU-hours — the compute ceiling caps aggregate ingestion at roughly 75–76M tokens/s cluster-wide [derived from public GB200/B200 FP8 FLOPS and DeepSeek-V3 throughput scaling] [derived, 2026]. At 4 bytes/token — the mandatory dtype for any modern >64k-vocabulary tokenizer, since Megatron-Core’s GPTDatasetConfig.__post_init__ sets token_dtype_code = 4 if vocab_size > 65535 else 8, meaning every 2026-era frontier tokenizer (100k–256k vocab) is always in the int32/4-bytes-per-token regime on disk github.com/NVIDIA/Megatron-LM [CODE, main branch, Jul 2026] — that ceiling implies an aggregate raw byte-ingestion rate of only ~300 MB/s across the entire cluster.

Three to four orders of magnitude below what a single NVMe drive delivers, let alone a parallel filesystem: that is the entire finding, stated as starkly as the notes state it. A single PCIe Gen5 NVMe drive sustains on the order of 14 GB/s direct to GPU memory over GPUDirect Storage [derived from GPUDirect Storage figures, notes corpus] [derived, 2026]; any shared parallel filesystem worth deploying at 100k-GPU scale — 3FS, Tectonic, DDN EXAScaler — is provisioned for multi-TB/s aggregate. Feeding 300 MB/s of pre-tokenized bytes to 100,000 GPUs is not a storage-engineering problem; it is closer to a rounding error against the provisioning headroom any of these systems already carries for checkpoint traffic. Databricks’ own engineers, writing about MosaicML Streaming, make exactly this point independently: benchmark comparisons against WebDataset and HF datasets were run on ImageNet+ResNet-50 because “for larger model training like LLMs…, this effect disappears as the models are not dataloader bottlenecked” Databricks: MosaicML StreamingDataset [OFFICIAL-BLOG, 2023-2026] — a vendor conceding the thing that would otherwise be its strongest performance-marketing angle.

It is worth being precise about what this bandwidth math does not cover, because the reader’s intuition that “data at this scale must be a TB/s problem” is not wrong — it is pointed at the wrong subsystem. Checkpoint I/O for a 10T-parameter model’s optimizer-plus-weight state is genuinely TB/s-class: at the standard 14 bytes/parameter convention, the full state is ~140TB, and a naive <60s synchronous save implies ~2.3TB/s sustained write VAST Data checkpoint-bandwidth survey [OFFICIAL-BLOG, vendor, 2026]. That belongs to Ch19 — production systems dodge it via local-NVMe-first, async-drain architectures, and VAST’s own 40-run survey measures actual global checkpoint bandwidth at just 50–200 GB/s. Dataloading and checkpoint-output are architecturally distinct problems that happen to share the word “storage”; conflating them is the most common category error in how this question gets asked. This chapter is about the former.

Where dataloading genuinely can bite

Given that raw bandwidth is not the risk, the notes converge on exactly three places where the input pipeline can stall a 100k-GPU job, and all three are about orchestration and compute overhead, not bytes moved.

Cold start. Megatron-Core’s own documentation is unusually candid: for “large blends or many file prefixes,” building the document/sample/shuffle indices “can take several minutes and leave all GPUs idle while rank 0 performs CPU-only work,” and DataLoader initialization broadly “can take several minutes” because it “involves opening and memory-mapping multiple files and can significantly stress the filesystem” github.com/NVIDIA/Megatron-LM, readme.md §“Fast DataLoader initialization” [CODE, 2026]. For a mixture with hundreds to thousands of source-file prefixes — exactly the shape of a 10T-run’s curated blend — that is minutes of 100,000 idle GPUs, priced at whatever the cluster’s hourly burn rate is. Three purpose-built mitigations exist: --dataloader-fast-cache-load (skip sync points, assuming the cache exists), --dataloader-defer-npy-index-mmap (lazy-mmap indices behind worker prefetch), and --per-dataset-sequences-path (one consolidated JSON of per-prefix metadata instead of one open() per prefix). tools/prepare_cache.py builds the index entirely offline, off the critical path of GPU-hour-billed job startup [same source, CODE]. This is standard “precompute once, cache, never pay for it live” discipline, but it must be deliberately wired in — a naive deployment at 10T-run blend complexity will hit this wall by default.

Multi-source workload imbalance. The more interesting and less obvious risk, quantified directly in ByteDance’s MegaScale-Data paper. Because attention FLOPs scale with sequence length squared, non-uniform per-rank sample-length and sample-count distributions from multi-source data create real compute imbalance even when sample counts are balanced across data-parallel ranks. In an 8-GPU VLM trial, MegaScale-Data measured max-vs-min microbatch FLOPs at 3.2× (image) and 6.9× (full sequence) purely from dataloader composition arXiv:2504.09844, “MegaScale-Data: Scaling Dataloader for Multisource Large Foundation Model Training” [PAPER, EuroSys 2026]. That imbalance is invisible to a naive “samples/sec” metric — it shows up only as pipeline-stage idle-waiting. For a 10T MoE with heterogeneous per-source document-length distributions sharded across expert- and pipeline-parallel groups, an unlucky microbatch composition is a genuine, dataloader-induced bubble, architecturally distinct from — and additive to — the EP and pipeline bubbles covered in Ch16.

On-the-fly filtering, tokenizing, and metadata sampling. The notes are unanimous: no frontier-scale system does any of these three things live, in the training hot loop. Offline pre-tokenization to a packed, fixed-width or bin-packed token format is the unanimous 2026 default — Megatron-Core, OLMo-core, MosaicML Streaming, Grain/ArrayRecord all assume pre-tokenized arrays as the on-disk format; on-the-fly tokenization shows up only in smaller-scale or fine-tuning pipelines, never in a frontier training-time hot loop. The same is true of quality filtering and metadata-conditional sampling — both happen once, upstream, at curation time, producing fixed per-category shards the dataloader consumes as opaque, already-decided inputs (Ch11’s territory for the curation side). The risk this chapter flags is not that these operations happen live — they don’t, by design — but that a team tempted to add live filtering or metadata-conditioned resampling for adaptive curricula is reaching for something no OSS loader currently does natively, and would be building genuinely new infrastructure.

Loader-by-loader against the requirements matrix

The 10T/200B run’s dataloading requirements reduce to six properties: bit-exact resume under a fixed world size, bit-exact (or at least correctness-preserving) resume across a changed world size, mixture hot-swap without a full rebuild, per-example metadata-conditional sampling, native varlen/packing metadata emission, and object-storage-native operation for a cold/archival data tier. No single OSS system satisfies all six.

The picture is consistent: Megatron-Core wins on varlen-metadata-nativity and index determinism but does not solve world-size-elastic resume or mixture hot-swap; MosaicML Streaming wins on exactly the two properties Megatron-Core lacks but says nothing about native packing metadata; nobody solves per-example metadata-conditional sampling; and MegaScale-Data — the system solving the largest number of confirmed properties simultaneously (bit-exact resume, elastic resharding, and Planner-driven mixture hot-swap) — is a bespoke, unreleased ByteDance system, not something to pip install. Its published description does not establish whether it emits native packing metadata or supports object storage, so those two cells are left undocumented rather than assumed.

Megatron-Core GPTDataset / BlendedMegatronDatasetBuilder. The storage primitive is IndexedDataset: a .bin file of raw token bytes plus a .idx file of sequence lengths, byte offsets, per-document sequence-index ranges, and (for multimodal) a per-sequence mode tag github.com/NVIDIA/Megatron-LM [CODE, 2026]. Determinism comes from three cached index arrays built once at construction: a shuffled document index (Do_idx), a sample index mapping sample number to document position and token offset (Sa_idx), and an independent shuffle permutation on top (Sh_idx). Querying sample k is j = Sh_idx[k] → look up (i, offset) in Sa_idx → concatenate token ranges from Do_idx[i:i_next+1]. This three-level indirection gives bit-exact reproducibility under restart at fixed world size — the whole epoch’s sample order is a pure deterministic function of (seed, indexed_indices, N, S), cached once and memory-mapped by every rank [same source, CODE]. It does not, by itself, give MosaicML-style guaranteed resume across a changed world size: the global sample order is world-size-independent by construction, so re-sharding is safe, but exact mid-epoch resume requires the training loop to correctly recompute --consumed-samples under the new sharding — a training-script responsibility, not a dataset-class guarantee.

BlendedDataset draws from weighted sources via a deterministic error-feedback scheme — greedily assigning the next sample to whichever source is furthest under its target weight, not i.i.d. sampling — and bakes those weights into cached Da_idx/Sa_idx arrays at construction time. There is no code path to hot-swap blend weights mid-run without rebuilding BlendedDataset, and therefore its cache, from a new config. Multi-stage curricula in Megatron-Core are implemented as separate job phases sharing model/optimizer checkpoints, each phase getting a fresh, fixed blend, not a live re-weighting inside one continuous process github.com/NVIDIA/Megatron-LM, blended_dataset.py [CODE, 2026] — which directly shapes how Ch12’s staged curriculum has to be implemented: discrete checkpointed phase transitions, not smooth retargeting.

Two 2025/2026-era additions are easy to miss for a reader whose mental model of Megatron-LM is frozen at the 2021 paper. object_storage_utils.py implements direct S3 (s3://) and Multi-Storage-Client (msc://) paths for .bin/.idx files with a tunable bin_chunk_nbytes and a local index-cache path — Megatron-Core can now stream pre-tokenized shards from object storage rather than requiring everything pre-staged on POSIX, relevant to a 10T-run’s cold/archival tier. And GPTDatasetConfig.inter_document_masking, when true, computes cu_seqlens via numpy.cumsum inside __getitem__ and returns it directly in the batch dict — the dataloader itself, not a separate preprocessing pass, produces the varlen metadata consumed downstream by FlashAttention-varlen and TransformerEngine kernels [same source, gpt_dataset.py lines 80–332, CODE]. A small, instructive historical war story: GitHub issue #907, a race condition where GPTDataset._build_document_sample_shuffle_indices failed to build indices on non-root nodes absent NFS — a concrete example of the “shared state assumed but not enforced across ranks” bug class that recurs through Megatron-Core’s history github.com/NVIDIA/Megatron-LM/issues/907 [CODE/GitHub issue].

Megatron-Energon. NVIDIA’s dedicated multimodal loader — standardized WebDataset-based on-disk format plus metadata, optimized for multi-rank reads and mixing — with a savable/restorable state via get_savable_loader(), checkpointing shard position, sample-within-shard, and worker RNG state for deterministic resume after preemption github.com/NVIDIA/Megatron-Energon [OFFICIAL-DOCS/CODE, 2026]. It is used only in Megatron-LM’s multimodal/mimo example paths, not the core GPT pretraining path — for a text-centric run GPTDataset remains the relevant stack unless a genuine multimodal component is present (Ch9). Its state-save design is architecturally closer to Streaming and Grain below than to Megatron-Core’s index-precompute-and-cache approach.

MosaicML StreamingDataset. Databricks’ writeup makes an unusually strong claim: elastic determinism — sample order identical regardless of GPU/node/worker count, demonstrated at 1–64 GPUs × 8 workers with bit-identical loss curves — achieved by shuffling across all samples assigned to a machine, decoupling shuffle granularity from parallelism topology Databricks: MosaicML StreamingDataset [OFFICIAL-BLOG, 2023-2026]. “Begin on 8 GPUs, resume on 32, then 128” with matching loss curves is the one requirement-matrix item Megatron-Core does not solve as cleanly. Instant mid-epoch resume follows the same design — sample order is a deterministic function of small state rather than a replayable stream position. Sharded, disjoint downloads mean no machine re-downloads another’s shard — directly avoiding the N-way redundant-read cost MegaScale-Data measured below. Storage format is MDS (Mosaic Data Shard). The team states it evaluated TorchData and WebDataset first and built Streaming in-house because neither gave the “correctness guarantees, performance, and ease of use” needed — the same source supplying this chapter’s opening concession that LLM training is not dataloader-bottlenecked.

Grain (JAX) + ArrayRecord. Google’s JAX-first loader, built on ArrayRecord, layered on the Riegeli file format: indexed chunks with a metadata index at file-end enabling true random access (unlike TFRecord’s sequential-only reads), so global shuffle can shuffle indices rather than approximate with a windowed buffer developers.googleblog.com, “Building High-Performance Data Pipelines with Grain and ArrayRecord” [OFFICIAL-BLOG]. Checkpointing is get_state()/set_state() on the iterator; MaxText and other 2025/2026 JAX stacks use it for the determinism-plus-recovery combination maxtext.readthedocs.io [OFFICIAL-DOCS]. No public throughput numbers were surfaced anywhere in Google’s materials — a real documentation gap next to Mosaic’s marketing-forward disclosures.

torchdata.nodes. PyTorch’s torch.data was refocused in 2025 away from the abandoned DataPipes/DataLoaderV2 line toward StatefulDataLoader (drop-in, adds state_dict()/load_state_dict()) and torchdata.nodes, a composable-iterator DAG wrapped by a stateful Loader meta-pytorch.org/data [OFFICIAL-DOCS, 2026]. The migration guide claims nodes “performs at least as well” as the prior stack, but no quantitative benchmarks are published — a real gap for deciding whether to build on it versus reusing Megatron-Core’s stack directly.

tf.data service, Ray Data, HF streaming, DALI — mostly not the frontier-text answer. tf.data service’s disaggregation result — 31.7× throughput, 26.2× cost reduction for input-bound jobs arXiv:2210.14826, SoCC 2023 [PAPER] — is a striking anchor for “how bad naive co-located dataloading can get,” but a TensorFlow-era, vision/recsys result with no evidence of use in any 2025/2026 frontier text-LLM stack. Ray Data has confirmed production use for batch inference and RL/agentic pipelines, but MegaScale-Data explicitly benchmarks against it as a baseline it beats by up to 3.63×. HF datasets streaming solves a different problem (avoiding local materialization of data you don’t control) than the 10T run’s problem (feeding 100k GPUs from data whose layout you already control). DALI’s reader library is overwhelmingly vision/audio-centric and essentially irrelevant here.

MegaScale-Data — the closest thing to a purpose-built answer, and nobody’s open-sourced it. ByteDance’s MegaScale-Data (EuroSys 2026) is the single most directly relevant production dataloader paper in the corpus: scaling the dataloader for multisource large-foundation-model training, tested to 4,096 GPUs, benchmarked against PyTorch DataLoader, tf.data, Cachew, Ray Data, and Pecan arXiv:2504.09844 [PAPER, 2026]. Its actor-model architecture: Source Loaders hold per-source file-access state as one shared instance rather than one replicated per rank, directly fixing a redundancy that consumed over 70% of preprocessing pipeline memory in ByteDance’s own production systems before the fix; Data Constructors aggregate into batches, sharing data across CP/PP ranks to eliminate redundant copies; a Planner auto-scales based on mixing-ratio changes via offline source-cost clustering plus online moving-average-threshold-triggered rescaling. Two declarative abstractions — DGraph (dataflow graph, sample-lifecycle tracking) and ClientPlaceTree (device-mesh-aware placement) — expose mix(), distribute(), cost(), balance(), broadcast_at(). Results: 4.5× end-to-end throughput and 13.5× CPU memory reduction on multimodal VLM training, up to 3.63× iteration-time speedup versus the best baseline, context-length-dependent gains of 1.71× at 4k and 3.09× at 16k. Fault tolerance is shadow-loader hot failover with differential checkpointing; elastic resharding supports dynamic parallelism changes mid-run. This is the field’s closest answer to “what a dataloader for a 10T-MoE-scale run should look like” — and it is a bespoke, unreleased system, not something to pip install.

Sequence packing: a solved problem, and what remains open

The packing-efficiency conversation in most secondary literature treats padding waste as a live tradeoff to tune. The primary-source numbers say it is essentially a closed problem at any reasonable context length. Best-fit Packing’s two-stage algorithm — segment documents longer than context length L into ≤L chunks, then solve the resulting bin-packing problem via Best-Fit-Decreasing — achieves 0.0024% additional sequences at 2k context and 0.00063% at 8k versus pure concatenation on RefinedWeb (0.0028% on Stack/code at 2k), meaning BFD packing is compute-equivalent to naive concatenation in wasted tokens while eliminating essentially all mid-document truncation arXiv:2404.10830, “Fewer Truncations Improve Language Modeling” [PAPER, ACL 2024]. The algorithmic trick making this tractable at billions-of-documents scale: naive BFD is O(N log N) sort plus O(N log N) pack, but since L ≪ N, the sort reduces to O(N) via counting sort, and tracking bins with an O(L)-leaf tree structure gets the whole pass to O(N log L). OLMo-core’s Optimized-BFD implements this in production, applied per-source-file by default with an optional source_group_size to pack across small source files jointly, reporting a 60% runtime reduction at 1B-document scale versus unoptimized BFD olmo-core.readthedocs.io, NumpyPackedFSLDataset [OFFICIAL-DOCS, 2025/2026]. NeMo offers both first-fit-decreasing (most-optimal, risks clustering same-domain sequences non-randomly) and first-fit-shuffle (slightly less optimal, preserves randomness), defaulting to the latter docs.nvidia.com/nemo-framework [OFFICIAL-DOCS, 2024] — reported gains (fine-tuning context, Llama-7B + Dolly, not pretraining-scale) of up to 10× FLOPs efficiency and 6× wall-clock time with no convergence impact.

Downstream, the payoff from eliminating truncation is larger than intuition suggests: Best-fit Packing reports, at 7B–13B scale, 300–500B tokens, 2k–8k context, gains of +4.7% reading comprehension, +9.3% NLI, +16.8% “context following,” +15.0% HumanEval Pass@100, and up to −58.3% closed-domain hallucination [same source, PAPER]. This correctly reframes the packing question: the remaining design decisions are document-boundary masking and packing order for convergence/randomness reasons, not compute waste, which is functionally zero regardless of method.

The kernel chain that makes packing free at training time is, as of Blackwell, genuinely co-designed rather than bolted on. flash_attn_varlen_func takes cu_seqlens_q/cu_seqlens_k directly — the exact tensor Megatron-Core’s dataloader emits — with causal, sliding-window, and document-boundary mask patterns natively supported. FlexAttention generalizes to arbitrary block-mask and expression-based masks for cases the fixed enum doesn’t cover. FlashAttention-4, as of roughly March 2026, ships a Blackwell-native FlexAttention backend with CuTeDSL score/mask-modifier JIT support, and its scheduling specifically targets the load-imbalance that varlen attention creates on the SM scheduler pytorch.org/blog/flexattention-flashattention-4-fast-and-flexible [TALK/BLOG, Mar 2026] — the 2026 kernel stack treats packed/varlen training as the default case, not an afterthought (Ch15 has the full kernel inventory). This is the THD-format story in practice: total-tokens×hidden layout with cumulative-length boundary metadata riding alongside it, from dataloader emission through attention kernel with no materialization step in between.

A related but distinct idea is Apple’s Dataset Decomposition (VSL curriculum): decompose the corpus into same-length-chunk buckets each drawn from a single document — no cross-document contamination even without explicit masking — then run variable-sequence-length, variable-batch-size training with a curriculum across buckets. An 8k-context 1B model trained at the same compute cost as a 2k-context baseline, up to 6× faster to reach target accuracy on long-context benchmarks arXiv:2405.13226, “Dataset Decomposition” [PAPER, NeurIPS 2024]. Where BFD accepts cross-document packing plus masking, Dataset Decomposition avoids masking entirely by construction — a genuine alternative point in the design space, worth keeping in mind for a curriculum that intentionally staggers sequence length across phases (Ch12).

Shuffle quality at 50T tokens

The core finding across the shuffle literature: shuffle-buffer-to-dataset-size ratio, not absolute buffer size, governs quality, and that ratio’s qualitative behavior is largely scale-invariant as the dataset grows. The CorgiPile line of work finds buffer sizes of roughly 2% of dataset size achieve parity with a full shuffle, performance “noticeably dips” below that, and — the more load-bearing number for a 50T-token corpus — viable buffer sizes as low as 0.25% are achievable for datasets in the hundreds-of-terabytes range, given the right offline/online hybrid design: pre-shuffle shard order offline (cheap, once) and do only local buffer shuffling online arXiv:2309.01640, “Corgi²: A Hybrid Offline-Online Approach to Storage-Aware Data Shuffling for SGD” [PAPER, 2023]. A separate, smaller-scale genomics/DNA-LM study found no shuffle buffer at all costs roughly 4% task performance versus a buffered baseline arXiv:2510.12617 [PAPER, 2025] — cited for the qualitative “buffering unambiguously matters” signal, not as a directly transferable magnitude.

For a 50T-token run, the practical two-level pattern used by essentially every system in this chapter — Megatron’s shuffle-index, Streaming’s machine-level shuffle, Grain’s index-shuffle — is shard-order shuffle (coarse, once, cheap) plus within-shard or within-buffer shuffle (fine, per epoch). None do a literal single-pass global shuffle of 50T tokens, because none need to: the two-level scheme is provably close to full-shuffle quality at a small fraction of the I/O cost. Shuffle quality at 10T-run scale is a solved engineering pattern, not an open research question, provided the implementation does the two-level decomposition rather than approximating a single windowed buffer against the full unshuffled stream.

Pre-tokenized vs on-the-fly: the economics, worked

Offline pre-tokenization to Megatron .bin/.idx format, or the MDS/ArrayRecord equivalents, is the unanimous frontier-2026 default; every system reviewed here assumes pre-tokenized, fixed-width or packed token arrays as the on-disk format, and none tokenize on-the-fly from raw text during the training loop. The economics explain why this is not even a close call. Single-thread tokenizer benchmarks put tiktoken at “2-3× faster than HuggingFace Tokenizers on single strings,” with HF’s Rust-backed “fast” tokenizers closing much of the gap in batch mode; HF’s own docs state under 20 seconds to tokenize 1GB of text on a single CPU core, roughly 50 MB/s/core huggingface.co/docs/datasets [OFFICIAL-DOCS, 2025/2026]. At that rate and a rough 4–5 bytes/token English-text ratio, re-tokenizing a full 50T-token corpus (~200–250TB of raw text) costs on the order of 1,000–1,400 core-hours — trivially parallelizable to well under a day on a few thousand cores. Re-tokenization is essentially never the bottleneck for a curation-pipeline pivot, such as switching tokenizer or vocab mid-project; the actual cost driver for a 50T-token retokenization pass is I/O and orchestration, not CPU-bound BPE throughput. This is the same shape of finding as the classifier-cascade labeling economics: running a 0.5B taxonomy labeler over a 100T-token raw pool costs on the order of 0.2–0.4% of the training run’s own FLOPs, corroborated three independent ways — a direct FLOPs computation, Essential-Web’s disclosed 90,000 MI300X-GPU-hours to label 24T tokens scaled linearly to ~375,000 GPU-hours at 100T, and an independent filtering-pipeline study reporting sub-1% overhead arXiv:2506.14111, Essential-Web v1.0 [PAPER, 2025]. Tokenization and labeling are dollar-denominated, low-six-to-seven-figure line items against a run whose training compute runs into nine figures, not FLOPs-denominated engineering risks. One flagged gap: a circulating “51–72M tokens/sec/node” NeMo Curator tokenization figure could not be traced to a primary NVIDIA source and is excluded here as unverifiable — a documentation hole that doesn’t change the conclusion, since the core-hour derivation above already shows roughly three orders of magnitude of headroom.

The metadata-conditional sampling gap

This is the one place in the input-pipeline stack where the notes are unambiguous about an unfilled gap. Essential-Web v1.0 is the reference example of taxonomy-labeled data the reader is likely picturing: 24T tokens, every document annotated with a 12-category taxonomy (topic, format, complexity, quality) via a distilled 0.5B annotator model, explicitly designed so downstream users can apply SQL-style filters to carve out domain-specific sub-corpora — math, code, STEM, medical — at curation time arXiv:2506.14111 [PAPER, 2025]. Checking every loader reviewed here against that use case: none of Megatron-Core’s BlendedDataset, Megatron-Energon, MosaicML StreamingDataset, or Grain expose a native “filter/reweight by per-example metadata label at load or sample time” API. The pattern is identical everywhere — apply the taxonomy or quality filter once, upstream, at curation, producing separate per-category .bin files, MDS shards, or ArrayRecord files, then use the dataloader’s source-level blend-weight mechanism to control the proportion of already-materialized categories. Metadata-conditional sampling exists, but only at the granularity of “which pre-filtered source file or shard,” never “which individual example, filtered by its metadata field, at read time.”

For a 10T run wanting to dynamically reweight by fine-grained per-document taxonomy labels without a full re-materialization pass — the shape of adaptive curriculum Ch12 gestures toward — this is a genuine build-it-yourself gap, not a configuration choice hiding in some loader’s advanced options. The closest existing precedent is MegaScale-Data’s Planner, architecturally the right shape (offline source-cost clustering, online moving-average-threshold-triggered rescaling) but operating on source-level mixing ratios in the published description, not per-example predicates. Whether any frontier lab runs a genuinely online, per-example metadata-conditional resampling system internally could not be confirmed either way in the underlying research — the online-reweighting research line suggests active interest, but nothing public documents an implementation at this granularity. The practical consequence: treat this as a planned engineering deliverable, most plausibly implemented as pre-materializing a modest number of taxonomy-sliced shard sets per anticipated curriculum stage, then hot-swapping source-level blend weights across those pre-cut sets at each stage boundary, rather than assuming an off-the-shelf loader will grow the capability by the time the run starts.

War stories

Four data points calibrate where the input pipeline has actually hurt real production systems, as opposed to where it theoretically could:

  • torchtitan #650 (“[Multimodal] Adding OBELICS DataLoader”): maintainers of PyTorch’s own reference trainer flag that multimodal loading — image download, decode, resize, pad — is CPU-intensive enough to bottleneck, recommending more DataLoader workers as the first mitigation github.com/pytorch/torchtitan/issues/650 [CODE/GitHub issue] — a concrete acknowledgment that dataloader CPU cost is live for anything beyond pure-text streaming, relevant if the run carries a multimodal component (Ch9).
  • MegaScale-Data’s 3.2×/6.9× per-microbatch FLOP imbalance is a rare quantification of dataloader composition creating compute imbalance via attention’s quadratic scaling — usually discussed only qualitatively. Directly load-bearing for a 10T MoE with heterogeneous per-source document-length distributions layered on expert-parallel sharding, where an unlucky microbatch composition could stall a pipeline stage arXiv:2504.09844 [PAPER, 2026].
  • Megatron-Core issue #907, the non-root-node NFS index-race bug, is small and closed but genuinely instructive: an example of “shared filesystem state assumed but not synchronized across ranks,” a bug class that recurs across Megatron-Core’s dataset stack github.com/NVIDIA/Megatron-LM/issues/907 [CODE/GitHub issue].
  • tf.data service’s 31.7×/26.2× disaggregation result arXiv:2210.14826 [PAPER, SoCC 2023] predates the MoE-at-scale era and comes from a non-LLM context, but is a useful anchor for “how bad naive co-located dataloading can get” — one that seems forgotten as the field’s tooling moved to PyTorch/JAX without an equivalent disaggregation benchmark being republished for text-LLM pretraining. Nobody has re-run this experiment for a 2026-era MoE workload.

Reference input-pipeline architecture that can never be the bottleneck

Synthesizing the above into a concrete architecture for a 10T/200B run on 100k GB200s:

  • Offline pre-tokenization, always. Tokenize the full corpus once to a packed fixed-width or bin-packed format (Megatron .bin/.idx, or MDS/ArrayRecord equivalents), with BFD or OBFD packing applied per curriculum stage at build time. The re-tokenization cost math above (~1,000–1,400 core-hours for a full 50T-token pass) makes it cheap enough to re-run per stage boundary or tokenizer revision without hesitation — there is no reason to ever tokenize live.
  • Native varlen metadata emission, not a separate preprocessing pass. cu_seqlens/THD-format boundary metadata should be produced by the dataloader at __getitem__ time — Megatron-Core’s inter_document_masking is the reference implementation — riding directly into FlashAttention-varlen/FlexAttention kernels with zero materialization step in between (Ch15 covers the kernel side in full).
  • Object-storage-backed cold tier, local-NVMe hot path, decoupled entirely from checkpoint I/O. Megatron-Core’s S3/msc:// support and Streaming’s sharded-disjoint-download model both point the same direction: pre-tokenized shards live in cheap object storage, get pulled once per machine, and cache locally. This subsystem shares no hardware or bandwidth budget with the checkpoint-save path in Ch19 — the ~300 MB/s ingestion requirement and the ~50–200 GB/s checkpoint-drain requirement are different orders of magnitude and should be provisioned as separate systems.
  • Cold-start elimination as a first-class deliverable. prepare_cache.py-style offline index construction, --dataloader-fast-cache-load, and consolidated per-dataset-sequences metadata should be mandatory for any blend with more than a handful of source prefixes — which a 10T-run’s curated mixture certainly has. This is the single most avoidable dataloading cost in this chapter, purely because the mitigation is “precompute it once, offline.”
  • A MegaScale-Data-shaped actor decomposition, if building bespoke. At 100,000 GPUs with heterogeneous EP/TP/PP/CP composition (Ch16), Megatron-Core’s per-rank monolithic index-consumption model is not what any validated system at this scale uses in production — the Source-Loader/Data-Constructor/Planner split, sharing per-source file state across ranks rather than replicating it, is the only architecture in this corpus demonstrated at 4,096 GPUs with the imbalance and redundancy problems already measured and fixed. Treat Megatron-Core’s dataset stack as the correctness-and-packing reference implementation to borrow logic from, not necessarily the throughput architecture to deploy unmodified at full scale.
  • Metadata-conditional sampling solved by pre-materialization, deliberately. Given no OSS loader natively supports per-example metadata filtering at load time, pre-cut a modest, curriculum-informed set of taxonomy-sliced shard groups at curation time and drive runtime adaptivity through source-level blend-weight changes across them — coarser than a true per-example predicate, but built on infrastructure that exists today.
  • Dataloader checkpoint state saved independently of model/optimizer state. Grain’s get_state()/set_state(), Energon’s get_savable_loader(), and Streaming’s native resume all point at the same principle: iteration state (shard position, worker RNG, consumed-sample count) is small and cheap to checkpoint, and should be persisted on its own cadence, decoupled from the far larger model-state checkpoint in Ch19.

Implications for the 10T/200B run

None of this chapter’s findings argue for heroic dataloader engineering. The bandwidth math is unambiguous: at ~300 MB/s aggregate raw-token demand against multi-TB/s-class storage systems already justified by checkpoint traffic, the input pipeline was never going to be the throughput bottleneck, and no amount of over-engineering on that axis buys anything. What the run does need, budgeted as real engineering line items: offline index and packing precomputation kept off the critical path of GPU-hour-billed startup; a microbatch-composition-aware scheduler or MegaScale-Data-style source disaggregation to prevent the FLOP-imbalance bubbles a naive multi-source blend will otherwise inject into the EP/PP pipeline; and an explicit build to close the metadata-conditional-sampling gap if Ch12’s curriculum plan depends on runtime-adaptive, fine-grained mixture reweighting rather than discrete, pre-cut stages. The single highest-leverage decision is architectural, not algorithmic: build on Megatron-Core’s dataset stack (mature, varlen-native, but source-blend-static and not world-size-elastic), or invest in a MegaScale-Data-shaped bespoke loader (elastic, imbalance-aware, but unreleased and therefore a from-scratch build). At 100,000 GPUs, with the heterogeneous parallelism composition Ch16 specifies, the latter is the more defensible bet — but it is a genuine systems-engineering project, not a configuration flag, and should be scoped and staffed as one from day one.
