The 100k-GPU machine: GB200 NVL72, Blackwell numerics, and what an exaflop-class cluster actually is

The unit of compute is no longer the GPU, nor the eight-GPU node — it is the rack. For a 10T-total / 200B-active MoE run, almost every architectural decision follows from one fact: the GB200 NVL72 makes a 72-GPU, fully NVLink-coherent domain the atomic building block.

The single most important fact about the machine you are about to commit to is that the unit of compute is no longer the GPU, and no longer the eight-GPU node. It is the rack. The GB200 NVL72 is engineered so that a 72-GPU, fully NVLink-coherent domain is the atomic building block, and almost every architectural decision in your 10T-total / 200B-active MoE run — where experts live, how large the expert-parallel group can be, where the all-to-all traffic spills from copper to optics, how the parameter and optimizer-state footprint shards — follows from that one design choice. This chapter establishes what the hardware actually is (not what the spec sheet markets), what Blackwell's FP4 numerics buy a trillion-scale MoE in 2026, what MFU and MBU you should realistically plan against, how GB200 compares to the credible alternatives (B300, TPU v7, Trainium3, Ascend/CloudMatrix), and what concretely changes versus a Hopper fleet for a run of this size.

GB200 NVL72 is not a faster H100 — it is a different unit of compute: a 72-GPU coherent rack whose NVLink domain and native FP4 reshape the parallelism and numerics of a trillion-scale MoE. Plan EP inside the rack, FP4 in the steady state with a higher-precision tail, and the build around power.

01The rack as the scale-up domain

A GB200 NVL72 packs 72 Blackwell GPUs — 36 GB200 superchips, each pairing two Blackwell dies with one Grace CPU — plus 36 Grace CPUs into a single liquid-cooled rack (NVIDIA). Each "GPU" here is itself a dual-die multi-chip module: two reticle-limited dies on one package, stitched together by a 10 TB/s NV-HBI die-to-die link and presented to software as one coherent device. This matters for your kernel authors — the chip you program against is already an internally-distributed system whose two halves communicate at bandwidth no external fabric can match, which is why CUTLASS/CuteDSL grouped-GEMM kernels for the expert layer should be written to keep an expert's weights resident on-package wherever the sharding allows.

The defining feature of the rack — the number to anchor every parallelism decision on — is NVLink5: 1.8 TB/s bidirectional per GPU (18 links × 100 GB/s each), aggregating through nine NVSwitch5 trays into a 130 TB/s all-to-all NVLink domain spanning all 72 GPUs (NVIDIA). The fabric is a copper backplane — roughly 5,000 cables, no optics inside the rack — which is exactly why the scale-up domain stops at 72 and why it is so cheap and reliable relative to optical scale-out. Compare to Hopper: an H100 NVLink4 node offers 900 GB/s per GPU across an 8-GPU domain (Spheron). Blackwell therefore delivers ~9× the per-GPU scale-up bandwidth across a domain that is itself 9× larger. For the expert-parallel all-to-all that dominates MoE communication, the entire EP group can live inside one NVLink domain up to EP=72 — versus EP≤8 on Hopper before the all-to-all spills onto InfiniBand at roughly a quarter of the bandwidth. This is the single largest systems lever Blackwell hands you, and the rest of this report's parallelism advice is downstream of it.

The one lever to anchor on

The 72-GPU, 130 TB/s flat NVLink5 domain lets the entire expert-parallel all-to-all live inside the rack up to EP=72 — versus EP≤8 on Hopper before spilling onto InfiniBand at ~1/4 the bandwidth. Every parallelism decision in your run is downstream of this one fact.

Memory follows the same rack-as-unit logic. Each GPU carries 192 GB of HBM3e, aggregating to ~13.4–13.8 TB of HBM per rack at ~576 TB/s (8 TB/s/GPU) (NVIDIA). The 36 Grace CPUs add ~17 TB of LPDDR5X, producing the marketed "~30 TB fast memory" figure — a coherent pool that changes how you think about expert placement and offload. For a 10T-parameter weight set plus optimizer state, the doubling of per-GPU capacity versus H100 (80 GB) or H200 (141 GB) directly shrinks the minimum sharding factor and relaxes the ZeRO/FSDP partitioning math relative to a Hopper-era plan.

02Power and cooling: the binding physical constraint

A fully loaded NVL72 draws ~120–132 kW of IT load (commonly cited near 120 kW, up to ~140 kW for higher-power SKUs), and it is liquid-cooled by mandate — direct-to-chip cold plates fed by ~45 °C facility water, with no air-cooling option for the GPU trays (Introl). Provisioned cooling capacity, including overhead, runs in the ~2.3–2.4 MW class per rack when you account for CDUs, secondary loops, and redundancy. The practical consequence for the build: you are not designing a GPU cluster that happens to need cooling; you are designing a 200+ MW thermal plant that happens to run GPUs. Megawatts, not silicon, are the gating resource.

03Blackwell numerics: what FP4 actually buys a 10T/200B MoE

The most consequential change from Hopper is not raw FLOPs — it is that 4-bit floating point is now a training format, not merely an inference format, and the empirical case for betting a 10T-token run on it is, as of mid-2026, settled rather than speculative. Two microscaling formats matter, and you will use both.

MXFP8 (OCP)
block-32 · UE8M0 scale
The safe production default for the bulk of GEMMs. 50–60% throughput over BF16 with BF16-matching convergence, paired with stochastic rounding.
NVFP4
block-16 · E2M1 · two-level scale
E4M3 per-block micro-scale on FP32 per-tensor scale. The finer block + higher-fidelity scale is what makes it survive training, not just inference.

MXFP8 (OCP microscaling): blocks of 32 elements share a single UE8M0 power-of-two scale (NVIDIA NVFP4 blog). This is the safe production default for the bulk of your GEMMs. NVIDIA and multiple labs report 50–60% throughput gains over BF16 with BF16-matching convergence when you combine power-of-two block scaling with stochastic rounding. MXFP8 is the format you reach for when you want to de-risk a layer without dropping to 4-bit.

NVFP4: block size 16, element format E2M1, and a two-level scale — an E4M3 per-block micro-scale on top of an FP32 per-tensor scale (NVIDIA NVFP4 blog). The combination of a tighter block (16 vs. MXFP4's 32) and a higher-fidelity E4M3 scale (vs. MXFP4's E8M0) is precisely what makes NVFP4 survive training and not just inference. The finer granularity captures block-level dynamic range that MXFP4 throws away.

The load-bearing result — and the one you should treat as the template for your steady-state recipe — is NVIDIA's Pretraining Large Language Models with NVFP4, which trained a 12B Mamba-Transformer to 10T tokens, the exact horizon of your run, and held validation loss within ~1% of an FP8 baseline through the stable phase, widening to ~1.5% in the decay phase. The recipe is concrete and should be copied wholesale:

  1. Keep numerically sensitive layers in higher precision — final layers and embeddings stay in BF16/FP8; do not quantize them to 4-bit.
  2. Random Hadamard transforms applied before quantization to spread block-level outliers across the block, so a single large activation does not blow out the shared scale.
  3. 2D block scaling on weights, so the forward and backward passes see a consistent quantization of the same weight tensor (asymmetry here is a silent convergence killer).
  4. Stochastic rounding on the gradient path for unbiased low-precision gradients.
  5. A mixed-precision decay tail: switch the forward pass from NVFP4 back to BF16 at roughly the last 18% of tokens (in their run, at 8.2T of 10T), which cut the relative loss error from 1.5% down to 0.5% (NVIDIA NVFP4).

quant_recipe = dict(
    fwd_dtype       = "nvfp4",      # [1] block-16 E2M1, E4M3 micro-scale on FP32 tensor-scale
    bwd_dtype       = "fp8_e4m3",   # [2] conservative backward + comms path
    hadamard_pre_q  = True,         # [3] spread block outliers before quantization
    weight_scaling  = "2d_block",   # [4] consistent fwd/bwd quantization of one weight
    grad_rounding   = "stochastic", #     unbiased low-precision gradients
    keep_bf16_layers = ("embed", "lm_head", "final_norm"),  # [5] sensitive carve-outs
)
# mixed-precision decay tail: flip the forward pass back to BF16 near the end
decay_tail_frac   = 0.18           # [6] last ~18% of tokens (8.2T of 10T)
fwd_dtype_at(step) = "bf16" if step > (1 - decay_tail_frac) * total_steps else "nvfp4"

2FP8 in the backward. The asymmetric forward/backward split — aggressive FP4 forward, conservative FP8 backward and comms — is the empirically validated MoE recipe.

3Hadamard before quant. Random Hadamard transforms spread block-level outliers so one large activation cannot blow out the shared scale.

42D weight scaling. Forward and backward must see a consistent quantization of the same weight tensor — asymmetry here is a silent convergence killer.

5Sensitive carve-outs. Final layers and embeddings stay BF16/FP8 — never quantized to 4-bit.

6Decay tail. Flipping the forward pass back to BF16 over the last ~18% of tokens cut the relative loss error from 1.5% to 0.5%.

For your 10T-total / 200B-active MoE, the engineering directive is: plan FP4 for the steady state and budget an explicit BF16/FP8 decay tail over the final ~15–20% of tokens. At 30T tokens that is roughly the last 5–6T tokens in higher precision — a non-trivial fraction of the compute budget, so size your throughput projections against a blended precision, not pure FP4.

For the expert GEMMs specifically, the empirically-validated recipe is asymmetric across the forward/backward split: aggressive FP4 in the forward pass, conservative FP8 in the backward and communication paths. The Practical FP4 Training for Large-Scale MoE Models work demonstrates exactly this on a 671B DeepSeek-class MoE — MXFP4 (E2M1, block-32, UE8M0 scale) forward GEMMs paired with FP8 (E4M3, block-128) backward — yielding +0.61% relative loss versus BF16 after 160B tokens alongside a 16.0% throughput speedup over BF16 and 12.5% over the best FP8 configuration (1157 → 1302 tokens/GPU/s), plus a 14.8% peak activation-memory reduction. The crucial subtext: that result was achieved on Hopper, which has no native FP4 tensor cores — the speedup came purely from the memory and bandwidth side. Blackwell's native FP4 tensor cores leave substantially more on the table, so treat the +16% Hopper number as a floor, not a ceiling, for what FP4 gives your expert layer.

FP4 forward, FP8 backward — the asymmetric MoE recipe

04Realized throughput: MFU and MBU reality on Blackwell

Spec sheets are not telemetry. The best independent grounding for your MFU/MBU assumptions comes from Microbenchmarking NVIDIA's Blackwell Architecture, which measured a B200 in isolation. The tensor cores essentially hit their marks: 96.2% of peak FP4 (7,700 TFLOPS), 96.3% of peak FP8 (3,851 TFLOPS), and 96.3% of peak BF16 (1,926 TFLOPS) in isolated GEMMs. The new warp-level matrix instruction, tcgen05.mma, has a single-instruction latency of 11.0–11.4 cycles versus Hopper's wgmma at 32–128 cycles — a 2.9–11.2× reduction that is a genuine, underappreciated win for the small, latency-bound expert GEMMs that dominate a high-sparsity MoE (your config activates a small fraction of experts per token, so individual expert matmuls are narrow and latency-sensitive).

The sobering counterpart is memory. The same study measured STREAM Triad HBM bandwidth at only 4.14 TB/s — 51.8% of the 8 TB/s spec (microbenchmark). Memory-bound kernels will not see anything close to peak bandwidth. The discipline this enforces: for the decode, attention, and all-to-all-bound phases, MBU (memory-bandwidth utilization), not MFU, is the governing metric, and a realized ~52% of nameplate HBM is the planning assumption, not 100%.

A critical correction on the headline FLOP numbers, because they are routinely misquoted: NVIDIA's marketed per-rack figures of 1,440 PFLOPS FP4 / 720 PFLOPS FP8 / 360 PFLOPS BF16 are with-sparsity (2:4) numbers, footnoted on NVIDIA's own page as "Dense is one-half sparse spec shown" (NVIDIA). For dense training FLOPs — what you actually compute against for 6 × active-params × tokens — the honest per-rack figures are ~360 PFLOPS dense FP8 and ~180 PFLOPS dense BF16, i.e. ~4.5 PFLOPS dense FP8 / ~2.25 PFLOPS dense BF16 per B200 GPU. Carry the dense numbers and state the sparse caveat explicitly; do not let a 2× sparsity marketing factor inflate your throughput model.

End-to-end, a large MoE on GB200 in BF16/FP8 realistically lands in the ~30–45% MFU band, with heavy-routing MoE often at the lower end (~18–35%) because the all-to-all and grouped-GEMM overheads eat into the tensor-core duty cycle. FP8 MFU for large MoE rose roughly from ~29.5% to ~39.5% over 2024 as kernels matured, so ~35–40% FP8 is a defensible 2025–2026 planning figure — referenced against the dense peak, never the sparse one. Note the counterintuitive FP4 dynamic: switching to NVFP4 typically lowers nominal MFU, because the dequant, Hadamard, and scale-management overhead means you do not realize the full 2× FLOP advantage — yet it raises tokens/sec. The correct optimization targets are therefore tokens/GPU/s and $/token, not MFU. MFU is a diagnostic, not a goal; a run that drops MFU by 10 points while raising tokens/sec by 15% is a win.

B300 / GB300 NVL72 (Blackwell Ultra) is the obvious upgrade path: 72× B300 with 288 GB HBM3e each (20 TB total HBM per rack), ~1.1–1.4 EFLOPS sparse FP4 (≈1.5× GB200), and ConnectX-8 at 800 Gb/s per GPU for scale-out, with each B300 rated ~15 PFLOPS dense FP4 at a 1,400 W TDP (NVIDIA GB300). For a 10T-parameter model the 50% larger HBM (288 vs. 192 GB) is the standout — it materially eases weight + optimizer-state placement and lets you keep more experts resident per GPU. If your procurement window allows, GB300 is the better substrate for a model this large; the HBM headroom alone changes the sharding calculus.

TPU v7 Ironwood is the only credible at-scale alternative, and it is a serious one. Per chip: 2,307 BF16 / 4,614 FP8 TFLOPS, 192 GB HBM3e, 7.38 TB/s, 1.2 TB/s bidirectional ICI, deployed in a 9,216-chip pod arranged as a 3D torus with optical circuit switching (OCS) — reconfigurable topology, no blast radius from a failed link, roughly 1.5 optical transceivers per chip at 800G routed through 48 144×144 OCS switches (Google; SemiAnalysis). SemiAnalysis estimates Ironwood at ~30–44% lower TCO than GB200/GB300. For very large expert parallelism the torus-plus-OCS design is arguably better than NVL72's 72-GPU copper island: you are not capped at a 72-device scale-up domain, and OCS lets you reconfigure the topology around failures rather than absorbing a blast radius. The cost is leaving CUDA — you trade the most mature kernel ecosystem on Earth for JAX/Pathways and Google's stack. For a single-vendor 100k commitment, Ironwood is the alternative that should actually keep you up at night on TCO grounds; the reason most labs still pick GB200 is software maturity and the CUDA moat, not raw efficiency.

06Building a 100,000-GB200 cluster

100,000 GB200 GPUs is ~1,389 NVL72 racks. At ~120–132 kW IT per rack, and budgeting ~150–180 kW per rack all-in once cooling, networking, and PUE overhead are included, the facility lands at ~200 MW IT / ~250 MW facility power. The cleanest real-world reference is xAI's Colossus 2: SemiAnalysis reconstructs ~200 MW of cooling supporting roughly 110k GB200 NVL72 GPUs, while Colossus 1's ~300 MW carried ~200k Hopper plus ~30k GB200 (SemiAnalysis). For a clean 100k GB200 design, plan ~200 MW IT and ~250 MW at the facility meter.

Topology. With NVLink5 confined to 72 GPUs, racks group into pods of typically 8–16 NVL72 racks behind a scale-out layer (NDR/XDR InfiniBand or 800G Spectrum-X Ethernet), then a fat-tree or rail-optimized spine connects pods. The crucial design fact: because the coherent NVLink domain stops at the rack, the inter-rack network is the bottleneck for all pipeline-parallel and data-parallel traffic — everything that crosses a rack boundary runs at scale-out bandwidth, roughly a quarter or less of intra-rack NVLink. The standard compromise is 1:1 rail-optimized non-blocking connectivity at the pod level with a tapered (oversubscribed) spine across pods, exploiting the fact that DP gradient all-reduce traffic is more tolerant of taper than the EP all-to-all (which you have already confined inside the rack). Your parallelism layout should therefore map EP onto the NVLink domain, pipeline stages onto rails within a pod, and data parallelism across the tapered spine.

Capex order of magnitude. GB200 NVL72 racks run ~$3–3.5M each at street prices, putting ~$4.5–5B into 1,389 racks of compute alone, with networking, storage, and datacenter shell pushing the total to ~$6–8B — consistent with the ~$7B figure cited for the 200k-GPU Colossus build (Colossus). And the recurring cost is dominated by power: ~250 MW continuous is on the order of $120M+/yr in electricity at industrial rates before any cooling or staffing overhead. The strategic read is unambiguous — power, not silicon, is the binding constraint. You can buy the GPUs faster than you can energize and cool them.

A reliability caveat you must price in. As of late 2025, SemiAnalysis reported that no large-scale training run had yet completed on GB200 NVL72, citing software and reliability immaturity, and put GB200 all-in cost-per-GPU at ~1.6–1.7× H100 — meaning the platform needs to clear a ~1.6× throughput-per-GPU break-even just to match Hopper economics (SemiAnalysis). By mid-2026 the picture has improved — the MAI-Thinking-1 report documents a from-scratch 1T-total MoE trained to 30T+ tokens on 8,192 production GB200 GPUs, which is the existence proof that the platform is now viable for serious runs — but the lesson stands: goodput and determinism tooling matter more than peak FLOPs at this scale. MAI devotes an entire "Cluster Environment" section to goodput for a reason. Budget for failure-domain isolation, fast checkpoint/restart, and silent-data-corruption detection as first-class engineering, not afterthoughts; on a ~250 MW machine, every percentage point of goodput is worth millions.

08What concretely changes versus Hopper for the 10T run

  1. Expert parallelism fits inside the rack. NVLink5's 72-GPU, 1.8 TB/s coherent domain keeps the EP all-to-all on-NVLink up to EP=72, versus EP≤8 on Hopper before spilling to InfiniBand at ~1/4 the bandwidth. This is the single largest architectural change, and it is why a Blackwell MoE beats a Hopper MoE by more than the raw FLOP ratio. Set EP to the NVLink domain and place experts to keep all-to-all on copper.
  2. FP4 is a training format now. NVFP4 (block-16, E4M3 scale) with Random Hadamard transforms, stochastic rounding, 2D weight scaling, and sensitive-layer carve-outs holds <1% loss to 10T tokens; budget a BF16/FP8 decay tail over the final ~18% of tokens. Hopper had no native FP4 tensor cores, so this lever simply did not exist before Blackwell.
  3. Per-GPU capacity doubles (192 GB vs. 80/141 GB, 288 GB on B300), shrinking the parameter-sharding factor and easing the 10T-weight + optimizer-state footprint. The coherent ~13.8 TB rack memory pool (~20 TB on GB300) changes where experts live.
  4. The bottleneck moves from FLOPs to bandwidth and power. Tensor cores hit ~96% of peak; HBM hits ~52% of nameplate; the win is gated by MBU and by the ~250 MW facility envelope. Optimize for tokens/GPU/s and $/token, plan the build around megawatts, and treat NVL72 EP-locality and the NVFP4 decay-tail recipe as the two highest-leverage decisions in the entire run.
The summary judgment

GB200 NVL72 is not a faster H100 — it is a different unit of compute, a 72-GPU coherent rack whose NVLink domain and native FP4 reshape the parallelism and numerics of a trillion-scale MoE. Plan EP inside the rack, FP4 in the steady state with a higher-precision tail, and the build around power. If your procurement timeline reaches GB300, take the 288 GB HBM. And keep one eye on Ironwood's TCO — it is the only number on the competitive board large enough to make a 100k-GB200 commitment something other than the obvious default.
