ML-engineering for a 100k-GPU run: Stas Bekman's handbook, distilled to a checklist

A 10T-total / 200B-active MoE on ~100,000 GB200 does not fail on math — it fails on moving bits, on a corrupt checkpoint, on a falsely-low resume loss, on a single $SLURM_PROCID that interpolated at the wrong time. This is the executable field manual for the un-glamorous 90% of the run, compressed from Stas Bekman's ML-Engineering handbook into thresholds, commands, and formulas you re-measure on your own stack.

01Network: topology, benchmarking, and the bandwidth reality

Lock down units before trusting any number. The #1 source of confusion (network/README): 1 GBps = 8 Gbps, and vendors routinely quote bidirectional (duplex) figures that are ~2× the unidirectional number. Diagnostic rule: real measured bandwidth should land at ~80% of the unidirectional theoretical. If a measurement comes in at ~40% of spec, the spec was almost certainly duplex — halve it, and you're back to ~80%. Before any capacity math, pin down GBps-vs-Gbps and uni-vs-duplex for every link.

The duplex trap

A number at ~40% of spec is not a broken link — it is almost always a duplex spec compared against a unidirectional measurement. Halve the spec and the same number is now a healthy ~80%. Never compare a measured uni number to an advertised duplex one.

Know your two domains and the order-of-magnitude gap

The whole parallelism mapping is governed by the gap between scale-up (intra-node) and scale-out (inter-node) bandwidth, not by raw FLOPS.

  • Intra-node (scale-up): GB200 NVL72 fuses 72 Blackwell GPUs into ONE NVLink-5 domain at 900 GBps unidirectional per GPU via NVSwitch. This is the key architectural lever of the whole machine. NVLink generations, unidirectional all-to-all per GPU: v3 (A100)=300, v4 (H100/H200)=450, v5 (B200/GB200)=900, v6 (Rubin)=1800 GBps. PCIe5=63, PCIe4=31 GBps.
  • Inter-node (scale-out): per-node unidirectional totals — Quantum-2 IB (8×400G)=400, InfiniBand XDR800 (8×800G)=800, AWS EFA v3=400, EFA v4=800, Gaudi3=600 GBps. IB per-link signaling: EDR=25, HDR=50, NDR=100, XDR=200 Gbps; latency ~0.5–0.6 µs.
  • The gap: ~5600 Gbps intra-node vs ~3200 Gbps/node scale-out. This gap, not raw FLOPS, governs your parallelism mapping.

Map parallelism to the fabric

Keep the heaviest-traffic collectives inside the 72-GPU NVLink domain: tensor parallelism (TP) and MoE expert-parallel all-to-all. TP "requires very fast network; not advisable across nodes" (model-parallelism); on NVL72 you can push TP beyond 8 if it helps, but mind comms-overlap difficulty. Reserve scale-out IB/EFA for DP and PP, where comms either overlap compute (PP send/recv) or can be made intra-node via hpZ/HYBRID_SHARD. Sequence/context parallelism (Ulysses all-to-all) and the MoE expert all-to-all are the new heavy hitters a 200B-active MoE adds — neither is characterized by the all-reduce proxy, so benchmark them separately.

Run the bottleneck calc per parallelism config. Compare comm_time = payload_bytes / bandwidth against compute_time; the network is the bottleneck whenever comm_time > compute_time. DDP all-reduce payload = params · 4 bytes · 2 (all-reduce moves data ~2×). Worked: 2B params → 16 GB payload; intra-node 300 GBps → 0.053 s vs ~0.42 s compute (compute-bound, fine); 4 nodes @200 Gbps inter-node → ~0.64–0.8 s (≈23× slower than NVLink, network-bound); @400 Gbps → 0.32 s < 0.42 s (hidden again). Every model-parallel scheme incurs far more comms than plain DDP.

The actionable sizing formulas (model-parallelism), with n_transmissions = 3 (ZeRO-3/FSDP) or 2 (ZeRO-1); n_passes = 3 without activation recompute, 4 with; tflops_wo_comms ≈ 75% of peak:

For GAS>1 under ZeRO-2/3 the all-gather repeats per accumulation step, so comms blows up to GAS·3·2·M/GBps; ZeRO-1 needs no extra comms for GAS>1 — a strong reason to use ZeRO-1 when combining with PP/TP.

Payload size is everything — small messages are catastrophic

A100 all-reduce busbw by payload: 32KB→~1.3, 1MB→18, 8MB→110, 128MB→177, 1GB→229, 8GB→235 GBps; throughput only saturates around ~536 MB and is poor below 8 MB. Fragmentation penalty: 1×4GB = 1257 Gbps vs 1000×4MB = 374 Gbps busbw (~3×). Bucket and fuse gradients/collectives so payloads land in the saturated regime (≥128–536 MB). Never use object-based collectives (all_gather_object pickles Python objects) on a hot path — ~23× slower than tensor all_reduce (network/benchmarks).

Network benchmarking checklist
  • Benchmark on ≥4 nodes (ideally full production count) — sub-4-node results do not extrapolate to 100k GPUs; cross-rail/spine contention only appears at scale.
  • Reserve an isolated/dedicated partition; noisy neighbors make numbers non-reproducible (BLOOM-176B used an isolated SLURM partition).
  • On GB200 NVL72, benchmark the intra-rack NVLink all-reduce and the cross-rack IB all-reduce separately, then the hierarchical combined collective.
  • Confirm busbw saturates to ≥80–85% of NVLink-5 900 GBps inside each NVL72 domain; flag any node/link materially below the cluster median.
  • Enable SHARP/NVLS (NCCL_NVLS_ENABLE=1, NCCL_ALGO=NVLS): in-network reduction turns all-reduce from 2N sends into N+1 (~25–30% gain; NVLink4 ~370→480 GBps). Requires all GPUs in the collective to activate — verify the uplift appears.
  • For >64-GPU scale set NCCL_IB_QPS_PER_CONNECTION=4 (default 1); verify adaptive routing is enabled for IB/RoCEv2.
  • Disable PCI ACS for GPU-Direct (or enable ATS on VMs where ACS can't be disabled).
  • On shared/cloud fabric, repeat benchmarks at different times of day — single-run numbers are unreliable.
Inter-node bandwidth floors (ZeRO-3, 64+ GPU)

100 Gbps inter-node is not enough; 200–400 Gbps ok; 800–1000 Gbps ideal. General target ≥400 Gbps inter-node (BLOOM-176B survived on 50 Gbps — slow but workable). On GB200 these floors scale up with the FLOPS jump.

The single most important grep at 100k scale: search every node's startup log for NCCL INFO NET/. This one line is where most multi-node perf disasters are diagnosed.

node startup loggrep "NCCL INFO NET/"transport selection

1Correct. IB RDMA does the collectives; an OOB Ethernet NIC does bootstrap. This is what you want on every rank.

2Wrong plugin. The wrong transport grabbed the IB devices — you're running TCP over IB-named interfaces, massively degraded.

3Plain Ethernet. Acceptable only if no faster fabric exists.

4Picked docker0 or lo. The job hangs or crawls — interface selection went wrong.

Interface selection is the main lever. NCCL_SOCKET_IFNAME (TCP) and NCCL_IB_HCA (IB/RDMA) share a prefix grammar: bare = "starts with" (eth→eth0,eth1…), = exact (=eth0,eth1), ^ exclude prefix (^docker), ^= exclude exact. Classic gotcha: a bonded RoCE interface [8]mlx5_bond_0:1/RoCE sneaks into the comm set and tanks throughput — fix by excluding it (NCCL_IB_HCA=^mlx5_bond_0:1) or explicitly whitelisting the 8 real HCAs (=mlx5_0,...,mlx5_7). Discover with ifconfig (TCP) and ibstat/ibstat | grep mlx5 (IB).

Verbosity knobs. NCCL_DEBUG ∈ {VERSION, WARN, INFO, TRACE}; NCCL_DEBUG_FILE=/path/nccl-log.%h.%p.txt splits the firehose per host/PID so you can diff a slow rank against healthy ones across 100k GPUs; NCCL_DEBUG_SUBSYS=COLL (or INIT,GRAPH,ENV,TUNING) narrows it. Gate verbose collective counting to rank 0 (if [[ $SLURM_PROCID == "0" ]]) then grep -a "NCCL INFO AllReduce" main_log.txt | wc -l.

Intra-node P2P/NVLink path. nvidia-smi topo -p2p r shows a matrix (legend X=self, OK, CNS/GNS/TNS/NS = various "not supported", U=unknown). Per-pair: python -c "import torch; print(torch.cuda.can_device_access_peer(torch.device('cuda:0'), torch.device('cuda:1')))" — False means no P2P → silently halved intra-node bandwidth. Remedies: disable PCI ACS and/or disable IOMMU in BIOS. NCCL_P2P_DISABLE=1 is diagnostic only (big slowdown).

Cross-cloud gotcha + the exit criterion

Containers: docker run --shm-size=1g --ulimit memlock=-1 (NCCL needs large shm and unlimited locked memory for RDMA pinning); install libibverbs1 + librdmacm1; add --privileged if RDMA init fails. Cross-cloud: NCCL env vars that worked on cloud A frequently break on cloud B — strip ALL inherited NCCL_* and reconfigure from scratch per platform. Exit criterion: after NCCL looks right, benchmark and require ~80% of advertised line rate before declaring a node-type healthy.

03Fault tolerance & checkpointing

At 100k-GPU MTBF you will see failures every few hours — spare capacity + auto-restart is mandatory, not optional (fault-tolerance).

Auto-restart via self-healing SLURM job arrays

sbatch --array=1-N%1 train.slurm — %1 forces one element at a time, so any crash/exit auto-schedules the next. The training script must always resume from the latest checkpoint so restart is a no-op transition. Append a recovery queue behind a live job without killing it: --dependency=<jobid>. Wrap with a thin source actual-training-script.slurm so you can hotfix the inner script between auto-restarts. Note --max_restarts 0 in torchrun is deliberate: you rely on the array for restart, not torchrun in-process.

Provision 5–10% spare nodes (need 64 → allocate 67–70) so SLURM auto-draining can remove bad hardware without dropping below world size. Prefer fixed allocation over dynamic — dynamic keeps handing you other users' marginal rejected nodes that pass cursory checks but fail under load. sinfo shows node states (idle/alloc/drain/drained). Track GPU UUIDs (nvidia-smi -L) to catch the same broken GPU cycled back after "cooling."

Checkpoint overhead budget

mem overhead% = (n_checkpoints · t_save) / total_training_seconds · 100 BLOOM-176B: 2.3 TB checkpoint, ~40 s save (384 procs to GPFS-NVME ≈ 57.5 GB/s aggregate), every 3 h → ~720 saves / 90 days = 8 h = 0.37% overhead; at 5× slower IO → ~2%. Treat 2%+ as a bottleneck to fix via async/sharded checkpointing.

At 10T/200B your checkpoint is far larger than BLOOM's — re-architect to async/sharded writes if t_save is large.

Keep the 2 most recent checkpoints LOCAL, and fall back to N-1

Make resume fall back to N-1 if N is corrupt: "it's possible that the very last checkpoint got corrupted or didn't finish saving if a crash occurred during its saving." The handbook does not prescribe atomic writes or checksums — add tmp-then-rename atomic writes + checksum-on-write yourself for 10T-scale safety. Keep recent checkpoints on local NVMe for fast crash-resume; async-offload older ones to cloud via cron/SLURM (fetching from cloud to resume is prohibitively slow).

Kill/save switch. Poll a sentinel file at the top of each iteration (and at main() entry, to burn through a queued array fast): if a kill-switch file exists → save_checkpoint(); sys.exit(). Lets non-sudo operators stop training by touch. Save-switch variant saves-and-continues and auto-removes the trigger (good for snapshotting a loss spike).

Graceful preemption (three patterns): (A) pass a timer at launch — 100 h job (--time=100:00:00) → --exit-duration-in-mins 5990; loop checks elapsed and all-reduces the exit flag (ReduceOp.MAX over a torch.cuda.IntTensor) so all ranks exit together. (B.1) #SBATCH --signal=USR1@600 delivers SIGUSR1 600 s before end; register signal.signal(signal.SIGUSR1, handler) to checkpoint+exit (default SIGTERM gives only ~30–60 s — too little). (B.2) For launchers that don't forward signals, #SBATCH --signal=B:USR1@600 + a bash trap that pgrep/kill -USR1 its children; requires srun ... & plus explicit wait. Check QoS grace time: sacctmgr show qos format=name,priority,preempt,GraceTime; request an increase if it's < your save time + margin.

Memory-leak self-exit guards (stopgaps, not fixes): CPU — if psutil.virtual_memory().percent > 95 → save+exit; GPU — gc.collect(); torch.cuda.empty_cache() then free,_=torch.cuda.mem_get_info(), if free < 2 GB → save+exit (next array job starts clean). Fix root-cause fragmentation via PYTORCH_ALLOC_CONF / max_split_size_mb. MMAP growth is not a leak.

04Training instabilities: causes and fixes

Most spikes trace to a "bad data pocket" (poor shuffling / uncleaned web garbage), not the model — and the batch immediately before a spike usually looks unremarkable because divergence often starts developing many steps earlier (instabilities).

Init std — the single most actionable lever

Megatron-LM's default --init-method-std=0.02 is far too large for big-hidden models and was the root cause that blocked the 104B experiments past the first few thousand iterations. Init std must scale with hidden size. Prefer the smaller 530B-paper formula √(1/(NHIDDEN·3)) = √(0.3333/NHIDDEN) over "Transformers without Tears" √(0.4/NHIDDEN). Worked: NHIDDEN=14336 → √(1/(14336·3)) = 0.00482 — the value BLOOM-176B used, credited as a crucial reason it had no stability issues. Never ship --init-method-std=0.02 at large NHIDDEN.

Precision. The 104B failures are attributed to fp16 + dirty data. The three-part fix that made BLOOM-176B stable: bf16 (not fp16) + much cleaner data + an embedding LayerNorm. You'll be in bf16/fp8 already; the embedding-LayerNorm and data-cleanliness lessons carry over.

Adam epsilon → 0. "A Theory on Adam Instability in Large-Scale ML" (arXiv 2304.09871, studied to 546B) shows time-domain correlation drives divergence spikes when epsilon isn't small enough relative to the gradient-estimate components. Practical fix (§7.1): set Adam epsilon to 0 and handle the resulting division-by-zero explicitly.

Attention numerical scaling. Fold the 1/√(norm_factor) scale into Q and K before the matmul rather than applying it after (identity n·(A·B) ≡ (√n·A)·(√n·B)) — applies to custom kernels too, since CuBlas GemmStridedBatchedEx applies alpha post-matmul.

Bad-data × model-state — the canonical recovery

PaLM saw "dozens of loss spikes at irregular intervals" and never found root cause; the mitigation that worked was roll back to an earlier checkpoint and skip the problematic data batches (arXiv 2204.02311 §5.1). This is why your checkpoint cadence and data-skip-and-rollback tooling must be solid. Inspecting the offending batch is impractical: at large GBS × long seqlen it can be "the size of a book."

Classify every spike. (1) fast-recovering — bounces back in a handful of steps (BLOOM's single spike recovered in ~200 steps), generally let it ride; (2) slow-recovering; (3) not-fully-recovering — spikes, partially recovers, then diverges → rollback+skip. Also "non-spike diverging" (each restart makes a little progress then diverges). For mixed datasets (a 200B MoE will mix many), track per-dataset loss — spikes can hit one distribution and not another.

Resume-related spikes — the repeat-data false-low loss documented

A perfect resume must restore all RNG states, advance the DataLoader to the exact prior index, and reproduce complex DataLoader behavior. Imperfect resume → a spike on every resume (IDEFICS-80B got a recoverable spike on each of ~a dozen resumes). The most non-obvious failure: if your framework doesn't restore the DataSampler (pytorch-lightning does not), every resume restarts the data stream from scratch; the model re-sees and overfits already-seen data and reports a falsely low loss, then "spikes" UP to the true level when it finally hits unseen data — the loss becoming honest, not getting worse. Worst case cited: training "6× the same 50B tokens" of a planned 300B budget without noticing. Detection: do 2 early resumes with a FIXED seed before the real run (reshuffling hides the bug). Start a fresh wandb run per rollback-resume and stitch manually — wandb drops new data after a rollback until old step numbers are surpassed.

Don't panic at grokking

A sharp loss drop (4→2.5 in ~480 samples) at tiny global batch (8) is benign generalization, not instability — and not seen at large batch. Meta-advice: read public training logbooks (BLOOM, IDEFICS m4-logs, the 104B chronicles) before the run to pattern-match failures.

05Precision discipline (the fp32-accumulator rules)

Each dtype step down ~doubles throughput (A100 bf16=312, fp8=624 TFLOPS) — which is why fp8, and on Blackwell MXFP4, matter at 100k scale — but each step down increases the precision-preservation burden (dtype.md).

The precision checklist
  • Enable TF32 at program start (off by default): torch.backends.cuda.matmul.allow_tf32 = True; torch.backends.cudnn.allow_tf32 = True — free ~8× speedup on any residual fp32 matmuls.
  • bf16, never fp16 as the mixed-precision baseline — fp16 LLM training is documented as unstable and needs loss scaling; bf16 needs none (fp32-matching exponent range, no overflow).
  • fp32 master weights + fp32 optimizer states to avoid tiny-gradient-into-large-weight swamping. To shrink the 200B-param optimizer footprint, use bf16 master weights only with Kahan summation / stochastic rounding (AnyPrecision optimizer, arXiv 2010.06192) — never plain bf16.
  • Force fp32 accumulation for: LayerNorm/RMSNorm internals, bf16 reduction collectives (all-reduce) — critical at 100k-GPU all-reduce fan-in — and gradient accumulation (mandatory for bf16). Audit custom kernels: half-precision accumulation silently corrupts.
  • fp8 (E4M3 fwd / E5M2 bwd) via Transformer Engine for matmul-heavy paths only; keep norms, reductions, master weights, and optim states higher-precision. On Blackwell, evaluate MX block formats (float8_e8m0fnu scale + MXFP8/MXFP4) for the next ~2× step, with the same accumulate-in-higher-precision discipline and explicit fp4 numerics validation.
  • Never serve/finetune a bf16-trained model in an fp16 runtime without overflow handling — fp16 max is 65504 and bf16 weights routinely exceed it (HF PR #10956). fp16→bf16 is the safe direction.
python — precision setup at program start (illustrative)
# free speedup on residual fp32 matmuls

06Performance & MFU accounting

Measure achieved TFLOPS from wall-clock, not a profiler (performance):

compute TFLOPS = model_size_B · factor · 2 · seqlen · GBS / (time_sec_per_iter · total_gpus · 1e12) factor = 4 WITH activation/gradient checkpointing, 3 WITHOUT. For 100B+/MoE runs checkpointing is "almost always on," so use 4. 3·2 = model FLOPs (→ MFU); 4·2 = hardware FLOPs incl. recompute (→ HFU); HFU > MFU when recompute is on. GBS = MBS · DP · GAS, DP = N_GPUs / (TP·PP).
The timing must include DataLoader + logging overhead

Not just fwd/bwd — otherwise comparisons are invalid.

Compute MFU against measured MAMF, not theoretical peak. The handbook's central compute thesis (compute/accelerator): vendor peak TFLOPS are unreachable; use MAMF (Maximum Achievable Matmul FLOPS) measured for YOUR shapes/dtype/stack.

Measured bf16 efficiency (MAMF/theory): A100 86.9%, H100 80.3%, GH200 83.6%, B200 77.6%, GB200 72.9% (1822/2500 per-chip), Gaudi3 74.1%; AMD CDNA3 is much lower (MI300X 51.4%, MI355X 68.0%) and far more software-sensitive. Plan GB200 around ~73% bf16 MAMF until you measure better. fp8: B200 76.3%. Run mamf-finder.py on the real GB200 SKU with planned dtypes and your real M/N/K shapes (50 warmup + 100 measured iters/shape, fastest reported). Re-run after any driver/CUDA/cuBLAS/NCCL bump and at least every 6 months. Halve any published number that's ~2× a credible one — it's a 2:1 sparsity figure dense MoE can't use. Convert marketing GB to real GiB (192GB → 180GiB; subtract ~6.7%) when sizing memory.

Pace to the slowest accelerator, then stop

Silicon-lottery variance is >5% MAMF on identical SKUs on the same node; across 100k chips it compounds and bottlenecks collectives. Profile every node, pool/retire stragglers. Measure SM/memory clocks UNDER sustained load (not idle); liquid-cooled nodes sustain higher clocks than air. Reference targets: A100 bf16 >50% MFU (155 TFLOPS) was "fantastic"; FlashAttention era ~180 TFLOPS; Megatron A100 MFU 22B=41.5%, 175B=51.4%, 530B+=56–57%. Once you hit a good number, stop optimizing and start training — cost/wall-clock scale as 1/MFU (50% MFU = 2× days, 2× $$).

Software tune-up tricks, ordered by leverage

Tune-up checklist
  • Tensor-shape divisibility / tile-&-wave quantization: Tensor Cores want multiples of 64 (A100); A100 schedules 108 thread-blocks/wave (109 → a second nearly-idle wave). Rules: vocab%64==0; b·s, h/a, h/t divisible by powers of 2; (b·a)/t integer; minimize TP degree t.
  • SwiGLU MLP sizing: the naive 8/3·h is misaligned and slow. Llama-2 H100 h=4096: 8/3·h=10922 → 272.73 TFLOPS, but nearby 10944 → 398.38 TFLOPS — a 46% free speedup. Brute-force-search near 8/3·h with swiglu-maf-bench.py.
  • Gradient checkpointing: ~20–25% per-batch slowdown but 2–4× larger batch → higher net throughput.
  • Gradient accumulation: GAS=8 cuts DDP all-reduce calls 8× → 20–30% speedup.
  • GC stragglers: automatic gc fires asynchronously across ranks → MFU spikes. gc.disable() at start, then gc.collect() at a fixed interval on all ranks.
  • DataLoader: num_workers=0 is blocking (5.39 s); start at 2 (1.33 s). pin_memory=True + .to(cuda, non_blocking=True) (0.459 s vs 0.646 s). Avoid multiple workered DataLoaders (open hang bug).
  • NUMA affinity: cross-NUMA traffic degrades perf; map with nvidia-smi topo -m, bind via numactl/pynvml/SLURM --gres-flags=enforce-binding --ntasks-per-socket=4 or accelerate launch --enable_cpu_affinity (≥0.28.0).
  • Fused optimizer (apex FusedAdam / torch AdamW(fused=True)).
Memory accounting profilers miss

Mixed-precision AdamW = 18 bytes/param (weights 6 + optim 8 + grads 4) + activations + logits. Logits (fp32) = 4·bs·seqlen·vocab often dominate (Llama-3.1-8B @ seq32768: logits 16 GB vs checkpointed activations 8 GB). Hidden costs: torch.distributed init 1–2 GB/GPU (grows with GPU count); CUDA kernel preload 0.5–2 GB. Set PYTORCH_ALLOC_CONF=expandable_segments for heavy-reshaping fragmentation OOMs.

07Storage & IO

Treat storage as three distinct workloads wanting three filesystems (storage): (1) sustained fast READS for the DataLoader stream; (2) fast BURST WRITES for checkpoints (accelerators idle during a synchronous save unless async/offloaded); (3) medium shared R/W for the codebase (millions of tiny Python files). Optimizing one FS for all three is the wrong instinct.

mem Throughput = IOPS · block_size Small files → optimize IOPS; large files → optimize throughput. A device with huge IOPS but small block size will NOT deliver checkpoint throughput.

Benchmark with fio — two mandatory flags. (1) --direct=1 (O_DIRECT) so the page cache doesn't fabricate 3×-too-good numbers — real training rarely re-reads the same blocks. (2) --unlink=1 or fio reuses prior files and gives "seriously wrong" results. Before any usability timing, flush caches: sudo sync; echo 3 | sudo tee /proc/sys/vm/drop_caches. Use ./fio-scan (6 benchmarks over 16KB/1MB/1GB at 4KB block, markdown output), but match parameters to YOUR workload (file size ~ checkpoint shard size, numjobs ~ writer count) — the default 16-job/16k-file example simulates Python files, not checkpoints.

Distributed-FS reality — the highest-value section

Only ~80% of distributed large storage is reliable (GCP FileStore admits 89%; some deploys failed at 83%) — provision 125 TB to reliably get 100 TB, plus ~25% buffer for hotspot saturation and in-partition backups that don't show in df. Prefer GPFS/IBM Storage Scale (or WekaIO) over Lustre: GPFS dynamically creates inodes (never exhausts), distributes metadata (no central bottleneck killing ls/du/find on millions of small files), reliably uses 100% capacity, and packs tiny files into sub-blocks. Lustre centralizes metadata, can hit "disk full" at ~80% when one sub-disk fills before rebalancing, and misreports offloaded file sizes (du --apparent-size, still imperfect). Avoid NFS; use the native NSD client.

Checkpoint & data placement. Keep the last few checkpoints on local NVMe for fast crash-resume; async-offload older ones via cron/SLURM. Reference target: JeanZay 2021 saved a 2.3 TB checkpoint across 384 processes in 40 s on GPFS-over-NVMe (~57.5 GB/s aggregate) — the "insanely fast" class to aim for. Do NOT stream training data from cloud at train time — IDEFICS-80B was forced onto cloud dataloading, "spent many weeks" hardening it, and "it sucked"; the killer was RNG/DataSampler state sync causing repeated data. Keep data local. Verify transfer tools do MD5/SHA256 checksums (slow-but-safe beats fast-but-corrupt).

Perception benchmarks — catch a slow shared FS after dropping caches

time python -c "import torch" (20 s on slow NFS vs ~2 s NVMe); time git status (3.7 s vs 0.02 s SSD); time conda create; time pytest --collect-only. Run these after dropping caches — if they're slow, your shared FS is the problem, not your code.

08Orchestration / SLURM realities

Launcher. For the main run use the torchrun (c10d rendezvous) launcher — it tolerates worker restarts (slurm). Canonical wrapper: MASTER_ADDR=$(scontrol show hostnames $SLURM_JOB_NODELIST | head -n 1), MASTER_PORT=6000, then the launcher below.

train.slurmtorchrun c10d launcherthe #1 gotcha
LAUNCHER="python -u -m torch.distributed.run \
  --nproc_per_node $GPUS_PER_NODE --nnodes $NNODES \
  --rdzv_endpoint $MASTER_ADDR:$MASTER_PORT \
  --rdzv_backend c10d --max_restarts 0 \
  --tee 3 --node_rank \$SLURM_PROCID"            # [1]
srun --wait=60 --kill-on-bad-exit=1 \            # [2]
  bash -c "$LAUNCHER $PROGRAM" 2>&1 | tee -a logfile

1Escape \$SLURM_PROCID. THE #1 gotcha across every launcher: the backslash makes it interpolate at srun time, not sbatch parse time — otherwise every node reads PROCID=0 and the launcher hangs silently at rendezvous.

2One task per node. Use --ntasks-per-node=1 for torchrun/accelerate (torchrun spawns the local procs itself), --ntasks-per-node=<gpus> for the native srun launcher. Always --exclusive.

Verify GB200's real per-node GPU count (don't assume 8 — check the NVL72 domain layout) when setting GPUS_PER_NODE.

Scale gotchas that silently halve throughput
  • SLURM 22.05+ regression: srun no longer inherits --cpus-per-task — your DataLoader workers silently get 1 core. Detect: srun python -c 'import os; print(len(os.sched_getaffinity(0)))'; fix: export SRUN_CPUS_PER_TASK=$SLURM_CPUS_PER_TASK.
  • CPU sizing: cores/node ≥ num_gpus × (1 + dataloader_workers); e.g. 8×(1+2)=24.
  • Hyper-threading can CUT network throughput up to 4× (observed on AWS p4) — benchmark on/off, disable via #SBATCH --hint=nomultithread (check with lscpu | grep Thread).

Fault tolerance & diagnosis. Self-healing array #SBATCH --array=1-50%1 (see §03). Per-node logs #SBATCH --output=%x-%j-%N.out (%N=node) to pinpoint a bad node. Test all GPUs across the allocation: srun --jobid=$SLURM_JOBID bash -c 'python -c "import torch,socket; print(socket.gethostname(), torch.cuda.is_available())"'; GPU soak torch.ones((77*2**28)).cuda(local_rank) on 80GB cards. Run diagnostics without consuming GPUs via --gres=gpu:0. Quarantine a bad node: sbatch --exclude=bad-node script.slurm; drain scontrol update nodename=node-5 state=drain (reclaim state=idle); catch stuck-process drains with sinfo -R | grep "Kill task failed"; extend wall time live scontrol update JobID=$SLURM_JOB_ID TimeLimit=+10:00:00.

External to this section — must source elsewhere for 100k GB200 gap

Topology-aware placement (switch/rack/NVLink-domain), sbcast binary broadcast, and srun-at-scale startup-latency mitigation — known gaps. Also enforce node proximity (GCP compact placement policy / Azure availability set) so tightly-coupled jobs don't take extra inter-switch hops.

09Reproducibility & debugging at scale

The dual-mode reproducibility pattern

Call enforce_reproducibility() with NO seed in the production 10T-token run: it picks a random seed, sets only the cheap RNG seeds (random, numpy, torch.manual_seed, torch.cuda.manual_seed_all) — NOT the cuDNN determinism flags, so zero throughput penalty — and logs the chosen seed. If anything goes wrong, change one line to enforce_reproducibility(<logged_seed>) to reproduce the exact RNG state, which then also sets torch.backends.cudnn.deterministic=True; benchmark=False. You get post-hoc reproducibility of a production incident without ever paying the determinism slowdown. Never set the cuDNN determinism flags in production. Gotcha: seeding the RNGs is not enough to make the DataLoader feed data in the same order — the DataLoader needs its own seed (reproducibility).

Per-job environment snapshot in the SLURM launcher ($REPRO_DIR=$SAVE_DIR/repro/$SLURM_JOB_ID): module list 2> modules.txt (writes to STDERR), /usr/bin/printenv | sort > env.txt, pip freeze > requirements.txt (use uv pip freeze), plus a Perl one-liner walking *.dist-info/direct_url.json to dump git diff per editable install — because pip freeze captures only the SHA and silently misses uncommitted local edits.

The war story — diff env before suspecting code

Two engineers ran the identical SLURM script from the same directory and got very different TFLOPs; matching conda packages exactly did NOT close the gap — the culprit, found by diffing printenv, was LD_PRELOAD set on one and not the other (that cloud needed it for full HW perf). Takeaway: capture printenv on EVERY job; when two launches differ in throughput, diff env vars (LD_PRELOAD, LD_LIBRARY_PATH, vendor paths) BEFORE suspecting code or packages.

Hang/deadlock debugging — the highest-value tool

py-spy attaches to a live, hung process without modifying it (debug): py-spy dump -n -p PID. The first line of the dumped stack is where it's stuck — for hangs almost always a NCCL collective or barrier. Per-node, dump all rank children: pgrep -P $(pgrep -o python) | xargs -I {} py-spy dump --pid {}. Across all nodes (have this one-liner ready):

bash — fleet-wide py-spy dump (have this ready before the hang)
srun --jobid=$JID --gres=gpu:0 --nodes=N --tasks-per-node=1 \
  --output=trace-%N.out \
  sh -c 'pgrep -P $(pgrep -o python) | xargs -I {} py-spy dump --native --pid {}'

--gres=gpu:0 is mandatory (else the monitor srun blocks until training exits); the squeue jobid may differ from SLURM_JOB_ID; use sh not bash. Pre-install py-spy on every node image and enable ptrace fleet-wide ahead of time: kernel.yama.ptrace_scope=0 in /etc/sysctl.d/10-ptrace.conf — a hang at scale is debugged by attaching, not restarting. When two ranks show divergent first-stack-lines (one in barrier, one elsewhere), you've found a missing collective.

When py-spy can't attach (hardware fault) + async-CUDA gotcha

For fully-unresponsive processes, force a core dump (gcore <pid> / kill -ABRT <pid>, with ulimit -c unlimited and kernel.core_pattern=/tmp/core-%e.%p.%h.%t), then gdb python core-file → thread apply all bt (read bottom-to-top; a ProcessGroupNCCL::WorkNCCL::handleNCCLGuard frame = NCCL watchdog deliberately crashed on an async IB error). Async CUDA: failing CUDA gives nonsensical tracebacks — rerun with CUDA_LAUNCH_BLOCKING=1 for a perfect in-context trace (BLOOM-176B ran with it at zero throughput loss). WARN: hangs on NCCL 2.14.3/pytorch 1.13 — pin NCCL≥2.17 / pytorch≥2.0. NaN/Inf: DebugUnderflowOverflow(model) registers forward hooks, prints the last 21 frames on first inf/nan (read bottom-up; the overflow is in the previous frames where magnitudes climbed) — then force fp32 / disable autocast around the offending module.

Pre-flight checklist (before every real run)
  • Probe each node group with torch-distributed-gpu-test.py (validates NCCL all-reduce + barrier + GPU alloc, prints pt/cuda/nccl versions).
  • Bake --role $(hostname -s): --tee 3 into the launcher so all logs are [host:rank]-prefixed and interleaved tracebacks are grep-recoverable.
  • Set a SHORT torch.distributed init timeout (default 30 min) so a bad node fails fast.

10Synthesis: the comparison that drives every decision

Across all nine domains the same dichotomy recurs: the vendor's marketing number versus the number you measure on your own stack. The handbook's discipline is to never act on the former.

The one law to remember

At 100k GB200 the bottleneck is moving bits, not FLOPS. Vendor specs lie — benchmark units, fabric, MAMF, and storage on your stack; map the heaviest collectives (TP, MoE all-to-all) inside the 72-GPU NVLink domain; provision spares and self-healing arrays for the failure-every-few-hours reality; keep two checkpoints local with atomic writes you add yourself; classify every spike before reacting and rollback-plus-skip the data, not the model; and pace the entire cluster to its slowest accelerator and link. Then stop optimizing and train.
