10. Optimizers and hyperparameter transfer at scale: Muon, MuonClip, Shampoo/SOAP, muP, and schedules

For a 10T-total / 200B-active MoE on 100,000 GB200s, the optimizer is not a tuning knob — it is a systems-and-economics decision that propagates into your token budget, your inter-node communication volume, your optimizer-state memory (~tens of TB), and your stability envelope across a multi-month run. The frontier has, over 18 months, converged hard: every trillion-scale MoE shipped or rumored in 2025–2026 — Moonlight, Kimi K2, DeepSeek-V4 — runs Muon on the 2D hidden weights and AdamW on the embeddings, head, and norms, with a logit-stability patch (QK-Clip or RMSNorm-on-QK) bolted on. This chapter argues that stack is correct for your target, quantifies the real (and over-claimed) efficiency gains, and grounds every choice in the user's actual codebase (MaxText), whose optimizers/optimizers.py and utils/muon_utils.py already implement exactly this split via the optax.contrib._muon.muon transform.

10.1 Muon: steepest descent under the spectral norm

Muon (MomentUm Orthogonalized by Newton-Schulz, Keller Jordan, Oct 2024) is the practical instantiation of one idea: the right geometry for a 2D weight matrix is the spectral norm, and steepest descent in that norm orthogonalizes the update. Concretely, for each 2D hidden weight, Muon maintains an SGD-momentum buffer M (β=0.95 default) and replaces the raw update with O = NS(M) ≈ UVᵀ, the orthogonal factor of M's SVD, computed without an SVD via five Newton-Schulz iterations of the quintic φ(X) = aX + bXXᵀX + c(XXᵀ)²X with the now-canonical coefficients (a,b,c) = (3.4445, −4.7750, 2.0315), all in bf16 (Jordan).

The coefficient choice is the part most readers get wrong. They are deliberately not tuned to make φ converge to the identity on [0,1]. Instead φ'(0) = a ≈ 3.44 is maximized so that small singular values — which dominate a freshly-accumulated momentum matrix — are amplified toward 1 as fast as possible. Jordan tolerates a spectral error of ε ≈ 0.3 in the resulting near-orthogonal matrix without measurable loss degradation, which is precisely why 5 steps suffice rather than the 15–30 a convergent iteration would need (Jordan). The FLOP overhead of the NS iterations is <1% of training compute (0.7% at NanoGPT width m=768, projected ~0.5% at a Llama-405B-scale m=16,384 with B=16M tokens). At your 200B-active scale the matmul-heavy NS iterations are trivial against the MoE GEMMs — the cost of Muon is communication and memory, not FLOPs (§10.7).

Muon is matrix-only by construction. Embeddings, the unembedding/classifier head, RMSNorm gains, and all biases are 1D or have a privileged basis where orthogonalization is meaningless or harmful, and are handed to AdamW. This is not a heuristic — it is the same split Jeremy Bernstein's modular/spectral-norm framework predicts, and it is exactly what MaxText encodes. In utils/muon_utils.py, transform_logic returns None (→ routed to AdamW) for any parameter path containing scale, bias, embedding, or logits_dense, and otherwise emits a MuonDimensionNumbers with hand-specified reduction/output-axis tuples per weight class (the user's actual codebase, MaxText):

Weight class Path match MuonDimensionNumbers (reduction, output)
MoE expert (wi_0, wi_1, wo) MoeBlock_0 ((-2,), (-1,))
Attention out-proj self_attention + out ((0,-2), (-1,))
QKV / MLA wq_b,wkv_b self_attention + query/key/value/wq_b/wkv_b ((0,), (-2,-1))
Standard 2D weight fall-through ((0,), (-1,))

The per-class tuples matter because MaxText's weights carry a leading layer axis when scan_layers=True (the L in the comments) and the expert/attention reshapes have non-trivial layouts; orthogonalization must contract the matmul axes, not the layer or expert-batch axes. Note that MLA's down-projections wq_a/wkv_a are deliberately excluded from the QKV rule — they are low-rank compression factors, not the head-shaped projections Muon is designed for.

10.2 The two fixes that made Muon scale (Moonlight)

Out-of-the-box Muon — as it won NanoGPT speedruns — does not scale to billions of parameters. Moonshot's Muon is Scalable (Liu et al., arXiv:2502.16982) diagnosed two failures, both fixable with zero retuning of the base LR:

  1. Add weight decay. Plain Muon lets weight norms grow unbounded; the singular values of W drift and the orthogonalized update stops being well-conditioned. Moonlight applies decoupled AdamW-style decay λW with λ = 0.1 for every training stage.

  2. Per-parameter update-scale matching. The NS output O has RMS ≈ 1/√max(A,B) for an A×B matrix — a shape-dependent update magnitude wildly different from AdamW's empirically-observed update RMS of 0.2–0.4. Without correction, a single LR cannot serve matrices of different shapes. Moonlight's rule is:

    Wₜ = Wₜ₋₁ − ηₜ · (0.2 · Oₜ · √max(A,B) + λ·Wₜ₋₁)

    The 0.2·√max(A,B) factor rescales every matrix's effective update RMS to a constant ≈ 0.2, so a single shared learning rate — reused verbatim from an existing AdamW recipe — works across all shapes (arXiv:2502.16982).

In MaxText this is the muon_consistent_rms config (base.yml default None, recommended 0.2). When set to a float, the optax muon transform applies the constant-RMS rescaling; when None, it falls back to width-transfer scaling √max(1, fan_out/fan_in) — the muP-flavored path (§10.5). The other knobs are muon_beta=0.95, muon_weight_decay (set to 0.1 for a real run; base default is 0), inheriting adam_b1/b2, adam_eps, mu_dtype, and adam_weight_decay for the AdamW sub-optimizer that handles the excluded params (the user's actual codebase, MaxText).

Moonlight itself is a DeepSeek-V3-small-style MoE — 2.24B activated / 15.29B total, 64 experts top-2, 5.7T tokens — and its headline claim launched the field: Muon reaches compute-optimal loss at ~52% of AdamW's training FLOPs (~1.95× token-efficiency), fitted over 399M–1.5B scaling-law runs (arXiv:2502.16982).

10.3 The "2×" claim, interrogated

The 1.95× figure is the most over-cited and most fragile number in the optimizer literature, and for a 100k-GPU capital commitment you must not budget against it. Essential AI's Practical Efficiency of Muon for Pretraining (arXiv:2505.02222) is the decisive adversarial check. Sweeping 100M–4B params, two data distributions, and batch sizes from 128K to 16M tokens — and crucially tuning AdamW as carefully as Muon — they find Muon needs only ~10–15% fewer tokens for iso-loss, not ~50%. The gap from 1.95× to 1.1× is almost entirely a baseline-tuning artifact: Moonlight froze an AdamW recipe and ported it, handing Muon an under-tuned opponent.

So why still run Muon? Because Essential AI's durable finding is the one that actually maps to your hardware. The data-efficiency ratio R_L(B) of Muon over AdamW is non-decreasing in batch size and stays above 1 deep into the over-large-batch regime, out to 16M-token batches (arXiv:2505.02222). AdamW's relative efficiency collapses once you push past its critical batch size; spectral-norm descent degrades gracefully. At 100k GB200s your data-parallel width forces enormous global batches (K2 used 67M tokens, DeepSeek-V4 peaks at 75–94M; §10.6) — exactly the regime where AdamW falls off the cliff and Muon does not. The reason to adopt Muon is large-batch robustness, not a mythical 2× token saving. Budget for ~10–15% fewer tokens and treat large-batch resilience as the real prize.

The mechanism has a recent first-principles backing. Spectral Scaling Laws of Muon (MIT, arXiv:2606.04058, June 2026) shows the singular-value quantiles of momentum matrices follow clean power laws in model size with layer-dependent exponents: mid-depth layers degrade as ~M^−0.25 (so a fixed 5-step NS keeps them well-orthonormalized at much larger scale), but final-layer projections degrade as steeply as ~M^−0.96, meaning the output projections are the first to outrun a 5-step iteration and need more polishing as you scale. This is the predictive justification for DeepSeek-V4's two-stage NS below — and a warning that a single global ns_steps is a scale-dependent choice.

10.4 MuonClip, QK-Clip, and the V4 alternative

Muon excites the one failure mode AdamW largely suppresses: exploding attention logits. Because Muon equalizes the update RMS across all singular directions of Wq and Wk, it actively drives the product of their singular values upward, and the pre-softmax score S = QKᵀ/√d grows unbounded. Kimi K2 (arXiv:2507.20534) reports max logits "rapidly exceeding 1000" under vanilla Muon at a 9B-activated proxy scale; past ~O(10³) the bf16 softmax saturates and diverges.

MuonClip = Muon + weight decay + consistent-RMS + QK-Clip. QK-Clip is a post-step surgical rescale, not a forward-pass modification. After each optimizer step, for every head h whose max logit S_max^h exceeds threshold τ=100, compute γ_h = min(1, τ/S_max^h) and split it across the two projections: Wq^h ← γ^α·Wq^h, Wk^h ← γ^(1−α)·Wk^h with α=0.5 (each gets √γ). It is per-head and conditional — only offending heads are touched — and self-extinguishing: K2 reports the cap stops binding after ~30% of steps, logits self-decay to a normal range, and τ never needs retuning. Result: 15.5T tokens, zero loss spikes (arXiv:2507.20534). For MLA the handling is component-specific: the non-RoPE q^c, k^c each get √γ, the decoupled per-head RoPE query q^r gets the full γ, and the shared RoPE key k^r is left untouched to avoid cross-head contamination.

MaxText ships this exactly, in utils/qk_clip_utils.py (the user's actual codebase, MaxText): _scale_from_max_logits returns min(1, τ/(s_max + 1e-6)); _clip_mla_weight scales the nope head-slice of wq_b/wkv_b by √scale, scales the q^r tail of wq_b by the full scale, and leaves the V-slice of wkv_b untouched. Max logits are sown from the attention op (attention_mla.py, record_max_logits=use_qk_clip), and a config guard (types.py) forbids use_qk_clip together with attn_logits_soft_cap — QK-Clip must read raw dot products, so tanh-capping them first would corrupt the measurement.

DeepSeek-V4 (report ~Apr 2026) reaches the same end by a different route, and the differences are load-bearing for a GB200 build:

  • It discards QK-Clip and instead applies RMSNorm directly on attention queries and KV entries, bounding logits structurally in the forward pass rather than surgically after each step. (This is the OLMo-2/Gemma-3 lineage: Gemma-3 itself dropped attention soft-cap in favor of QK-norm.)
  • Two-stage Newton-Schulz: 8 steps at (3.4445, −4.7750, 2.0315), then 2 steps at (2, −1.5, 0.5) — an aggressive amplifier followed by a near-identity polisher. This is precisely the remedy Spectral Scaling Laws predicts the final-layer projections need at scale (arXiv:2606.04058).
  • Update RMS rescaled to 0.18 (not Moonlight's 0.2) to reuse its AdamW LR.
  • Optimizer split: AdamW for the embedding module, prediction head, mHC static biases/gating factors, and all RMSNorm weights; Muon for everything else; λ=0.1 for both.

V4 scales: V4-Flash 284B/13B-active, 32T tokens, peak batch 75.5M; V4-Pro 1.6T/49B-active, 33T tokens, peak batch 94.4M (report). MaxText's deepseek4-284b.yml config (hash routing layers, use_qk_norm) is the closest in-repo template (the user's actual codebase, MaxText).

Recommendation for 10T/200B. Both stability paths work. RMSNorm-on-QK (V4) is the cleaner default — it is a forward-pass invariant, composes trivially with FP8 attention kernels, needs no per-step max-logit reduction across the mesh, and is already a supported flag (use_qk_norm) in your codebase. QK-Clip (K2) is the better choice only if you are forced into vanilla Muon without QK-norm and need a retrofit, or if you want the empirical comfort of K2's exact 15.5T-token zero-spike result. Use τ=100 if you go that way. For the NS schedule, adopt V4's two-stage 8+2 at the trillion scale — your output projections are deep into the M^−0.96 regime and a flat 5-step iteration under-orthonormalizes them.

10.5 muP, u-muP, and the awkward overlap with Muon

muTransfer (Tensor Programs V, Yang & Hu) gives width-invariant optima via abc-parameterization: hidden-layer LR ∝ 1/fan_in, init variance ∝ 1/fan_in, with constants tuned on a small proxy and transferred to the full model. The subtlety here is that Muon already implements much of muP's per-layer LR scaling for free. Bernstein's derivation shows that dualizing the gradient in the correct (spectral) norm is the muP LR scaling; the √max(A,B) factor in Moonlight's rule and the √max(1, fan_out/fan_in) width-scaling in MaxText's consistent_rms=None path are the same object muP would prescribe. Naive double-application — full muP on top of consistent-RMS Muon — double-counts the width factor and breaks transfer. Resolve it explicitly: either use Muon's consistent_rms=0.2 (a single global LR, width handled internally) or Essential AI's telescoping muP-for-Muon, which accounts for all muP error sources at O(C log N) overhead and demonstrates clean LR and coupled-weight-decay transfer up to 3.7B params at seqlen 8192 (final sweep λ=0.027, η₀=0.0612, 1.61 nats) (arXiv:2505.02222). Do not stack both.

For the AdamW-governed params (embeddings, head, norms) muP still earns its keep, and the version to actually adopt at this scale is u-µP (Blake et al., Graphcore, arXiv:2407.17465, ICLR 2025). u-µP fuses Unit Scaling with µP so all weights init at unit variance, hyperparameters become independent (transfer error 0.005 vs µP's 0.03), and every HP defaults to 1 except a single global η — collapsing the sweep from a multi-dimensional grid to ~9 LR-only runs. Decisively for GB200: u-µP trains stably in FP8 at 7B with near-BF16 loss, where standard µP diverges from gradient underflow (arXiv:2407.17465). On Blackwell's FP8/FP4 tensor cores that is not a nicety — it is the difference between a usable and a diverging low-precision run, and it pairs naturally with MaxText's float32_logits / fp8 paths in layers/quantizations.py.

The one caveat to state loudly: depth transfer remains the weak axis for all of these. Every clean transfer claim above — telescoping muP, u-µP, vanilla µP — is width and batch transfer. Depth transfer (more layers) is not reliably solved; mHC-style residual normalization (the V4 doubly-stochastic Hyper-Connections trick) is doing structural work that HP transfer alone cannot. For a 10T MoE, fix your depth early on the proxy, transfer width/batch/LR/λ, and validate the final depth with at least one full-width short run before committing the cluster.

Concrete transfer recipe for the 10T/200B target:

  1. Pick proxy width ~1/16–1/32 of target, same depth as target, same seqlen.
  2. u-µP parameterization; sweep ~9 global-LR runs to find η₀.
  3. Pick λ via Power Lines (§10.6), not by sweeping — it transfers as a power law.
  4. Transfer η₀, λ, init to full width with Muon consistent_rms=0.2; do not re-apply µP width scaling on the Muon params.
  5. One full-width, short-horizon validation run before the real launch.

10.6 Batch size, weight decay, and the WSD schedule at 10T tokens

The load-bearing scaling law for HP selection here is Power Lines (Bergsma et al., Cerebras, arXiv:2505.13738), which reframes weight decay through the AdamW EMA timescale τ = B/(η·λ·D) — the fraction of past iterations effectively averaged into the final weights. Its fitted laws:

Quantity Law Use
Optimal EMA timescale τ_opt ∝ (D/N)^−0.52 = TPP^−0.52 derive λ from it
Optimal weight decay λ_opt = B/(c_τ·η·D)·TPP^0.52 set λ, don't sweep
Optimal batch B_opt ∝ D^0.4 target throughput batch
Critical batch B_crit ∝ D^0.5 data-parallel ceiling
Over-batch penalty D = D_min·(1 + B/B_crit) token cost of going wide

The penalty term is the one that prices your data-parallel ambitions: running at B = B_crit costs 2× the minimum tokens (arXiv:2505.13738). For a 10T-token budget, B_crit ∝ D^0.5 lands in the tens of millions of tokens, consistent with the observed K2 (67M) and V4 (75–94M) peak batches. This is exactly where Muon's persistence past B_crit (§10.3) becomes the lever that lets you exploit 100k GB200s: AdamW would pay a steep extra-token penalty to run that wide, Muon pays less. Pragmatically: target B somewhat below B_crit at the throughput sweet spot, accept the (1 + B/B_crit) token penalty as the cost of parallelism, and let Muon absorb the over-batch regime. McCandlish's gradient-noise-scale gives a cheap online B_crit proxy but is explicitly distrusted as a sole guide — pair it with a direct empirical CBS measurement (the Ai2 protocol) on the proxy.

Schedule: WSD over cosine. Use warmup-stable-decay. The constant stable phase lets you branch short decays off a single trunk run to probe the scaling law continuously — invaluable when one full run costs weeks of 100k-GPU time. The river-valley-landscape analysis (arXiv:2410.05192) explains why the sharp final decay recovers the loss a cosine would have spent the whole run earning. K2's concrete instance: constant 2e-4 for 10T tokens, then cosine to 2e-5 over the final 5.5T (arXiv:2507.20534).

MaxText supports this natively. base.yml exposes lr_schedule_type: 'wsd' with warmup_steps_fraction (linear warmup), a stable phase, then a wsd_decay_steps_fraction (default 0.1) decay of wsd_decay_style linear or cosine down to learning_rate_final_fraction (default 0.1) of peak (the user's actual codebase, MaxText). For the 10T run: warmup ~1–2% of steps, a long stable trunk, and a 10–20% terminal decay; branch your scaling-law probes off the stable phase rather than running independent cosine schedules. Complement with batch-size warmup — ramp B during early training while the gradient noise scale is small — to avoid wasting the expensive large batch on the easy early loss.

10.7 Optimizer-state memory and sharding at 10T

Muon's first structural win at this scale is memory: it stores one momentum buffer vs AdamW's two (μ + ν), halving optimizer state on the matrix params. But "halved" is still enormous. At 10T total parameters even bf16 momentum is ~20 TB, and a naive fp32 master copy doubles that — it must be sharded, and the sharding strategy interacts with the NS iteration.

Approach Comm volume / step Unshards full matrix? Notes
Distributed Muon (Moonlight §A) (1, 1.25]× of distributed AdamW Yes (transient DP all-gather for NS) ZeRO-1 partition; all-gather full matrix, NS in bf16, scatter back
Dion (arXiv:2504.05295) O((m+n)r) factors only No amortized rank-r power iteration + error feedback (β=0.05); composes with FSDP+TP+DDP
AdamW (baseline) standard all-reduce n/a 2× the state of Muon

Distributed Muon (Moonlight) uses ZeRO-1: partition the momentum across the DP axis, all-gather the full matrix only transiently to run the NS iteration, do NS in bf16, scatter back. Communication lands in (1, 1.25]× of distributed AdamW — acceptable, but the all-gather of the full momentum matrix is the bottleneck at GB200 scale and grows with model width (arXiv:2502.16982).

Dion (Microsoft, arXiv:2504.05295) is the more scalable answer and the one to seriously evaluate for 100k GPUs. It replaces Newton-Schulz with amortized rank-r power iteration (warm-started right basis V, error feedback Mₙₑ𝓌 ← M − β·UWᵀ, β=0.05), exchanging only O((m+n)r) low-rank factors instead of O(mn), and — critically — never unshards the full matrix (1D/2D-sharded variants with distributed Cholesky-QR exchanging only O(r²) scalars). It composes with FSDP+TP+DDP simultaneously, and larger models tolerate lower rank (a 3B model matches full-rank Muon at r = d/4). For your DP-sync-bound regime, Dion turns the O(mn) all-gather into orders-of-magnitude less traffic — the difference between Muon being free and Muon being a network tax.

Two further upgrades worth ablating:

  • NorMuon (arXiv:2510.05491) adds per-neuron second-moment normalization after orthogonalization — Muon's orthogonalized updates have highly non-uniform per-neuron norms, letting a few neurons dominate. It reports +11.3% over Muon (and +21.7% over Adam) at 1.1B with comparable memory under FSDP2. A plausible drop-in once the base stack is validated.
  • Shampoo/SOAP as the AdamW-replacement alternative: SOAP (arXiv:2409.11321) runs AdamW in the eigenbasis of Shampoo's preconditioner (formally, Shampoo with the ½ power equals Adafactor in that eigenbasis), and beats both AdamW and Shampoo in large-batch LLM pretraining in iterations and wall-clock. SOAP is a serious contender, but it carries full L/R preconditioner state (more memory than Muon's single momentum buffer) and a heavier per-step eigendecomposition. At 10T params, Muon's halved state and <1% FLOP overhead make it the more economical second-order-flavored choice; reserve SOAP for if Muon's large-batch advantage fails to materialize on your data.

10.8 Spike guarding and the integrated train step

No amount of optimizer theory survives 100k GPUs over months without a runtime backstop, because most trillion-scale spikes are late-emergent (they appear only at high token counts a proxy never reaches) and some are not optimizer pathologies at all — OLMo-2 traced many to data batches with long repeated n-grams, and SDC can silently corrupt a node's gradients with a loss curve "identical" to a healthy one.

MaxText's skip_step_on_spikes (optimizers/optimizers.py, ported from OLMo-core's skip_step_optimizer) is the production guard. It wraps the inner optax optimizer (AdamW, Muon, or muon+skip), keeps rolling buffers of the last skip_step_interval=128 losses and grad-norms with Bessel-corrected std, and if value − rolling_mean > scaling_factor·std (default scaling_factor=6.0) for either loss or grad-norm, it zeros the update via lax.cond so the spike batch never enters the optimizer's moments — momentum is not poisoned (the user's actual codebase, MaxText). Two subtleties in the implementation matter: it still appends the spike value to the buffer ("so spikes can become the new baseline," preventing a permanent self-clamp), and it warms up for max(2, interval//2) steps before enforcing, so early-training transients don't trip it. This is strictly superior to global gradient clipping, which still lets a corrupted direction through at clipped magnitude; skip-step rejects the whole batch.

The full train step composes the optimizer with the other stability machinery (trainers/pre_train/train.py): z-loss (z_loss_multiplier, set ~1e-4 to bound the output-softmax log-partition and bf16 roundoff), MoE load-balance aggregation, and aux-loss-free router-bias updates applied post-optimizer via update_state_param — the DeepSeek-V3 sign(avg_load − count)·rate nudge that keeps experts balanced outside the gradient path, the correct default at 256+ experts. Gradient clipping (gradient_clipping_threshold=1.0) and gradient_accumulation_steps round it out. Note the layering: skip-step is the outer guard, z-loss/QK-norm are preventive, router-bias is post-hoc — and skip-step doubles as your SDC containment layer, stopping a corrupted batch from poisoning Adam/Muon momentum before the bad node is quarantined.

10.9 The recommended stack for 10T/200B on 100k GB200

Component Choice Rationale
Matrix optimizer Muon, β=0.95, λ=0.1, consistent_rms=0.2 (or 0.18 à la V4) half AdamW state, graceful past B_crit
Newton-Schulz 8+2 two-stage (V4), bf16 final-proj M^−0.96 needs polishing at scale
1D params (emb/head/norm) AdamW, λ=0.1, mask norms from decay orthogonalization meaningless on these
Logit stability RMSNorm-on-QK (default) or QK-Clip τ=100 (retrofit) bounds the dominant trillion-scale failure
HP transfer u-µP, ~9 LR-only runs, no double width-scaling on Muon FP8-stable, near-zero transfer error
Weight decay λ from Power Lines τ_opt ∝ TPP^−0.52, not swept transfers as a power law

Schedule WSD, long stable trunk + 10–20% terminal decay branchable scaling-law probes
State sharding Dion (rank-r, never unshards) if DP-sync-bound, else Distributed Muon ZeRO-1 O((m+n)r) vs O(mn) comm
Runtime guard skip_step_on_spikes (interval 128, 6σ) + z-loss 1e-4 + aux-loss-free router bias spike + SDC containment

The single most important framing to carry out of this chapter: do not justify Muon to your stakeholders with the 2× number. The honest case is ~10–15% token savings (arXiv:2505.02222) plus large-batch robustness plus halved optimizer-state memory plus a stability story (MuonClip/RMSNorm-on-QK) battle-tested to 15.5T tokens with zero spikes (arXiv:2507.20534). At 100k GB200s and 10T tokens, the second and third of those are worth far more than the first, and all four are already wired into the optimizer stack the user's codebase ships today.
