Chapter 15 · You cannot raise MTBF — collapse MTTR

Fault tolerance and reliability: keeping 100k GPUs training for months

A 10T-total / 200B-active MoE pre-trained to tens of trillions of tokens will hold 100,000 GB200 GPUs for two-to-four months and absorb thousands of interruptions along the way. The whole discipline reduces to one move: you cannot raise the mean time between failures, you can only collapse the mean time to recover — and convert hard-fails into soft-degrades.

At 100,000 GB200 GPUs the question is no longer whether something will break during your run — it is whether each break costs you 30 seconds or 30 minutes, and whether you ever notice the breaks that don't announce themselves. A 10T-total / 200B-active MoE pre-trained to a few tens of trillions of tokens will occupy this fleet for somewhere between two and four months of wall-clock. Over that horizon the cluster will sustain thousands of hardware-or-software interruptions. The entire discipline of reliability engineering at this scale reduces to one ruthless reframing, which you should tattoo onto the run's dashboard: you cannot raise MTBF, you can only collapse MTTR and convert hard-fails into soft-degrades. Every technique in this chapter — multi-tier checkpointing, in-memory replication, elastic meshes, hot spares, NTP-style degraded tensor parallelism, microsecond network failover — is an MTTR-reduction or a fail-to-degrade conversion. None of them buys you a single extra minute of MTBF. Internalize that and the design space collapses into something tractable.

01The MTBF/MTTR arithmetic, and why MTTR is the only lever

The governing identity for a synchronous data-parallel job, once you are past the regime where checkpoint save overhead dominates, is simply goodput ≈ MTBF / (MTBF + MTTR).

mem goodput ≈ MTBF / (MTBF + MTTR) Once save overhead is amortized, every point of goodput is an MTTR story. At MTBF=30 min you need MTTR ≈ 20 s to clear 99%.

The load-bearing fact is that MTBF scales inversely with the device count. A synchronous collective fails the instant any single participant faults; if one GB200 (counting each Blackwell die as a GPU) has a mean time to interruption of T_node, an N-GPU job fails at rate N / T_node. There is no architecture, no scheduler, no compiler that escapes this — it is a property of the all-reduce barrier.

Calibrate against the best public data point. Meta's Llama 3 405B pre-training logged 466 interruptions over a 54-day snapshot on 16,384 H100s — 47 planned and 419 unexpected, roughly one unexpected fault every ~3 hours (Llama 3 Herd, §3.3.4). Linearly extrapolating the per-GPU interruption rate from 16,384 to 100,000 GPUs scales the fault rate by ~6.1×, which moves the mean inter-fault interval from ~3 hours to roughly 25–35 minutes. And that is the optimistic extrapolation, because it holds the per-GPU rate constant. Blackwell does not. Higher power density per package (a GB200 superchip dissipates ~2.7 kW), the immature copper NVL72 backplane with sub-optimal diagnostics as of 2025–26 (SemiAnalysis), and a larger SRAM/HBM footprint per die (more bits to flip) all push the per-GPU rate up. The honest planning assumption for a 100k GB200 run is a mean time between job-affecting interruptions in the tens-of-minutes regime — call it 20–30 minutes as your design MTBF.

Now feel what that does to goodput as a function of MTTR. A naive restart loop at this MTBF — re-queue the job, re-init the JAX distributed runtime and NCCL/collective state, reload a multi-TB checkpoint from object storage, replay the lost steps since the last save — easily costs 20–40 minutes and lands you at 50–60% goodput, i.e. you pay for 100k GPUs and effectively train on ~55k. Drive MTTR to one minute via in-memory checkpoint restore and a warm runtime and you recover ~97%.

The one delta to remember

55% vs 97% goodput is tens of millions of dollars of compute on a run this size, and it is entirely an MTTR story. This is why the rest of the chapter spends its budget on recovery latency, not failure prevention.

A second-order consequence the Daly/Young checkpoint-interval result makes precise: the optimal checkpoint interval is ≈ sqrt(2 · checkpoint_cost · MTBF). As MTBF shrinks toward 20 minutes, the optimal interval shrinks with its square root, which forces you to checkpoint frequently. You cannot checkpoint a multi-TB MoE state to GCS every few minutes. The only resolution is to decouple the cheap, frequent checkpoint (host RAM / peer RAM, seconds) from the expensive, durable one (object store, minutes, rare) — which is exactly the multi-tier hierarchy in §03.

02Goodput, not MFU: measure badput or you are flying blind

MFU (fraction of dense peak FLOP/s a running step achieves) and goodput are orthogonal axes, and conflating them is the most common instrumentation failure at scale. A job can sustain a respectable 38% FP8 MFU on every step it runs and still bleed half its wall-clock to restarts, yielding an effective MFU of ~19%.

Quote MFU against dense peak, always

A GB200 NVL72 rack is 360 PFLOP/s dense FP8 / 180 dense BF16, not the 720/360 sparse marketing figures — NVIDIA's own page footnotes "Dense is one-half sparse spec shown." A 100k-GPU fleet is therefore ~0.45 EFLOP/s dense FP8 peak, ~4.5 PFLOP/s/GPU; the ~35–40% large-MoE FP8 MFU band of 2025–26 must be referenced against that denominator, not the sparse one (NVIDIA GB200 NVL72).

The right primary KPI is goodput, decomposed via an explicit badput taxonomy. Adopt Google's ML-Goodput-Measurement library — it is already wired into MaxText — and treat its categories as first-class dashboard rows.

The library also reports step_time_deviation — step-time jitter — which is disproportionately important at 100k because the slowest of 100,000 ranks sets the step time. A single straggler on a thermally throttled node drags the synchronous barrier; the tail, not the mean, is your throughput. Meta observed exactly this: diurnal datacenter temperature swings drove 1–2% throughput variation as ambient temperature modulated DVFS across the fleet, plus synchronized power swings of tens of MW as the whole job stalled and resumed on checkpoint boundaries — a substation-level concern on a >150 MW plant that you must address with staggered restart / power smoothing (Llama 3 Herd).

Crucially, MaxText attributes elastic events rather than burying them: src/maxtext/utils/elastic_utils.py calls record_custom_badput_event_start_time / record_custom_badput_event_end_time around scale-down, scale-up, and an explicit elastic_reinitialization event, so a scale-up that costs you 90 seconds shows up as CUSTOM_BADPUT_EVENTS, not as mysterious WASTED_PROGRESS. The reliability bar is concrete: Llama 3 sustained >90% effective training time at 16k with only 3 of 466 events needing significant manual intervention (Llama 3 Herd). Holding >90% goodput at 100k requires WASTED_PROGRESS + INFRASTRUCTURE_RECOVERY to stay in the single-digit-percent range, which is only achievable if recovery is automated and sub-minute.

03Checkpointing: the multi-tier hierarchy is mandatory

A 10T-total MoE has multi-TB optimizer state. With AdamW you carry two moments per parameter; at BF16 master weights plus FP32 moments the optimizer-plus-weights footprint is on the order of 80–120 TB of sharded state across the fleet. Writing that to a remote object store every step is physically impossible, and reading it back on restart is the single largest term in a naive MTTR. The state of practice is a multi-tier scheme that decouples save frequency from durability: cheap copies often, durable copies rarely.

Tier 0 — async device→host snapshot. Orbax (and DeepSpeed) snapshot device arrays into pinned host memory under a short synchronous blocking window, then flush to storage on a background thread while training continues. MaxText's save_checkpoint (src/maxtext/common/checkpointing.py) issues an ocp.args.PyTreeSave with OCDBT + Zarr3 and an explicit per-array chunk_byte_size, bounding file sizes for parallel I/O. The relevant config knobs are checkpoint_storage_use_ocdbt, a target_data_file_size of ~2 GB, and a checkpoint_storage_concurrent_gb ceiling (~96 GB) applied symmetrically to save and restore (save_concurrent_gb / restore_concurrent_gb). OCDBT's B-tree manifest is what makes a checkpoint with hundreds of thousands of expert shards openable without listing millions of objects.

Tier 1 — in-memory / peer / redundant. This is the near-zero-MTTR design and the most important tier for you. The canonical primary source is Gemini (SOSP '23, Wang et al.): checkpoint to the CPU DRAM of host machines — orders of magnitude more aggregate bandwidth than network storage — store redundant replicas across machines under a placement strategy that provably maximizes the probability that a surviving peer holds your shard after a failure, and use host-device traffic coordination to interleave the snapshot copy with training communication so it does not steal collective bandwidth. On recovery you pull weights from a peer's RAM in seconds rather than re-reading a multi-TB checkpoint from GCS over minutes. The placement-optimality proof is the non-obvious contribution and the reason Gemini, not a naive "copy to neighbor," is the design to copy.

Tier 2 — durable object store. The rare, authoritative copy in GCS/S3 that survives a correlated multi-rack loss or a datacenter-level event. Minutes to write, minutes to read, taken every N×(local interval).

Two pieces of supporting literature sharpen the tier design:

  • CheckFreq (FAST '21, Mohan et al.): two-phase checkpointing (snapshot to pinned/GPU memory, then persist) pipelined against compute, with adaptive rate tuning that algorithmically bounds runtime overhead to within 3.5% while cutting recovery from hours to seconds, plus a resumable data iterator so each sample is seen exactly once per epoch. The 3.5% overhead bound is the concrete budget your frequent-tier should respect.
  • ByteCheckpoint (ByteDance, arXiv 2407.20143): a parallelism-agnostic checkpoint representation enabling load-time resharding — resume on a different chip count or parallelism config without a separate offline conversion job. This is the missing capability for bringing a 100k job back on fewer GPUs after losing several racks, and for elastic scale events generally. It reports a 54.2× average reduction in checkpoint stall and up to 9.96× / 8.80× save/load speedups vs open-source baselines — a concrete benchmark target. Microsoft's Nebula (DeepSpeed/Azure) and Meta's internal tiered checkpointing are the other production points; all converge on the same three-tier shape: RAM peer copy (seconds) → local SSD/ramdisk (tens of seconds) → durable object store (minutes, rare).

The mechanism: LocalCheckpointOptions writes frequent checkpoints to a per-process local directory — on GPU the distributed process_id is appended to the path so each process owns its slice — while PersistentCheckpointOptions flushes rarely to GCS. The governing config keys in base.yml are enable_multi_tier_checkpointing, local_checkpoint_directory, local_checkpoint_period, and multi_tier_checkpointing_backup_interval_minutes; enable_emergency_checkpoint is the legacy flag now superseded by multi-tier checkpointing.

Restore is where the MTTR savings land. On recovery the EmergencyCheckpointManager reconstructs from the freshest available tier and broadcasts a single surviving replica across the replica mesh via ocp.type_handlers.SingleReplicaArrayHandler (gated by enable_single_replica_ckpt_restoring). A freshly swapped-in node therefore pulls its weights from a healthy replica's local store rather than re-reading GCS — exactly the Gemini "recover from a peer, not from storage" pattern, productionized. Because the array handler broadcasts one replica's data across the replica axis, you also avoid every replica independently hammering the object store.

Preemption handling. When enable_autocheckpoint is set, the save-decision policy composes a preemption policy with the fixed-interval policy:

Concretely in MaxText's maybe_save_checkpoint, the save fires when force_ckpt_save, or reached_preemption(actual_step), or (enable_emergency_checkpoint and step % local_checkpoint_period == 0), or (enable_autocheckpoint and reached_preemption(step)).

Failure path of the replication service. The in-memory tier itself can fail. replicator_error_handler(config) (called when the manager is an EmergencyCheckpointManager/EmergencyReplicatorCheckpointManager) polls replicator.errors / replicator.failed sentinel files, logs their contents, removes them, and escalates fatal replicator failures — so a sick replication daemon surfaces loudly instead of silently degrading your fastest recovery tier into a no-op.

A silent-degrade trap — watch the replicator

The fastest recovery tier can rot into a no-op without announcing itself: if the replication daemon is sick, your "in-memory restore" silently falls back to GCS and your MTTR quietly balloons. replicator_error_handler is what surfaces that — wire its sentinel polling into your run before you trust the peer tier.

Recommended cadence for the 10T run

Set the local tier every 5–15 steps (seconds of WASTED_PROGRESS, ~3.5%-class overhead per CheckFreq), the peer-replica tier every ~50–100 steps, and the durable GCS tier every ~30–60 minutes of wall-clock (multi_tier_checkpointing_backup_interval_minutes). Keep at least 3–5 generations of durable checkpoints retained, not for storage hygiene but because SDC rollback (§07) may require reverting past a silently corrupted save.

05Elastic training, hot spares, and fast restart

There are two restart philosophies, and a 100k run should run both simultaneously, each for the failure class it suits.

Hot spares (keep the mesh shape). Over-provision a few percent of nodes; on failure, swap a spare into the failed slot, preserving mesh shape, global batch, and the exact optimization trajectory. The cost is idle reserved GPUs — the explicit critique that SPARe-class work levels at the approach. For a 100k target, reserving ~2–3% (≈2,000–3,000 GPUs) is cheap insurance that keeps the common single-node failure entirely transparent: no reshard, no re-JIT, no batch-size change, identical training math. This is your default for the high-frequency, low-blast-radius failures (the ~58.7% GPU-issue bucket below).

Elastic (shrink the world, continue degraded). When you lose more capacity than your spare pool covers — several racks, a network plane, a power domain — shrink the live device set, re-derive the mesh, continue at reduced throughput, and scale back when capacity returns. MaxText implements this over Pathways (src/maxtext/utils/elastic_utils.py, pathwaysutils.elastic.manager.Manager): get_slices / active_slice_indices recompute the live device set (filtering jax.devices() by slice_index in elastic_manager.active_slice_indices), and maybe_elastic_scale_up(config, checkpoint_manager) re-admits recovered slices on checkpoint boundaries so a rejoin never tears a step. Incomplete checkpoints are GC'd after an elastic event so a half-written local checkpoint from a dying slice cannot poison restore.

The subtle correctness requirement under rescale is data-position invariance: if jax.process_count() changes, your input pipeline must not skip or repeat tokens. MaxText solves this with Grain's ElasticIterator, whose state is a single global scalar persisted to process_0.json (rather than per-process process_{i}-of-{n}.json files that would be invalidated by a process-count change). multihost_dataloading.py reads and writes that single global scalar, so the data cursor survives any rescale — the exact property that lets an elastic event resume exactly where it left off on a different world size. ByteCheckpoint-style load-time resharding (§03) is the complementary weight-side capability: together they let you bring the job back on a different chip count with neither a data-replay bug nor a checkpoint-conversion job.

The non-obvious rescale bug — data position

Per-process process_{i}-of-{n}.json iterator state is invalidated the instant process_count() changes, silently skipping or repeating tokens on every elastic event. Grain's ElasticIterator persists a single global scalar instead — that one design choice is what makes "resume exactly where it left off on a different world size" actually true.

Fast restart in either philosophy depends on two things the naive loop gets wrong: keeping the JAX distributed runtime and collective state warm across a recovery so you avoid full re-init and full re-JIT (PROGRAM_STARTUP badput), and restoring from the freshest in-memory tier rather than GCS. Get both right and a single-node failure under hot-spare swap is a sub-30-second soft event; a multi-rack loss under elastic rescale is a 1–3 minute event dominated by the rescale barrier and a peer-RAM reload, not a cold restart.

06The NVL72 blast radius: NTP and degraded tensor parallelism

The GB200 NVL72 reframes the failure-domain problem. A single GPU failure now jeopardizes a whole 72-GPU NVLink scale-up domain, because tensor-parallel groups live inside it. The sharpest quantitative statement is the Nonuniform Tensor Parallelism (NTP) paper (arXiv 2504.06095): for TP=64, as little as 0.1% of GPUs in a failed state drops fleet availability to 94%, because every failed GPU sterilizes its entire scale-up domain for tensor-parallel work — one dead die takes 72 GPUs offline for TP. SemiAnalysis independently flags the NVL72 copper backplane as a reliability weak point with immature diagnostics, reporting meaningful per-failure perf degradation via reconfiguration (SemiAnalysis).

One dead die sterilizes 72 GPUs for TP

At TP=64, just 0.1% of GPUs failed → 94% fleet availability, because each failed GPU takes its whole 72-GPU NVLink domain offline for tensor-parallel work. The NVL72 turns a single-die fault into a domain-scale blast radius — this is why TP sizing and EP-as-fault-boundary are reliability decisions, not just throughput ones.

NTP's mitigation is to run degraded, nonuniform TP groups: redistribute a failed GPU's shard across the survivors in its domain so the domain keeps running at reduced-but-nonzero throughput instead of going dark — graceful degradation rather than binary fail. The design implications for the 10T/200B MoE are direct and should shape your parallelism plan from the start:

  1. Keep TP modest. The bigger the TP group, the larger the blast radius per failed GPU. Prefer TP that fits comfortably inside a fraction of the NVL72 domain.
  2. Exploit expert parallelism as the natural fault boundary. Each expert is an independent computation. A lost expert-parallel rank degrades far fewer collectives than a lost TP rank, because the EP all-to-all touches only the routing of tokens, not a tightly-coupled matmul shard. In the user's actual codebase (MaxText), src/maxtext/layers/moe.py already exposes the levers that make this graceful: RoutedMoE with permute() / unpermute() / local_permute() / get_all_to_all_params() implementing the EP all-to-all, and crucially the dropless vs. memory tradeoff via ragged_buffer_factor and capacity_factor (where -1.0 provisions the dropless worst-case buffer). Under a degraded domain you can raise capacity tolerance to keep routing valid while a domain runs short-handed.
  3. CP-as-EP for context parallelism. MaxText's custom_mesh_and_rule (CP_AS_EP) lets context-parallel work ride the expert-parallel axis, keeping the high-bandwidth all-to-all inside the NVL72 domain while preserving the independent-shard fault property.
  4. Pair NTP degradation with the in-memory checkpoint tier. A domain loss then becomes a seconds-scale soft event — degrade the surviving 71 GPUs via NTP, pull the lost shard's state from a peer's RAM — rather than a minutes-scale cold restart of the whole NVLink domain.
The composite policy — three layers, three blast radii

Hot spares absorb single-GPU/single-node faults transparently; NTP keeps a wounded NVL72 domain producing while a spare is swapped in or a rebuild runs; elastic rescale handles capacity losses larger than the spare pool. Three layers, each tuned to a different blast radius.

07Silent data corruption: the failure that doesn't crash

SDCs are the uniquely dangerous failure class at 100k because they corrupt gradients, activations, or weights without any error signal, then propagate into the checkpoint and poison the run — you discover them days later as a loss curve that quietly diverged or plateaued, by which time every recent checkpoint is contaminated. Meta's foundational work — "Silent Data Corruptions at Scale" (Dixit et al., arXiv 2102.11245) and the SOSP '23 follow-up on a large production CPU population — quantifies device-level SDC prevalence (a non-trivial fraction of cores in a fleet exhibit data-dependent miscompute) and introduces the two-pronged detection strategy you should adopt: FleetScanner (out-of-production targeted testing — periodically pull suspect nodes and hammer them with deterministic test patterns) and Ripple (in-production opportunistic testing — interleave lightweight verification with real work). NVIDIA's OCP SDC-in-AI whitepaper covers the Blackwell-era hardware-detection direction.

Practical mitigations for the run, layered cheapest-first:

  • Cheap, always-on numerical guards. NaN/Inf detection plus gradient-norm spike guards. The user's actual codebase (MaxText) ships exactly this as skip_step_on_spikes() in src/maxtext/optimizers/optimizers.py: it maintains a rolling mean and Bessel-corrected (N−1) unbiased std over the last interval steps for both loss and gradient-norm, and zeroes the update when (loss − mean) > scaling_factor·std (and symmetrically for grad-norm), while preserving optimizer state and appending the spike to the buffer so a new regime can re-baseline. This is your first line against an SDC that manifests as a one-step numerical excursion — it neutralizes the bad step without rolling back. The companion z_loss (via z_loss_multiplier) regularizes logit magnitude and further suppresses the spiky regime SDCs love to create.
  • Periodic deterministic recompute / dual-run hashing. Sample a subset of collectives or a subset of layers and recompute them on a second device, comparing hashes. A mismatch with no hardware error is an SDC. Budget this at a small duty cycle (Ripple-style) so it costs <1% throughput.
  • Rollback depth. Keep enough durable checkpoint history (the 3–5 generations from §04) that when a divergence is finally diagnosed you can revert past the first contaminated save. This is the single reason not to aggressively GC old durable checkpoints.
python — MaxText skip_step_on_spikes (rolling-std gate, illustrative)
# rolling stats over the last `interval` steps, for loss AND grad-norm
mean = buf.mean()
std  = buf.std(ddof=1)                       # Bessel-corrected (N-1) unbiased std
is_spike = (value - mean) > scaling_factor * std
updates = jax.tree.map(lambda u: jnp.where(is_spike, 0.0, u), updates)  # zero the step
buf = roll_append(buf, value)                # keep optimizer state; re-baseline a new regime
The asymmetry to remember

Every other failure in this chapter is loud and self-announcing; SDC is silent and self-replicating. It deserves disproportionate instrumentation relative to its raw frequency.

08Straggler detection, hang detection, and flight recorders

At 100k the slowest rank sets the pace and a hung rank deadlocks the entire collective — a single stuck all-reduce can freeze the whole job until a watchdog fires. The production tool for diagnosing this is the PyTorch/NCCL Flight Recorder (c10d): a circular in-memory buffer (TORCH_NCCL_TRACE_BUFFER_SIZE ≈ 2000) logging the enqueue/start/end of every collective and p2p op with its process group, ranks, tensor sizes, and stack traces (PyTorch blog). On a watchdog timeout (TORCH_NCCL_DUMP_ON_TIMEOUT), every rank dumps its buffer over a side TCPStore channel — the clever bit being that even the hung rank dumps, because the dump path doesn't depend on the stuck collective — and an analyzer applies heuristics to localize the stuck collective and name the laggard rank. This turns a multi-hour "which of 100,000 ranks is wedged" investigation into minutes.

In the JAX/MaxText world you do not have the PyTorch c10d recorder, but you assemble the equivalent from three pieces:

  1. Coordination-service heartbeats. The JAX distributed runtime's coordination service detects a dead/unresponsive process and surfaces it; this is your hang detector.
  2. Step-time-deviation monitoring. The goodput library's step_time_deviation jitter metric is your straggler detector — a rank or slice that is consistently in the slow tail (e.g. from thermal throttling, a degraded NVLink, or a partial NIC failure) shows up as a persistent step-time outlier well before it fully fails.
  3. Orchestrator excision. On detection, the orchestrator kills and restarts the bad node (hot-spare swap) or elastically excises the bad slice (active_slice_indices), then restores from the freshest in-memory tier.
The detection→recovery loop must close in under a minute

The design pattern is uniform across stacks: watchdog detects the hang or straggler in seconds → recorder/jitter-metric localizes it → orchestrator excises and restarts → in-memory tier restores. The whole loop must close in well under a minute to keep INFRASTRUCTURE_RECOVERY in budget.

09Microsecond network failover: MRC turns link flaps into non-events

The network is a first-class failure source — in Llama 3's spectrum, network switch/cable faults were a named bucket, and at 100k the scale-out RDMA plane is enormous. The default failure mode of vanilla RoCEv2/Ethernet is brutal: a transient link flap that takes seconds of routing-protocol reconvergence will time out NCCL, which faults the collective, which restarts the job — minutes of MTTR for a flap that lasted milliseconds.

MRC (Multipath Reliable Connection), open-sourced May 2026 by OpenAI with AMD/Broadcom/Intel/Microsoft/NVIDIA via OCP and detailed in arXiv 2605.04333, is built precisely for this regime. It extends RoCEv2 Reliable Connection semantics but restricts the connection to one-sided write / write-with-immediate, so every data packet carries its RDMA virtual address and rkey and the receiving NIC can DMA each packet to its final location in any arrival order — eliminating the reorder buffer and enabling true packet spraying. Each QP rotates through 128–256 Entropy Values, each mapping via static SRv6 source routing to one physical path. On a path failure the NIC simply stops using the affected EV(s) in microseconds — one dead EV out of 256 costs ~0.4% of paths and is dropped without the collective ever observing a fault. PFC is disabled; loss is handled with SACK fast retransmit, ECN for load-balance signaling, and packet trimming.

The relevance to this chapter is not the throughput (though that is excellent — ~770 Gb/s point-to-point on ConnectX-8, 92 GB/s NCCL sustained at 42K GPUs); it is tail-latency suppression. At 100k GPUs a step waits on the slowest all-to-all, so the p99.9 tail of fabric transfers — which on vanilla RoCE spikes to seconds on a single link flap — is your goodput. MRC converts that class of transient flap from an NCCL-timeout-triggered job restart into a sub-millisecond, training-transparent reroute. In MTBF/MTTR terms it does not raise MTBF, but it removes an entire category of fault from the synchronous collective's view, which is functionally a fail-to-non-event conversion — the highest-value move in the playbook. For the 100k GB200 buildout, run scale-out as an 800G spray fabric (MRC over Spectrum-X / Tomahawk-5) on disjoint, Acceleron-style network planes rather than a single-plane fabric, so that even a whole-plane failure reroutes instantly instead of fate-sharing the job.

Fail-to-non-event is the highest-value move

MRC doesn't add a minute of MTBF — it removes link flaps from the synchronous collective's field of view entirely. Removing a whole fault class is strictly better than recovering from it faster, which is why a spray fabric on disjoint planes is the network design for a run this size.

The arithmetic closes the chapter the way it opened it. With a design MTBF of ~25 minutes, you need a blended MTTR under ~90 seconds to hold goodput above 90% — and the only way to a 90-second blended MTTR is the hierarchy above: MRC removing flaps entirely, hot spares making the common GPU fault a sub-30-second event, NTP keeping wounded NVL72 domains producing, the Orbax EmergencyCheckpointManager restoring from peer RAM rather than GCS, and the spike guards plus checkpoint-history depth catching the silent failures that would otherwise poison the whole run. Llama 3 proved >90% goodput is achievable at 16k because automation absorbed all but 3 of 466 interruptions. At 100k GB200 the fault rate is ~6× higher and the NVL72 blast radius is larger, so the automation bar is correspondingly higher — but every primitive needed to clear it now exists in the open, and most of it is already wired into the user's actual codebase (MaxText).

The meta-lesson

Build the run around MTTR, instrument it with the badput taxonomy, and treat every fault as a soft-degrade. There is no other path to keeping 100,000 GPUs training for months.
