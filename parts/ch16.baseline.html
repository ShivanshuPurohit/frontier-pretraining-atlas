16. Midtraining, annealing, and long-context: the stage that quietly makes the model

The bulk pre-train is where you spend 80–90% of your FLOPs; midtraining is where you decide what the checkpoint is actually good at. This is the stage that converts a high-entropy next-token predictor over web sludge into a base model that does arithmetic, reads code, follows instruction-shaped documents, and attends across 128K tokens — and it does all of this before any chat template, reward model, or RL touches the weights. It is also the stage labs disclose least and confuse most: "midtraining," "annealing," and "continued pre-training" are used interchangeably and contradictorily across reports. For a 10T-total / 200B-active MoE on 100,000 GB200 GPUs, getting this stage wrong is not a rounding error. The decay tail alone is 1–2T tokens — a low-LR phase where loss drops non-linearly and benchmark capability gets locked in — and the long-context extension is a systems problem that, done naively, wastes hundreds of billions of tokens and leaves 65% of your context-parallel ranks idle. This chapter treats midtraining, annealing, and long-context as one continuous design surface and gives the concrete recipe.

16.1 Definitions: where midtraining sits, and why the vocabulary is a minefield

The field has converged on a three-phase taxonomy, even where it disagrees on names (Mid-Training survey, 2510.06826).

Phase LR regime Data Tokens (frontier scale) Output format touched?
Pre-training warmup → stable peak (or cosine bulk) near-stationary, web-heavy DeepSeek-V3 14.8T; Qwen3 S1 ~30T; SmolLM2 ~11T; Olmo 3 up to 5.9T no
Mid-training decaying (WSD cooldown), low shift to HQ/code/math/reasoning/instruction; extend context; inject skills OLMo 2 explicitly "5–10% of training FLOPs" no chat tokens, no RL
Post-training SFT/DPO/GRPO/RLVR role/turn structure + reward small yes — turns, special tokens, reward

The operational boundary that matters: mid-training still uses LM loss over documents and still moves the base checkpoint; post-training introduces role/turn structure and reward signals (Mid-Training survey). OLMo 2 is the cleanest operationalization — it explicitly names a "mid-training" second stage consuming 5–10% of training FLOPs, distinct from both pre- and post-training (OLMo 2, 2501.00656). The survey frames the phase as the bridge that shifts the model "from memorization toward abstraction."

Beware the vocabulary collisions, because they will trip you when reading recipes (Vintage Data, "What's the deal with mid-training"):

  • Llama 3 and MiniCPM fold the data-mix shift into the pre-training LR annealing tail and call it "annealing," not "mid-training" (Llama 3, 2407.21783; MiniCPM, 2404.06395).
  • OLMo 2 calls essentially the same data-quality anneal "mid-training" (OLMo 2).
  • SmolLM3 uses "mid-training" to mean a 140B-token reasoning injection that is arguably early post-training (SmolLM3 blog).

For the 10T/200B build, collapse these into one engineering surface: a WSD-style decay tail whose data mixture and sequence length you control as two independent axes. Everything below is how to set those two axes.

16.2 WSD and the decay phase: anneal on good data, because the cooldown cashes in

The mechanism originates with MiniCPM's Warmup-Stable-Decay (WSD) schedule, which replaces cosine with warmup → long stable plateau at peak LR → short decay to ≈0 (MiniCPM, 2404.06395). Two empirical findings from MiniCPM drive the entire modern practice:

  1. Loss drops sharply and non-linearly during the decay phase. The stable-phase loss is a poor estimate of final loss; the cooldown "cashes in" a large fraction of the model's final capability in the last ~10% of steps. This is why you cannot judge a run from its plateau.
  2. WSD decouples the decay from total length. Because the plateau is flat, you can branch a single stable checkpoint into multiple short decays with different data mixtures and sequence lengths. This is what makes "anneal-as-evaluation" cheap — and it is the structural reason midtraining is a separable stage rather than a tuning detail.

MiniCPM uses exponential annealing with T≈5000 steps (~20B tokens), decay ≈10% of training, and — the load-bearing move — changes the data mixture during decay to upsample high-quality + SFT-format data, mixing new data strictly during the decay phase (MiniCPM). The survey corpus reports decay length is typically 8–20% of steps, and that √-decay or "lowered-linear" (α≈0.7) decay shapes beat pure linear (Mid-Training survey).

Why "anneal on good data" beats spreading good data uniformly

The intuition: during the high-LR plateau the model is still reorganizing representations and partially forgets specifics; concentrating your scarce high-quality math/code/reasoning tokens into the low-LR tail means the model commits them to weights with minimal subsequent overwriting. This connects to the scaling-law finding that LR decay "wastes" the best data if it is fed too early — you want your premium tokens arriving precisely as the LR collapses. It is the single highest-leverage curriculum decision in the whole pipeline.

The canonical recipes, side by side

Lab / corpus Decay budget HQ mixture during decay Souping? Key ablation
MiniCPM ~10%, T≈5000 steps (~20B tok) upsample HQ + SFT-format — loss drops sharply in decay
Llama 3 405B final ~40M tok, linear→0 at 128K upsample very-high-quality sources — anneal-as-probe: +24.0% GSM8K, +6.4% MATH on 8B; negligible on 405B
OLMo 2 (Dolmino) 50B/100B/300B stages ~50% DCLM / ~50% other (math + StackExchange QA) yes (weight-avg) removing HQ data in anneal "significantly drops" benchmarks
Olmo 3 100B integration anneal Dolma 3 Dolmino, microannealing-selected yes parallel microanneals + 5 integration rounds
SmolLM2 math 60B+40B; code 200B manual per-stage mixing-rate refinement — per-stage rate tuning beats fixed
Qwen3 Reasoning Stage S2 ~5T @ 4096 up-weight STEM/code/reasoning/synthetic — treated as mid-training-like up-weighting

OLMo 2 / Dolmino is the most reproducible blueprint. Dolmino Mix 1124 draws from an 832.6B-token high-quality pool — DCLM-Baseline ~752B, FLAN (decontaminated) ~17B — plus a math mix (TuluMath ~230M, Dolmino SynthMath ~28.7M, GSM8K, MathCoder2, MetaMath) and StackExchange QA. Stage-2 mixtures of 50B / 100B / 300B tokens are sampled at roughly 50% DCLM / 50% everything-else (Dolmino Mix 1124). The 7B mid-trains 3×50B runs with different data orders; the 13B does 3×100B + 1×300B; the resulting checkpoints are model-souped (weight-averaged) (OLMo 2, 2501.00656). The load-bearing ablation: removing high-quality data during annealing significantly drops benchmark performance — the anneal is not optional polish, it is where the capability lives.

Olmo 3 refines this into a feedback loop worth copying wholesale. It uses microannealing: many candidate sources are ablated in parallel via lightweight short anneals, the winners are combined into a 100B-token integration anneal (Dolma 3 Dolmino Mix), and the whole thing runs over five rounds of integration tests (Olmo 3, 2512.13961). This is the clearest published operationalization of "anneal-as-evaluation" as a distributed data-selection harness, not a one-shot.

The scale caveat you must not ignore

Llama 3 repurposes annealing as a data-quality probe: anneal a 50%-trained 8B from peak LR to 0 over 40B tokens with 30% weight on a candidate dataset / 70% default mix, and read the benchmark delta — Meta calls this "more efficient than scaling-law experiments for every small dataset." On the 8B, annealing on small high-quality math sets delivered +24.0% GSM8K and +6.4% MATH. But the gains were negligible on the 405B, which already had the in-context capability (Llama 3, 2407.21783).

This is the single most important caveat for a 200B-active target. Anneal-as-probe sensitivity shrinks with scale. A 7B or 8B proxy used to rank candidate decay datasets will over-state the gains your 200B-active model will see — the larger model has already absorbed much of that capability during bulk pre-training. Use the proxy to rank candidate sources (the ordering largely transfers), but discount the magnitude of predicted gains heavily, and confirm the top few survivors with at least one anneal at a meaningfully larger proxy (e.g. a 30–70B-active config) before committing the full decay. This is also corroborated independently by Apple's BETR finding that larger models require less aggressive filtering (BETR, 2507.12466) — the whole selection apparatus must be scale-adapted.

Decay recipe for 10T/200B

  • Budget the decay tail at ~10–20% of tokens (≈1–2T). Toward the lower end given that anneal value softens at this scale; reserve the upper end if long-context extension is folded in.
  • Use √-decay or lowered-linear (α≈0.7), not pure linear; decay to ~0 (or to the floor you will hold during RoPE extension — see §16.4).
  • Upsample math / code / reasoning / instruction-formatted data, with FIM injected here (§16.6). Decontaminate against the full eval suite (13-gram floor + embedding/semantic checks).
  • Run several short anneals from one stable checkpoint with different data orders/mixtures, then soup the survivors (weight-average). Souping buys robustness for free and is now standard at OLMo/Olmo scale.
  • A non-obvious alternative worth piloting: WSM argues the decay phase is equivalent to a form of checkpoint merging and proposes eliminating the explicit decay by merging stable-phase checkpoints instead (WSM, 2507.17634). At 10T scale, where every checkpoint is expensive, the merge-instead-of-decay reframing interacts directly with souping and with continued-pretraining branching; treat it as a high-value ablation, not yet a default.

16.3 Long-context extension via RoPE rescaling: the method lineage

Every current recipe extends a short-context base via RoPE frequency rescaling rather than training long from scratch — because attention is quadratic and long documents are scarce and low-quality. You train the bulk and the decay at 4K–8K (DeepSeek-V3, Qwen3) and then extend. The method lineage:

Method Core idea Limitation it fixes
Position Interpolation (PI) linearly compress positions; scale θ by 1/s extrapolation explodes; PI is stable but blurs all frequencies
NTK-aware scale the RoPE base, spreading interpolation pressure across dims PI crushes high-frequency (local) channels
NTK-by-parts interpolate low-freq dims, extrapolate high-freq dims uniform scaling over- or under-corrects per dimension
YaRN NTK-by-parts + attention temperature √t = 0.1·ln(s)+1 on logits recovers attention-distribution entropy at long ctx
LongRoPE evolutionary search over non-uniform per-dim rescale factors >2M-token extension where closed-form scaling fails

YaRN is the workhorse (YaRN, 2309.00071). Its two contributions: (1) NTK-by-parts interpolation that interpolates low-frequency dimensions and extrapolates high-frequency ones, and (2) a temperature/attention-scaling factor √t = 0.1·ln(s)+1 applied to the attention logits, which recovers the entropy of the attention distribution at long context. The payoff is efficiency: YaRN needs ~10× fewer tokens and 2.5× fewer steps than PI-style fine-tuning, and it was published at ICLR 2024. For a 100k-GPU run, that token efficiency is the difference between a 40B-token extension and an 800B-token one.

16.4 The reference MoE long-context recipe: DeepSeek-V3, and the alternatives

DeepSeek-V3 is the safest template for a sparse model, because it is the only fully-disclosed MoE long-context recipe and it is conspicuously conservative. Two YaRN stages of 1000 steps each, 4K → 32K → 128K, with YaRN s=40, α=1, β=32 and the √t=0.1·ln(s)+1 attention scale (DeepSeek-V3, 2412.19437):

Stage Seq len Batch Steps LR
1 32K 1920 1000 7.3×10⁻⁶
2 128K 480 1000 7.3×10⁻⁶

The two details that make this the recommended MoE template: the LR is held at 7.3×10⁻⁶ for both stages — exactly the final pre-training LR — and each stage is only 1000 steps. You do not re-warm. Holding the LR at the decay floor avoids the loss-spike and router-destabilization risk of re-warming a fully-annealed sparse model, where re-warming can scramble expert-routing balance that took trillions of tokens to settle. For a 200B-active MoE, router stability through extension is a first-order concern that dense recipes simply do not have, and DeepSeek's constant-low-LR two-stage approach is the de-risked default.

The alternatives, and when to reach for them:

  • Qwen2.5 / Qwen3 combine RoPE ABF (base 10,000 → 1,000,000), YaRN, and Dual Chunk Attention (DCA). DCA remaps relative positions into intra-/inter-/successive-chunk patterns so no pairwise distance exceeds the pre-train length, enabling 4× extrapolation at inference; Qwen2.5-Turbo hits 100% 1M-token passkey with RoPE base 10,000,000 + YaRN + DCA (Qwen3, 2505.09388; Qwen2.5-Turbo, 2501.15383). Reach for ABF+DCA if your inference-time target length materially exceeds your training length and you want to amortize extension cost.
  • SmolLM3 removes RoPE from every 4th layer (NoPE), trains the decay mixture at 64K with raised RoPE θ, then YaRN-extrapolates to 128K (SmolLM3 blog). NoPE is an architectural bet to make at design time, not retrofittable.
  • LongRoPE for true >2M-token via evolutionary per-dimension search — relevant only if you are chasing million-token contexts (§16.7).

16.5 Progressive sequence schedules, length mixing, and context parallelism

Extension is staged, not one-shot, for two reasons: attention cost is quadratic, and short-context skills regress if you jump too far too fast. The consistent guard across every disclosed recipe is length-distribution mixing — always retain a large fraction of short documents at each stage.

Lab Schedule Length mix per stage Gate to advance
Llama 3 405B 8K → 128K in 6 stages over ~800B tok — short-ctx evals fully recover and NIAH solved
Qwen2.5-Turbo 32K → 64K → 128K → 256K 40% at current max, 60% shorter passkey at length
Qwen3 long-ctx 32K seq len 75% in 16,384–32,768 tok, 25% in 4,096–16,384 —
Olmo 3 extend to ~65K, 600B-tok "Longmino" pool 34% long / 66% short YaRN on full-attention layers only

The numbers cluster tightly: keep 40–66% short data at each long stage. Llama 3's discipline is the model to emulate — advance to the next length only when (a) short-context evals fully recover and (b) needle-in-a-haystack (NIAH) is solved at the current length (Llama 3, 2407.21783). NIAH and passkey are the universal acceptance tests; they gate each stage. Olmo 3 adds two refinements worth adopting: apply YaRN only to full-attention layers (leave sliding-window layers untouched), and GZIP-compressibility filtering on the long pool, dropping the top and bottom 20% to remove both boilerplate-repetitive and near-random documents (Olmo 3, 2512.13961).

Context parallelism is the systems enabler — and its pathology will bite at 100k GPUs

At 128K tokens, the sequence does not fit in one device's activation budget; you shard it across a context-parallel (CP) group. The non-obvious lesson from the Llama 3 systems paper (distinct from the model card, and rarely cited alongside it) is the choice of CP algorithm and the load-balancing fix (Scaling Llama 3 Training, ISCA'25):

  • Llama 3 uses an all-gather-based pass-KV scheme — all-gather the K/V tensors, then compute local-query attention — rather than ring attention. The reason is decisive for any real pretraining corpus: pass-KV tolerates irregular document masks (intra-document attention only). Ring attention's pipelined structure fights document-boundary masking.
  • The cost: with document masks, 65.75% of CP exposed latency is spent waiting on the slowest rank, because causal + document-mask work is wildly unbalanced across the sequence (early positions attend to little; late positions attend to much). Separately, CP collective exposure is ~7.64% of step time. The slowest-rank wait is the killer.
  • The mitigation, and you must implement it: load-balanced sharding. Split the sequence into 2×CP chunks and give rank i both chunk i and chunk (2·CP − 1 − i). Pairing an early (cheap) chunk with a late (expensive) chunk equalizes the per-rank attention work, collapsing the slowest-rank tax.

Two systems mandates fall out of this for the 10T/200B plan. First, document packing with proper block-diagonal / intra-document masking is mandatory so packed sequences do not leak attention across document boundaries — and this masking interacts directly with CP load balance, because the mask determines the per-position cost the 2×CP sharding must equalize. Second, on a 100k-GB200 fabric the CP group should be sized to fit within high-bandwidth NVLink domains (the NVL72 rack) where possible, since pass-KV all-gathers K/V every step; pushing CP across the slower scale-out network turns the ~7.64% collective exposure into something far worse. Reserve CP for the long-context stages only — there is no reason to pay it during the 4K–8K bulk.

16.6 Synthetic long-context data and capability injection

Synthetic long data, because real long documents are scarce and bad

Real documents at 128K+ are rare and low-quality, so synthesis dominates the long stages. Llama 3 found naive long-SFT regressed short capabilities and built synthetic data around three use-cases (Llama 3, 2407.21783):

  1. (Multi-turn) QA over long contexts.
  2. Hierarchical long-document summarization — summarize 8K chunks with the 8K model, then summarize the summaries, and train on the full document. This bootstraps long-doc competence from a short-context model.
  3. Reasoning over code repositories — pack and reason across repo-scale code.

The contrarian budgeting result is ProLong: data engineering beats token budget. Continuing Llama-3-8B on 20B@64K then 20B@512K beats Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct on most long-context tasks using ~5% of the long-context budget (40B vs 800B tokens), while emphasizing two things — retain short data and pack code repositories (ProLong, ACL 2025; arXiv 2410.02660). For the 10T plan this means: do not brute-force the extension with a giant long-context budget. A well-engineered 40–80B-token long mixture, gated by NIAH/passkey, will outperform a poorly-curated 800B one. Spend the saved tokens on the decay-phase capability mix instead.

For true million-token recipes, the pattern is stepwise RoPE scaling with document concatenation — e.g. 180K → 350K → 650K → 1M, concatenating 4/8/12 docs, with per-length sample counts shrinking from 2000 to 200 as length grows (Hierarchical synthetic data to 1M, 2504.12637). Sample counts shrink because each long example is enormously expensive and the marginal capability per example flattens.

Capability injection: midtraining is the cheapest place to add discrete skills

Because the decay phase commits data to weights with minimal overwriting, it is the optimal point to inject targeted capabilities — without introducing chat special tokens, preserving the base format for downstream post-training.

  • Code fill-in-the-middle (FIM). DeepSeek-Coder applies FIM at rate 0.5 in PSM (Prefix-Suffix-Middle) format with sentinel tokens; FIM rates of 50–90% show no left-to-right degradation while enabling infilling (DeepSeek-Coder, 2401.14196). Inject FIM during the decay/code anneal, at the document level after packing, with sentinels reserved in the vocab.
  • Math / reasoning. Up-weight synthetic chain-of-thought (MetaMath, MathCoder2, TuluMath) as Qwen3 S2 and OLMo 2 Dolmino do. This is where the GSM8K/MATH gains in §16.2 come from.
  • Multilingual. SmolLM3 injects 6 native + 3 additional languages during the staged curriculum (SmolLM3 blog).
  • Tool-use / agentic traces. Increasingly seeded in midtraining as instruction-like documents (function-call traces, ReAct-style transcripts) before formal post-training. Olmo 3 explicitly includes "instruction and thinking data" in midtraining while avoiding special tokens to preserve the base format (Olmo 3, 2512.13961). This is a meaningful 2026 shift: agentic capability is being moved earlier, into the base, rather than left entirely to post-training.

Continued pre-training rides the same machinery

Domain adaptation and continued pre-training are not a separate pipeline — they are the WSD branch pattern reused. Branch from a stable-phase checkpoint and run a short decay over domain data, exactly the MiniCPM "mix new data strictly during decay" pattern (MiniCPM, 2404.06395). The reason to branch from the stable checkpoint rather than re-warming a fully-annealed one is the loss-spike and (for MoE) router-rebalancing risk of re-warming a collapsed-LR model. Keep your last stable-plateau checkpoint as the canonical fork point for every downstream domain variant; this is also why the plateau checkpoint, not the final souped model, is the asset to preserve.

16.7 The integrated midtraining pipeline for 10T/200B on 100k GB200

Pulling the stage together into one executable plan. Bulk pre-train consumes ~8–8.5T tokens at stable peak LR, 4K–8K context. Then:

Step 1 — Decay tail (≈1–2T tokens, 10–20%). √-decay or lowered-linear (α≈0.7) from peak to the floor. Upsample math/code/reasoning/instruction; inject FIM at rate 0.5 PSM; seed agentic/tool traces as instruction-like docs without special tokens. Decontaminate against the full eval suite. Run several short anneals from one stable checkpoint via a microannealing harness (Olmo 3 style), validate with anneal-as-probe deltas, and soup the survivors. Discount the proxy: rank candidate datasets on a 7–8B proxy but verify the top survivors at a 30–70B-active config, because the 405B-scale insensitivity means your small proxy over-states gains for the 200B target.

Step 2 — Staged RoPE/YaRN extension at constant low LR. Use the DeepSeek-V3 MoE template: hold LR at the decay floor (~7×10⁻⁶), two YaRN stages of ~1000 steps each, s=40, α=1, β=32, √t attention scale, 32K then 128K. Do not re-warm — protect router balance. Retain 40–66% short data per stage. Gate each stage on full short-context recovery and NIAH/passkey. Apply YaRN only to full-attention layers if you use a sliding-window/full hybrid.

Step 3 — Long-context systems. CP with all-gather pass-KV (not ring) to tolerate document masks; 2×CP load-balanced sharding (rank i gets chunks i and 2·CP−1−i) to kill the 65.75% slowest-rank tax; block-diagonal intra-document masking on packed sequences; keep CP groups inside NVLink domains.

Step 4 — Long synthetic data. Hierarchical-summary, multi-doc QA, and repo-reasoning data (Llama 3 use-cases). Keep the long budget small and well-engineered (ProLong: ~40–80B beats 800B). If chasing >256K, switch to ABF+DCA (Qwen) or LongRoPE with stepwise concatenation and shrinking per-length sample counts.

The strategic point. Midtraining is where a generic 200B-active predictor becomes your model — its math, code, reasoning, long-context, and agentic competence are all decided in the ~1–2T decay tokens plus the few-thousand-step extension, a single-digit percentage of total FLOPs. The asymmetry is the whole reason to take it seriously: the bulk pre-train is a commodity you can mostly buy with compute; the midtraining recipe is where the differentiation, and the failure modes, actually live. Preserve the stable-plateau checkpoint, run the microannealing harness, soup, extend conservatively, and gate ruthlessly on NIAH.
