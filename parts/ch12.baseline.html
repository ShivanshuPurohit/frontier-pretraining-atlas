12. Modern scaling laws: beyond Chinchilla, for MoE, for data quality, and for your own dataset

Chinchilla is not your operating manual. It is a foil. Hoffmann 2022 corrected Kaplan's allocation artifact — Kaplan's untuned warmup, mismatched LR decay, and loss read at non-converged points had inflated parameter scaling to the famous ~0.73/0.27 split — and settled the dense world on roughly equal exponents, a≈b≈0.5, ~20 tokens/parameter (Hoffmann 2022 via DeepSeek-LLM). For a 10T-total / 200B-active MoE on 100,000 GB200 GPUs, that constant is worse than useless: it will mis-size your model by a factor of several and mis-allocate tens of millions of dollars of compute. Three structural facts break it.

First, the loss surface is no longer L(N, D). It is at minimum L(N, D, N_active, G, S, P) — a six-dimensional object in total params, tokens, active params, expert granularity, sparsity, and numerical precision — and the cross-terms are not separable. Second, the objective is not compute-optimal loss at fixed training FLOPs. With a billion-plus inference requests amortized against one training run, the correct objective is lifetime cost, which pushes you far into the over-trained regime, to 50–100+ tokens per active parameter, well past the Chinchilla ratio. Third — and this is the fact that makes the other two operational rather than academic — every exponent in the law is a function of your data quality. DeepSeek demonstrated this directly: refitting one functional form on "early in-house," "current in-house," and OpenWebText2 moved the optimal-allocation exponents from a=0.450/b=0.550 to a=0.524/b=0.476 to a=0.578/b=0.422 (DeepSeek-LLM). Better data shifts budget toward parameters. There is no transferable Chinchilla constant; there is only the law you fit on your own corpus.

This chapter assembles the modern multi-axis law piece by piece — granularity, sparsity, the comprehensive coupled form, data-constrained repetition, inference-aware over-training, precision, hyperparameters, and distillation — gives the fitted formulas you would actually regress, and closes with the concrete shape of a scaling-law campaign for the 10T/200B target. The recurring instruction is the same throughout: published exponents tell you the form; only your own sweep tells you the coefficients, and at 10^26 FLOPs the coefficients are where the money is.

12.1 The MoE multiplicative axes: granularity and sparsity

Granularity and sparsity are not knobs that perturb a dense law. They are independent multiplicative axes, and the literature converges on one through-line: the optimal value of each rises monotonically with training compute. A bigger budget wants finer experts and more total params per active param.

Granularity. Define G = d_ff / d_expert: the ratio of the dense FFN width to a single expert's width. G=1 is a "standard" MoE where each expert is a full dense FFN; G=8 slices that FFN into eight narrower experts and routes to more of them to keep active params fixed. The Krajewski/DeepSeek line (Krajewski 2024) fits

L(N, D, G) = c + (g/G^γ + a)·N^(−α) + b·D^(−β)

with, for E=64 experts: a=18.1, α=0.115, b=30.8, β=0.147, g=2.1, γ=0.58, c=0.47. Granularity enters only the parameter term, as a multiplicative discount g/G^γ. Because γ=0.58 < 1, returns to granularity are sublinear but the fit is unbounded — there is no internal optimum, so compute-optimal G simply climbs with budget: G=8 at 64×100M, G=16 at 1–3B active, G=32 at 7B, G=64 by 300B/2.96T. The headline that paper sells is dramatic and partly real: at 10^25 FLOPs an optimized fine-grained MoE needs ~40× fewer FLOPs than a dense model for equal loss, and G=1 (expert = full FFN) is suboptimal at nearly every budget.

But the G=64 recommendation does not survive contact with a more complete law, and you should not build to it. The 2024 fit omits the costs that finer experts actually incur — the activated-parameter term, the shared-expert contribution, and the routing plus all-to-all communication that fine granularity multiplies. Add those and the optimum collapses, as §12.2 shows.

Sparsity as its own axis. Frantar's pre-MoE-hype result (Frantar 2023) is the under-cited origin of the key fact, and it is architecture-general (fit on T5/C4 and ViT/JFT, not just MoE):

L(S, N, D) = (a_S·(1−S)^b_S + c_S)·N^(−b_N) + (a_D/D)^b_D + c

with a_S=16.8, b_S=0.722, c_S=45.0, b_N=0.245, b_D=0.203, c=0.651. Here S is the sparsity fraction. The load-bearing conclusion: optimal sparsity increases with training data — roughly 50% sparsity is optimal at 2–3× Chinchilla training length, and it keeps climbing past that. Apple's "Parameters vs FLOPs" (Yun 2025) restates this for MoE with S=(E−K)/E:

L = a·N^(−α) + b·D^(−β) + c·(1−S)^(−λ) + d·(1−S)^(−δ)·N^(−γ) + e

and pushes the result to its aggressive limit: at fixed training compute, raising S while proportionally growing total params always lowers loss. Optimal sparsity approaches ~0.9 and trends toward 1.0 for large models; as S rises, optimal total N rises and optimal active N falls. The practical implication for the build is direct. DeepSeek-V3 sits at 37B/671B ≈ 0.945 sparsity. A 200B-active / 10T-total model is S ≈ 0.98 — meaningfully sparser than V3, and exactly consistent with "sparser wins as you scale." If anything the law argues you are under-sparse: at this compute scale, the optimum may want even more total params behind your 200B active.

The two axes are coupled through compute. More budget → finer G and higher S and more tokens, simultaneously. Treating them independently (sweeping G at fixed S, or vice versa) will give you a saddle, not the optimum. You need the coupled form.

One more subtlety the separable laws hide: the granularity and sparsity optima interact through the routing cost. Finer experts (higher G) and higher sparsity both increase the number of experts E, which increases all-to-all token-routing traffic and load-balancing variance. The Frantar and Apple laws price the statistical benefit of sparsity but not the systems cost of the routing it implies; the comprehensive law's collapse of G_opt from 64 to ~7 is largely the systems cost reasserting itself. This is why the right place to fit is the coupled form with real wall-clock-equivalent FLOPs, not an idealized FLOP count that pretends routing is free.

12.2 The comprehensive coupled law — fit against this one

The single most useful template for the 10T target is the first law to couple all five MoE axes in one fitted form (Cao 2025):

L(N, D, N_a, G, S) = (eG + f/G + mS² + nS)·(1/N^α + k/N_a^α + h·N_a/N)
                     + a/N^α + b/D^β + c/N_a^α + ε

with e=0.1577, f=7.2446, m=5.1395, n=−3.2363, k=0.0013, h=0.0450, a=38.05, α=0.2383, b=27129, β=0.4694, c=31.10, ε=1.8182. Here N is total params, N_a active params, D tokens, G granularity, S the shared-expert ratio. Two facts from this fit should reset your priors.

(1) MoE exponents are not dense exponents with a substitution. This law gives β ≈ 0.47 and α ≈ 0.24, versus dense Chinchilla's roughly 0.67/0.35. The data exponent is markedly steeper and the parameter exponent shallower. Concretely: MoE rewards tokens more and raw parameters less than a dense intuition predicts — another vector pointing at over-training (§12.3) and at not over-spending on total param count beyond what sparsity buys.

(2) Optimal granularity is ~7, not ~64. Minimizing this coupled law yields G_opt ≈ 6.78 (build to G ∈ [7,9], allow up to 12) and a shared-expert ratio around 0.31. That is not a lone theoretical result — it is the independently-discovered production frontier. DeepSeek-V3.1 uses G=9, Kimi-K2 uses G=9, Qwen3-235B-A22B uses G=8. Three frontier labs converged on G=8–9 without coordinating, because once you charge for activated params, shared experts, and all-to-all routing, the G=64 of the 2024 law is a mirage. Build recommendation: G ∈ [8,12], 1–2 shared experts. Do not build G=32–64. The shared experts matter: at ~0.31 shared ratio you keep one or two always-on experts that capture the common-token computation, letting the routed experts specialize and reducing routing-induced variance.

A complementary lens is "efficiency leverage" (Ling team 2025), which reframes the whole question as EL = C_dense / C_moe, the dense-to-MoE compute ratio for equal loss:

EL(A, G, C) = Â^(α + γ·(log G)² + β·log G),   α = a + d·log C

with a=1.23, d=−0.0761, γ=0.0167, β=−0.117, where A is the activation ratio. The crucial term is α = a + d·log C: the d·log C dependence means MoE's efficiency advantage amplifies with compute. The bigger your budget, the more the sparse architecture pays off relative to dense — which is the entire economic thesis of a 100k-GB200 run. Their validated pilot, Ling-mini-beta (0.85B active / 17.5B total, 3.4% activation, G=12, 1 shared expert), hit >7× efficiency versus a 6.1B dense baseline at equal loss on 1T tokens, with optimal G ≈ 12 and the finding that lower activation is always better, down to 0.8%. For the 200B-active build at ~2% activation, this is corroborating evidence that you are in the right sparsity neighborhood and could defensibly go sparser.

12.3 Inference-aware over-training: train smaller and longer

Compute-optimality minimizes loss at fixed training FLOPs. That is the wrong objective for a model you will serve a billion times. Sardana & Frankle (2024) replace it with lifetime cost:

minimize   6·N·D_tr + 2·N·D_inf    subject to   L(N, D_tr) = ℓ

and a real-cost version that weights each term by hardware utilization:

6·N·D_tr·C_tr/MFU_tr  +  2·N·C_inf·(D_inp/MFU_inp + D_out/MFU_out)

The conclusion is sharp and one-directional: with large inference volume you train a smaller model on more tokens. Across 47 models from 150M to 6B they pushed to 10,000 tokens per parameter with no loss flat-lining — fifty times the Chinchilla ratio, and the curve still had not bent. The penalty for ignoring this is large and counterintuitive: their 70B example "costs 36% more than cost-optimal" despite using only 1.3% more training FLOPs, because compute-optimality picked too large a model that is then expensive to serve forever.

For the 10T/200B build this settles the token budget. A 200B-active model is in the cost-optimal zone at 50–100+ tokens per active parameter — i.e. tens of trillions of tokens, not the ~4T that naive Chinchilla (≈20 tok/active-param) would prescribe. This is the reason your scaling-law campaign must not optimize min-loss-at-fixed-FLOPs. You are fitting L(N, D) to then plug into a lifetime-cost objective, and the optimum lives far to the right of the IsoFLOP frontier's tangent point. Practically: pin total/active params from the MoE sparsity argument, then ride D out to wherever the marginal training FLOP stops beating the marginal inference saving — which, at frontier inference volumes, is very far indeed.

Note the MoE-specific accounting subtlety: the 2·N·D_inf inference term should use active params for the dense-equivalent forward FLOPs (you only pay for routed experts per token), while the 6·N·D_tr training term also runs on active params per token but must carry the total-param memory and all-to-all communication footprint that determines your achievable MFU. The decoupling of total from active params is precisely what makes MoE attractive under this objective: you get the representational capacity of 10T params at the inference FLOP cost of 200B, so the inference term in the lifetime cost is ~50× smaller than a dense model of equal capacity would incur. This is the quantitative core of why a 100k-GB200 owner builds sparse and over-trains hard rather than building a smaller dense model — the sparsity multiplies the inference-amortization argument, not just the training one.

There is a tension here you must hold in mind, surfaced in §12.6: over-training is exactly what makes a checkpoint fragile under cheap post-training quantization. You cannot independently maximize over-training and minimize inference precision.

12.4 Data-constrained scaling: the binding constraint at 10T

Here is the collision. The sparsity argument wants more total params. The inference-aware argument wants tens of T of tokens. At 200B active, Chinchilla-optimal is ~4T unique tokens; the over-trained, inference-aware target wants far more. But only ~15–20T high-quality unique tokens realistically exist. Data, not compute, is the binding constraint of this build, and Muennighoff's data-constrained law (2023) becomes the operative object.

Repeated tokens are worth less than fresh ones, and the law quantifies the decay. Effective data from R_D repetitions of U_D unique tokens:

D' = U_D + U_D·R_D*·(1 − e^(−R_D / R_D*)),     R_D* ≈ 15.4

and an analogous diminishing return on "excess" parameters:

N' = U_N + U_N·R_N*·(1 − e^(−R_N / R_N*)),     R_N* ≈ 5.3

The operating rules fall straight out of the constants:

  • ≤4 epochs of repetition are nearly free — repeated-token loss sits within noise of unique-token loss.
  • Returns decay sharply past R_D* ≈ 15 and are effectively nil by ~40 epochs.
  • Because R_N* < R_D* (5.3 < 15.4), in the data-constrained regime you should scale epochs faster than parameters — the inverse of the Chinchilla allocation. Repeating data buys more than adding params once you are data-bound.

For the build: plan ~5T unique high-quality tokens at ≤4 epochs ≈ ~20T effective tokens before repetition decay bites hard. Beyond that, marginal compute is better spent on data acquisition, cleaning, and synthetic generation than on naively growing parameters — data sourcing is a compute-equivalent lever and should be budgeted as one. A useful nuance: larger datasets tolerate more repetition before decay (2025 follow-up), so the 4-epoch rule is a floor for a clean corpus, not a hard ceiling for a large one. The campaign should fit R_D* and R_N* on your corpus rather than inheriting 15.4/5.3 — these too are quality-dependent.

12.5 Precision scaling and the FP4/GB200 angle

Precision is now an axis of the loss law, and on GB200 it is the axis with the most money attached. Kumar et al. (2024), across 465+ runs, model an effective parameter count that degrades as precision drops:

N_eff(P) = N·(1 − e^(−P/γ))³     (when weight/activation/KV precisions match)
L = A·N_eff^(−α) + B·D^(−β) + E + δ_PTQ

Two results bind the precision plan:

  1. Compute-optimal training precision is ~7–8 bits when N, D, and P are jointly optimized. FP8 is near-optimal; BF16 is over-precise at scale — you are paying for bits that buy no loss. This is the theoretical license for low-precision pretraining.
  2. PTQ degradation δ_PTQ grows with D/N. The more you over-train — which §12.3 mandates — the worse the model tolerates cheap post-training quantization at inference. Over-training and aggressive low-precision inference are in direct tension; you cannot have both for free.

For GB200 specifically, the relevant evidence is NVIDIA's NVFP4 pretraining recipe (2025) — pretraining, not just inference quant. NVFP4 uses E2M1 elements with FP8 (E4M3) scales over 16-element micro-blocks and an FP32 tensor-global scale. Stable FP4 pretraining required a specific stack of tricks:

  • Random Hadamard transforms (16×16) on the Wgrad inputs, to spread outliers across the block before quantization.
  • Stochastic rounding for gradients only — deterministic rounding on the forward pass, stochastic on the backward, to keep gradient estimates unbiased.
  • 2D 16×16 weight scaling for consistency between forward and backward.
  • ~16% of linear layers kept in BF16 — specifically the first 2 and last 8 blocks, which carry disproportionate sensitivity.
  • BF16 for the last ~18% of training tokens — without this the loss gap widens from <1% to ~1.5% during the decay phase; the FP4 numerics that are fine in the stable phase hurt precisely where the WSD decay is recovering loss.

The result: a 12B model on 10T tokens hit MMLU-pro 62.58% versus 62.62% for FP8 — a tie. Nemotron 3 (2025) pushed NVFP4 to 25T tokens on a hybrid Mamba-MoE, evidence the recipe scales to the token horizon this build needs.

Build implication. FP4 bulk GEMMs are defensible on GB200 — that is the whole reason to buy Blackwell. But budget BF16 for the head and tail layers (~16%) and for the final ~15–20% of tokens, and treat the precision schedule as part of the WSD decay plan. And crucially: because δ_PTQ grows with D/N and you are over-training hard, do not plan aggressive INT4 PTQ on the over-trained checkpoint — the very over-training that makes the model cheap to serve also makes it the worst candidate for the cheapest inference quantization. The capacity to be quantized at inference is something you spend by over-training.

A note on the hardware roofline these FLOPs run against: NVIDIA's headline 720 PFLOP/s FP8 / 360 PFLOP/s BF16 per GB200 NVL72 rack are with-sparsity (2:4) figures — the page footnotes "Dense is one-half sparse spec shown." Dense peak is 360 PFLOP/s FP8 and 180 PFLOP/s BF16 per rack (~4.5 / ~2.25 PFLOP/s per B200 GPU), so a 100,000-GPU cluster is **~0.45 EFLOP/s dense FP8 peak**, not ~1.0 EFLOP/s (the EFLOP figure only holds counting sparsity, which is not meaningful for dense training FLOPs). Realistic large-MoE FP8 MFU is ~35–40% for 2025–2026, lower for heavy-routing configs (NVIDIA GB200 NVL72). Reference every FLOP estimate against the dense peak.

12.6 Hyperparameter scaling laws: LR, batch size, weight decay

You cannot tune learning rate and batch size by hand at 10T tokens — a single bad LR wastes a run worth more than a small company. The HP scaling laws let you fit η(N,D) and B(D) from cheap small-scale proxies and extrapolate. Three converging fits matter.

DeepSeek-LLM (2024) fits against compute C directly:

η_opt = 0.3118·C^(−0.125),    B_opt = 0.2920·C^(0.3271)

and — importantly at 200B scale — uses non-embedding FLOPs/token M = 72·n_layer·d_model² + 12·n_layer·d_model·l_seq as the scale variable instead of N, because the 6N bookkeeping distorts at large model size where embeddings and attention shift the real per-token compute.

StepLaw / Predictable Scale (2025) is the empirical backbone — 3,700 LLMs, ~1M H800-hours, 100T tokens of sweeping:

η(N, D) = 1.79·N^(−0.713)·D^(0.307),    B(D) = 0.58·D^(0.571)

Two properties make this directly usable. The optimum is a convex plateau, not a sharp peak — being somewhat off-optimal costs little, so coarse grids suffice. And the prediction error is <0.5% across sparsity levels, meaning the law transfers to MoE. This is the law to extrapolate your LR and batch from.

MoE-specific HP fit (Ling team 2025):

η_opt = 1.1576·C^(−0.1529),    B_opt = 0.0694·C^(0.3644)

The MoE correction relative to dense at equal compute is larger batch, slightly lower LR. The mechanism: each expert is updated only on the routed subset of a batch, so the per-expert effective batch is smaller than the global batch — you compensate by enlarging the global batch and softening the LR to keep per-expert update noise in the same regime. At 200B active with top-k routing over G=8–12, this correction is not negligible; do not inherit a dense LR/batch schedule.

Weight decay and its batch coupling are the usually-ignored axis. "Power Lines" (2025) extends HP scaling to weight decay and its coupling with batch size, and at 10T tokens the effective-LR / WD interaction materially shapes the late-training loss that the WSD decay phase is trying to recover. The Power Scheduler line (2024) gives batch- and token-agnostic LR schedules that survive the extrapolation.

WSD makes all of this affordable at 10T scale. MiniCPM's Warmup-Stable-Decay scheduler (2024) decouples the stable phase's LR from a pre-committed token count: you train at constant LR through the stable phase, then decay to recover most of the loss, and crucially you can fork annealing from a single stable checkpoint to run cheap proxy sweeps without retraining the stable trunk. This is what let MiniCPM measure its own data-to-model optimum at ~192× (versus Chinchilla's ~20×) rather than assume it — a tenfold disagreement with Chinchilla, discovered only because WSD made the measurement cheap. For the 10T campaign, WSD forks are how you run the dozens of IsoFLOP configs in §12.8 without paying full freight for each.

12.7 Distillation scaling laws — for the serving fleet, not the flagship

Distillation has its own law, and it tells you not to distill the flagship. Busbridge et al. (Apple 2025) find that student loss is governed by the teacher's cross-entropy L_T, not the teacher's size, through a broken power law that produces a capacity gap: past an optimum, a stronger teacher actively hurts the student, because the teacher's KL target grows faster than the student can fit it. The optimal teacher is only "slightly larger than the student," then the benefit plateaus.

The hard truth follows: supervised learning always wins given enough student compute and tokens. Distillation pays only when the teacher already exists or is amortized across many students (where it fits at ~1% error). For the 200B flagship this means distillation is a tool for the downstream serving fleet — the smaller models you deploy in cost-sensitive contexts — sized with a ~1×-student teacher, not a scheme for compressing the full 200B into a tiny student. Do not budget the flagship's quality on a distillation shortcut; budget it on supervised pretraining tokens, and reserve distillation for spinning out the small-serving variants once the flagship exists and its inference cost is already sunk.

12.8 Running the scaling-law campaign for the 10T/200B target

Everything above is prologue to one decision: how do you actually fit the law that sizes a 10^26-FLOP run? Labs keep these laws private not as theater but because a 1% loss-prediction error at this scale is tens of millions of dollars of mis-allocated compute, and the exponents are corpus-specific. Here is the campaign.

(1) Fit your own law on your own data. This is non-negotiable — DeepSeek's quality-exponent result means published a/b/α/β are wrong for your corpus. Run an IsoFLOP/IsoLoss sweep at 1e18–3e20 FLOPs over ~30–50 MoE configs, varying active params N_a, granularity G ∈ {4…16}, sparsity S, and shared-expert count. Fit the coupled form L(N, D, N_a, G, S) from §12.2 (Cao 2025), or your own variant, with Huber loss in log-space (Huber for robustness to the noisy small-scale runs, log-space because the law is multiplicative). Use IsoFLOP profiles — train each config to a fixed FLOP budget, read the loss minimum across the param/token tradeoff — and stack several FLOP levels to pin the exponents.

(2) Tune HPs with StepLaw/WSD proxies. Extrapolate η(N,D) and B(D) from small-scale fits (StepLaw), exploiting the convex plateau so a coarse grid suffices. Apply the MoE correction (larger batch, lower LR). Use WSD annealing forks from a shared stable checkpoint to make the per-config cost cheap.

(3) Apply the data-constrained correction. Set the unique-token budget (~5T high-quality), cap epochs at ≤4 (Muennighoff), fit your own R_D*/R_N*, and treat data acquisition as a compute-equivalent lever in the budget — every clean trillion tokens you source is worth more than the parameters that trillion of FLOPs would otherwise buy.

(4) Re-optimize allocation under the inference-aware objective. Do not stop at compute-optimal. Plug the fitted L(N, D) into the lifetime-cost objective 6N·D_tr + 2N·D_inf (Sardana & Frankle) with your real inference volume, and read off fewer active params / more tokens than naive compute-optimal — the optimum sits far to the right of the IsoFLOP tangent.

(5) Choose precision per the precision law. FP8/NVFP4 for bulk GEMMs, BF16 for head/tail layers and the final ~15–20% decay (NVFP4), and avoid INT4 PTQ on the over-trained checkpoint because δ_PTQ grows with your D/N (Kumar).

The output of this campaign is not a single number. It is a fitted five-dimensional surface plus a precision schedule plus an HP extrapolation, all conditioned on your corpus. Build to G=8–12, 1–2 shared experts, S≈0.98, tens of T tokens at ≤4 epochs of a ~5T-token clean core, FP4 bulk with BF16 head/tail/decay, and LR/batch from a StepLaw fit with the MoE correction. Anyone handing you a Chinchilla constant for this run is handing you a foil.
