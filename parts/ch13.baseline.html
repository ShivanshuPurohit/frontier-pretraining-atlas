13. Data curation in 2026: from Common Crawl to taxonomied corpora, custom crawlers, and active selection

For a 10T-total / 200B-active MoE on 100k GB200, data is not a preprocessing step you finish before the run — it is the binding constraint and the single highest-leverage variable in the whole campaign. The architecture and the systems plan are, by mid-2026, largely solved problems (G=8–12, S≈0.98, FP4 bulk GEMMs, WSD schedule); what separates a frontier model from a mediocre one trained on the same FLOP budget is the corpus. This chapter is the data-engineering counterpart to the scaling-law campaign in §7 and §8: it argues that the field has undergone a structural phase change — from monolithic filtering, where one classifier makes one keep/discard decision per document and 90% of the web is thrown away forever, to queryable taxonomied corpora plus model-aware active selection, where annotation is decoupled from selection and the discarded tail is recycled rather than deleted. We walk the open-corpus lineage (FineWeb, DCLM, Nemotron-CC, RefinedWeb, RedPajama-v2, TxT360), the Essential-Web taxonomy template, the classifier/dedup/decontam stack, data mixing (DoReMi → RegMix → CLIMB → online bandits), synthetic data (WRAP → BeyondWeb → REWIRE → Nemotron-CC-Math), and the genuine frontier — datamodels, influence functions, learnability, and per-token selection — before specifying a concrete 10T-token stack.

13.1 The structural shift: decouple annotation from selection

The defining move of 2025–2026 is to stop baking a single quality decision into the artifact. Pre-2025 web corpora — FineWeb-Edu, DCLM-baseline, Nemotron-CC — score every document with a classifier, keep the top X%, and the discarded 90% survives only as a URL and a scalar score. That is lossy in a way that compounds: every time you want to change the mix (upsample medical, downsample boilerplate, concentrate math late in training), you must retrain a bespoke high-recall classifier and re-filter trillions of tokens, a weeks-to-months loop. Essential-Web v1.0 (Essential AI, arXiv 2506.14111, 24T tokens, 23.6B documents) inverts this. Every document carries the EAI-Taxonomy: 12 categories in 5 groupings — (1) FDC, Free Decimal Correspondence, a public-domain Dewey analogue with 3 hierarchical levels (e.g. 5→Science, 51→Mathematics, 512→Algebra); (2) Bloom (Cognitive Process × Knowledge Domain); (3) Document Type (V1 broad / V2 fine); (4) Content Quality = Reasoning Depth + Educational Level (the FineWeb-Edu axis) + Technical Correctness; (5) Extraction (HTML artifacts + Missing Content). The corpus contains 14.1M unique primary-label combinations and 1.2B primary/secondary combos, which is the whole point: selection becomes a WHERE clause over columns, written in under fifteen minutes, instead of a classifier you train and validate.

The empirical payoff is that pure SQL-style filters over the taxonomy produce datasets competitive with or beating task-specialized SOTA pipelines: math −8.0% vs SOTA, web code +14.3%, STEM +24.5%, medical +8.6% (the medical figure is +3.2pp / 8.6% over DCLM-baseline, +4.9pp over TheBlueScrubs), all reached without training a single domain classifier (arXiv 2506.14111). For a 100k-GB200 run this is the correct template — but it is a template, not a ceiling. The recommendation is unambiguous: pre-annotate your entire crawl once with a rich, orthogonal taxonomy, then drive every per-stage mix, curriculum decision, and domain upsampling via cheap queries. Do not re-filter 10T tokens each time the mix changes. And do not treat the EAI category set as the final word — extend it with axes your evals care about (license class, language, toxicity tier, code-vs-prose, instruction-density, paywall/provenance flag, recency bucket). The taxonomy is metadata you own; over-annotate it.

The load-bearing insight is the annotator economics. Essential AI chose the teacher by orthogonality, correctness, and expressivity, measured via inter-category normalized mutual information (NMI) on held-out sets, and Qwen2.5-32B-Instruct won over Qwen2.5-72B and DeepSeek-V3 — a larger teacher was not better for labeling. They then distilled it into EAI-Distill-0.5b, a fine-tuned Qwen2.5-0.5B-Instruct trained on ~104.6M Common Crawl documents labeled by the teacher (two annotation passes to add categories), reaching within 3% annotator agreement, 14% NMI, and 1% domain-recall of the 32B teacher. Full-corpus inference over 23.6B documents cost **~90k AMD MI300x GPU-hours** (512 MI300x, roughly one week), vLLM for the Qwen models, SGLang for DeepSeek-V3 (arXiv 2506.14111). The lesson for 10T-scale: a 0.5–1B distilled annotator makes whole-corpus rich labeling a one-off ~10⁵ GPU-hour cost — negligible against a 100k-GB200 pretrain that will burn 10⁷–10⁸ GPU-hours. There is no excuse not to over-annotate. The Essential-Web appendix even exposes the exact pre-classifier heuristic gates (frac_chars_dupe_Ngrams thresholds, top-2/3-gram fractions, word_count < 50) and the two-pass + NMI teacher-selection methodology (PDF) — a copyable spec for building your own distilled annotator rather than re-deriving it.

13.2 The open-corpus lineage and what each contributes

Before designing a stack, fix the reference points. The open web-corpus lineage is the empirical record of what filtering does and does not buy, and a frontier lab should treat each as a tested recipe rather than a finished dataset to consume.

Corpus Scale Core method Key result / lesson
RefinedWeb (2023) ~5T (600B released) Heuristic + MinHash, CC-only, no LLM classifier Web-only can match curated mixes; heuristics + strong dedup go far
RedPajama-v2 (2023) 30T tokens, 100B docs Raw CC + 40+ precomputed quality signals, no filtering applied The original "ship signals, let users filter" idea — proto-taxonomy
FineWeb / FineWeb-Edu (2406.17557) 15T / ~1.3T Linear regressor on Snowflake-arctic-embed-m, 460k Llama-3-70B edu scores, threshold ≥3 Edu-classifier filtering is the workhorse; F1≈82%; per-snapshot (not global) dedup
DCLM-baseline (2406.11794) 3.6T (from 240T pool) fastText on OH-2.5 + ELI5 positives, top-~10% 7B@2.6T → 64% MMLU; established the fastText-classifier baseline
Nemotron-CC (2412.02595) 6.3T (4.4T real + 1.9T synth) Classifier ensemble + rephrasing Recall fix: HQ subset beats DCLM by +5.6 MMLU @8B/1T with 4× more unique tokens
TxT360 (LLM360) 40.21B docs → 15T+ Global near-dedup of 99 CC snapshots + 14 curated sources, rich metadata Controlled upsampling beats FineWeb-15T; ~17% exact dupes removed
Dolma 3 / Olmo 3 (2512.13961) ~9.3T pool → ~5.9T mix Global dedup (Duplodocus) + quality-aware upsampling ≤7× Most fully-disclosed end-to-end SOTA stack; rebukes "discard 90%"

Two structural reads. First, the lineage bifurcates into a filtering school (RefinedWeb → DCLM → FineWeb-Edu: train a classifier, keep the top slice) and a metadata school (RedPajama-v2 → TxT360 → Essential-Web → Dolma 3: ship signals/labels, defer selection). The metadata school has won at the frontier because, as §13.6 makes precise, the selection decision is scale- and target-dependent and you cannot bake it once. Second, the absolute scales matter for a 10T MoE: DCLM-Pool is 240T raw CC tokens, FineWeb is 15T, RedPajama-v2 is 30T raw, Essential-Web is 24T labeled — but the unique high-quality yield after aggressive filtering and dedup is realistically only ~15–20T tokens across the whole open web. That ceiling, not the raw pool, is what your epoch budget must respect (§13.5).

A third read, easy to miss, is that these corpora are recipes plus ablations, and the ablations are the asset. DCLM's contribution was less the 3.6T-token artifact than DCLM-Bench — a controlled 1B/7B testbed that let the field A/B filtering choices on a fixed compute budget; FineWeb's was the methodical demonstration that per-snapshot dedup, a simple edu classifier, and good extraction beat elaborate hand-tuned heuristic cascades. A frontier lab should mine each release for its decision (what was kept, what was thrown away, and the measured downstream delta) and reproduce the decision on its own corpus, not ingest the tokens. The tokens are downstream-stale the moment your crawler and taxonomy diverge from theirs; the methodology is durable. This is also why the right internal artifact is not "our FineWeb" but a versioned, queryable, fully-annotated pool from which any of these recipes can be re-derived in an afternoon by writing a different query.

13.3 Quality classifiers: the recall problem and the fastText renaissance

The 2024 canon converged on two designs. FineWeb-Edu uses a linear regressor on Snowflake-arctic-embed-m embeddings, trained on ~410k of 460k Llama-3-70B-Instruct educational 0–5 annotations (an additive rubric — score points for distinct desiderata — which beats single-rating prompts), threshold ≥3, F1≈82%, yielding ~1.3T tokens from FineWeb's 15T (arXiv 2406.17557). DCLM-baseline uses a fastText classifier with OpenHermes-2.5 + r/ExplainLikeImFive (ELI5) instruction-formatted positives, top-~10% cut → 3.6T tokens, with a 7B@2.6T reaching 64% 5-shot MMLU (arXiv 2406.11794).

The recognized failure mode — stated explicitly in Essential-Web's related work — is that aggressive monolithic classifiers remove ~90% of the data, which is fatal for long-horizon training (Llama-3.1's 15T, your 10T-active run) because it destroys unique-token yield, and these classifiers lag badly on math and code where "educational prose" signal is a poor proxy. Nemotron-CC (arXiv 2412.02595, 6.3T tokens) is the canonical recall fix: ensemble the FineWeb-Edu and DCLM classifiers plus custom model-labeled classifiers, reducing reliance on any single heuristic → 4.4T unique real tokens + 1.9T synthetic; the high-quality subset beats DCLM by +5.6 MMLU at 8B/1T while keeping 4× more unique tokens. Ultra-FineWeb (arXiv 2505.05427, OpenBMB, 1T en + 120B zh) pushes efficient verification: bootstrap a fastText classifier with a verification step — does adding a candidate set actually lower validation loss in a short MiniCPM run? — making filtering cheap enough to iterate.

The net guidance for the frontier run is a division of labor: fastText is back as the inference-time workhorse (millions of docs/sec/CPU core, near-free over 10T tokens) while LLM-as-judge and large embedders supply the labels. Ensemble multiple classifiers for recall rather than trusting one aggressive cut, and verify a classifier by its downstream-loss effect, not its F1 — the Ultra-FineWeb verification loop and Essential-Web's NMI-based teacher selection are the same instinct: optimize the metric you actually care about (loss/eval), not a proxy. For a 200B-active MoE specifically, filter less aggressively than the 7B-proxy literature implies (§13.6) and lean on the recall-preserving ensemble + taxonomy rather than a top-10% guillotine.

13.4 Deduplication and decontamination

The standard dedup stack has four tiers, and a frontier corpus uses all of them:

  • Exact substring / suffix-array dedup — remove verbatim repeats; cheap, high-precision, the floor.
  • MinHash-LSH fuzzy dedup — the dominant near-duplicate method. FineWeb deliberately deduped per-snapshot, not globally, after finding that global dedup over-removes and the survivors skew toward low-quality boilerplate that happens to be unique (arXiv 2406.17557). This is a genuine design tension, see below.
  • Bloom-filter dedup (BFF) — DCLM's approach; memory-efficient global dedup at pool scale.
  • Semantic dedup — SemDeDup (embed, cluster, drop near-duplicate embeddings), D4 (dedup + diversify via SSL-prototype pruning), and SoftDeDup (reweight by n-gram "data commonness" instead of hard removal). This tier catches paraphrase-level redundancy that MinHash misses.

TxT360 globally near-deduplicated 99 CC snapshots + 14 curated sources to 40.21B docs (~17% exact dupes removed), with rich per-document metadata enabling controlled upsampling into a 15T+ corpus that beats FineWeb-15T (LLM360). The 2026 frontier reference is Olmo 3 / Dolma 3 (arXiv 2512.13961): aggressive global dedup at trillion-token scale via their new "Duplodocus" tool, plus quality-aware upsampling that repeats high-quality docs up to 7× rather than flat top-k filtering — a direct rebuke of the "throw away 90%" school. The FineWeb-vs-Dolma tension on global dedup resolves as follows: global dedup is correct when paired with quality-aware upsampling and rich metadata (so you re-add the high-quality survivors deliberately), and harmful when it is the only knob (you over-remove and keep the unique junk). For a 10T MoE, run global dedup to establish the unique-token inventory, then upsample by taxonomy class — do not let global dedup silently set your mix.

Decontamination remains GPT-3-era 13-gram overlap as the floor, but it is known-leaky to paraphrase. Modern pipelines (Olmo 3 emphasizes "stronger decontamination") add embedding-similarity and LLM-semantic checks against the full eval suite — not just public benchmarks but your private evals, since a leaked private eval is undetectable from the outside and corrupts your own go/no-go decisions. The decontamination ordering matters operationally: it must run after synthesis, not before, because a rephraser pointed at a document that happens to paraphrase a benchmark question will manufacture fresh contamination that no 13-gram filter on the source corpus would have caught. Re-run the full decontam sweep after every synthetic-generation and translation pass, and decontaminate the synthetic tokens against the eval suite, not just the raw web.

For a 200B-active MoE, dedup matters more than for a dense model: Muennighoff data-constrained scaling shows repeats decay in value (≈4 epochs is the practical degradation point for dense; MoE folklore puts it lower, ~2 epochs, because sparse experts overfit their routed subset faster), so unique-token yield is the binding constraint and every duplicate you fail to remove is an epoch you cannot afford (arXiv 2305.16264). Concretely, Muennighoff's effective-data law is D' = U_D + U_D·R_D*·(1 − e^(−R_D/R_D*)) with R_D* ≈ 15.4 for the dense regime: returns on repetition are nearly free up to ~4 epochs, decay sharply past R_D*, and are essentially nil by ~40 epochs. The corollary for the data pipeline is that a duplicate you fail to catch does not merely waste a slot — it inflates your measured unique-token count, so you under-acquire and discover the shortfall only when the loss curve flattens early. Budget dedup recall as conservatively as you budget GPU-hours.

13.5 Why frontier labs run private crawlers

Three converging pressures make a proprietary crawler mandatory by mid-2026, not optional.

(1) Coverage and freshness. Common Crawl is a sparse, lagging sample of any given domain — it captures a thin slice of each site at irregular intervals. Labs want full-site, near-real-time crawls. Anthropic's ClaudeBot at its June-2025 peak was crawling on the order of 70,900 pages per referred visitor, an extraction footprint far beyond CC's, precisely because CC's per-site depth is shallow.

(2) Access collapse. More than 35% of the top-1000 sites now block GPTBot — a 7× rise since August 2023 — and CCBot is the single most-blocked crawler, so Common Crawl's effective coverage is actively declining (Cloudflare). Cloudflare's mid-2025 flip to permission-default (block AI crawlers unless allowed) and the emerging RSL (Really Simple Licensing) standard adopted by Reddit, Yahoo, Medium, and People Inc. push the whole web toward pay-per-crawl. A corpus built on CC is built on a shrinking, increasingly stale base.

(3) Legal and quality provenance. A November 2025 investigation showed Common Crawl ships paywalled news despite claims to the contrary — a liability a frontier lab prefers to own via direct licenses rather than inherit. The economics are now explicit: Reddit↔︎OpenAI is reportedly ~$70M/yr for licensed access.

The conclusion for a 100k-GB200 program: run a proprietary full-coverage crawler for freshness, depth, and clean provenance; license the high-value walled gardens directly (Reddit and forums, news, Stack/code, scientific publishers); and use Common Crawl only as a baseline and supplement, never as the spine. The crawler is also where you enforce provenance metadata — license class, robots/RSL status, paywall flag — that becomes a taxonomy axis and a legal audit trail. Pair direct PDF acquisition with olmOCR-class extraction for scientific literature, which Dolma 3 weights at 13.6% of its mix (§13.8) — a signal of how much premium clean text now comes from PDFs rather than HTML.

13.6 Synthetic data: rephrasing has won over de-novo textbooks

The decisive empirical result of 2025–2026 is that rephrasing existing web text beats generating textbooks from scratch at scale. This reverses the early Phi/Cosmopedia intuition and is now the consensus.

The lineage: WRAP established source-rephrasing with a ~3× training speedup. Cosmopedia (Mixtral-8x7B, 25B tokens, 30M files) is the open textbook-style baseline. BeyondWeb (DatologyAI, arXiv 2508.10975) is the trillion-scale lesson: targeted, diverse-strategy rephrasing — format transformation, QA-pair extraction, pedagogical-tone rewrite, content restructuring — beats RedPajama by +7.1pp and Nemotron-Synth by +2.6pp at 8B/180B tokens, 7.7× faster than RedPajama; a 3B-on-BeyondWeb beats an 8B-on-Cosmopedia. Three findings reframe the whole synthetic-data cost model:

  1. Rephraser quality saturates by ~3B params. Generator quality does not predict synthetic quality; weak ~3B models rephrase fine. You do not need your flagship to generate data.
  2. Strategy diversity is what sustains gains across trillion-token budgets. Single-strategy synthetic plateaus; diverse-strategy does not. The cost lever is prompt/strategy engineering, not generator size.
  3. The "Demystifying Synthetic Data" scaling study (arXiv 2510.01631) confirms rephrased web shows no degradation at high synthetic fraction, whereas textbook-style synthetic degrades — so you can push the synthetic fraction high if and only if it is rephrasing-based and strategy-diverse.

Nemotron-CC-v2 operationalizes this: Qwen3-30B-A3B rephrasing + Diverse-QA (5 prompt templates), translated into 15 languages for +10.0 Global-MMLU over multilingual-CC-only. Nemotron-CC-Math (arXiv 2508.15096, 133.26B tokens @3+, 52.32B @4+) is the must-copy math pipeline, and its key lesson is non-obvious: the bottleneck for web math is not the quality classifier but HTML extraction. Trafilatura and Resiliparse silently corrupt inline LaTeX and flatten code blocks. Nemotron-CC-Math renders HTML through Lynx — a 1992 text browser — to preserve equations, then uses Phi-4 to standardize to canonical LaTeX, delivering +4.8 MATH, +16.2 MBPP+ over FineMath-4+ with 5.5× more tokens. Most teams have not internalized this; for a math/code-heavy frontier MoE it is free quality.

REWIRE (Meta, arXiv 2506.04689) is the clearest "beat the data wall" lever: it recycles the filtered-out low-quality tail by rewriting those discarded documents, outperforming both raw-web and synthetic-only baselines across 10B/70B/300B budgets, with an optimal mix of recycled + raw beating either alone. For a unique-token-constrained 10T budget this is orthogonal to and stackable with rephrasing the kept data — instead of discarding 90%, rewrite the discarded tail into usable tokens. The synthetic strategy for the frontier run is therefore: keep the synthetic fraction high but diversity-driven, never a textbook monoculture; rephrase the kept web with ≥4 distinct strategies; REWIRE-recycle the filtered tail; render math/code through structure-preserving extraction; translate the best synthetic into the target language set.

13.7 The frontier beyond annotation: model-aware and per-token selection

Even a perfect taxonomy plus SQL is heuristic selection — it matches documents to a human notion of "high quality." The genuine research frontier is model-aware selection: choose data by how the learner uses it to perform on target tasks.

Heuristic similarity is provably suboptimal. DsDm (Engstrom et al., ICML 2024, PMLR v235) shows that selecting by similarity to "high-quality" sources can hurt downstream performance; datamodels / influence functions that predict how training on a document changes target-task loss win instead. MATES (Yu et al., NeurIPS 2024) trains a small data-influence model, periodically refreshed to track the evolving pretraining state (oracle = one-step reference-loss reduction), beating static selection on scaling curves — the key being that influence is non-stationary, so a single up-front score is wrong by mid-training.

Apple's BETR ("Language Models Improve When Pretraining Data Matches Target Tasks," arXiv 2507.12466) is the cleanest 2025 result and the most directly actionable: embed benchmark examples, score corpus documents by similarity to them, distill into a classifier — yielding a 2.1× compute multiplier over DCLM-baseline and 4.7× over unfiltered, validated across 500+ models from 10¹⁹ to 10²² FLOP. Its scaling-law finding is the one to burn in for a 200B-active build: larger models need less aggressive filtering. Selection strategy must be scale-adapted. Tuning your 10T/200B mix on a 7B proxy's optimal filter threshold will silently over-filter and waste frontier compute — the proxy will tell you to cut harder than the real model wants.

On the learnability / online axis: RHO-Loss (select tokens that are learnable-but-not-yet-learned, via a reference model's holdout loss) is the seed; ESLM (arXiv 2505.19893) does risk-averse per-token selective language modeling, pushing selection below the document granularity to the token level. The RHO-Loss criterion is worth stating precisely because it captures what document-level quality scoring misses: a token is worth training on when the training model's loss on it is high (it has not been learned) but a reference model's holdout loss on it is low (it is learnable and not noise) — high training loss alone selects mislabeled and inherently-random tokens, which is why naive hard-example mining fails. Per-token selection is the logical endpoint of the recall argument: instead of discarding a document wholesale, keep its high-signal spans and skip its boilerplate, navigation chrome, and license footers. This is still research-grade at trillion-token scale — the reference-model forward pass over 10T tokens is a real cost, and the gains shrink as the base corpus quality rises — but it is where the field is heading, and a cheap approximation (mask obvious boilerplate spans flagged by the Extraction taxonomy axis) is already deployable today.

Per-domain mixing has matured well past DoReMi (Group-DRO to derive domain weights from a proxy). RegMix (arXiv 2502.10341 lineage) fits a regression over many small proxy models trained on random mixtures, matching or exceeding DoReMi at ~10% of the compute. WebOrganizer (weborganizer.allen.ai, Wettig et al.) organizes the web into 24 topic × 24 format domains and, combined with RegMix, shows that topic-defined domains beat source-defined domains for mixing — the "Topic Over Source" result (arXiv 2502.16802) — which is directly relevant because your taxonomy already gives you topic columns; mix over those, not over URLs. CLIMB / Nemotron-CLIMB (NVIDIA, arXiv 2504.13161) does k-means-cluster → proxy-eval → regression-predictor → iterative bootstrap of the mixture, with a 1B model +2.0% over Llama-3.2-1B at 400B tokens. Online Data Mixing (a multi-armed bandit over domains during training) closes the loop for a run far too expensive to re-sweep — you adjust the mixture from live loss signals rather than committing it up front. For a single 10T run you cannot afford a full mixture sweep at scale, so the practical recipe is: RegMix/CLIMB to set the initial topic-format mixture from proxies, then an online bandit to adapt it during the run.

13.8 A SOTA 10T-token data stack (June 2026)

Putting it together, here is the concrete stack for the 10T-total / 200B-active target. The numbers throughout are governed by the binding constraint from §13.4: realistically ~15–20T unique high-quality tokens exist, MoEs tolerate fewer epochs than dense models, so the unique-token budget — not the raw pool — is what you optimize, and acquisition/synthesis/recycling are compute-equivalent levers for beating the wall.

Stage Action Tooling / reference Frontier choice for 10T/200B
1. Acquire Proprietary full-coverage crawler + licensed walled gardens + CC baseline ClaudeBot-class crawler; RSL-licensed Reddit/news/code/science CC is a supplement, not the spine; provenance metadata captured at crawl
2. Extract Structure-preserving extraction Lynx for math/code, olmOCR for PDFs Never let Trafilatura mangle LaTeX; PDFs are a premium source
3. Dedup Global trillion-token dedup, all four tiers MinHash-LSH + suffix-array + BFF + SemDeDup; Duplodocus-class Global dedup to set unique inventory, then upsample by class
4. Annotate Once, into a rich orthogonal taxonomy EAI-Taxonomy template + distilled 0.5–1B annotator (~10⁵ GPU-hr) Queryable metadata, not a single score; extend EAI axes
5. Select SQL filters + model-aware overlays EAI WHERE clauses + BETR/datamodel influence, scale-adapted Filter less hard than 7B-proxy implies; influence for target alignment
6. Synthesize Diverse-strategy rephrasing + tail recycling + Diverse-QA BeyondWeb principles, REWIRE, Nemotron-CC-Math, multilingual High synthetic fraction, diversity-driven, ~3B rephraser suffices
7. Mix Topic-format mixing + quality-aware upsampling + curriculum RegMix/CLIMB → online bandit; Dolma-3 upsampling ≤7× Math/code/reasoning concentrated late, after LR decay
8. Decontaminate 13-gram floor + embedding/LLM-semantic Against the full eval suite incl. private evals Re-run after every synthetic-generation pass

Reference proportions. Dolma 3's ~5.9T mix is the most fully-disclosed SOTA baseline: CC 76.1%, olmOCR science PDFs 13.6%, Stack-Edu code 6.89%, FineMath-3+ 2.56%, arXiv 0.86% (arXiv 2512.13961). A frontier 200B-active MoE should shift materially away from that distribution — Dolma is deliberately CC-heavy for reproducibility and license-cleanliness, not because 76% web is optimal. The frontier shift is toward (a) a much larger code and math fraction (these carry the reasoning signal that MoE experts specialize on, and the granularity/sparsity scaling laws reward specialization), (b) a high synthetic fraction of strategy-diverse rephrased + recycled tokens, and (c) license-clean premium sources (licensed forums, news, publishers) replacing the riskiest CC tail. A plausible target mix: ~40% filtered+recycled web, ~20% code, ~12% math, ~20% strategy-diverse synthetic (rephrased web + Diverse-QA + REWIRE tail), ~8% premium licensed + science PDFs — tuned by RegMix proxies and adapted online.

Curriculum and the late-stage decay. The mixture is not static across training. The strong, repeatable finding is that the LR decay phase wastes your best data if you place it early — high-quality math/code/reasoning and the cleanest synthetic should be concentrated late, after the learning rate has decayed, where the model can actually absorb it (this couples directly to the WSD scheduler from §8: fork the annealing phase and feed it your premium mix). With ~5T unique high-quality tokens repeated at ≤4 epochs you reach ~20T effective tokens before repetition decay bites — and quality-aware upsampling (Dolma-3 style, repeating the best documents up to 7×) is the mechanism for getting there without flat top-k throwing away recall.

Pipeline cost and where it runs. The whole stack is cheap relative to the pretrain, which is the reason there is no excuse to skimp on it. Annotation is the largest single line item and it is ~10⁵ GPU-hours for a distilled 0.5–1B model over tens of billions of documents — at most a few tenths of a percent of a 10⁷–10⁸ GPU-hour pretrain. Dedup, extraction, and fastText classification are overwhelmingly CPU work and run on a commodity cluster off the critical accelerator path; the GPU-resident costs are the annotator, the synthetic rephraser (a ~3B model, embarrassingly parallel, run with vLLM/SGLang at high batch), and the optional reference-model forward pass for influence/learnability scoring. The right operational posture is to treat the data pipeline as a standing service that runs continuously and feeds a versioned pool, not a one-shot job before the run: the crawler is always crawling, the annotator re-labels new documents as they land, and the mixture is re-queried per training stage. The 100k-GB200 fleet should never idle waiting on data, and with the pipeline decoupled and CPU-bound it never will — the binding constraint is unique-token supply, not pipeline throughput.

The through-line. Every recommendation here points the same direction: stop deleting data. Annotate once and select by query; recall-preserve with ensembles and taxonomy; recycle the filtered tail with REWIRE; upsample quality rather than discarding the rest; adapt selection to your model's scale (filter less at 200B); and treat synthetic as a strategy-diversity problem, not a generator-size problem. The "discard 90%" orthodoxy was a small-model artifact. For a 10T/200B MoE on 100k GB200, where unique tokens are the binding constraint and acquisition/synthesis/recycling are the only ways past the data wall, the corpus is engineered, not filtered — and the lab that owns its crawler, its taxonomy, and its model-aware selection loop owns the variable that the FLOP budget cannot buy.
