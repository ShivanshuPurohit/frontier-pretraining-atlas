19. Public throughput signals: MLPerf Training, MFU leaderboards, and reading the tea leaves

The public throughput record is a hall of mirrors. MLPerf Training headlines — "10 minutes on 5,120 Blackwell GPUs" — are engineered to be read as performance leadership, and they are real numbers produced by real silicon. But every one of them is a sub-convergence sprint on a dense model, tuned for the test, with the precision story laundered into a single time-to-train figure. For a planner sizing a 10T-total / 200B-active MoE on 100,000 GB200 GPUs, the danger is not that these numbers are wrong; it is that they are right about the wrong question. They answer "how fast can clean silicon hit a fixed loss target on a 405B dense model over ~450 steps?" Your question is "what sustained MFU will a heavy-routing MoE hold over 10T tokens across a multi-week run on a 1,389-rack fault domain?" The gap between those two questions is the entire content of this chapter. We will extract what MLPerf legitimately tells you (a per-GPU peak-efficiency ceiling and a clean generation multiplier), name what it structurally conceals (MoE all-to-all, failure blast radius, long-horizon convergence, precision management at scale), and then re-anchor your planning numbers on the more honest signals: SemiAnalysis's sustained-MFU tracking, framework benchmark tables, and the single most load-bearing public datapoint of all — DeepSeek-V3's ~21% FP8 MFU over a full 14.8T-token MoE run.

19.1 What MLPerf Training v5.0/v5.1 actually measure

MLPerf Training is a closed-loop, fixed-quality benchmark: every submission trains to a predetermined eval-loss or accuracy target and reports only time-to-train. It deliberately publishes no MFU, no throughput, no TFLOPS. This is a defensible methodological choice — it makes submissions comparable across wildly different hardware — but it means every efficiency number you want must be backed out, and the back-out is fragile for reasons we will make precise.

The suite has been actively reshaped to track the frontier. v5.0 (results 2025-06-04) executed the pivotal substitution: it retired the long-standing GPT-3 175B pretraining benchmark and introduced Llama 3.1 405B pretraining as "the largest model in the suite," drawing 201 submissions from 20 organizations and debuting Blackwell silicon (GB200, B200-SXM-180GB) alongside AMD MI300X/MI325X and TPU Trillium (MLCommons v5.0). v5.1 (results 2025-11-12) retired BERT in favor of Llama 3.1 8B pretraining and replaced Stable Diffusion v2 with Flux.1, an 11.9B-param transformer diffusion model; it logged 65 unique systems, 12 accelerators, 20 orgs, with roughly half of submissions multi-node — an 86% jump in multi-node share over v4.1 (MLCommons v5.1). The LLM triad you should anchor on:

Benchmark Role as a signal Scale band
Llama 3.1 405B pretraining Frontier-scale signal; the only large-cluster datapoint 512 → 5,120 GPUs
Llama 2 70B LoRA fine-tuning Single-rack / single-node signal 8 → 72 GPUs
Llama 3.1 8B pretraining Single-node accessibility signal 8 → 64 GPUs

Note what is not here: there is no MoE pretraining benchmark at any scale. The entire MLPerf LLM track is dense. For a reader whose binding constraints are expert-parallel all-to-all and routing imbalance, the suite measures a different workload class than the one you will run. That is the first and largest caveat, and it is structural, not incidental.

19.2 The headline time-to-train record

The public record reads as a clean two-generation story, and on its own terms it is impressive.

Round Submitter GPUs Benchmark Time-to-train
v5.0 NVIDIA reference 512 GB200 Llama 3.1 405B ~20.8 min
v5.0 CoreWeave + NVIDIA + IBM 2,496 GB200 Llama 3.1 405B 27.3 min
v5.1 NVIDIA 2,560 Blackwell Llama 3.1 405B 18.79 min
v5.1 NVIDIA "Tyche" 5,120 Blackwell Llama 3.1 405B 10.0 min

The v5.0 largest submission — CoreWeave + NVIDIA + IBM at 2,496 GB200 GPUs completing Llama 3.1 405B in 27.3 minutes — was billed as the largest GB200 NVL72 cluster ever benchmarked under MLPerf, "34× larger than the only other cloud-provider submission" (CoreWeave). NVIDIA reported a 2.2× per-GPU Blackwell-over-Hopper speedup on this benchmark (121.09 vs 269.12 minutes at 512 GPUs). In v5.1, NVIDIA set the record at 10 minutes on 5,120 Blackwell GPUs, a 2.7× improvement over its best v5.0 Blackwell result, and claimed 85% scaling efficiency from 512 → 5,120 GPUs (a 10× GPU count increase) (NVIDIA v5.1). The cleanest apples-to-apples scaling datapoint across rounds is the 2,560-GPU → 18.79-min intermediate point, ~45% faster than v5.0's 2,496-GPU run at near-identical scale.

The single biggest lever between rounds was not a hardware change — it was precision. v5.1 was the first MLPerf Training round to use NVFP4 (4-bit) for the bulk of the math, holding the final training iterations in FP8 ("healing") to land the loss target (NVIDIA v5.1). Blackwell does FP4 at 2× its FP8 rate; Blackwell Ultra (GB300) at 3×. This single fact should govern how you read the 2.7× round-over-round jump: a large fraction of it is a 2× FP4-over-FP8 throughput artifact that only holds because the run is short enough that the FP8 healing tail stays cheap. More on why that matters in §19.4.

At the single-rack/node end, the LoRA and 8B signals fill in the lower scale band and — importantly — provide the only credible non-NVIDIA primary data. v5.0 NVIDIA single-node 8×GB200 hit Llama 2 70B LoRA in ~0.56 min against a widely-cited FP8 reference of 11.145 min at 8 GPUs. In v5.1, GB300 delivered ~5× over Hopper at equal GPU count; Lambda's GB300 NVL72 converged 1.27× faster than the best GB200 NVL72 and 1.6× faster than the top 64×B200. Independent Nebius numbers — 8×B300 LoRA = 8.48 min, 32×B200 = 3.10 min (~3.1× scaling 8→32 GPUs) — show Blackwell Ultra averaging ~12.6% training-time reduction over Blackwell (Nebius). And the genuine cross-vendor hedge: AMD MI355X completed Llama 2 70B LoRA in 10.18 min (8 GPUs) in v5.1, within 3% of B200 FP8 and ~10% faster than NVIDIA's 11.145-min v5.0 FP8 result, on ROCm 7.0 / PyTorch 2.8 / TE v2.2 (AMD ROCm). MI355X is the only non-NVIDIA primary LLM-training signal at this generation, and it lands close enough to matter as a second source against single-vendor framing.

19.3 Who submits at frontier scale — and who conspicuously does not

The composition of the submitter list is itself a signal, and it cuts against the grain of how the market is usually described. The frontier-scale submitters are now neoclouds, not hyperscalers: CoreWeave (2,496 GB200 in v5.0), Nebius, Lambda (GB300 NVL72), plus NVIDIA's own internal clusters ("Tyche," the source of the 5,120-GPU record). No hyperscaler submitted a frontier-scale GB200 pretraining run. Google submitted only TPU Trillium results, not a 405B-at-scale entry.

Two reads follow. First, the entities actually willing to expose large GB200 clusters to a public, audited benchmark are the ones selling capacity, for whom a leaderboard placement is marketing — not the ones running their own frontier models, for whom benchmark exposure is pure downside. Second, and more consequential for you: the public MLPerf record tops out around 5,120 GPUs on a single benchmark — roughly 20× smaller than your 100,000-GB200 target — and every large run is a sub-30-minute sprint. There is no public, audited datapoint within a factor of 20 of your cluster size, and none of any duration resembling a real run. When NVIDIA reports 85% scaling efficiency from 512 → 5,120 GPUs, you are watching the curve over one decade of GPU count; you need it over nearly three decades, into a regime where the inter-rack network — not NVLink — carries pipeline and data-parallel traffic, and where the 1,389-rack failure surface is live the entire time. The 85% figure does not extrapolate, and treating it as if it does is the most common way a planner gets burned by this data.

19.4 Backing out MFU — and the three reasons the number is a trap

You can recover an effective efficiency figure from time-to-train: take FLOPs-per-token × tokens ÷ time ÷ GPUs ÷ peak. NVIDIA's own v5.0 405B reference quotes ~1,960 effective TFLOPS per GPU (FP8) — roughly 40-50% of B200's dense-FP8 peak — and the reproduction guide publishes the exact run string needed to compute it independently: TP4-CP2-PP8-DP{n}, GBS=896 (GA112), micro-batch 1, FP8_PARAM_GATHER, TP_COMM_OVERLAP, MAX_STEPS≈450, eval-loss target 5.6, NeMo on NGC 25.04 (NVIDIA reproduce). That ~40-50% looks like a usable planning ceiling. It is not, for three structural reasons, each of which independently disqualifies it as a number you'd budget against.

(1) Sub-convergence sprints. MLPerf LLM benchmarks train a fraction of the data to a point "indicating further training would converge," not to convergence. The v5.0 405B run is ~450 steps at GBS=896. For the retired GPT-3 175B benchmark, MosaicML's Abhi Venigalla estimated the 11-minute record extrapolated to **~2 days of real full-scale training** (IEEE Spectrum) — i.e., the benchmark is two to three orders of magnitude shorter than a production run. Everything that dominates a 10T-token run — long-horizon optimizer-state dynamics, late-training loss-spike recovery, learning-rate-decay-phase behavior — happens entirely outside the measured window. A 450-step efficiency figure tells you about steady-state GEMM throughput on a warm cluster and nothing about the integral over a real run.

(2) Tuned-for-the-test parallelism. The reference configs are short-run-optimal and omit every long-run mechanism. TP4-CP2-PP8 with micro-batch 1 is chosen to minimize wall-clock over 450 steps on a clean cluster; the LoRA reference runs CP8 on a single 72-GPU rack. There is no checkpoint-restart, no fault recovery, no silent-data-corruption detection, no elastic reconfiguration in any of these runs — because over 10-30 minutes you will not hit a failure, so you do not pay for resilience. Over a multi-week run on 1,389 racks where the whole 72-GPU NVLink domain is a single failure domain, you pay for all of it continuously, and it is a real MFU tax that MLPerf structurally cannot show.

(3) Precision laundering. This is the subtlest and, in v5.1, the most important. The "10 min on 5,120 GPUs" headline conflates a hardware gain (Blackwell's FP4 tensor cores) with a numerical gain (running the bulk of training in 4-bit). The 2× FP4-over-FP8 throughput only fully cashes out because the run is short enough that the FP8 "healing" tail is a negligible fraction of total steps. At 10T-token scale, the precision-management overhead is not negligible: the NVFP4 pretraining recipe requires a BF16/FP8 decay tail over roughly the last ~18% of tokens to keep loss error in check (§19.7), plus Random Hadamard transforms and 2D weight scaling that erode the nominal FLOP advantage. MLPerf proves NVFP4 survives ~450 steps plus a short heal; it proves nothing about FP4 numerical stability over 10T tokens. Do not credit the FP4 throughput multiplier to your steady-state MFU as if the healing tail were free.

A methodological landmine compounds all three: MFU is ill-defined under low precision at all. TorchTitan explicitly refuses to report MFU when Float8 is enabled because the metric becomes "not well-defined" (TorchTitan). The peak-FLOPS denominator differs by 2× between FP8 and FP4, so an "effective TFLOPS" computed against one peak is not comparable to a BF16 framework MFU computed against another. Comparing v5.1's FP4 "effective TFLOPS" to a BF16 MFU table is a category error — exactly the trap a GB200 planner falls into when stacking MLPerf headlines against framework numbers.

19.5 The more honest framework signal

Framework-reported MFU, measured over realistic step counts, is more load-bearing than MLPerf for sizing a real run — though still dense, and still mostly Hopper.

  • Megatron-Core / NeMo (H100, BF16/FP8): up to ~47% MFU, but the shape matters more than the peak. MFU rises with model size (41% → 47-48%) then falls with scale as DP-exposed comms grow. The Llama-3 team's own 405B run used three distinct 4D recipes, and doubling cluster size dropped MFU 43% → 41% once intra-node TP saturated and marginal GPUs spilled into slower data-parallel comms (Megatron-LM). The gap-to-100% decomposes as roughly ~15% pipeline bubble, ~10-15% comms, ~5-10% recompute, plus non-GEMM and host overhead. That decomposition is your mental model for where MFU goes; note that none of those four buckets includes MoE all-to-all, which is additive on top for your workload.
  • TorchTitan (H100 95GiB): reports BF16-equivalent 33-42% MFU for Llama-3.1-8B FSDP, and declines to report MFU at all under Float8. Its 405B 3D config (FSDP4-TP8-PP16, 512 GPUs) yields ~130 tokens/s/GPU — useful strictly as a tokens/GPU anchor, not an MFU one (TorchTitan).
  • MaxText (JAX/TPU): defines MFU = train-step-FLOPs ÷ step-time ÷ peak-HW-FLOPS and tokens/s = global-tokens ÷ step-time ÷ devices. It recently corrected its TFLOPS formula (PR #1988) to account for causal attention — a pointed reminder that published MFUs are sensitive to the attention-FLOP convention (causal halving) and the 6ND-vs-exact-profiling debate. Two papers reporting "MFU" may differ by several points purely on FLOP-counting convention, before any hardware difference.

The through-line: well-tuned dense framework MFU on the prior generation lives in the high-30s to high-40s, degrades with scale, and is reported against conventions that vary by several points. None of these tables includes a 200B-active MoE, and none is at GB200 maturity.

19.6 SemiAnalysis: the sustained-MFU cross-check

If MLPerf is a sprint stopwatch, SemiAnalysis's "H100 vs GB200 NVL72 Training Benchmarks" is the only public artifact that measures sustained MFU and exposes the software-maturity curve MLPerf hides. It is the indispensable cross-check, for four reasons:

  1. Fresh-architecture MFU is a moving target — and the move is enormous. On identical H100 silicon, GPT-3 175B BF16 MFU rose 34% → 54% from Jan → Dec 2024 (and 29.5% → 39.5% in FP8) — pure software, same chips (SemiAnalysis). A 20-point MFU swing in eleven months from nothing but kernel, compiler, and framework maturation. GB200 in mid-2025 sat early on the equivalent curve. This is the most important single fact in the chapter for your timeline: whatever MFU GB200 shows at your cluster's first light, expect it to be 10-20 points below where it lands a year later, and budget a multi-month software-maturity ramp accordingly.
  2. Sustained dense MFU anchors. Llama-3 405B sustained MFU ≈ 41-43% across cluster sizes; Llama-3 70B ≈ 53-54% BF16 / ~35-38% FP8 (SemiAnalysis). These are real, multi-hour-or-longer figures, not 450-step sprints — and they are below the MLPerf-implied 40-50%+ once you account for the full run.
  3. Cost/energy grounding. 405B BF16 ≈ $1.95/M tokens, 8.8 J/token on a 2,304-GPU H100 cluster — concrete $/token and J/token anchors you can re-base for GB200.
  4. The killer caveat for your project. As of that analysis, "there are no large-scale production training runs yet on GB200 NVL72." Software immaturity and NVLink-backplane reliability — the entire 72-GPU rack is one failure domain, and one bad component drains the whole rack — were actively blocking frontier deployments. And the economic bar: GB200 NVL72 TCO is ~1.6× H100's, so GB200 must be ≥1.6× faster than H100 per-GPU just to break even on TCO (SemiAnalysis). The MLPerf 2.2× per-GPU sprint speedup clears that bar; the question is whether sustained GB200 MFU does, early on the maturity curve, with MoE comms layered on. SemiAnalysis's ClusterMAX 2.0 scores 80+ providers across the accelerator landscape, with CoreWeave the lone Platinum cloud — a useful read on which neoclouds can actually deliver the uptime that a multi-week run on 1,389 racks demands.

19.7 The DeepSeek-V3 reality check — weight this above everything

The most honest MoE-at-scale public number is not in MLPerf and not in any framework table. It is DeepSeek-V3: 14.8T tokens, 2,048 H800, 2.788M GPU-hours, FP8. Reconstructing effective MFU from the published GPU-hours via FLOP profiling (not the 6ND approximation) lands at ~21.4% FP8 MFU (≈42.9% BF16-equivalent) — about 424 effective TFLOP/s/GPU against a 1,979-TFLOPS FP8 peak (dlrover). This is sub-25% FP8 MFU on a 671B-total / 37B-active MoE over a full 14.8T-token run — the closest public analog to your workload class that exists, and it should be weighted far above any MLPerf 405B-dense sprint.

Why is it so much lower than the 41-47% dense framework ceiling? Because everything that makes an MoE an MoE shows up as MFU loss: expert-parallel all-to-all (token dispatch and combine, twice per layer, on the critical path), routing imbalance and token drop (under-utilized experts and dropped tokens are wasted FLOPs), and the load-balancing machinery itself. The dense framework tables price the pipeline bubble and DP comms but not the all-to-all; MLPerf prices none of it because it has no MoE benchmark. DeepSeek-V3 is the number that prices all of it, over a real horizon, on real silicon. For a 200B-active model at 10T tokens — roughly 5.4× the active params and ~0.68× the tokens of V3 — the routing-comms and imbalance penalties scale unfavorably with active param count and expert count, not favorably. There is no reason to expect your sustained MFU to land above V3's; the honest planning assumption is at or below it on the FP8 axis, before GB200's NVLink5 EP-locality advantage claws some back.

That NVLink5 clawback is real and is the one structural reason GB200 should beat a Hopper MoE by more than the raw FLOP ratio: with the entire expert-parallel group able to live inside the 72-GPU, 1.8-TB/s NVLink domain (EP ≤ 72), the all-to-all stays on NVLink instead of spilling to InfiniBand at ~1/4 the bandwidth, as it does on Hopper above EP=8. So the V3 ~21% floor is a Hopper MoE floor; GB200 EP-locality is the lever that lifts it. But it lifts it from that floor, not from the 40-50% MLPerf headline.

19.8 The honest read for the 10T/200B run on 100k GB200

Pulling the threads together into planning numbers:

MLPerf gives you a generation-multiplier and a per-GPU peak-efficiency ceiling — not a planning number. The 2.2× (v5.0) / 2.7× (v5.1) Blackwell-over-Hopper figures are legitimate relative signals; the absolute 40-50% effective FP8 efficiency is a clean-cluster, 450-step, dense-405B, FP4-inflated ceiling that will not survive contact with your run.

Your binding constraints are absent from MLPerf entirely. MoE all-to-all / EP comms, GB200 NVLink-domain failure blast radius (one bad component drains a 72-GPU rack), checkpoint/restart and silent-data-corruption recovery at 100k scale, long-horizon optimizer-state and convergence dynamics, and FP4/FP8 numerical stability over 10T tokens — none of these appear in a benchmark that proves a ~450-step sprint plus an FP8 healing tail.

A planning table, with the right anchors:

Signal Reported MFU What it actually represents Weight for your run
MLPerf v5.1 405B "10 min" ~40-50% effective FP8 (backed out) Dense, ~450 steps, clean cluster, FP4 + heal Ceiling only; do not budget against
Megatron/NeMo dense ceiling ~47% (degrades to ~41% at scale) Dense, realistic steps, Hopper Upper bound on a dense run
SemiAnalysis 405B sustained ~41-43% BF16 Dense, sustained, mature Hopper software Mature-software dense reference
DeepSeek-V3 (the anchor) ~21% FP8 / ~43% BF16-equiv 671B/37B MoE, full 14.8T tokens, FP8 Weight highest

Calibrate to a ~20-35% sustained-FP8 band for a 200B-active MoE — DeepSeek-V3's ~21% as the floor, the well-tuned dense framework ~47% as a ceiling you will not reach with heavy routing, and GB200 NVLink5 EP-locality as the lever that pushes you toward the upper end of that band rather than the lower. This is materially below the 40-50%+ that stacking MLPerf headlines would imply, and the gap is the cost of being an MoE at scale.

Budget a multi-month software-maturity ramp. Mirror the H100 34% → 54% BF16 curve: expect GB200 to come online 10-20 MFU-points below its eventual plateau, and plan the first quarter or two of the run as a ramp, not steady state. The single most valuable thing this whole body of public data tells you is that fresh-architecture MFU is a moving target that moves ~20 points in a year — so the worst time to lock your throughput model is at first light.

Treat the 1.6× TCO break-even as the real bar, not the 2.2× sprint. GB200 wins on perf/TCO only if sustained per-GPU throughput on your actual MoE clears 1.6× H100's — a much harder test than the MLPerf sprint multiplier, and one no public datapoint yet confirms at MoE-at-scale. The competitive hedge to keep live: TPU v7 Ironwood's 3D-torus-plus-OCS topology is arguably better for very large EP than NVL72's 72-GPU island and carries a SemiAnalysis-estimated ~30-44% lower TCO — the one alternative whose public efficiency story, if it materializes at scale, could make the GB200 break-even bar bind against you.

The final tea-leaf reading: the public throughput record is a leaderboard of sprints, run by capacity vendors, on dense models, at one-twentieth your scale, with precision gains folded invisibly into the clock. It is genuinely useful for exactly two things — ranking silicon generations and bounding per-GPU peak efficiency — and genuinely misleading for the one thing you most want, which is what your run will actually sustain. For that, ignore the 10-minute headline and write down 21%.
