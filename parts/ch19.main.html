Chapter 19 · Reading the leaderboard of sprints

Public throughput signals: MLPerf Training, MFU leaderboards, and reading the tea leaves

The public throughput record is a hall of mirrors: "10 minutes on 5,120 Blackwell GPUs" is a real number that answers the wrong question. This chapter extracts what MLPerf legitimately tells a planner sizing a 10T-total / 200B-active MoE on 100,000 GB200 GPUs — a generation multiplier and a per-GPU ceiling — names what it structurally conceals, and re-anchors the planning number on the one honest signal: DeepSeek-V3's ~21% FP8 MFU over a full 14.8T-token MoE run.

~22 min read5 figuresgrounded in MLPerf v5.0/v5.1 + SemiAnalysis tracking

MLPerf Training headlines — "10 minutes on 5,120 Blackwell GPUs" — are engineered to be read as performance leadership, and they are real numbers produced by real silicon. But every one of them is a sub-convergence sprint on a dense model, tuned for the test, with the precision story laundered into a single time-to-train figure. For a planner sizing a 10T-total / 200B-active MoE on 100,000 GB200 GPUs, the danger is not that these numbers are wrong; it is that they are right about the wrong question. They answer "how fast can clean silicon hit a fixed loss target on a 405B dense model over ~450 steps?" Your question is "what sustained MFU will a heavy-routing MoE hold over 10T tokens across a multi-week run on a 1,389-rack fault domain?" The gap between those two questions is the entire content of this chapter. We will extract what MLPerf legitimately tells you (a per-GPU peak-efficiency ceiling and a clean generation multiplier), name what it structurally conceals (MoE all-to-all, failure blast radius, long-horizon convergence, precision management at scale), and then re-anchor your planning numbers on the more honest signals: SemiAnalysis's sustained-MFU tracking, framework benchmark tables, and the single most load-bearing public datapoint of all — DeepSeek-V3's ~21% FP8 MFU over a full 14.8T-token MoE run.

The public throughput record is a leaderboard of sprints, run by capacity vendors, on dense models, at one-twentieth your scale, with precision gains folded invisibly into the clock. For what your run will actually sustain, ignore the 10-minute headline and write down 21%.
The structural caveat that frames the whole chapter

Every large MLPerf run is a sub-30-minute, ~450-step, dense-model sprint on a clean cluster — two to three orders of magnitude shorter than a 10T-token production run, with no MoE benchmark anywhere in the suite. These are short non-frontier runs by construction. Treat every efficiency number backed out of them as a ceiling, never a budget.

01What MLPerf Training v5.0/v5.1 actually measure

MLPerf Training is a closed-loop, fixed-quality benchmark: every submission trains to a predetermined eval-loss or accuracy target and reports only time-to-train. It deliberately publishes no MFU, no throughput, no TFLOPS. This is a defensible methodological choice — it makes submissions comparable across wildly different hardware — but it means every efficiency number you want must be backed out, and the back-out is fragile for reasons we will make precise.

The suite has been actively reshaped to track the frontier. v5.0 (results 2025-06-04) executed the pivotal substitution: it retired the long-standing GPT-3 175B pretraining benchmark and introduced Llama 3.1 405B pretraining as "the largest model in the suite," drawing 201 submissions from 20 organizations and debuting Blackwell silicon (GB200, B200-SXM-180GB) alongside AMD MI300X/MI325X and TPU Trillium (MLCommons v5.0). v5.1 (results 2025-11-12) retired BERT in favor of Llama 3.1 8B pretraining and replaced Stable Diffusion v2 with Flux.1, an 11.9B-param transformer diffusion model; it logged 65 unique systems, 12 accelerators, 20 orgs, with roughly half of submissions multi-node — an 86% jump in multi-node share over v4.1 (MLCommons v5.1). Note what is not here: there is no MoE pretraining benchmark at any scale. The entire MLPerf LLM track is dense. For a reader whose binding constraints are expert-parallel all-to-all and routing imbalance, the suite measures a different workload class than the one you will run. That is the first and largest caveat, and it is structural, not incidental.

The dense/MoE mismatch is structural

MLPerf prices the pipeline bubble and data-parallel comms of a dense transformer, but it has no mechanism to price MoE token dispatch/combine, routing imbalance, or token drop — because there is no MoE benchmark. Your binding constraint is invisible to the suite by design.

The v5.0 largest submission — CoreWeave + NVIDIA + IBM at 2,496 GB200 GPUs completing Llama 3.1 405B in 27.3 minutes — was billed as the largest GB200 NVL72 cluster ever benchmarked under MLPerf, "34× larger than the only other cloud-provider submission" (CoreWeave). NVIDIA reported a 2.2× per-GPU Blackwell-over-Hopper speedup on this benchmark (121.09 vs 269.12 minutes at 512 GPUs). In v5.1, NVIDIA set the record at 10 minutes on 5,120 Blackwell GPUs, a 2.7× improvement over its best v5.0 Blackwell result, and claimed 85% scaling efficiency from 512 → 5,120 GPUs (a 10× GPU-count increase) (NVIDIA v5.1). The cleanest apples-to-apples scaling datapoint across rounds is the 2,560-GPU → 18.79-min intermediate point, ~45% faster than v5.0's 2,496-GPU run at near-identical scale.

The single biggest lever between rounds was not a hardware change — it was precision. v5.1 was the first MLPerf Training round to use NVFP4 (4-bit) for the bulk of the math, holding the final training iterations in FP8 ("healing") to land the loss target (NVIDIA v5.1). Blackwell does FP4 at 2× its FP8 rate; Blackwell Ultra (GB300) at 3×. This single fact should govern how you read the 2.7× round-over-round jump: a large fraction of it is a 2× FP4-over-FP8 throughput artifact that only holds because the run is short enough that the FP8 healing tail stays cheap. More on why that matters in §04.

prec FP4 throughput = 2 × FP8 (Blackwell) · 3 × FP8 (GB300) The v5.1 round's 2.7× jump is part FP4 hardware, part numerical: most of training ran in 4-bit, with an FP8 "healing" tail short enough to be free over ~450 steps. Over 10T tokens the tail is not free.

At the single-rack/node end, the LoRA and 8B signals fill in the lower scale band and — importantly — provide the only credible non-NVIDIA primary data. v5.0 NVIDIA single-node 8×GB200 hit Llama 2 70B LoRA in ~0.56 min against a widely-cited FP8 reference of 11.145 min at 8 GPUs. In v5.1, GB300 delivered ~5× over Hopper at equal GPU count; Lambda's GB300 NVL72 converged 1.27× faster than the best GB200 NVL72 and 1.6× faster than the top 64×B200. Independent Nebius numbers — 8×B300 LoRA = 8.48 min, 32×B200 = 3.10 min (~3.1× scaling 8→32 GPUs) — show Blackwell Ultra averaging ~12.6% training-time reduction over Blackwell (Nebius). And the genuine cross-vendor hedge: AMD MI355X completed Llama 2 70B LoRA in 10.18 min (8 GPUs) in v5.1, within 3% of B200 FP8 and ~10% faster than NVIDIA's 11.145-min v5.0 FP8 result, on ROCm 7.0 / PyTorch 2.8 / TE v2.2 (AMD ROCm). MI355X is the only non-NVIDIA primary LLM-training signal at this generation, and it lands close enough to matter as a second source against single-vendor framing.

03Who submits at frontier scale — and who conspicuously does not

The composition of the submitter list is itself a signal, and it cuts against the grain of how the market is usually described. The frontier-scale submitters are now neoclouds, not hyperscalers: CoreWeave (2,496 GB200 in v5.0), Nebius, Lambda (GB300 NVL72), plus NVIDIA's own internal clusters ("Tyche," the source of the 5,120-GPU record). No hyperscaler submitted a frontier-scale GB200 pretraining run. Google submitted only TPU Trillium results, not a 405B-at-scale entry.

Two reads follow. First, the entities actually willing to expose large GB200 clusters to a public, audited benchmark are the ones selling capacity, for whom a leaderboard placement is marketing — not the ones running their own frontier models, for whom benchmark exposure is pure downside. Second, and more consequential for you: the public MLPerf record tops out around 5,120 GPUs on a single benchmark — roughly 20× smaller than your 100,000-GB200 target — and every large run is a sub-30-minute sprint. There is no public, audited datapoint within a factor of 20 of your cluster size, and none of any duration resembling a real run.

When NVIDIA reports 85% scaling efficiency from 512 → 5,120 GPUs, you are watching the curve over one decade of GPU count; you need it over nearly three decades, into a regime where the inter-rack network — not NVLink — carries pipeline and data-parallel traffic, and where the 1,389-rack failure surface is live the entire time. The 85% figure does not extrapolate, and treating it as if it does is the most common way a planner gets burned by this data.

Do not extrapolate the 85% scaling figure

85% efficiency over 512 → 5,120 GPUs says nothing about 5,120 → 100,000, where DCN replaces NVLink for PP/DP traffic and the 1,389-rack fault domain is continuously live. Extrapolating it is the single most common planner error against this dataset.

04Backing out MFU — and the three reasons the number is a trap

You can recover an effective efficiency figure from time-to-train: take FLOPs-per-token × tokens ÷ time ÷ GPUs ÷ peak. NVIDIA's own v5.0 405B reference quotes ~1,960 effective TFLOPS per GPU (FP8) — roughly 40–50% of B200's dense-FP8 peak — and the reproduction guide publishes the exact run string needed to compute it independently: TP4-CP2-PP8-DP{n}, GBS=896 (GA112), micro-batch 1, FP8_PARAM_GATHER, TP_COMM_OVERLAP, MAX_STEPS≈450, eval-loss target 5.6, NeMo on NGC 25.04 (NVIDIA reproduce). That ~40–50% looks like a usable planning ceiling. It is not, for three structural reasons, each of which independently disqualifies it as a number you'd budget against.

yaml — the v5.0 405B reference recipe (the exact knobs you'd back MFU out of)
# MLPerf v5.0 Llama-3.1-405B reference — short-run-optimal, NOT a real run
model: llama3.1-405b
parallelism:
  tensor_parallel:   4      # TP4
  context_parallel:  2      # CP2
  pipeline_parallel: 8      # PP8
  data_parallel:     auto   # DP{n} fills the cluster
global_batch_size:   896    # GBS, grad-accum GA112
micro_batch_size:    1
precision:           fp8
flags: [FP8_PARAM_GATHER, TP_COMM_OVERLAP]
max_steps:           450    # sub-convergence: ~2 days of real training, extrapolated
eval_loss_target:    5.6    # train only until "further training would converge"
# absent by design: checkpoint-restart, fault recovery, SDC detection, elastic reconfig
python — backing effective FP8 efficiency out of a time-to-train headline
# MLPerf reports ONLY time-to-train; recover effective efficiency by hand.
flops_per_token = 6 * 405e9            # 405B dense, 6ND approximation
tokens         = 896 * 8192 * 450     # GBS=896 * seq * ~450 sub-convergence steps
time_s         = 27.3 * 60            # v5.0 2,496-GPU run
gpus, peak_fp8 = 2496, 1979e12        # B200 dense-FP8 peak (TFLOPS)

eff_tflops = flops_per_token * tokens / time_s / gpus / 1e12   # ~1,960 TFLOPS (NVIDIA)
mfu_fp8    = (eff_tflops * 1e12) / peak_fp8                     # ~0.40-0.50 — a CEILING
# ...but this is dense, ~450 steps, clean, and FP4-inflated in v5.1. Do NOT budget on it.

(1) Sub-convergence sprints

MLPerf LLM benchmarks train a fraction of the data to a point "indicating further training would converge," not to convergence. The v5.0 405B run is ~450 steps at GBS=896. For the retired GPT-3 175B benchmark, MosaicML's Abhi Venigalla estimated the 11-minute record extrapolated to ~2 days of real full-scale training (IEEE Spectrum) — i.e., the benchmark is two to three orders of magnitude shorter than a production run. Everything that dominates a 10T-token run — long-horizon optimizer-state dynamics, late-training loss-spike recovery, learning-rate-decay-phase behavior — happens entirely outside the measured window. A 450-step efficiency figure tells you about steady-state GEMM throughput on a warm cluster and nothing about the integral over a real run.

(2) Tuned-for-the-test parallelism

The reference configs are short-run-optimal and omit every long-run mechanism. TP4-CP2-PP8 with micro-batch 1 is chosen to minimize wall-clock over 450 steps on a clean cluster; the LoRA reference runs CP8 on a single 72-GPU rack. There is no checkpoint-restart, no fault recovery, no silent-data-corruption detection, no elastic reconfiguration in any of these runs — because over 10–30 minutes you will not hit a failure, so you do not pay for resilience. Over a multi-week run on 1,389 racks where the whole 72-GPU NVLink domain is a single failure domain, you pay for all of it continuously, and it is a real MFU tax that MLPerf structurally cannot show.

(3) Precision laundering

This is the subtlest and, in v5.1, the most important. The "10 min on 5,120 GPUs" headline conflates a hardware gain (Blackwell's FP4 tensor cores) with a numerical gain (running the bulk of training in 4-bit). The 2× FP4-over-FP8 throughput only fully cashes out because the run is short enough that the FP8 "healing" tail is a negligible fraction of total steps. At 10T-token scale, the precision-management overhead is not negligible: the NVFP4 pretraining recipe requires a BF16/FP8 decay tail over roughly the last ~18% of tokens to keep loss error in check (§07), plus Random Hadamard transforms and 2D weight scaling that erode the nominal FLOP advantage. MLPerf proves NVFP4 survives ~450 steps plus a short heal; it proves nothing about FP4 numerical stability over 10T tokens.

The one mistake to refuse

Do not credit the FP4 throughput multiplier to your steady-state MFU as if the healing tail were free. The 2× FP4 artifact in the v5.1 headline holds only because ~450 steps make the FP8 heal tail negligible. Over 10T tokens, the ~18% precision-decay tail is a first-order cost.

A methodological landmine compounds all three: MFU is ill-defined under low precision at all. TorchTitan explicitly refuses to report MFU when Float8 is enabled because the metric becomes "not well-defined" (TorchTitan). The peak-FLOPS denominator differs by 2× between FP8 and FP4, so an "effective TFLOPS" computed against one peak is not comparable to a BF16 framework MFU computed against another. Comparing v5.1's FP4 "effective TFLOPS" to a BF16 MFU table is a category error — exactly the trap a GB200 planner falls into when stacking MLPerf headlines against framework numbers.

compute MFU = FLOPs/token · tokens ÷ time ÷ GPUs ÷ peakHW peakHW differs 2× between FP8 and FP4. An "effective TFLOPS vs FP4 peak" is not comparable to a "BF16 MFU vs BF16 peak" — TorchTitan refuses to report MFU under Float8 for exactly this reason.

05The more honest framework signal

Framework-reported MFU, measured over realistic step counts, is more load-bearing than MLPerf for sizing a real run — though still dense, and still mostly Hopper.

  • Megatron-Core / NeMo (H100, BF16/FP8): up to ~47% MFU, but the shape matters more than the peak. MFU rises with model size (41% → 47–48%) then falls with scale as DP-exposed comms grow. The Llama-3 team's own 405B run used three distinct 4D recipes, and doubling cluster size dropped MFU 43% → 41% once intra-node TP saturated and marginal GPUs spilled into slower data-parallel comms (Megatron-LM). The gap-to-100% decomposes as roughly ~15% pipeline bubble, ~10–15% comms, ~5–10% recompute, plus non-GEMM and host overhead. That decomposition is your mental model for where MFU goes; note that none of those four buckets includes MoE all-to-all, which is additive on top for your workload.
  • TorchTitan (H100 95GiB): reports BF16-equivalent 33–42% MFU for Llama-3.1-8B FSDP, and declines to report MFU at all under Float8. Its 405B 3D config (FSDP4-TP8-PP16, 512 GPUs) yields ~130 tokens/s/GPU — useful strictly as a tokens/GPU anchor, not an MFU one (TorchTitan).
  • MaxText (JAX/TPU): defines MFU = train-step-FLOPs ÷ step-time ÷ peak-HW-FLOPS and tokens/s = global-tokens ÷ step-time ÷ devices. It recently corrected its TFLOPS formula (PR #1988) to account for causal attention — a pointed reminder that published MFUs are sensitive to the attention-FLOP convention (causal halving) and the 6ND-vs-exact-profiling debate. Two papers reporting "MFU" may differ by several points purely on FLOP-counting convention, before any hardware difference.

The codecard below makes the convention sensitivity concrete: this is the shape of MaxText's per-step MFU accounting, where the causal-attention halving (the PR #1988 fix) is the line that moves a reported MFU by several points before any hardware enters the comparison.

MaxText — train-step MFU accountingPR #1988 (causal halving)illustrative
# MFU = train-step FLOPs / step-time / peak-HW-FLOPS  (MaxText definition)
def per_token_flops(n_params, d_model, n_layers, seq_len):
    dense = 6 * n_params                                   # [1] 6ND approximation
    # exact attention term, halved for causality (PR #1988):
    attn  = 12 * n_layers * d_model * seq_len * 0.5        # [2] causal halving
    return dense + attn

def mfu(step_flops, step_time_s, devices, peak_flops):    # [3]
    return step_flops / step_time_s / devices / peak_flops  # peak differs 2x FP8 vs FP4

tokens_per_s = global_tokens / step_time_s / devices      # [4] the MFU-free anchor

16ND is an approximation. The flat 6·N·D rule omits the attention term entirely; "MFU" reported on it differs from exact profiling by the 6ND-vs-profiling debate the source flags.

2The causal halving (PR #1988). Attention is half-masked, so its FLOPs halve — the correction that shifts a published MFU by several points before any hardware difference enters.

3The 2× denominator trap. peak_flops is 2× larger for FP4 than FP8; an "effective TFLOPS vs FP4 peak" is not comparable to a BF16-peak MFU — the §04 category error in code.

4tokens/s is convention-free. Unlike MFU, tokens/s/GPU needs no peak or FLOP convention — which is why TorchTitan's ~130 tok/s/GPU survives as an anchor where its Float8 MFU does not.

The through-line: well-tuned dense framework MFU on the prior generation lives in the high-30s to high-40s, degrades with scale, and is reported against conventions that vary by several points. None of these tables includes a 200B-active MoE, and none is at GB200 maturity.

06SemiAnalysis: the sustained-MFU cross-check

If MLPerf is a sprint stopwatch, SemiAnalysis's "H100 vs GB200 NVL72 Training Benchmarks" is the only public artifact that measures sustained MFU and exposes the software-maturity curve MLPerf hides. It is the indispensable cross-check, for four reasons.

1 · Fresh-architecture MFU is a moving target — and the move is enormous

On identical H100 silicon, GPT-3 175B BF16 MFU rose 34% → 54% from Jan → Dec 2024 (and 29.5% → 39.5% in FP8) — pure software, same chips (SemiAnalysis). A 20-point MFU swing in eleven months from nothing but kernel, compiler, and framework maturation. GB200 in mid-2025 sat early on the equivalent curve. This is the most important single fact in the chapter for your timeline: whatever MFU GB200 shows at your cluster's first light, expect it to be 10–20 points below where it lands a year later, and budget a multi-month software-maturity ramp accordingly.

2 · Sustained dense MFU anchors

Llama-3 405B sustained MFU ≈ 41–43% across cluster sizes; Llama-3 70B ≈ 53–54% BF16 / ~35–38% FP8 (SemiAnalysis). These are real, multi-hour-or-longer figures, not 450-step sprints — and they are below the MLPerf-implied 40–50%+ once you account for the full run.

3 · Cost/energy grounding

405B BF16 ≈ $1.95/M tokens, 8.8 J/token on a 2,304-GPU H100 cluster — concrete $/token and J/token anchors you can re-base for GB200.

4 · The killer caveat for your project

As of that analysis, "there are no large-scale production training runs yet on GB200 NVL72." Software immaturity and NVLink-backplane reliability — the entire 72-GPU rack is one failure domain, and one bad component drains the whole rack — were actively blocking frontier deployments. And the economic bar: GB200 NVL72 TCO is ~1.6× H100's, so GB200 must be ≥1.6× faster than H100 per-GPU just to break even on TCO (SemiAnalysis). The MLPerf 2.2× per-GPU sprint speedup clears that bar; the question is whether sustained GB200 MFU does, early on the maturity curve, with MoE comms layered on. SemiAnalysis's ClusterMAX 2.0 scores 80+ providers across the accelerator landscape, with CoreWeave the lone Platinum cloud — a useful read on which neoclouds can actually deliver the uptime that a multi-week run on 1,389 racks demands.

The real economic bar is 1.6×, not 2.2× SemiAnalysis

07The DeepSeek-V3 reality check — weight this above everything

The most honest MoE-at-scale public number is not in MLPerf and not in any framework table. It is DeepSeek-V3: 14.8T tokens, 2,048 H800, 2.788M GPU-hours, FP8. Reconstructing effective MFU from the published GPU-hours via FLOP profiling (not the 6ND approximation) lands at ~21.4% FP8 MFU (≈42.9% BF16-equivalent) — about 424 effective TFLOP/s/GPU against a 1,979-TFLOPS FP8 peak (dlrover). This is sub-25% FP8 MFU on a 671B-total / 37B-active MoE over a full 14.8T-token run — the closest public analog to your workload class that exists, and it should be weighted far above any MLPerf 405B-dense sprint.

The one number to write down

DeepSeek-V3: ~21% FP8 MFU over a full 14.8T-token MoE run. Not a 450-step sprint, not a dense model, not a clean cluster — a real MoE over a real horizon on real silicon. This is your anchor; everything in MLPerf is a ceiling above it.

Why is it so much lower than the 41–47% dense framework ceiling? Because everything that makes an MoE an MoE shows up as MFU loss: expert-parallel all-to-all (token dispatch and combine, twice per layer, on the critical path), routing imbalance and token drop (under-utilized experts and dropped tokens are wasted FLOPs), and the load-balancing machinery itself. The dense framework tables price the pipeline bubble and DP comms but not the all-to-all; MLPerf prices none of it because it has no MoE benchmark. DeepSeek-V3 is the number that prices all of it, over a real horizon, on real silicon. For a 200B-active model at 10T tokens — roughly 5.4× the active params and ~0.68× the tokens of V3 — the routing-comms and imbalance penalties scale unfavorably with active param count and expert count, not favorably. There is no reason to expect your sustained MFU to land above V3's; the honest planning assumption is at or below it on the FP8 axis, before GB200's NVLink5 EP-locality advantage claws some back.

That NVLink5 clawback is real and is the one structural reason GB200 should beat a Hopper MoE by more than the raw FLOP ratio: with the entire expert-parallel group able to live inside the 72-GPU, 1.8-TB/s NVLink domain (EP ≤ 72), the all-to-all stays on NVLink instead of spilling to InfiniBand at ~1/4 the bandwidth, as it does on Hopper above EP=8. So the V3 ~21% floor is a Hopper MoE floor; GB200 EP-locality is the lever that lifts it. But it lifts it from that floor, not from the 40–50% MLPerf headline.

Calibrate to a 20–35% sustained-FP8 band

DeepSeek-V3's ~21% is the floor; the well-tuned dense ~47% is a ceiling you will not reach with heavy routing; GB200 NVLink5 EP-locality is the lever pushing you toward the upper end of that band, not above it. A 200B-active MoE at 10T tokens scales the routing/imbalance penalty unfavorably vs V3 — anchor low.

08The honest read for the 10T/200B run on 100k GB200

Pulling the threads together into planning numbers.

MLPerf gives you a generation-multiplier and a per-GPU peak-efficiency ceiling — not a planning number. The 2.2× (v5.0) / 2.7× (v5.1) Blackwell-over-Hopper figures are legitimate relative signals; the absolute 40–50% effective FP8 efficiency is a clean-cluster, 450-step, dense-405B, FP4-inflated ceiling that will not survive contact with your run.

Your binding constraints are absent from MLPerf entirely. MoE all-to-all / EP comms, GB200 NVLink-domain failure blast radius (one bad component drains a 72-GPU rack), checkpoint/restart and silent-data-corruption recovery at 100k scale, long-horizon optimizer-state and convergence dynamics, and FP4/FP8 numerical stability over 10T tokens — none of these appear in a benchmark that proves a ~450-step sprint plus an FP8 healing tail.

Budget a multi-month software-maturity ramp. Mirror the H100 34% → 54% BF16 curve: expect GB200 to come online 10–20 MFU-points below its eventual plateau, and plan the first quarter or two of the run as a ramp, not steady state. The single most valuable thing this whole body of public data tells you is that fresh-architecture MFU is a moving target that moves ~20 points in a year — so the worst time to lock your throughput model is at first light.

Treat the 1.6× TCO break-even as the real bar, not the 2.2× sprint. The final tea-leaf reading: the public throughput record is a leaderboard of sprints, run by capacity vendors, on dense models, at one-twentieth your scale, with precision gains folded invisibly into the clock. It is genuinely useful for exactly two things — ranking silicon generations and bounding per-GPU peak efficiency — and genuinely misleading for the one thing you most want, which is what your run will actually sustain. For that, ignore the 10-minute headline and write down 21%.
