Chapter 11 · Instability is a budgeted line item

Training stability and failure modes for trillion-scale MoE

At 10T-total / 200B-active on 100,000 GB200 GPUs, a divergence that forces a rollback costs order $105 per recoverable hour — and the unrecoverable mode, silent weight drift, has no checkpoint to return to because you never saw it happen. This is the field manual for engineering against instability from step zero: the taxonomy, the mitigation stack, the router decision, the Kimi K2 zero-spike mechanism, and the exact MaxText knobs that implement all of it.

At 10T total / 200B active on 100k GB200s, instability is not a tail risk you patch reactively — it is a budgeted line item you engineer against from step zero. A single divergence that forces a rollback to the last good checkpoint costs, conservatively, hours of a fleet whose dense FP8 peak is ~0.45 EFLOP/s (100k B200 dies at ~4.5 PFLOP/s dense FP8 each; the headline 720 PFLOP/s-per-rack figures NVIDIA publishes are 2:4-sparse, and dense is exactly half that, so do not budget against them). At ~35–40% large-MoE FP8 MFU, every recoverable hour is order $105 in opportunity cost, and the unrecoverable failure mode — silent weight drift to a worse optimum — has no checkpoint to roll back to because you never saw it happen. The governing fact of the modern literature, from Wortsman et al.'s small-scale proxies (arXiv:2309.14322) through Kimi K2's 15.5T-token zero-spike run (arXiv:2507.20534), is that the instabilities that kill trillion-scale runs are late-emergent: they surface only at high token counts or high learning rate that a small-scale proxy with identical hyperparameters never reaches. The job is therefore preventive instrumentation plus structural fixes, with a runtime backstop for the residual. This chapter gives the taxonomy, the mitigation stack, the router-stability decision, the Kimi K2 zero-instability mechanism dissected, and the exact MaxText knobs that implement all of it.

Trillion-scale stability is a composition property, not a single trick: each mitigation closes exactly one failure family, and the run is stable only when all five are closed simultaneously. Diagnose by family, instrument the canaries, and never let a runtime guard quietly absorb a root cause.

01A five-family taxonomy of trillion-scale instability

Every loss spike you will see decomposes into one of five mechanistically distinct families. Conflating them is the most common operational error: z-loss does nothing for attention-logit explosion, QK-Clip does nothing for a poisoned data batch, and gradient clipping does nothing for silent data corruption. Diagnose the family first.

 Five mechanistically distinct families. The cardinal rule: diagnose the family before reaching for a mitigation — each fix closes exactly one.

Family (1), attention logit explosion, is the dominant trillion-scale failure and the single most-monitored stability scalar. The pre-softmax score grows because nothing in standard scaled-dot-product attention regularizes the product of the Wq and Wk singular values. Once max-logit exceeds ~O(10³), the softmax saturates, gradients vanish for that head, and bf16 rounding of the exponent triggers divergence. Wortsman et al. first showed this is reproducible in small models at high LR and is the same instability seen at scale (arXiv:2309.14322). The Kimi K2 team reports that under vanilla Muon, max attention logits "rapidly exceed 1000" at a 9B-activated proxy scale (arXiv:2507.20534). This is critical for our target because Muon — the near-mandatory optimizer at this scale — actively amplifies family (1): its Newton-Schulz orthogonalization equalizes update RMS across all singular directions, which is precisely the dynamic that drives the Wq·Wk product to grow. Choosing Muon for its large-batch data-efficiency persistence (arXiv:2505.02222) means you have signed up for QK-side logit control as a non-negotiable.

The one scalar to remember

The per-head max attention logit is your canary — the earliest-firing, highest-signal stability scalar, and on a Muon run the one most likely to move. Wire it to a hard alert at >100–500. Everything in §03 exists to keep it bounded.

Family (2), router collapse, is structurally unique to MoE and gets worse with expert count. At 256+ experts with expert parallelism (EP) sharded across thousands of GB200s, a slightly-favored expert receives more tokens, its gradients improve faster, the router favors it more, and the rest atrophy. Beyond the quality loss of dead experts, this produces catastrophic load imbalance: stragglers stall the all-to-all, and tokens overflow expert capacity and are dropped. A distinct sub-mode is gradient-norm spikes from the router logits themselves — the gate is a tiny matrix whose logits can blow up independently of the experts, so a grad-norm spike does not always implicate the experts. For a 200B-active design with the DeepSeek-V3-style ~256–384 fine-grained experts, this family is co-equal with (1) in priority.

Family (3), gradient-norm spikes, is most often the symptom the skip-step machinery keys on rather than a root cause. OLMo-2's most actionable finding traced many spikes to specific data batches containing long repeated n-gram sequences — a data pathology, not an optimizer pathology (arXiv:2501.00656). The engineering implication is two-layered: aggressive corpus-level deduplication offline, plus a runtime spike guard that rejects the occasional pathological batch that slips through.

Family (4), bf16 numerics, splits cleanly. (4a) is the softmax normalizer log-Z drifting far from zero so output logits round badly in bf16 — the original motivation for z-loss. (4b) is Adam second-moment / update underflow, which is largely solved by keeping optimizer state in fp32 (mu_dtype=float32). On GB200, the FP8 attention and GEMM paths re-introduce overflow risk and make max-logit control mandatory rather than optional; if you push to FP4 on the Blackwell tensor cores, the dynamic-range margin shrinks further.

Family (5) is the slow structural leak: untied output embeddings and final logits grow without bound and couple into both (1) and (4). Gemma-2's final-logit soft-cap (30.0) and z-loss both target this surface (arXiv:2408.00118).

02z-loss: the bf16-numerics and output-head anchor

z-loss adds λ·(log Z)² to the cross-entropy, where Z is the softmax partition function, penalizing drift of the log-partition from zero. In MaxText this is implemented directly inside the stabilized cross-entropy at src/maxtext/utils/max_utils.py (cross_entropy_with_logits), which computes log_z = logsumexp(logits) and adds z_loss · square(log_z), surfaced through the z_loss_multiplier config (default 0.0). The docstring states both purposes verbatim: keep the logits from drifting too far from zero so bf16 roundoff stays bounded, and encourage the logits to be normalized log-probabilities. The canonical coefficient is 10⁻⁴·log²Z — OLMo-2 uses it (arXiv:2501.00656), and the PaLM/Chinchilla-era runs used the same value. This is the fix for families (4a) and (5).

prec Ltotal = LCE + λ · (log Z)² , Z = Σv exp(logitv) , λ ≈ 10⁻⁴ Penalizes drift of the log-partition from zero so bf16 roundoff of output logits stays bounded. Applies to the output softmax only — orthogonal to attention-logit control.

The load-bearing caveat — z-loss is NOT your attention-stability story

z-loss is applied to the output softmax only; it does nothing for attention logit explosion (family 1). Engineers routinely set z_loss_multiplier=1e-4, watch the output-logit RMS stabilize, and assume they are protected against divergence — then a head's pre-softmax score blows past 1000 and the run dies anyway. z-loss and QK-side intervention are orthogonal and both required. Set z_loss_multiplier=1e-4 as a default and treat it as table stakes, not as your attention-stability story.

03The QK-side stack: QK-norm, soft-caps, and QK-Clip

Three techniques attack family (1) at different points in the forward/update path. They are largely alternatives, and the choice has real consequences at scale.

QK-norm applies RMSNorm to Q and K per head before the dot product, bounding ‖q‖ and ‖k‖ so the logit cannot grow via activation magnitude. MaxText gates this with use_qk_norm in src/maxtext/layers/attentions.py. OLMo-3 uses a global variant (a single RMSNorm over the flattened heads·dim) where other implementations use per-head. The crucial OLMo-2 result is that QK-norm and reordered-norm yield no stability gain in isolation — only together do they tame gradient-norm growth and spikiness (arXiv:2501.00656). QK-norm is the cheapest structural option, costs nothing at inference, and is compatible with FlashAttention; it is the default choice for a dense-attention model and is what Gemma-3 switched to after dropping soft-caps. Its limitation: it bounds the activation magnitudes but not the weight-singular-value product directly, so under Muon's orthogonalized updates it is a softer guarantee than QK-Clip.

Attention soft-cap (Gemma-2) replaces the raw logits with C·tanh(logits/C), using C=50.0 for attention and C=30.0 for the final head (arXiv:2408.00118). MaxText implements this inside the attention kernels (splash_attention_kernel.py, jax_flash_attention.py) and exposes attn_logits_soft_cap / final_logits_soft_cap. The drawback is decisive for a 100k-GB200 build: tanh-capping is incompatible with the fused-softmax path in Flash/Splash attention in some configurations, and you cannot afford an unfused softmax at this throughput. Gemma-3 itself removed the attention soft-cap in favor of QK-norm. Treat soft-cap as legacy; the only place it survives in a modern recipe is the final-logits cap as a cheap belt-and-suspenders for family (5), and even there z-loss subsumes most of its value.

QK-Clip (MuonClip) is the trillion-scale state of the art and the right default if you run Muon with MLA. Rather than altering the forward pass, it post-hoc rescales the projection weights after each optimizer step. Per head h, define S_max^h = (1/√d)·max over batch,i,j of Qᵢ^h·Kⱼ^hᵀ; if S_max^h > τ, compute γ_h = min(1, τ/S_max^h) and apply Wq^h ← γ^α Wq^h, Wk^h ← γ^(1−α) Wk^h with α=0.5 so each projection absorbs √γ (arXiv:2507.20534v1). Only the heads exceeding τ are touched — "only a small subset of heads exhibit exploding logits" — so it is per-head and conditional, becoming a no-op once logits settle. For MLA, the decomposition matters: the non-RoPE components q^C and k^C are each scaled by √γ, the decoupled per-head RoPE query q^R is scaled by the full γ, and the shared RoPE key k^R is left untouched to avoid cross-head coupling (clipping a shared component would contaminate every head). Kimi K2 used τ=100.

attn Smaxh = (1/√d) · maxb,i,j Qih·Kjhᵀ ; γh = min(1, τ/Smaxh) ; Wqh ← √γ · Wqh, Wkh ← √γ · Wkh Per-head, conditional, post-optimizer. α=0.5 splits γ symmetrically across the two projections. A no-op once Smaxh ≤ τ.

MaxText implements exactly this in src/maxtext/utils/qk_clip_utils.py. The key routines:

  • _scale_from_max_logits(max_logits_batch, τ) computes s_max = max over batch then returns min(1.0, τ/(s_max + 1e-6)) — the γ factor, per head, with an epsilon guard.
  • _clip_mla_weight(layer_name, param, scale, qk_nope) splits the kernel into a head slice [..., :qk_nope] (the nope/non-RoPE part) and a tail. The nope slice is multiplied by √scale. For wq_b, the tail is the q^R RoPE slice and is multiplied by the full scale; for wkv_b, the tail is the V slice and is left untouched (tail_new = tail).
  • calculate_max_logit_metric extracts the global max logit across all layers from sown intermediates, supporting both the Linen sow shape (array,) and the NNX nnx.Intermediate(array) shape.

def _scale_from_max_logits(max_logits_batch, tau):            # [1]
    s_max = jnp.max(max_logits_batch, axis=0)                 # per-head max over batch
    return jnp.minimum(1.0, tau / (s_max + 1e-6))            # gamma, eps-guarded

def _clip_mla_weight(name, param, scale, qk_nope):           # [2]
    nope, tail = param[..., :qk_nope], param[..., qk_nope:]
    nope_new = nope * jnp.sqrt(scale)                         # non-RoPE: sqrt(gamma)
    if name == "wq_b":                                       # [3]
        tail_new = tail * scale                               # decoupled q^R: full gamma
    else:                                                    # wkv_b
        tail_new = tail                                       # shared k^R / V: UNTOUCHED
    return jnp.concatenate([nope_new, tail_new], axis=-1)

# applied post-optimizer, only to heads exceeding tau; no-op once logits settle

1γ, not a gradient. The scale is read from sown max-logits with a +1e-6 epsilon guard and clamped to ≤1 — a multiplicative weight rescale, never a loss term.

2nope gets √γ. The non-RoPE head slice [..., :qk_nope] on both Wq and Wk absorbs √scale, splitting γ symmetrically (α=0.5).

3RoPE asymmetry. The decoupled per-head q^R tail takes the full γ; the shared RoPE key k^R (and the V slice in wkv_b) is left untouched so clipping cannot contaminate every head.

A config guard in types.py forbids use_qk_clip together with attn_logits_soft_cap, because QK-Clip must read the raw dot products to compute S_max — a soft-cap would clamp the very signal QK-Clip needs to measure. The max logits are sown from the attention op (attention_mla.py, record_max_logits=use_qk_clip), and _check_attention_type enforces that QK-Clip is only valid for attention_type='mla'.

Recommendation for the 10T/200B target

If your attention is MLA (it should be, for KV-cache economy at 200B active) and your optimizer is Muon (it should be), run QK-Clip with τ=100 as the primary family-(1) defense, and add per-head QK-norm only if you observe residual spikiness in the proxy sweeps. Do not stack soft-cap on top — the guard will reject it, and rightly so.

04Residual-stream stability: init scaling, sandwich norm, and DeepSeek-V4 mHC

Residual-stream growth across depth is the structural analog of logit growth, and at 100+ layers it is its own failure surface. The classical fixes are 1/√(2L) residual scaling (so the per-layer contribution shrinks with depth) and sandwich/post-norm placement, which bound the residual's growth rate. These remain the baseline and you should ship them: sandwich norm (RMSNorm before and after each sublayer's residual add) plus depth-scaled init is cheap insurance.

The 2026 advance is mHC — Manifold-Constrained Hyper-Connections (arXiv:2512.24880, Xie et al., Dec 31 2025), shipped in DeepSeek-V4-Pro (1.6T total / 49B active) and V4-Flash (284B / 13B active). Plain Hyper-Connections (ByteDance) widen the residual stream by an expansion rate n to give parallel mixing paths, but the learned mixing matrix breaks the identity-mapping property: the paper reports a loss surge at ~12k steps in a 27B model with the composite gain magnitude peaking near ~3000 — a residual explosion correlated with gradient-norm instability. mHC constrains the residual mixing matrix H^res ∈ ℝ^{n×n} to be doubly stochastic (rows and columns non-negative and summing to 1 — the Birkhoff polytope), enforced by 20 Sinkhorn-Knopp projection iterations. Double-stochasticity guarantees the spectral norm ‖H^res‖₂ ≤ 1, making each composite layer non-expansive and conserving the feature mean, so the residual neither explodes nor collapses with depth — recovering the identity-residual benefit while permitting rich cross-path mixing. The bound on composite gain drops from HC's ~3000 to mHC's ~1.6. At n=4 (a 4× wider residual stream) the overhead is only 6.7% of training time, with gating init α=0.01. MaxText ships an mHC-lite variant in src/maxtext/layers/mhc.py (sinkhorn(t, iters=20), get_permutation_matrices over the Birkhoff vertices, expand/reduce broadcast), following the mHC-lite note's explicit permutation-matrix parameterization rather than the full Sinkhorn-projected form.

The recommendation — validate, then adopt DeepSeek-V4 validated

Validate mHC on a 9B–27B proxy at the full target depth (depth is the weak transfer axis — see §07), and if it reproduces the residual-norm flattening, adopt n=4; the 6.7% overhead buys you out of an entire failure family. If you cannot validate in time, fall back to sandwich norm + 1/√(2L) init, which is well-trodden and safe.

05skip-step-on-spike: the runtime backstop and momentum-poisoning subtlety

Structural fixes prevent families (1), (4), (5), and the residual mode. They do not prevent a single pathological data batch (family 3) or a silently corrupted node (§08) from injecting one catastrophic update. The runtime backstop is skip-step-on-spike, and MaxText's implementation in src/maxtext/optimizers/optimizers.py (skip_step_on_spikes) is a faithful port of OLMo-core's skip_step_optimizer with the critical correctness property intact.

The mechanism: the wrapper keeps rolling buffers of the last interval losses and grad-norms, computes their mean and Bessel-corrected std (_compute_rolling_stats, using N−1 to align with torch.std), and on each step checks (loss − mean) ≤ scaling_factor·std AND the analogous condition for grad-norm. If either is violated, it zeros the update via lax.cond — do_update calls the inner optimizer, skip_update returns zero updates and the unchanged inner state. The default skip_step_scaling_factor is 6.0 and skip_step_interval is 128.

src/maxtext/optimizers/optimizers.pyskip_step_on_spikesport of OLMo-core skip_step_optimizertrimmed
mean_l, std_l = _compute_rolling_stats(losses)       # Bessel-corrected (N-1)  # [1]
mean_g, std_g = _compute_rolling_stats(grad_norms)
ok = ((loss - mean_l) <= scaling_factor * std_l) & \
     ((gnorm - mean_g) <= scaling_factor * std_g)    # default scaling=6.0

is_warmup = (count + 1) < min_history               # max(2, interval//2)      # [2]
updates, new_state = lax.cond(                       # [3]
    ok | is_warmup,
    lambda: inner.update(grads, state, params),      # do_update
    lambda: (zeros_like(grads), state))              # skip_update: state UNCHANGED

new_losses = losses.at[idx].set(loss)                # spike still appended      # [4]

1Bessel-corrected std. _compute_rolling_stats uses N−1 to align exactly with torch.std, so the 6σ band matches the OLMo-core reference.

2Warmup guard. A warmup of max(2, interval//2) steps prevents the guard firing before the buffer has enough history to give a meaningful std — avoiding spurious early skips.

3Zero, not clip. On a spike, skip_update returns zero updates and the unchanged inner state — the corrupt batch never enters Adam's or Muon's moment buffers.

4Spike becomes baseline. The skipped loss is still appended so a legitimately shifted regime (data-mix change, WSD decay onset) is not rejected forever.

The subtlety that makes this strictly superior to global gradient clipping: zeroing the update means the spike batch never enters Adam's (or Muon's momentum buffer's) moments. A clipped-but-nonzero update still injects a corrupt direction into the first-moment EMA, where it persists for ~1/(1−β₁) steps and biases every subsequent update; clipping limits the magnitude but not the poisoning. skip-step rejects the whole batch, so momentum is never contaminated. Two further details from the MaxText source matter operationally:

  • The spike value is still appended to the rolling buffer (new_losses = losses.at[idx].set(loss)) even when the step is skipped — "so spikes can become the new baseline." This prevents the guard from getting stuck permanently rejecting a legitimately shifted loss regime (e.g., after a data-mix change or a WSD decay phase begins).
  • A warmup of max(2, interval//2) steps (is_warmup = (count + 1) < min_history) prevents the guard from firing before the buffer has enough history to give a meaningful std, avoiding spurious early skips.
Recommendation — treat skip-step as containment, not cure

Enable skip_step_on_spikes with interval=128, scaling_factor=6.0. Monitor the fraction of skipped steps as a first-class health metric (§09) — a rising skip rate is your earliest warning that data quality or hardware is degrading. If it fires repeatedly, find and fix the root cause (a bad shard, a degrading node) rather than letting it silently absorb a steady stream of corruption.

06Router stability: aux-loss versus aux-loss-free bias

The MoE router is the other co-dominant failure surface, and MaxText supports both regimes.

Aux-loss balancing is the Switch/GShard approach. MaxText's load_balance_loss (src/maxtext/layers/moe.py) computes num_experts² · mean(density · density_prob) where density is the fraction of tokens dispatched per expert and density_prob the mean routing probability, weighted by load_balance_loss_weight (default 0). Its drawback, isolated cleanly by the Auxiliary-Loss-Free Load Balancing paper (arXiv:2408.15664), is interference gradients: the balance term is in the gradient path, so it trades model quality for load balance on every step.

Aux-loss-free bias balancing (DeepSeek-V3) is the recommended default at trillion scale. MaxText's calculate_load_balance_updates (src/maxtext/layers/moe.py) computes expert_counts = bincount(top_k_indices), average_load = total_tokens / num_experts, direction = sign(average_load − expert_counts), and returns direction · rate. This nudges a per-expert routing bias by ±routed_bias_update_rate; the bias steers the top-k selection but is not in the gradient path, so balance is decoupled from the loss surface entirely (arXiv:2412.19437). No interference gradients, no quality tax.

For 200B active across 100k GPUs there is a second-order choice that matters: global (cross-DP) balancing of the bias statistics versus purely sequence-local balancing. Per-sequence balance over-constrains the router and hurts expert specialization — you want experts to specialize, which means accepting per-sequence imbalance as long as the global batch is balanced. DeepSeek-V3 uses global bias balancing plus a negligible-weight complementary sequence-wise aux loss purely as a guardrail against any single sequence's pathological imbalance. Replicate that: aux-loss-free global bias as the primary mechanism, with load_balance_loss_weight set to a tiny value (DeepSeek-V3 territory) as a sequence-level safety net, not as the main balancer.

Aggregate the bias statistics across the DP mesh — don't re-introduce locality

Aggregate the bias-update statistics across the DP mesh before applying — a per-shard update would re-introduce the locality problem you are trying to avoid. The bias is a control signal applied post-optimizer, not a learned parameter; conflating the two re-couples balance to the loss surface.

07The Kimi K2 zero-instability mechanism, dissected

Kimi K2 (1.04T total / 32B active, 384 experts top-8) was pretrained on 15.5T tokens with zero loss spikes and zero training instability (arXiv:2507.20534). This is the single most important existence proof for the trillion-scale stability stack, and the claim is not luck — it is the composition of four interlocking pieces:

  1. MuonClip's QK-Clip preventively caps the one failure mode Muon amplifies. Muon's orthogonalized updates equalize update RMS across singular directions, which is exactly what drives the Wq·Wk product growth that produces attention logit explosion. QK-Clip (τ=100) clamps it before it can compound. The two are co-designed: you adopt Muon for its large-batch persistence, and QK-Clip is the tax you pay for it.
  2. Weight decay 0.1 plus consistent-RMS scaling (the 0.2·√max(A,B) factor that rescales every matrix's update to RMS≈0.2) bounds weight norms globally, so no single projection drifts.
  3. A constant-then-decay WSD schedule (constant 2e-4 for 10T tokens, then cosine to 2e-5 over the final 5.5T) avoids the late-training LR transients that classically trigger spikes — there is no cosine mid-run LR ramp to perturb a settled basin.
  4. 67M-token batches sit near the critical batch size where Muon's data-efficiency advantage persists.

The empirical signature is the tell that the system found a stable basin rather than being permanently clamped: logits pin at τ=100 early in training, then decay on their own to a normal operating range with no retuning of τ. A system that were merely being held down by a hard clamp would stay pinned; the self-decay means the weights relaxed into a configuration where the logits are naturally bounded and the clip stops binding (after ~30% of steps it becomes a no-op). For the 10T/200B target, this is the blueprint: Muon + QK-Clip(τ=100) + λ=0.1 + consistent-RMS + WSD, and watch for the pin-then-decay signature in your max-logit trace as confirmation the basin is stable.

One forward-looking note: DeepSeek-V4 reaches the same zero-spike end state by a different route — it discards QK-Clip and instead applies RMSNorm directly to attention queries and KV entries (structural QK-norm rather than post-hoc clip), pairs it with a two-stage Newton-Schulz (8 aggressive steps then 2 near-identity polishing steps, the latter to handle the steep M^−0.96 spectral degradation of final-layer projections), and rescales update RMS to 0.18 (arXiv:2606.04058). Both stacks work. QK-Clip is the more battle-tested public recipe (15.5T tokens, fully described, with a correct MaxText implementation); QK-norm is structurally simpler and FlashAttention-compatible.

Do not mix the two QK stacks blindly

If you value reproducing a known-good run, take K2's QK-Clip; if you value forward-pass simplicity and are willing to ablate, take V4's QK-norm. They are alternatives that attack family (1) at different points — pick one deliberately and validate it, do not stack them.

08Silent data corruption: the failure with no checkpoint

At 100k GB200s, silent data corruption (SDC) is a first-class stability risk and the deepest reason skip-step and gradient-replay exist. Meta's Llama-3 54-day run saw 466 interruptions (419 unexpected, 78% hardware), with ~6 attributed to SDC; Google reports SDC "every week or two" in Gemini training (arXiv:2502.12340v1). Over a multi-month run on 100k GPUs, permanent-fault probability approaches certainty, and the controlled 2026 SDC study using 15 known-bad production nodes is sobering:

  • SDC is silent: corrupted nodes can show loss curves "identical" to healthy ones, and do not necessarily produce NaNs — so aggregate statistics are unreliable as a detector.
  • ABFT checksums detected only 1 of 15 bad nodes. Checksum-based detection is nearly useless here.
  • Observed submodule corruption rates reach 2.89e-2 (forward attention) with mismatch severity up to 3.79e11×, and worst-case gradient noise-to-signal of 5.1%.
The failure with no checkpoint — treat "mysterious late spikes" as a hardware problem

The effect is not a clean crash; it is slow weight drift to a worse optimum, occasionally surfacing late as a loss spike — which is why "mysterious late spikes" should be treated as a hardware-detection problem, not only an optimizer one. The only reliable detection is recomputation: shadow DP replicas comparing gradients during all-reduce, or deterministic replay (Gemini's hot-standby scanners).

skip-step alone will NOT catch SDC — the "new baseline" behavior is a liability here

For the 10T/200B build, budget explicitly for cross-DP gradient comparison plus periodic golden-value stress tests, and treat skip-step-on-spike as the containment layer that prevents an SDC-corrupted batch from poisoning Adam/Muon momentum before the offending node is quarantined. The skip-step buffer's "spike becomes the new baseline" behavior is a liability here — a slowly drifting corruption can normalize into the rolling mean, so skip-step alone will not catch SDC; you need the recomputation layer on top.

 Six primary scalars, each mapped to a failure family. The max attention logit is the canary; the skip-step fraction is the systemic-degradation alarm.

Two operating principles. First, the max attention logit is your canary — it is the earliest-firing, highest-signal scalar, and on a Muon run it is the one most likely to move. Wire it to a hard alert. Second, a rising skip-step fraction is never benign. skip-step is designed to absorb the occasional bad batch silently; if it is firing more than transiently, you have a systematic problem — a corrupt shard, a degrading node, or a router that is starting to collapse — and the guard is masking it. Page on the skip rate, not just on outright divergence.

  • Schedule: WSD — long constant phase for scaling-law probing, sharp final cosine decay.

The unifying claim

Trillion-scale stability is a composition property, not a single trick — validated by Kimi K2's 15.5T-token zero-spike run: each mitigation closes exactly one failure family, and the run is stable only when all five are closed simultaneously. Diagnose by family, instrument the canaries, and never let a runtime guard quietly absorb a root cause.
