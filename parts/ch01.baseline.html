01. The mid-2026 frontier landscape: who is training what, at what scale, and what it costs

The single most useful fact for anyone planning a 10T-total / 200B-active MoE on 100,000 GB200 GPUs is that, as of mid-2026, almost nobody has done anything close, and the one party that has trained a from-scratch trillion-parameter MoE on production GB200 silicon — Microsoft AI, with MAI-Base-1 / MAI-Thinking-1 — did it at one-eighth your GPU count, one-twentieth your total parameters, and one-sixth your active-parameter budget. The frontier you are joining is real, but the part of it that overlaps your exact target is a single 109-page technical report and a handful of leaked cluster-power numbers. Everything else is either (a) a closed Western run whose parameter counts are state secrets and whose compute must be inferred from energy and rack counts, or (b) a Chinese / Microsoft open(-ish)-weight run that publishes honest architecture tables but tops out an order of magnitude below your total-parameter target and 4–6× below your active-parameter target. This chapter maps that distribution run-by-run with explicit confidence labels, builds the comparison table you will return to repeatedly, and then positions your target inside it: which tier it sits in, who is nearest, and whether the configuration is plausible at all.

1.1 The bifurcation: closed-inferred vs. open-documented

By June 2026 the frontier has cleanly split into two epistemic regimes, and you must treat them differently.

The Western closed-weight tier — OpenAI, Google DeepMind, Anthropic, xAI — publishes essentially no architecture. Parameter counts, token counts, and FLOP budgets are absent from every GPT-5.x release note, the Gemini 3 Pro model card, and every Claude release. What is knowable is infrastructural: cluster GPU counts, datacenter power envelopes, and chip-purchase contracts, all of which leak through supply-chain reporting, satellite imagery of buildouts, and energy filings. From those you reconstruct compute via the standard identity (FLOPs ≈ 6 × active-params × tokens, plus an RL multiplier), and groups like Epoch AI publish methodologically transparent estimates with 90% confidence intervals. For this tier, treat every parameter number as rumor and every compute number as inferred unless it carries an Epoch-style derivation.

The open-documented tier — DeepSeek, Qwen/Alibaba, Moonshot's Kimi, Zhipu's GLM, MiniMax, and Microsoft AI — publishes real architecture tables, and in DeepSeek's and MAI's cases publishes (or all-but-publishes) the training recipe down to optimizer betas and FP8 cast points. This tier is where your actual engineering evidence lives. The irony worth internalizing before you spend a billion dollars of compute: the labs telling you how to train a trillion-parameter MoE are not the labs at the top of the capability leaderboard, and the labs at the top are telling you nothing. Your recipe must be assembled from the documented tier and extrapolated to a scale none of them have published.

1.2 The GB200 reference point: MAI-Base-1

MAI-Thinking-1 / MAI-Base-1 ("Building a Hill-Climbing Machine," Microsoft AI, 2 June 2026) is the load-bearing document of this entire field manual, because it is the only public, detailed report of a from-scratch trillion-parameter MoE trained on 8,192 GB200 GPUs — your exact substrate, at 8% of your scale. Confirmed specifics:

  • 35B active / 1T total sparse decoder-only MoE, activating 8 of 512 experts per token in a compressed latent space with softmax gating.
  • Alternating dense-FFN and high-sparsity-MoE layers (first FFN layer dense), rather than the uniform medium-sparsity stacking most open MoEs use — an architecture choice the report ablates and defends.
  • Interleaved 5:1 local:global attention: sliding-window-512 with RoPE base 10k on local layers, NoPE (no positional encoding) on global layers, which is what lets them run FlashAttention-4 and Ulysses context parallelism cleanly.
  • 30T-token main pretrain + 3.55T tokens of mid-training (64K then 256K context phases), 256K final context.
  • Numerics: BF16 master weights, FP8 E4M3 for the forward GEMM, FP8 E5M2 for the data-gradient, BF16 weight-gradient with FP32 accumulation, delayed scaling.
  • Parallelism: expert-parallel 64 + ZeRO-2 for pretrain and mid-training phase 1, ZeRO-3 / FSDP for mid-training phase 2, context parallelism for the long-context phases.
  • Global batch 134M tokens; AdamW with β1 = 0.95, β2 = 0.925, peak LR 2e-4 → 2e-5 cosine, ~12B-token warmup. The unusually low β2 is itself a stability signal worth noting.
  • A custom "YOLO" training framework with Triton/CUDA/CuteDSL/CUTLASS FP8 grouped-GEMM kernels for the 512-expert layer, and a full "Cluster Environment" section dedicated to goodput.

Three disclosures are strategically decisive for your planning. First, MAI deliberately over-trains at 500–1000 tokens-per-active-parameter (TPP) — a 35B-active model on 30T tokens is ~857 TPP — versus Chinchilla-optimal ~20 and their own 100–200 TPP ablation ladder, trading training compute for inference cheapness. Second, no distillation, no LM-generated synthetic data, no open training sets — a deliberately clean-data stance that flags clean-token scarcity as the binding constraint at scale. Third, an attention-output zero-initialization trick to fix early MoE router imbalance, a cheap stability fix you should adopt on day one. Reported benchmarks: 52.8% SWE-Bench Pro, 97.0% AIME 2025, 87.7% LiveCodeBench v6. This is the recipe to clone and scale; the rest of the landscape tells you which knobs to turn as you 5–8× it.

1.3 The Western closed tier (compute inferred)

OpenAI GPT-5.x. GPT-5 launched 7 August 2025. Epoch AI's best estimate puts it at ~3e25 FLOP pretraining and ~5e25 FLOP total (RL contributing 10–200% of pretrain), built on an assumed ~100B active params and ≥30T tokens — and critically, below GPT-4.5's >1e26 FLOP (Epoch). The clearest public read on the frontier's strategic shift: OpenAI traded raw pretraining scale for a smaller, over-trained, RL-heavy model. By June 2026 the line has iterated through GPT-5.1 toward GPT-5.5, trained at the Abilene "Stargate I" site (Oracle/OCI, GB200 NVL72 racks, ~1.2 GW planned, energized September 2025). Circulating 3–5T total-param claims are rumor.

Google Gemini 2.5 → 3 / 3.1. Gemini 3 Pro released 18 November 2025 (LMArena 1501 Elo, HLE 37.5% no-tools, GPQA-Diamond 91.9%). The model card confirms a sparse, natively-multimodal MoE trained on Google TPUs via JAX/Pathways but discloses no parameter count, token count, or FLOPs — Google's consistent posture across the 2.5/3 line. Training ran on TPU v5e + v6e (Trillium) pods on the Jupiter fabric (>13 Pb/s bisection, >100k-chip pods). "Deep Think" is a test-time-compute mode, not a separate pretrain. Widely-guessed 500B+ total / 50–100B active is rumor. The relevance to you: Gemini proves a hyperscaler can run frontier MoE pretraining at >100k-accelerator scale, but on a non-NVIDIA fabric whose collective-communication economics differ fundamentally from your NVLink-domain GB200 racks.

Anthropic Claude Opus 4.x. Zero parameter/token/FLOP disclosure anywhere. The confirmable facts are infrastructural: Project Rainier, AWS's ~500,000 Trainium2 cluster, is online and training Claude; Anthropic states it now uses >1M Trainium2 chips and has contracted (Nov 2025, expanded 2026) for up to 5 GW of Trainium2/3 capacity, with Rainier providing "more than 5×" the training compute of prior Claude models (Anthropic). Opus 4.6 is the SWE-Bench-Pro comparison target that MAI and others cite. Architecture is entirely undisclosed. Like Gemini, this is a non-NVIDIA datapoint — a proof of frontier scale, not a transferable recipe.

xAI Grok 3/4 (Colossus). The best-documented Western compute story. Colossus (Memphis) stood up 100k H100 in 122 days and doubled to ~200k mixed H100/H200/GB200 in 92 days; by December 2025 the mix was reportedly ~150k H100 + 50k H200 + 30k GB200, with Colossus 2 scaling toward **~110k GB200 NVL72 and ~1.1 GW** and the broader site reportedly ~555k GPUs / ~2 GW by early 2026 (SemiAnalysis; leaked/estimated). The headline for Grok 4 (July 2025): xAI claims RL compute on the same order of magnitude as pretraining — a ~10× RL scale-up over Grok 3, RL ≈ 50% of the training budget (xAI) — the cleanest public signal that the frontier RL:pretrain ratio is approaching 1:1. Epoch AI estimates Grok 4 at **~246M H100-hours, ~310 GWh, median ~$490M, ~154,000 t CO₂**, with large explicit uncertainty (Epoch). Colossus 2 is, crucially, the only ~100k-GB200-class cluster actually being stood up in the West — it is your closest real-world infrastructure analog, and the SemiAnalysis power-buildout reconstruction is the most concrete reference you have for the datacenter side of a 100k-GB200 deployment.

1.4 Meta Llama 4: the cautionary tale at your scale

Llama 4 (April 2025) shipped Scout (109B total / 17B active, 16 experts) and Maverick (~400B total / 17B active, 128 experts), both on >30T tokens across 200 languages (VentureBeat). But the model that matters to you is Behemoth — ~2T total / 288B active — Meta's frontier teacher, which as of mid-2026 is consistently reported shelved / never shipped, with mid-training MoE-routing and chunked-attention instabilities at 2T scale cited as why Meta lost confidence (leaked/rumor, but reported by multiple outlets). This is the single most important cautionary datapoint in the entire landscape for your target. Behemoth's 288B active is the only shipped-or-attempted active-parameter budget comparable to your 200B, and it did not ship — and it failed on exactly the two axes (routing stability, attention stability) that dominate trillion-parameter MoE risk. Read Behemoth not as "2T is impossible" but as "2T total with ~290B active, attempted without the MAI/Kimi stability stack, broke." Your job is to not repeat it.

1.5 DeepSeek V3 → V4: the efficiency frontier

DeepSeek defines the cheap-frontier datapoint everyone calibrates against. V3 (December 2024): 671B total / 37B active, 14.8T tokens, 2,788,000 H800 GPU-hours ≈ $5.576M marginal compute cost — all confirmed in the technical report. That run is also the most honest public MoE-at-scale efficiency anchor you have: derived from the published GPU-hours it lands at ~21.4% FP8 MFU (~42.9% BF16-equivalent), ~424 effective TFLOP/s/GPU over a full 14.8T-token run — a number that should weight far above any short MLPerf dense-405B sprint when you forecast your own sustained throughput.

V4 (released 24 April 2026, MIT): V4-Pro 1.6T total / 49B active, V4-Flash 284B / 13B active, both with 1M context, both reportedly trained on >32T tokens (leaked — DeepSeek published no token count, optimizer, hardware, or cost for V4) (Morph). The architectural pivot is the open-weight playbook for cheap 1M context: from V3's MLA, through V3.2's DeepSeek Sparse Attention (DSA), to V4's hybrid Compressed Sparse Attention + Heavily Compressed Attention with top-k compressed-token selection plus a 128-token uncompressed local window, KV compressed ~4× along sequence. V4-Pro-Max reportedly hits ~80.6% SWE-Bench Verified. For your long-context mid-training phase, DSA-lineage attention is the technique to lift; V4-Pro at 1.6T/49B is also the largest shipped open MoE total-param count, and still 6× below your total target.

1.8 Positioning the 10T / 200B / 100k-GB200 target

Total parameters. Your 10T total is ~6× DeepSeek-V4-Pro (1.6T), ~5–10× Kimi K2 / Qwen3-Max (1T), and ~5× the shelved Llama-4 Behemoth (2T). It would be the largest dense-equivalent capacity ever attempted by a wide margin — roughly one order of magnitude above the largest shipped open MoE in total params. No one has publicly shipped past ~1.6T total; the only attempt past 2T (Behemoth) was abandoned. You are, in total-parameter terms, off the right edge of the entire distribution.

Active parameters — the real outlier. Your 200B active is the genuinely radical bet. It is ~4–6× the active budget of every shipped open MoE (V4-Pro 49B, K2 32B, MAI 35B, Qwen3 22B) and comparable only to Behemoth's 288B-active teacher, which did not ship. Meanwhile the field has spent all of 2025–2026 lowering activation ratios (MiniMax ~4%, V4-Flash 4.6%, Qwen3 9%). Your configuration is 2% sparsity in total terms (200B/10T — sparser than most) but a very heavy per-token compute load in absolute terms. Active params drive both training FLOPs and inference cost; choosing 200B active is a deliberate, expensive bet against the prevailing efficiency trend, justified only if you believe per-token capability at that active budget buys capability the sparser configs cannot reach. This is the decision to interrogate hardest before committing.

Compute budget — corrected hardware math. Be careful with the GB200 NVL72 spec sheet: NVIDIA's headline 720 PFLOP/s FP8 / 360 PFLOP/s BF16 per 72-GPU rack are with-sparsity (2:4) figures — the GB200 NVL72 page footnotes "Dense is one-half sparse spec shown." For ordinary dense training FLOPs the meaningful peaks are 360 PFLOP/s FP8 and 180 PFLOP/s BF16 per rack, i.e. ~4.5 PFLOP/s dense FP8 / ~2.25 PFLOP/s dense BF16 per B200 GPU. Consequently a 100,000-GPU cluster is ~0.45 EFLOP/s dense FP8 peak, not the ~1.0 EFLOP/s you get only by counting sparsity. Derate this hard for MFU. Realistic sustained large-MoE FP8 MFU on GB200 in 2025–2026 sits in the **~20–35% band** — the DeepSeek-V3 floor is ~21% FP8 and well-tuned dense frameworks ceiling near ~47% (SemiAnalysis) — with heavy routing and expert-parallel all-to-all dragging real MoE numbers toward the bottom of that range. At ~35% MFU on ~0.45 EFLOP/s dense FP8 you sustain roughly 0.16 EFLOP/s.

Training FLOPs ≈ 6 × active-params × tokens. For 200B active:

Tokens Pretrain FLOP vs. frontier Wall-clock @ ~0.16 EF/s sustained
20T ~2.4e25 ≈ GPT-5 pretrain ~1.7 days*
30T ~3.6e25 ~1.2× GPT-5 pretrain ~2.6 days*
60T ~7.2e25 approaching GPT-4.5 ~5.2 days*
~85T ~1.0e26 GPT-4.5 territory ~7.4 days*

*Compute-only lower bounds; real wall-clock is multiples higher once you add checkpoint/restart, fault recovery, the NVLink-domain failure blast-radius, optimizer-state churn, and the multi-month software-maturity ramp GB200 still demands. SemiAnalysis cautioned in late 2025 that no large-scale training run had yet completed on GB200 NVL72, blocked by software immaturity and NVLink-backplane reliability (the whole 72-GPU rack is one failure domain), and that GB200 must run ≥1.6× faster than H100 just to break even on TCO (SemiAnalysis). Treat the 1.6× break-even as the real bar, not the marketing FLOPs.

The strategic reading of this table: a single 30T-token run lands at roughly GPT-5-pretrain scale (~3e25) but with ~2× the active params, and pushing into GPT-4.5 territory (~1e26) requires ~85T tokens or a heavy RL stage on top — and with the frontier RL:pretrain ratio approaching 1:1 (Grok 4, GPT-5), the RL stage is no longer an afterthought to your budget.

TPP check — why you cannot over-train your way out. This is the cleanest constraint on the whole plan. MAI over-trained a 35B-active model to 500–1000 TPP (200T tokens would be needed at the top of that range). Your 200B active at even 1000 TPP would demand 200T tokens — flatly infeasible on the clean data MAI flags as scarce. At a sane 100–200 TPP you train on 20–40T tokens, i.e. mild over-training, decisively not the heavy-over-training regime MAI used. The lesson: your active budget is simply too large to over-train, so you cannot recover inference efficiency the way smaller-active models do. Above ~40–50T tokens data, not compute, becomes the binding constraint — clean-token scarcity bites long before your 100k GB200 cluster runs out of FLOP/s. Your run is data-bound, not compute-bound, which inverts the usual frontier intuition.

Stability and recipe risk. The two largest publicly-attempted MoEs near your scale converged on the same lessons, and your run must adopt all of them: MuonClip-style optimization (Kimi: zero spikes at 1T/15.5T) or carefully-tuned AdamW with attention-output zero-init and global-batch load balancing (MAI); dense + MoE layer interleaving rather than uniform medium-sparsity; FP8 forward GEMMs with FP32 accumulation; and expert-parallel ≈ 64 as the practical EP degree. Behemoth's reported failure mode — routing plus chunked-attention instability at 2T total / 288B active — is the single biggest flag for going past ~2T total, and your 10T sits 5× beyond it. At 10T total you will need pipeline + expert + context parallelism stacked simultaneously, and goodput/determinism tooling (MAI dedicates an entire Cluster Environment section to it) will matter more to time-to-results than peak FLOPs ever will.

What tier you sit in. There are, functionally, three tiers in mid-2026. Tier 1, the shipped open MoEs, tops out at ~1.6T total / ~49B active (DeepSeek-V4-Pro, Kimi K2, Qwen3-Max). Tier 2, the inferred Western closed runs, is unknown in parameters but ~3e25–1e26 FLOP in compute (GPT-5, Grok 4, Gemini 3, Claude Opus 4.x). Tier 0, the abandoned frontier, contains exactly one entry: Llama-4 Behemoth at 2T/288B. Your target sits above all three on total parameters and in a tier of one on the active-parameter axis — the only company at 200B active is a model that was cancelled for instability. On compute you are squarely Tier 2 (a 30T-token run ≈ GPT-5 pretrain), which is reassuring: the FLOP budget is precedented even if the parameter configuration is not. The plausibility verdict: the compute is plausible and precedented; the total-parameter scale is unprecedented but a smooth ~6× extrapolation of documented MoE practice; the 200B-active choice is the one genuinely contrarian, genuinely risky bet, and the recipe to de-risk it is MAI-Base-1 scaled up, with Kimi's MuonClip for spike-free optimization and DeepSeek's DSA-lineage sparse attention for the long-context phase. You are not doing something the field has proven impossible — Behemoth's cancellation is a stability-engineering failure, not a scaling-law wall — but you are doing it with thinner margins for error than any shipped run, on silicon that, as of late 2025, no one had yet completed a large run on.
