This is the payoff chapter. The framing assumption is uncomfortable but load-bearing: MaxText was built for TPU pods, and almost every item below is a place where the GPU/NVLink/InfiniBand reality of an NVL72 fleet diverges from the ICI-mesh abstractions baked into base.yml. The punch-list is ordered so that each item de-risks the next; do not reorder it, because the framework decision (item 0) gates everything, and the fault-tolerance and dataloader items (8, 6) are the ones that quietly determine whether you ever finish.

MaxText-on-GPU is a real option, not a TPU toy — but the bet is whether XLA:GPU plus the JAX MoE kernel zoo can hit the ~1,000+ TFLOPS/GPU that Megatron-Core demonstrably does on GB200, on your schedule. Gate the whole run on a 1,000-GPU bake-off.

Throughout, I quote dense hardware numbers, not the marketing sparse ones. A GB200 NVL72 rack is 360 PFLOP/s dense FP8 / 180 PFLOP/s dense BF16 (the 720/360 on NVIDIA's page are 2:4-sparse figures; the page's own footnote says "Dense is one-half sparse spec shown"), i.e. ~4.5 dense PFLOP/s FP8 per B200 GPU, so 100k GPUs is ~0.45 EFLOP/s dense FP8 peak, not 1.0. At a defensible 35–40% large-MoE FP8 MFU that is ~160–180 dense PFLOP/s of useful throughput — every percentage point of MFU you leave on the floor in the items below is ~4 PFLOP/s, and every minute of badput is ~10 EFLOP wasted.

00The framework decision: MaxText-on-GPU vs Megatron-Core / TorchTitan

Decide this first; it is the only irreversible item. The honest position: the most-proven 10T-MoE-on-GB200 stack today is Megatron-Core, which reports 1,233 TFLOPS/GPU on GB200 for DeepSeek-V3-685B (vs 1,048 on H100) and 974 (GB200)/919 (H100) for Qwen3-235B — real, published, GB200 MFU envelopes you can design against (Megatron-Core MoE, arXiv 2603.07685). It ships Parallel Folding (separate attention vs MoE meshes), the Flex dispatcher wired to DeepEP/HybridEP, TEGroupedMLP grouped-GEMM, and — critically — it is what NVIDIA optimizes the GB200 software stack for. The DeepSeek lineage (DualPipe, DeepEP, DeepGEMM MegaMoE) is all CUDA/PTX and drops into the PyTorch world with zero impedance mismatch.

MaxText's countervailing strengths are real and the reason you might stay: it already contains a Muon optimizer wired through optax.contrib._muon (optimizers.py, opt_type: "muon"), DeepSeek-V3/V4 routing variants in moe.py, MLA, Orbax emergency/multi-tier checkpointing, Grain elastic dataloading, and a TransformerEngine-JAX bridge that already exposes te_nvfp4/te_mxfp8 recipes (quantizations.py). NVIDIA actively co-develops NVFP4-on-Blackwell support for JAX/MaxText. So the framework decision is not "MaxText is a TPU toy" — it is "can XLA:GPU + the JAX MoE kernel zoo hit the same ~1,000+ TFLOPS/GPU that Megatron-Core demonstrably does on GB200, on your schedule?"

The first risk is the most dangerous. DeepSeek-V3's recipe promotes WGMMA partial sums to FP32 CUDA-core registers every N_C=128 elements (≈4 WGMMAs) to dodge Hopper/Blackwell tensor cores' ~14-bit accumulation and its up-to-2% GEMM error (DeepSeek-V3, arXiv 2412.19437). JAX cannot natively express this two-level accumulation; you depend on TransformerEngine-JAX or a Pallas:Mosaic-GPU kernel to do it for you, and you must verify it does — this is the single most likely correctness/quality landmine in a JAX FP8 port. The second: DeepSeek-V4's MegaMoE fuses EP-dispatch → FP8×FP4 GEMM → SwiGLU+requant → FP8×FP4 GEMM → EP-combine into one SymmBuffer kernel for 1.50–1.73× general / up to 1.96× latency speedup (DeepSeek-V4, arXiv 2606.19348). MaxText's path is unfused: jax.lax.ragged_all_to_all for dispatch/combine, then a separate tokamax.ragged_dot/megablox gmm for the expert GEMM.

The recommendation

If your team's center of mass is JAX and you already have MaxText running at scale on TPU, commit to MaxText-on-GPU but with a hard dependency on TransformerEngine-JAX for the FP8/NVFP4 GEMMs and DeepEP (via the NVIDIA hybrid-ep branch) for the all-to-all — do not try to ride pure XLA collectives for EP. Budget one senior kernel engineer for two quarters to land the FP8 accumulation path and the DeepEP binding, and gate the full-scale run on a 1,000-GPU bake-off against the Megatron-Core MFU numbers above. If that bake-off comes in below ~30% MFU, switch to Megatron-Core; the published GB200 evidence is on its side. TorchTitan is the wrong choice at this scale today — treat it as a parallelism-pattern reference, not a destination.

01Parallelism config for 10T/200B on 100k GB200

The memory math, not the FLOPs, dictates the layout. With Muon as the primary optimizer (the right choice, see item 5), the per-parameter resident budget is ≈ 2 B bf16 compute weight + 4 B fp32 master + 2 B bf16 grad + 4 B fp32 Muon momentum ≈ 12 B/param, vs ~16 B for AdamW. For 10T params that is 120 TB (Muon) vs 160 TB (Adam) of resident, non-replication-shardable state (Muon quantization, arXiv 2509.23106). Against 100k×192 GB = ~19.2 PB aggregate HBM this is only ~0.6%, which is the whole point: this is a sharding-and-bandwidth problem, not a capacity problem. Pure FSDP/ZeRO-3 fails not on capacity but because its all-gather of the full 10–20 TB weight set is batch-independent and becomes the wall as the FSDP degree grows toward 100k, and because it replicates the all-to-all-bound expert FFNs instead of sharding experts (JAX Scaling Book).

The composition rule is a strict fabric hierarchy: keep every high-volume collective (TP, EP all-to-all) inside the 72-GPU NVLink domain (1.8 TB/s/GPU); let only PP and DP cross InfiniBand (~90 GB/s/GPU, the ~20:1 cliff) (DeepEP, GB200 NVL72). 100k GPUs ≈ 1,389 NVL72 racks. The target layout:

With EP×TP=64 ≤ 72 inside a rack, PP=16–32 racks/replica, one replica is ~1,150–2,300 GPUs and 100k GPUs gives ~43–87 DP replicas. This mirrors DeepSeek-V3's 2,048-H800 layout (PP=16, EP=64 across 8 nodes, ZeRO-1 DP, no TP during training) but collapses the 8-node EP domain into a single NVL72 rack because the 72-GPU NVLink domain is what 8 H800 nodes used to be (DeepSeek-V3, arXiv 2412.19437). For the dense parts you can run TP=8 where V3 ran TP=1, because NVL72 makes 8-way TP free of InfiniBand.

MaxText-specific gotchas you must get right:

The logical mesh is ['diloco','data','stage','fsdp','fsdp_transpose','context','context_autoregressive','tensor','tensor_transpose','tensor_sequence','expert','autoregressive'] and DCN axes must appear earlier in the list than ICI axes — your PP (stage) and DP (data) are DCN-mapped and must precede the ICI-mapped tensor/expert. The product of all ici_* must equal chips-per-slice (72). Map ICI → NVLink rack, DCN → InfiniBand.

Never shard experts or TP over DCN

Leave dcn_expert_parallelism=1. Sharding experts over DCN puts the EP all-to-all on InfiniBand — fatal. Likewise dcn_tensor_* and dcn_sequence_* stay 1; base.yml literally annotates them "never recommended," and on a GB200 build TP/SP-over-DCN is a guaranteed comm-bound stall.

Pipeline schedule. MaxText's layers/pipeline.py does circular pipelining (num_pipeline_microbatches, pipeline_delay_activation_forwarding, num_layers_per_pipeline_stage) but does not ship DualPipe/DualPipeV. For a 10T run where the EP all-to-all crosses NVL72 boundaries during the long-context phase, DualPipe's native comm-compute overlap — bubble (PP/2−1)(F&B+B−3W), cost 2× param copies (+20 TB, absorbable) — is worth it, and DualPipeV halves the activation cost (PP/2 devices, PP/2 stages) (DualPipe). This is a real engineering item: either implement a DualPipeV schedule in pipeline.py or accept the 1F1B bubble (PP−1)(F+B) plus zero-bubble ZB-H1 (~1/3 bubble at equal memory, arXiv 2401.10241) as a stopgap. If you stay 1F1B, run PipeOffload-style activation offloading (arXiv 2503.01328) to keep PP=16–32 of a 10T model memory-feasible.

Watch the vocab/output-head imbalance

A 10T model's vocab projection is large and will unbalance any zero-bubble or DualPipe schedule unless you apply vocabulary parallelism (arXiv 2411.05288). MaxText keeps logits_dense on AdamW (item 5) but does not balance its PP-stage cost.

02MoE kernels, all-to-all, and comms overlap

MaxText's MoE substrate lives in moe.py (RoutedMoE, sparse_matmul, permute/unpermute/get_all_to_all_params). The defaults in base.yml are megablox: true, sparse_matmul: true, capacity_factor: -1.0 (dropless), ragged_buffer_factor: -1.0, use_tokamax_gmm: false, use_ring_of_experts: false, use_ragged_sort: false. Here is what to change.

Ragged buffer sizing — the dropless-vs-memory knob

With ragged_buffer_factor ≤ 0 MaxText computes the worst-case dropless size as min(ep_degree, global_experts/top_k) * balanced_size; for a DeepSeek-style 256-expert/top-8 config imbalance is never worse than 256/8 = 32×, and once EP>32 the bound is global_exp/top_k independent of EP. The worst-case dropless buffer is a real activation-memory tax at 32×.

moe worst_case_factor = min( |EP| , global_exp / topk ) Once |EP| > global_exp/topk the bound stops growing — for 256/8 it is 32×, independent of EP.
Set ragged_buffer_factor ≈ 3.0

Set ragged_buffer_factor ≈ 3.0 — the in-code recommended near-dropless, memory-bounded compromise — and accept rare token drops, which are quality-neutral once the load balancer converges (moe.py). You trade a vanishingly small (un-guaranteed) drop probability for ~10× less activation memory than the 32× worst-case buffer.

Grouped-GEMM backend

Three dispatch paths in gmm(): jax.lax.ragged_dot, forked megablox mblx.gmm (quantized, gmm-v2), and tokamax.ragged_dot (Mosaic). On GB200 the Mosaic-GPU path via use_tokamax_gmm: true is the JAX-native ragged-GEMM you want for the expert FFN, but note use_gmm_v2: true "Currently incompatible with quantization" — so if you need FP4 experts you are on the megablox quantized gmm path, not tokamax. Benchmark both against the published Hopper/GB200 numbers from MegaBlocks (1.38–4.35× over Tutel, 1.8–2.4× over dense Megatron-LM, arXiv 2211.15841) and read SonicMoE, arXiv 2512.14080 — the freshest IO/tile-aware grouped-GEMM design, benchmarked on H100 and GB200 — before locking a kernel.

All-to-all

MaxText uses jax.lax.ragged_all_to_all (not dense a2a) with offsets/sizes from get_all_to_all_params. The ragged Pallas kernels (a2a_ragged_sort/ring_ragged_sort under use_ragged_sort/use_ring_of_experts) gather only the valid prefix rather than the padded buffer — enable these at EP in the hundreds, where padded sort is pure waste. But the harder truth: at 100k-GB200 scale the right all-to-all substrate is DeepEP V2 / the NVIDIA hybrid-ep branch, which measures 726/740 GB/s NVLink dispatch/combine at EP8 on SM100/Blackwell (max-perf) and reduces SM consumption to 4–6 SMs, with FP8 dispatch + BF16 combine halving the dispatch leg (DeepEP README). MaxText has no binding to DeepEP today; writing one is the highest-leverage MoE-systems item after the FP8 GEMM. Two systems landmines: low-latency RDMA dispatch sags to 194µs/39 GB/s at 256 experts (per-token RDMA bandwidth falls as expert count grows), and DeepEP issue #427 reports low-latency dispatch can corrupt under heavily imbalanced expert load — exactly the early-training regime before your bias balancer converges (DeepEP #427).

Step-0 hazard — #427 imbalance corruption filed upstream

Run the high-throughput dispatch path (not low-latency) until the bias balancer converges, or use hash-routed early layers (which cannot hit #427) as a clean warm-up. And treat the 39 GB/s low-latency RDMA floor at ≥128 experts as a hard design constraint — per-token RDMA bandwidth structurally falls as expert count rises.

Routing and balancing

MaxText's get_topk() already implements DeepSeek-V3 sigmoid + learnable-bias aux-loss-free balancing (routed_bias_update_rate, default 0.0, feeding calculate_load_balance_updates, applied post-optimizer via update_state_param), DeepSeek-V4 hash routing (tid2eid lookup, is_hash_routing gating lb-loss off), and expert_group_mask/deepseek_routing for node-limited (group-limited) routing. Set routed_bias_update_rate on a decay schedule mirroring V3: γ=0.001 for the bulk of training, then 0.0 for the final ~500B tokens (DeepSeek-V3). Configure node-limited routing so each token reaches ≤2–4 racks (the GB200 analog of M=4 node-limited routing) and an expert group fits one NVL72 domain, so the RDMA leg only carries cross-group traffic. At deployment you will need EPLB-style redundant-expert replication of the hottest experts (essentially mandatory at 100k GPUs); LPLB — per-batch dynamic redistribution as an LP solved single-SM in ~100µs — is the research-frontier online variant (LPLB). The decisive ratio to remember when deciding whether comm hides under compute is ~6,144 FLOPs/Byte (V4's framing): below it the all-to-all is exposed.

Map one routing group to one NVL72 domain

Set the group structure so an expert group fits one NVL72 rack and node-limited M=2–4 routing keeps the dominant traffic on NVLink — the RDMA leg then carries only cross-group tokens, and you live on the high-throughput curve rather than the 39 GB/s low-latency floor.

03Precision: FP8 now, FP4 QAT path

Ship FP8 first. The reference floor is the DeepSeek-V3 recipe: per-tile 1×128 activations, per-block 128×128 weights, E4M3 on all forward/dgrad/wgrad operands, power-of-two scales, with the FP32-promotion accumulation fix (item 0) (arXiv 2412.19437). The non-negotiable "precision breaks" map — keep router/gating, all norms, attention operators, embeddings, and output head in BF16/FP32; FP32 master weights + FP32 grad accumulators — is invariant across every FP4 paper since, because a single mis-quantized gate flips an expert assignment.

In MaxText, the modern path is Qwix (use_qwix_quantization: true; AQT is deprecated, per base.yml). The get_quantization_rule rules scope quantization to decoder/.*layers.* and route over op_names ("dot_general","gmm","ragged_dot") — note gmm/ragged_dot are precisely the MoE expert grouped/ragged GEMMs, which is exactly where you want low precision and where the router/norms are automatically excluded (quantizations.py). Set quantization: "fp8_full" for static-scaling FP8, or wire the TransformerEngineQuantization recipe te_mxfp8 (block_size=32) for MX-FP8.

Why NVFP4 over MXFP4 — the data-efficiency gap is concrete

NVFP4 uses 16-element micro-blocks with a per-block E4M3 scale + FP32 per-tensor scale, preserving the ±4/±6 FP4 points that MXFP4's power-of-two E8M0 scale clips. The cost of getting this wrong: MXFP4 needs 36% more tokens (1.36T vs 1.0T) to match NVFP4 loss — at a 10T budget that is the difference between a 10T and a 13.6T run (NVFP4 pretraining, arXiv 2509.25149).

The FP4 path is a sequenced upgrade, not a day-1 commit. NVFP4 pretraining (the 12B Mamba-Transformer to 10T tokens, the longest public 4-bit run) requires all four of: ~16% of linear layers in BF16 (first 2 + final 8 blocks), a 16×16 Random Hadamard Transform on Wgrad inputs only (fixed ±1 sign vector shared across all layers), 2D 16×16 block scaling on weights, and stochastic rounding on gradients only (SR on forward weights/acts is detrimental). End-game: switch NVFP4→BF16 at ~82% of tokens to cut LR-decay-phase loss error from 1.5% to 0.5% at ~6% high-precision compute.

MaxText already wires these toggles: te_nvfp4 (NVFP4BlockScaling) and te_nvfp4_no_rht, with a code TODO confirming NVFP4's native 16-block needs fused RHT (currently block_size=128 until unfused RHT lands). The AQT/Qwix vjp_*_stochastic_rounding is per-tensor configurable — set SR on the gradient tensors only. The mixed-precision regex JSON (dense_llm_subchannel.json, per-layer w_bits/a_bits/tile_size) is the machinery that expresses "experts FP4, router/attention/norms high-precision." Follow DeepSeek-V4's production pattern: FP4 QAT on MoE expert weights (the parameter bulk) + the lightning indexer, FP8 everywhere else, FP8 KV with BF16 only on RoPE dims, with a lossless FP4→FP8 dequant reusing the FP8 pipeline (DeepSeek-V4 blog).

FP4×FP8 runs at FP8 speed on today's silicon stated in V4

FP4×FP8 currently runs at FP8×FP8 speed on existing silicon — on GB200 today FP4 experts are a memory/comms win (smaller weights, smaller all-to-all), not a compute win. And heed the precision scaling-law warning: more pretraining tokens makes a model more fragile to post-hoc quantization (δPTQ ∝ Dγ/Nγ), which is precisely why V4 went QAT, not PTQ (Scaling Laws for Precision, arXiv 2411.04330).

04Optimizer: Muon is already in the repo — finish wiring it

MaxText already has Muon (opt_type: "muon" via optax.contrib._muon.muon, with get_muon_weight_dimension_numbers emitting bespoke reduction/output-axis tuples for MoE expert weights, attention out-proj, and QKV). The matrix-only / AdamW-for-the-rest split is correct and matches the field consensus (embeddings, unembed, RMSNorm gains, biases → AdamW). Take Muon: it halves the optimizer state (4 B vs 8 B/param), and — the real reason at 100k-GPU width — its data-efficiency advantage persists and even grows into the over-large-batch regime up to 16M-token batches, exactly where AdamW falls off its critical-batch cliff (Essential AI, arXiv 2505.02222). Be honest about the headline: the "2× FLOPs" claim shrinks to ~10–15% fewer tokens once AdamW is well-tuned; you adopt Muon for batch-scaling robustness and the memory halving, not a 2× miracle.

Three Muon settings, in order

Set muon_consistent_rms: 0.2 (the Moonlight rule: rescale every matrix's update to RMS≈0.2 so a shared LR works across shapes; Moonlight, arXiv 2502.16982; V4 uses 0.18). Set muon_weight_decay: 0.1 (default 0 is a trap — out-of-the-box Muon needs decoupled WD to scale). And patch in V4's two-stage Newton-Schulz: the stock 5 steps at (3.4445,−4.7750,2.0315) under-polishes final-layer projections, which degrade as steeply as M−0.96 in singular-value quantiles at 10T scale (Spectral Scaling Laws, arXiv 2606.04058).

Distributed Muon at 10T. Even bf16 momentum is ~20 TB and must be ZeRO-1 sharded across DP; Moonlight's Distributed Muon all-gathers the full matrix transiently for the NS iteration (overhead in (1, 1.25]× of distributed AdamW). At 100k-GB200 the all-gather of full momentum is the bottleneck — evaluate Dion (amortized rank-r power iteration, exchanges only O((m+n)r), never unshards the full matrix, composes with FSDP+TP) as the scalable alternative if you become DP-sync-bound (Dion, arXiv 2504.05295).

Hyperparameter transfer — and do not double-apply µP

For width/batch HP transfer, adopt u-µP (fuses Unit Scaling with µP, collapses the sweep to ~9 LR-only runs, and trains stably in FP8 at 7B where standard µP diverges from gradient underflow — directly relevant on GB200 FP8/FP4 tensor cores, arXiv 2407.17465). Note Muon's √max(A,B)/RMS scaling already implements much of µP's per-layer LR scaling, so do not naively double-apply. Pick batch size and weight decay from Power Lines: τopt ∝ (D/N)−0.52, Bcrit ∝ D0.5. At 10T tokens Bcrit lands in the tens-of-millions, consistent with K2/V4 peak batches of 75–94M; running at B=Bcrit costs 2× minimum tokens, so size below it (arXiv 2505.13738).

skip_step_on_spikes (train.py wrapper, skip_step_interval: 128, skip_step_scaling_factor: 6.0): a rolling Bessel-corrected mean ± 6σ guard that zeros the update and freezes momentum when loss/grad_norm spikes, preserving optimizer state — ported from OLMo-core. Turn it on (skip_step_on_spikes: true). It is the practical spike guard that layers under any schedule and prevents a single bad batch from poisoning the Muon momentum. z-loss (z_loss_multiplier, default 0.0): set it (~1e-4) to keep logits from drifting; it is aggregated in the train_step alongside the MoE lb-loss.

Muon excites exploding attention logits — enable use_qk_norm

Muon excites exploding attention logits (Kimi-K2 saw max logits >1000). MaxText has use_qk_norm (default false) and attn_logits_soft_cap (default 0.0). Enable use_qk_norm: true — this is DeepSeek-V4's structural fix (RMSNorm on Q and KV entries to bound logits), which V4 chose over QK-Clip and which delivered spike-free training. If you instead want Kimi-K2's QK-Clip (per-head conditional rescale by min(1, τ/Smax) with τ=100), that is not in the repo and would need adding; for a V4-shaped MLA model, use_qk_norm is the lower-effort, proven path. The Qwen3-480B config in-repo already sets use_qk_norm — a working reference.

06Dataloader: Grain at 100k-host scale

At ~12,500 hosts the dataloader stops being a per-host concern and becomes a distributed storage problem governed by object-store request ceilings, not bandwidth. A 10T-token corpus at 4 B/token read at 2e7–1e8 tok/s is only 80–400 GB/s — trivial bandwidth — but 12,500 hosts × even 10 reads/s = 125k req/s, far past a flat GCS bucket's ~5,000-reads/s cold-start ceiling (S3: 5,500 GET/prefix), guaranteeing 503 SlowDown/429 storms at launch and after every preemption (GCS request rate, S3 performance). MaxText's input_pipeline/* routes dataset_type → grain | hf | olmo_grain | tfds | c4_mlperf | synthetic; use Grain + ArrayRecord (the only Grain format supporting true global shuffle via Riegeli random-access). The fixes, in priority order:

The single highest-leverage knob: expansion_factor_real_data

Set expansion_factor_real_data > 1 so only jax.process_count()/factor hosts read GCS and broadcast batches over the device mesh (get_process_loading_real_data + PlaceHolderDataIterator). A factor of 8 turns 12,500 readers into ~1,560 — cutting the object-store fanout directly.

  1. Cut the object-store fanout directly. expansion_factor_real_data > 1 (above) is the single highest-leverage knob.
  2. Colocate the loader. colocated_python_data_input: true runs Grain on the accelerator host so shard decode/collation use host CPU/RAM with NVLink-adjacent transfer, eliminating PCIe-roundtrip host stalls.
  3. Use the elastic/pure-function resume path. Per-host process_{i}-of-{N}.json checkpoints break on resize and create N reads on restart. Prefer grain_use_elastic_iterator: true, whose ElasticIterator state is a single global scalar — only process 0 writes process_0.json and every process reads it, so the iterator survives a change in jax.process_count() (multihost_dataloading.py). The in-repo OLMo Grain loader (olmo_data_grain.py) is the even cleaner pattern: a pure-function OlmoIndexSampler keyed by SHA-256(seed,epoch) Fisher-Yates needing zero serialized iterator state — you pass initial_step = train_step * per_host_batch at construction. Note its documented scaling defect: shuffled_global_indices materializes a full numpy permutation (~5.8 GB at uint64 for 724M instances) per epoch per host; at trillions of instances move to OLMo-core's on-disk build_and_save_global_indices memmap (compute once, mmap-share, uint32 — switch to uint64/block-structured above 4.29B instances).
  4. Storage layout. HNS buckets (up to 8× initial QPS), ≥256 MB ArrayRecord shards (one GET amortizes thousands of samples), thousands of name-hashed prefixes to partition the keyspace, and a staggered exponential-backoff ramp so 12,500 hosts don't cold-start the same prefix simultaneously.
  5. Mixtures. Use grain.IterDataset.mix(weights) (not MapDataset.mix) so each component checkpoints independently and the curriculum can be re-weighted after a restart — critical for long curricula (grain_data_processing.py).
  6. Determinism as run identity. Pin data_shuffle_seed and treat it (plus the elastic global-scalar state) as part of run identity so any elastic resize stays bit-deterministic.

07Data pipeline: offline prep, taxonomy curation, and the scaling-law campaign

The discipline at this scale: do all heavy work offline, keep the training loader a dumb deterministic index→fetch→collate function. The proof point is Essential-Web v1.0 — 24T tokens / 23.6B docs processed in a 7-day window with "0 crashes" on a Daft pipeline sustaining 32k req/s/VM (Eventual/Daft). Concretely:

Pre-tokenize and pre-pack offline, always

Pre-tokenize offline. HF Rust tokenizers do ~12.5M tok/s/core, so 10T tokens is ~106 core-seconds — a few-thousand-core-hours embarrassingly-parallel job. Store as uint32 token IDs in ArrayRecord; online tokenization would burn the host CPU the loader needs for shard decode. Pre-pack offline with best-fit-decreasing bin-packing (sort docs descending, place in tightest bin) so packing is deterministic and checkpointable; MaxText's packing/sequence_packing.py produces inputs_segmentation/inputs_position consumed by flash-attention varlen for intra-document masking.

Decide the masking question deliberately: intra-doc masking gives a real perplexity win (arXiv 2402.13991), but DeepSeek-V3 deliberately declined cross-sample masking during pretraining (only at SFT) as an efficiency choice — weigh the splash/flash-varlen kernel cost against the win. Note the splash-attention constraint visible in the OLMo loader: q_seq_len must be divisible by 512, so mask the boundary position rather than emitting L-1.

Taxonomy curation. A 200B-active model is data-bound; the curation taxonomy (domain mix, dedup aggressiveness, quality-classifier thresholds, code/math/multilingual ratios) is set offline and re-weighted live via the IterDataset.mix per-component checkpointing above. This decoupling — heavy global shuffle/dedup/classification in Daft/Ray/Spark, emit large shards, re-weight at runtime — is the pattern.

Run the scaling-law campaign on WSD — the switch points are outputs, not guesses

Run the campaign on a WSD schedule (warmup-stable-decay): a constant stable phase lets you branch short decays to probe the loss frontier continuously without committing the full run, and the river-valley analysis explains why the sharp final decay works. Fit the precision (item 3), batch/WD (item 4), and Muon-vs-Adam iso-loss curves on 399M–4B proxies before the 10T commit. The NVFP4→BF16 switch point (~82%) and the routed-bias γ decay (~95%) are campaign outputs, not guesses.

08Fault tolerance: the item that determines whether you finish

At 100k GB200 the MTBF is brutal. Llama-3 405B logged 419 unexpected interruptions in 54 days on 16,384 H100 (~1 every ~3 hours); linearly extrapolating the per-GPU rate to 100k GB200 gives an interruption every ~25–35 minutes, likely worse given Blackwell's power density and the immature NVL72 copper backplane (Llama-3, arXiv 2407.21783). The governing identity is goodput ≈ MTBF/(MTBF+MTTR), and you cannot raise MTBF at this scale — every technique is an MTTR-reduction. If MTTR is 20 min against a 30-min MTBF, goodput collapses to ~60%; cutting MTTR to 1 min restores ~97%.

mem goodput ≈ MTBF / (MTBF + MTTR) MTBF ~30 min is fixed; MTTR=20 min → ~60% goodput, MTTR=1 min → ~97%. Every item below is an MTTR cut.

MaxText already has the machinery in common/checkpointing.py; wire it correctly:

Enable multi-tier checkpointing — peer-RAM restore, not GCS

Set enable_multi_tier_checkpointing: true, local_checkpoint_directory=/local, local_checkpoint_period=20, multi_tier_checkpointing_backup_interval_minutes=20. This writes frequent checkpoints to a per-process local dir (the EmergencyCheckpointManager appends the distributed process_id to the path on GPU) and flushes rarely to GCS (PersistentCheckpointOptions). On restore, SingleReplicaArrayHandler / enable_single_replica_ckpt_restoring broadcasts a single surviving replica across the replica mesh — a swapped-in node pulls weights from a healthy replica's RAM, not from GCS (cf. Gemini SOSP'23) — converting a minutes-scale GCS restore into a seconds-scale broadcast.

Set the optimal interval by Young/Daly: interval ≈ √(2·ckpt_cost·MTBF). At 100k where MTBF shrinks, the cheap local checkpoints must be frequent and the durable GCS ones rare — exactly the two-tier split. CheckFreq-style adaptive tuning bounds overhead to ~3.5% (CheckFreq, FAST'21). Async + OCDBT: save_checkpoint issues ocp.args.PyTreeSave with OCDBT + Zarr3 and explicit chunk_byte_size (target_data_file_size 2 GB, concurrent 96 GB) for parallel I/O; it block_until_readys only to clean the measurement. Keep enable_autocheckpoint so a preemption signal forces a save (PreemptionCheckpointingPolicy composed with FixedIntervalPolicy via AnySavePolicy); maybe_save_checkpoint drains the async save via wait_until_finished() before exit. Grain checkpoint via GrainCheckpointHandler, using the elastic global-scalar state (item 6) so data position is invariant under rescale.

Make DP the elastic outer axis — and measure goodput, not MFU

The layout in item 1 deliberately confines a failure's blast radius to one PP-stage rack inside one DP replica; DP is the only axis you can elastically resize without re-laying-out EP/TP/PP. MaxText's Pathways elastic path (utils/elastic_utils.py, pathwaysutils.elastic.manager.Manager, maybe_elastic_scale_up) implements shrink-the-world-and-continue. Adopt load-time resharding (ByteCheckpoint-style, 54× stall reduction) so you resume on fewer GPUs after losing racks without a conversion job (ByteCheckpoint, arXiv 2407.20143). And measure goodput, not MFU: adopt Google's ML-Goodput-Measurement badput taxonomy (WASTED_PROGRESS, INFRASTRUCTURE_RECOVERY, CHECKPOINT_SAVE/RESTORE, step_time_deviation) as the run's primary KPI — MaxText already wires record_custom_badput_event_start/end into elastic events (ML-Goodput). The bar is Llama-3's >90% effective training time.

SDC and stragglers — the failures with no error signal

Silent data corruption poisons checkpoints with no error signal — keep enough checkpoint history to roll back past a corrupted save, add NaN/Inf + grad-norm guards (item 5 covers the spike side), and sample-recompute a subset of collectives (Meta SDC, arXiv 2102.11245). For hangs, the slowest of 100k ranks sets the pace and a hung rank deadlocks the collective; on the JAX side rely on coordination-service heartbeats and the goodput jitter metric, escalating to kill+elastic-excise.

09Networking assumptions: MRC and Spectrum-X

The network is a first-class failure source, and MaxText assumes nothing about it — the assumptions are operational, not code, but they gate the items above. Two to lock:

Microsecond path failover (MRC) — assume it

Multipath Reliable Connection (MRC) (OpenAI + AMD/Broadcom/Intel/Microsoft/NVIDIA via OCP, open-sourced May 2026) spreads one RDMA connection over multiple SRv6-source-routed paths and blacklists a failed path within tens of microseconds (vs seconds of RoCE routing convergence), handling loss via selective-ACK + packet trimming (OCP). This converts transient link flaps — which would otherwise time out NCCL and trigger a full job restart costing minutes of MTTR — into training-transparent reroutes. If your fabric supports MRC, your perceived MTBF improves because the synchronous collective never observes the flap. Design the InfiniBand/Spectrum-X scale-out (~800 Gb/s/GPU per-GPU scale-out network) around it.

NVL72 is the new blast radius — keep TP modest

A single GPU failure now jeopardizes a whole 72-GPU NVLink domain because TP groups live inside it. For TP=64, 0.1% of GPUs failed drops fleet availability to 94% (NTP, arXiv 2504.06095). This is the systems argument for keeping TP modest (8, not 64) and leaning on MoE expert-parallel sharding — a lost EP rank degrades fewer collectives than a lost TP rank. Pair with NTP-style degraded (nonuniform) TP groups so a domain loss is a soft event. The copper backplane remains a 2025–26 reliability weak point with immature diagnostics; budget for it.

10Build order, in one paragraph

Do the framework bake-off (0) on 1,000 GPUs first — it gates everything. Then, in parallel-ish: land the FP8 GEMM with the accumulation fix and the DeepEP all-to-all binding (0/2/3), because MFU lives there; wire Muon fully (consistent_rms=0.2, weight_decay=0.1, two-stage NS, use_qk_norm=true, skip_step_on_spikes=true) (4/5); stand up the offline Daft pre-tokenize/pre-pack pipeline and the Grain elastic dataloader with expansion_factor_real_data and colocated_python_data_input (6/7); and before you go past ~10k GPUs, prove multi-tier emergency checkpointing + DP-elastic resize + goodput measurement end-to-end (8), because at 100k the MTTR term — not the MFU term — is what decides whether the run converges. Set the parallelism config (1) last, after the kernels and checkpointing are proven, because it is the cheapest thing to change and the most expensive thing to debug at scale.

The one ordering to remember

Item 0 (framework) is irreversible — do it first. Item 8 (fault tolerance) determines whether you finish — prove it before 10k GPUs. Item 1 (parallelism) is cheapest to change — do it last. Everything in between is MFU work that only pays off once those three bookends hold.
