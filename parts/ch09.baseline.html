09. Low-precision training: FP8, MXFP8, FP4/NVFP4 QAT, and stability at the 4-bit frontier

Precision is the single largest lever on the effective FLOP/$ of a 10T-total/200B-active run, and it is also the lever most likely to silently corrupt a multi-month training job. On GB200, the arithmetic ratio is stark: a B200 delivers roughly 4.5 PFLOP/s dense FP8 and ~9 PFLOP/s dense FP4 per GPU (the NVIDIA-marketed 720 PFLOP/s FP8 / 1,440 PFLOP/s FP4 per NVL72 rack are with 2:4 sparsity; the rack's dense figures are 360 PFLOP/s FP8 and 180 PFLOP/s BF16, per NVIDIA's own GB200 NVL72 page footnote). The headline is that on Blackwell, FP4 is not a memory trick — it is a 2× compute multiplier on the dominant GEMM class (the MoE expert grouped matmuls), and the entire field has, over 2025–2026, converged on the engineering recipe required to actually realize it without divergence. This chapter lays out that recipe end-to-end: the DeepSeek-V3 FP8 floor everyone builds on, the MXFP8/MXFP4/NVFP4 microscaling fork, the four non-negotiable techniques for 4-bit pretraining, where precision provably breaks, what the precision scaling laws predict at the 10T horizon, and how every one of these maps onto switches that already exist in the user's actual codebase (MaxText).

9.1 The DeepSeek-V3 FP8 floor: fine-grained scaling and the accumulation fix

DeepSeek-V3 is the reference recipe that every subsequent low-precision paper either extends or argues against, so it is worth stating its mechanics precisely. V3 runs the forward GEMM and both backward GEMMs (dgrad/wgrad) in FP8, with two distinct scaling granularities (DeepSeek-V3 report, §3.3):

  • Activations: per-tile 1×128 — one scale per token, per group of 128 channels.
  • Weights: per-block 128×128 — one scale per 128-input × 128-output block.

Critically, V3 uses E4M3 on every tensor in these GEMMs, abandoning the older hybrid convention of E5M2 for gradients. The justification is that fine-grained scaling "shares exponent bits among grouped elements" — a per-128 scale recovers the dynamic range that E4M3's 4 exponent bits lack, so you never need the wider exponent of E5M2 if your blocks are small enough. All scale factors are round-scaled to integral powers of two, which guarantees that an operand quantized in the forward pass and re-quantized in the backward pass shares the identical representation — a chain-rule-consistency property that reappears, in a stronger 2D form, in NVFP4.

The load-bearing hardware insight in V3 is the accumulation fix. Hopper's FP8 tensor cores accumulate WGMMA results in only about 14 bits of mantissa, which produces up to 2% relative error on a large-K GEMM — enough to destabilize training on its own. V3 promotes partial sums into FP32 CUDA-core registers every N_C = 128 elements (≈ every 4 WGMMA instructions), implementing a manual two-level accumulation. This is the one part of the V3 recipe that does not port cleanly to a JAX/XLA stack: XLA's dot_general does not expose the inner-product chunking needed to force FP32 promotion every 128 elements, so a MaxText reproduction relies on the library's accumulator-dtype settings (AQT's fwd_accumulator_dtype, the Flax fp8_ops compute dtype) rather than V3's bespoke CUDA kernel. On Blackwell this concern largely evaporates: the 5th-gen tensor cores accumulate FP8 in higher precision and the microscaling formats are hardware-native, so the manual N_C trick becomes unnecessary — but the principle (never trust the tensor core's native accumulator for a long-K MoE GEMM) carries forward to FP4 directly.

The other half of the V3 recipe is the precision-breaks map — the list of operators deliberately not quantized:

Component V3 precision Why it stays high
Embedding / output head BF16/FP32 Vocabulary-projection logits are wide-dynamic-range and feed the loss directly
MoE gating / router BF16/FP32 Top-k/argmax is discontinuous; one mis-quantized gate flips an expert assignment
All normalization (RMSNorm) BF16/FP32 Norms compute reciprocal sqrt of a sum; quantization error compounds
All attention operators BF16/FP32 Softmax logits and QKᵀ products are dynamic-range-sensitive
Optimizer master weights FP32 Small-update accumulation needs full mantissa
Optimizer gradient accumulators FP32 Summing many micro-batch grads in FP8 loses tail bits
Optimizer 1st/2nd moments BF16 Tolerable; the memory savings are large

This map is essentially invariant across every later FP4 paper. The router in particular is non-negotiable, and the reason is structural rather than empirical: routing is a discrete decision, and the gradient of a discrete decision does not smooth out quantization noise the way a dense matmul does. This is precisely why the user's codebase scopes its FP8/FP4 quantization rules to decoder/.*layers.* and routes them only over the op names ("dot_general", "gmm", "ragged_dot") — the router's GateLogit einsum in moe.py is not in that set, so it stays in the working dtype by construction (the user's actual codebase, MaxText, quantizations.py). The gmm/ragged_dot ops in that list are exactly the grouped/ragged expert GEMMs — the parameter bulk and the only place where low precision pays for itself.

9.2 The microscaling fork: MXFP8, MXFP4, and why NVFP4 broke from the OCP spec

The OCP Microscaling (MX) v1.0 spec is the normative definition of block-scaled low precision: a block of k = 32 elements shares a single E8M0 scale — an 8-bit, sign-less, power-of-two value with bias 127, i.e. scale = 2^(s − 127) (OCP MX v1.0 spec). The spec defines MXFP8 (E4M3 or E5M2 elements), MXFP6, MXFP4 (E2M1 elements), and MXINT8. The user's codebase ships the ground-truth MXFP4 decoder in dequantize_mxfp4.py (the gpt-oss checkpoint layout): FP4 is E2M1 with the value set {0, ±0.5, ±1, ±1.5, ±2, ±3, ±4, ±6}, two nibbles packed per uint8, 32 values per block, and scales = 2.0 ** (scales_e8m0 − 127) (the user's actual codebase, MaxText). That 16-value codebook with a max magnitude of 6 is the entire numerical budget of a 4-bit weight; everything in FP4 training is about keeping the interesting values away from the {±4, ±6} clipping cliff and away from the {±0.5} underflow floor.

NVFP4 — NVIDIA's Blackwell-native format — deliberately breaks the MX spec in two ways that matter (NVFP4 pretraining paper, §3):

  1. 16-element micro-blocks, not 32. Smaller blocks mean a single outlier pollutes the shared scale for fewer neighbors.
  2. A two-level scale: a per-block E4M3 scale (not a power-of-two E8M0) multiplied by an FP32 per-tensor scale.

The E4M3 block scale is the consequential choice. Because it is not rounded to a power of two, it can place the block's max value precisely at a representable E2M1 point, preserving the ±4 and ±6 codes that MXFP4's UE8M0 power-of-two rounding tends to clip. With a 16-element block, at least 6.25% of values (the per-block max) land at near-FP8 fidelity. The empirical payoff is direct and large: on an 8B/1T-token run, MXFP4 shows ~2.5% relative loss error vs ~1.5% for NVFP4, and MXFP4 needs 36% more tokens (1.36T vs 1.0T) to match NVFP4's loss (§5). For a 10T-token budget that 36% tax is the difference between a 10T and a 13.6T run — on a 100k-GB200 cluster, the cost of choosing the wrong 4-bit format is measured in millions of GPU-hours. The recommendation for the target run is unambiguous: if you train in 4-bit, train in NVFP4, not MXFP4. MXFP4 is the right format only for the comms/storage path on hardware that cannot do native NVFP4 (discussed in §9.5).

The format choice is exposed directly in the user's codebase. quantizations.py wires three TransformerEngine recipes (the user's actual codebase, MaxText):

MaxText recipe TE class Block size Notes
te_mxfp8 MXFP8BlockScaling 32 OCP-spec MXFP8, E8M0 scale
te_nvfp4 NVFP4BlockScaling 128 today # TODO(set this to 16 when unfused RHT is supported)
te_nvfp4_no_rht NVFP4BlockScaling(disable_rht=True) 128 RHT toggled off

The block_size for te_nvfp4 returns 128, with an explicit TODO to drop it to 16 once unfused Random Hadamard Transform is supported — a concrete, current signal that the native NVFP4 16-element block in JAX is gated on fused-RHT support, and that the no-RHT path exists precisely to ablate the technique that §9.4 will show is mandatory. This is the kind of production-readiness detail no paper discloses: as of HEAD, a MaxText NVFP4 run is operating at a coarser-than-native 128 block and will tighten to 16 when the kernel lands.

9.3 The four mandatory techniques for 4-bit pretraining

The NVFP4 pretraining paper is the load-bearing primary source for the 4-bit go/no-go decision because it is the longest publicly documented 4-bit run: a 12B hybrid Mamba-Transformer trained to 10T tokens — exactly the horizon the target run sits at. Its central finding is blunt: pure NVFP4 GEMMs diverge early, and convergence requires all four of the following techniques, none optional (NVFP4 paper, §4):

1. Keep ~16% of linear layers in BF16. For the 12B, that is the first two blocks plus the final eight blocks. Ablations show stability holds with only the final four blocks in high precision, but the final layers are the sensitive ones — the LM-head-adjacent representations whose errors flow straight into the loss. This is the same precision-breaks intuition as V3's "keep the output head high," generalized to a band of layers rather than a single op. For the 10T/200B target, this translates to a concrete config: of the model's decoder layers, hold the first ~2 and the last ~8 (scaled appropriately for total depth) in BF16, and let the middle run NVFP4. In MaxText this is exactly the per-layer regex mechanism that the mixed-precision JSON configs (e.g. dense_llm_subchannel.json) already express — w_bits/a_bits/tile_size keyed by a layer-name regex — so "first 2 + last 8 BF16, middle NVFP4, router/norms/attention always high" is a configuration, not a code change.

2. Random Hadamard Transform on Wgrad inputs only. A 16×16 Hadamard matrix multiplied by a single fixed random ±1 sign vector shared across all layers and all of training. The RHT Gaussianizes block-level gradient outliers so a single spike does not dominate the per-block scale. The dimensions matter: 4×4 degrades, 128×128 gives only marginal gains over 16×16 — so 16×16 is the sweet spot, and it is applied only to the inputs of the weight-gradient GEMM, not the forward or dgrad GEMMs. This is the technique gated behind the te_nvfp4 / te_nvfp4_no_rht toggle in the user's codebase; the fact that the native 16-block is waiting on fused RHT support tells you the RHT must be fused into the quantization kernel to be free.

3. 2D 16×16 block scaling on weights (vs 1×16 for activations and gradients). The motivation is chain-rule consistency, the same principle V3 enforced via power-of-two rounding but stronger: a weight quantized one way in the forward GEMM and a different way in the backward GEMM produces an inconsistent gradient. 2D scaling forces a single quantized weight representation shared across the forward and backward passes.

4. Stochastic rounding on gradients only. SR makes gradient quantization unbiased — the rounding error has zero expectation, so it averages out over an accumulation. Crucially, applying SR to forward activations or weights is explicitly detrimental: it injects variance where you want a deterministic value. This asymmetry is exactly what the user's codebase encodes. In quantizations.py, AQT's set_stochastic_rounding(...) defaults to vjp_lhs_stochastic_rounding=False, vjp_rhs_stochastic_rounding=False and is configurable per-tensor (the user's actual codebase, MaxText) — i.e. the framework lets you turn SR on for the gradient GEMMs and leave it off for the forward, which is precisely the NVFP4 prescription.

The end-of-training behavior is the most important practical detail for the target run. NVFP4 loss tracks FP8 to <1% through the stable phase but widens to >1.5% during LR decay — the model becomes precision-sensitive exactly when the learning rate shrinks and updates become small relative to quantization noise. The mitigation is a precision switch: flipping NVFP4→BF16 at 8.2T tokens (82% of training) drops relative loss error from 1.5% to 0.5%, at a cost of only ~6% of total compute spent in high precision (NVFP4 paper). And the downstream result is real parity, not just loss parity: MMLU-Pro 62.58% (NVFP4) vs 62.62% (FP8). For a 10T-token plan this dictates a concrete schedule: run NVFP4 for the bulk of training and budget a BF16 (or FP8) tail for roughly the final ~15–20% / the LR-decay phase. The decay-phase precision switch is the single highest-leverage stability intervention in 4-bit pretraining, and it must be planned into the LR schedule and the checkpoint cadence from the start.

9.4 Where precision breaks: router, norms, logits, and the stability map

Synthesizing V3 and the NVFP4 work, the precision-sensitivity ranking is stable across the field and should be treated as a hard constraint, not a tuning knob:

Tensor / op Safe precision Failure mode if quantized too low
MoE router / gate logits BF16/FP32 Discontinuous top-k flips expert assignment; load-balance collapse
RMSNorm / all normalization BF16/FP32 Reciprocal-sqrt error compounds; activation blowup
Attention logits / softmax input FP32 accumulation Logit explosion (the Muon failure mode); NaN softmax
Embedding + LM head BF16/FP32 Direct loss corruption; wide vocab dynamic range
First ~2 + last ~8 decoder blocks BF16 Early/late representation sensitivity (NVFP4 ablation)
Forward/backward expert GEMMs NVFP4 (w/ recipe) — (this is where 4-bit pays off)
Gradients (Wgrad input) NVFP4 + RHT + SR Outlier-dominated block scale → divergence

Two failure modes deserve emphasis because they intersect with the optimizer chapter. First, attention-logit explosion: low precision in the QKᵀ path amplifies the runaway-logit pathology that MuonClip was designed to bound (Kimi K2's per-head logit cap at τ=100, arXiv 2507.20534). The defense is to keep attention out of FP4 entirely — V3 keeps "all attention operators" high-precision, and MaxText exposes float32_qk_product and float32_logits flags in base.yml to force the QK product and the softmax input to FP32 even when the rest of the layer is low-precision (the user's actual codebase, MaxText). For the target run, set both to true if attention is anywhere near the precision floor. Second, router/load-balance interaction: the aux-loss-free balancing in moe.py (calculate_load_balance_updates via routed_bias_update_rate, applied post-optimizer as a bias update) depends on accurate per-expert assignment counts; quantizing the gate would corrupt the very signal the balancer integrates, which is why the router is excluded from the quant scope.

A complementary stability tool in the codebase is skip_step_on_spikes() in optimizers.py, which computes a rolling mean ± scaling_factor·std (Bessel-corrected) over the loss and zeroes any update exceeding the threshold while preserving optimizer state (the user's actual codebase, MaxText). At 4-bit, where quantization noise raises the baseline variance of the loss, this guard is doubly valuable: a transient precision-induced spike (a block scale momentarily clipping) gets skipped rather than poisoning the moment estimates. Budget for it.

9.5 FP4 on Hopper without FP4 tensor cores

Not every part of a real fleet is Blackwell, and even on GB200 the expert-parallel all-to-all is a place where FP4 helps for bandwidth independent of compute. The Hopper-FP4 paper is the answer for both (arXiv 2603.02731). Hopper has FP8 tensor cores but no native FP4 instructions, so the design keeps core MoE GEMMs in FP8 while compressing activations and the expert-parallel all-to-all to MXFP4 (32-element blocks, UE8M0 scale, RTNE rounding — explicitly not stochastic, since SR's benefit is a backward-pass-only property and this is a forward/comms compression). The clever bits:

  • A scaling-aware direct FP8↔︎FP4 conversion done by bitwise integer-domain remapping (exponent adjust plus a 1-bit mantissa left-shift), avoiding an FP4→BF16→FP8 round-trip.
  • Hierarchical scale alignment mapping four 32-element MXFP4 blocks onto one 128-element FP8 block, with the target scale = max of the four sub-block scales.
  • The combine phase keeps BF16 accumulation.

On DeepSeek-V3 671B (MLA), 256 Hopper GPUs, 160B tokens, this yields +0.61% relative loss (MXFP4) vs +0.29% (FP8) against BF16, throughput 1157→1302 tok/GPU/s (+12.5%), and −14.8% peak activation memory (−11.8 GB) (arXiv 2603.02731). The crucial caveat: there is no FP4 matmul speedup on Hopper — this is purely a memory-and-communication play. The 12.5% throughput gain comes from smaller all-to-all payloads and reduced activation memory enabling larger micro-batches, not from faster math.

For the 10T/200B/GB200 target the lesson transfers even though the hardware is Blackwell: the expert-parallel all-to-all is the dominant cross-rack traffic in a wide-EP MoE, and compressing the dispatched tokens to FP4 (NVFP4 on Blackwell, where the conversion is hardware-native) shrinks that payload roughly in half. On a 100k-GPU NVL72 fabric where EP spans many racks, halving all-to-all bytes is a first-order win even before the FP4 compute speedup. MaxText's MoE path (permute()/unpermute()/get_all_to_all_params(), the ring-of-experts Pallas ragged kernels) is where this compression would live; quantizing the dispatched activations to FP4 before the all-to-all and dequantizing on arrival is the GB200 analog of the Hopper paper's comms compression.

9.6 DeepSeek-V4: FP4 QAT lands in production

DeepSeek-V4 is the existence proof that FP4 in the training loop is production-viable at trillion scale, and its design is the single most relevant signal for the target build. V4 applies FP4 quantization-aware training to MoE expert weights (FP4×FP8 matmuls) and to the CSA "lightning indexer" QK path, with FP8 for everything else, FP8 KV storage, and BF16 only on the RoPE dims (V4 HF discussion; V4 report). The indexer's precision dropped from FP8 in V3.2 → MXFP4 in V4, with QAT holding accuracy. Reported configs: V4-Pro 1.6T total / 49B active, V4-Flash 284B / 13B active, both 1M context, trained on 32–33T tokens.

Two architectural decisions in V4 are directly actionable:

  1. The QAT-on-experts pattern. Experts are the parameter bulk, they tolerate aggressive quantization better than any other component, and they benefit most from FP4 storage plus FP4×FP8 compute. Everything precision-sensitive (router, indexer-output, norms, attention) stays at FP8 or higher. For a 200B-active model this is the obvious partition: the experts are where ~90% of the parameters live, so FP4 on experts captures nearly all the memory and compute savings while leaving the fragile 10% untouched.

  2. FP4 as a storage/comms format that decodes into the FP8 GEMM. V4's economically important claim is a lossless FP4→FP8 dequantization that reuses the existing FP8 pipeline unchanged — FP4 is the storage representation, and it decodes into the same FP8 matmul, anticipating FP4×FP8 GEMMs up to ⅓ faster on future hardware (treat the exact speedup as medium-confidence; HF blog). This is exactly the pattern the user's codebase anticipates: the V4 "store FP4, compute FP8" model maps onto MaxText's AQT mixed-precision path where weights are held at one bit-width and the GEMM accumulates at another, and onto the Qwix dot_general_qt config where lhs/rhs_qtype=float8_e4m3fn with dlhs/drhs_grad_qtype=float8_e5m2 defines the compute precision independent of storage.

The reason V4 uses QAT rather than post-hoc quantization is not stylistic — it is forced by the scaling laws, as the next section shows.

9.7 Precision scaling laws and the QAT escape hatch

Kumar et al.'s "Scaling Laws for Precision" (ICLR 2025) fit, on 465+ runs up to 1.7B params / 26B tokens, an effective-parameter law treating precision and parameter count as interchangeable contributors to capacity (arXiv 2411.04330):

N_eff(N, P) = N · (1 − e^(−P_w/γ_w)) · (1 − e^(−P_a/γ_a)) · (1 − e^(−P_kv/γ_kv))

with weight, activation, and KV precision effects independent and multiplicative, and small single-digit γ constants (the exact values live in Appendix K and were not machine-extractable in research; treat the precise γ as medium-confidence). The two headline results both bear on the target run:

  • Compute-optimal training precision is ~7–8 bits, roughly independent of compute. Taken at face value this says BF16 is wasteful (you are paying for mantissa bits the model cannot use) and naive ≤4-bit is also sub-optimal (it forces a disproportionate model-size inflation to recover the lost effective parameters). This is the case against unmitigated 4-bit.

  • Post-train-quantization degradation grows with the data-to-parameter ratio: δ_PTQ ∝ (D^(γ_D)/N^(γ_N)) · e^(−P_post/γ_post). Plainly: the more tokens you train on, the more fragile the model becomes to later quantization. For a 10T-token/200B-active model — an aggressive 50:1 token-to-active-param ratio — this is a direct red flag. PTQ to INT4/FP4 after a 10T-token run will degrade super-linearly. This single result is why V4 moved to QAT for FP4 experts rather than quantizing post-hoc, and it is the reason the target run should treat any "we'll quantize for inference later" plan with deep suspicion.

But the pessimistic 4-bit reading must be reconciled against the empirical NVFP4 success — a 12B trained to 10T tokens in 4-bit matching FP8 downstream. The resolution (synthesis by Wang) is that Kumar et al.'s law characterizes unmitigated low precision. The NVFP4 recipe — RHT + 2D scaling + SR + selective BF16 + the decay-phase precision switch — recovers near-FP8 quality, which is a partial refutation of the "4-bit is expensive" conclusion in the engineered-recipe regime. And the PTQ-fragility result is not a refutation at all; it is precisely the argument for QAT. The two laws together give a clean decision for the target run:

Train in 4-bit (NVFP4 QAT on experts) with the full recipe, never PTQ a long-horizon model to 4-bit. The QAT path sidesteps the δ_PTQ fragility, and the engineered NVFP4 recipe sidesteps the unmitigated-4-bit penalty. The 7–8-bit "optimum" from the isolated law is what FP8-everywhere-except-experts already targets; pushing experts to 4-bit via QAT is a throughput optimization that the QAT-specific scaling laws (e.g. arXiv 2505.14302) show is recoverable, not the capacity loss the PTQ law would imply.

9.8 The MaxText precision stack: three engines, one config surface

A few mappings make the recipe concrete:

  • V3-style tiling is the AQT _tiling_fn/TiledDotGeneral path: the 1×128 activation / 128×128 weight granularity that V3 hand-rolled is a tiling_fn partial with the appropriate tile_size. The mixed-precision JSON (dense_llm_subchannel.json) shows the regex-per-layer mechanism — e.g. query w4/tile_size=128, key w4/tile_size=256 — which is exactly the machinery to express "experts NVFP4, router/attention/norms BF16, first-2/last-8 blocks BF16."
  • Delayed scaling (the classic TE algorithm) lives in fp8_ops with amax_history_length=1024 and _overwrite_with_gradient scale variables — a tracked amax history that lags the current tensor by up to 1024 steps. For a stable long run this is fine; for the LR-decay phase where dynamic range shifts, the per-tensor current-scale AQT/Qwix path or a shorter history is safer.
  • NVFP4 on Blackwell is the te_nvfp4 recipe, currently at block 128 pending the fused-RHT kernel that unlocks the native 16-block. The NVIDIA "Train Models Faster with JAX and MaxText Using NVFP4 on Blackwell" blog documents this integration end-to-end and is the bridge between the NVFP4 paper and the MaxText/JAX stack the target run will use (NVIDIA blog).
  • KV-cache quantization is orthogonal and already wired: quantize_kvcache + kv_quant_axis (default heads_and_dkv) in base.yml. V4's "FP8 KV with BF16 on RoPE dims" maps onto this — quantize the non-RoPE KV, keep the rotary dims high.

9.9 Concrete recommendation for the 10T/200B/100k-GB200 run

Putting the recipe together into a single prescription:

  1. Baseline everything in FP8 (E4M3 forward, fine-grained scaling), experts in NVFP4 QAT. This mirrors V4's "FP4 on experts, FP8 elsewhere" partition and captures the ~2× FP4 compute multiplier on the dominant GEMM class while keeping the precision-sensitive 10% safe. On GB200's dense ~9 PFLOP/s FP4 vs ~4.5 PFLOP/s FP8 per B200, the expert GEMMs are where the throughput is won.
  2. Use NVFP4, not MXFP4, for compute. The 36%-more-tokens penalty of MXFP4 is unaffordable at 10T scale. Reserve MXFP4 only for the expert-parallel all-to-all comms compression if profiling shows the fabric is the bottleneck (the §9.5 pattern, RTNE not SR).
  3. Apply the full four-technique NVFP4 recipe: ~16% of layers BF16 (first ~2 + last ~8 of the decoder, scaled to depth), 16×16 RHT on Wgrad inputs with a fixed shared sign vector, 2D 16×16 weight block scaling, SR on gradients only. In MaxText, that is te_nvfp4 (not te_nvfp4_no_rht) plus the AQT set_stochastic_rounding asymmetry, scoped via the per-layer regex.
  4. Keep router, all norms, embeddings, LM head, and all attention operators out of FP4 — out of the dot_general/gmm/ragged_dot quant scope. Set float32_qk_product and float32_logits true. Keep FP32 master weights and FP32 gradient accumulators; BF16 optimizer moments are fine.
  5. Plan a decay-phase precision switch. Run NVFP4 for ~80–85% of tokens, then flip experts to FP8/BF16 for the LR-decay tail. This recovers ~1% relative loss for ~6% of compute — the highest-leverage single intervention. Wire it into the LR schedule and checkpoint cadence up front, not as an afterthought.
  6. Never plan to PTQ this model to 4-bit for inference. At 50:1 tokens-to-active-params the δ_PTQ fragility is severe; if 4-bit inference is wanted, the experts are already QAT-FP4 and decode losslessly into FP8, exactly as V4 does. INT4 weight-only QAT on MoE in post-training (the Kimi K2-Thinking pattern, ~594GB / ~2× decode) is the inference move, applied as QAT, not PTQ.
  7. Keep skip_step_on_spikes armed with a tuned scaling_factor to absorb the elevated loss variance that 4-bit introduces, and treat any sustained loss-gap widening (the >1.5% decay-phase signal) as the trigger to advance the precision switch.

The strategic read across the field is consistent: FP8 is the floor, NVFP4-QAT-on-experts is the frontier that DeepSeek-V4 and the NVIDIA 12B/10T run have jointly proven trainable, and the engineering — not the format — is what separates a converging 4-bit run from a diverging one. For a build at this scale, the precision recipe is not an optimization to bolt on later; it is a first-class architectural decision that shapes the LR schedule, the layer-precision map, the optimizer config, and the comms layout from day one.
