A book about how to train a 10-trillion-parameter model has to start by admitting that the labs who have actually done something like it will not tell you how. What exists instead is a patchwork: engineering blogs about racks and fabrics, a handful of technical reports that stopped coming after one generation, sworn courtroom testimony, unscripted podcast asides, community microbenchmarks of silicon the vendor never fully specified, and an analyst shop that rates its own confidence on a three-point scale because nothing sturdier exists. These routes are not equally trustworthy, and conflating them is the most common failure mode in writing about frontier AI. So, before anything else: how sourcing works here, a tier-by-tier tour of the 2026 evidence base, and a triangulated answer to what is actually knowable about frontier-run scale — which turns out to be considerably less than the discourse implies.

Sources, and How Hard to Lean on Each

Every claim in the chapters ahead is cited to a named source and dated. That sounds like table stakes; in this field it is not, because the sources span wildly different evidentiary classes, and most writing about frontier AI silently mixes them. What each class buys you, and what it doesn’t:

This is not pedantry. Tier-laundering — a single rumor hardening into apparent consensus through repetition — is the failure mode this discipline exists to catch, and the field supplies a clean, dated worked example. In November 2025 an outlet called MarketNewsData asserted flatly that “Gemini 3 was trained entirely on Google’s own TPUs, with no Nvidia GPUs involved,” crediting the Ironwood (TPU v7) generation specifically. The sourcing: unnamed “multiple industry sources,” no individual named, no primary document linked (MarketNewsData, “What’s Actually New About Gemini 3 on TPUs”, Nov 20, 2025). Within months, IntuitionLabs, WioNews, Ynetnews, and BusinessWorld had recycled the same framing without adding new reporting — one unsourced blog post fanning out into what reads, by mid-2026, like multi-outlet consensus. The more careful outlet on the same beat corroborates only the narrower claim: Gemini 3, “like all earlier versions of Gemini, was trained entirely on TPUs” — and explicitly declines to confirm the Ironwood detail or supply any chip count or duration (SemiAnalysis, “Google TPUv7: The 900lb Gorilla In the Room,” fetched July 13, 2026). “All-TPU, no Nvidia fallback” and “trained exclusively on Ironwood” are not the same claim. Only tracing the second back to its unnamed-sources origin exposes the gap — and tracing claims back to origin is the standing move on every page that follows, not just this one.

A second discipline runs alongside: convergence as an explicit, deliberate upgrade mechanism. Where several independent secondary accounts of an unfetchable primary source agree on specifics, we treat the convergent core as higher-confidence paraphrase and flag anything appearing in only one account as lower-confidence. This is how OpenAI’s “Pre-Training GPT-4.5” retrospective video (April 11, 2025), which could not be directly transcribed, was reconstructed — from three independent secondary accounts (a Medium deep-dive, a Followin.io writeup, an OpenTools summary) that substantially agree. The run’s jump past 100,000 GPUs, the multi-cluster requirement it forced, the torch.sum bug, and the “we’re no longer compute constrained” framing each appear in at least two of the three accounts: treated as solid. The detail that the bug was found when the run was “roughly 40% complete” appears in exactly one: flagged, not folded silently into the narrative.

What Closed Labs Actually Say

One organizing fact governs 2026 frontier-lab disclosure, and Chapter 2 develops it lab by lab: labs are extraordinarily voluble about physical infrastructure and almost completely silent about what any specific flagship model consumed to train. The two categories barely overlap.

The infrastructure record is the richest the field has ever produced. OpenAI’s production scale-out fabric is public in real engineering detail: the Multipath Reliable Connection (MRC) protocol — published with AMD, Broadcom, Intel, Microsoft, and Nvidia as an Open Compute Project contribution — is a two-tier fabric supporting “131,072 GPUs at full bisection bandwidth,” keeps routing state on the NIC rather than in switches, and ships with production-resilience receipts: training jobs surviving simultaneous optical-transceiver failures on four links, core switches rebooted mid-training without job interruption (OpenAI, “MRC supercomputer networking”, May 2026). Microsoft operates Fairwater Wisconsin and Fairwater Atlanta as one logical training fabric over a dedicated “AI WAN” built on 120,000+ new fiber miles in a single year, with Azure CTO Mark Russinovich on record that “the amount of infrastructure required now to train these models is not just one datacenter, not two, but multiples of that… nobody’s really run into that problem yet because they haven’t gotten to the scale we’re at now” (Microsoft, Nov 12, 2025). Google goes further still: 134,000 TPU 8t chips in a single non-blocking Virgo fabric per site at 47 Pb/s bisectional bandwidth, plus the claim that JAX+Pathways can orchestrate “more than 1 million TPU chips in a single training cluster” (Google Cloud, April 22, 2026). Anthropic discloses compute partnerships on a two-to-four-month cadence, each real, named, and dollar-figured — “nearly half a million” Trainium2 chips for Project Rainier (Oct 2025), up to one million TPUs from Google (Oct 23, 2025), a Google-Broadcom follow-on adding 3.5 GW starting 2027 (April 7, 2026).

None of that candor extends to the models the infrastructure actually trains. Anthropic’s system cards describe pretraining data sources — web crawl, licensed data, self-generated synthetic data, opted-in user data — but disclose zero GPU/TPU/Trainium counts, FLOPs, or token counts for any specific model; compute scale lives entirely in partnership press releases, never in the model cards. Google’s Gemini 1.0 technical report remains, as of mid-2026, the single most quantified infrastructure disclosure any closed lab has produced — SuperPod topology, a goodput figure (“increased from 85 to 97 percent” on the largest-scale job), an explicit silent-data-corruption expectation (“every week or two”) (Gemini Team, arXiv:2312.11805, December 2023) — and no Gemini release since, the March 2024 Gemini 1.5 report included, has matched it. Meta’s Llama 3 paper is the field’s other landmark: a full infrastructure chapter, 16,384-of-24,576 H100s, 38-43% BF16 MFU, 419 unexpected interruptions across a 54-day window (arXiv:2407.21783, July 2024). Its successor got one number. Llama 4 Behemoth — ~2T total, 288B active — yielded exactly “390 TFLOPs/GPU” on 32K GPUs in FP8 before being quietly shelved, never released, and apparently never resumed at that scale by Meta Superintelligence Labs (Meta AI blog, April 2025; the shelving itself per SemiAnalysis-aggregated reporting, 2026). The pattern is close to universal across the labs Chapter 2 surveys: one technical-report-grade disclosure per lab, typically early in a lineage, then infrastructure-only disclosure for every generation after.

The Insider-Journalism Layer: Track Record and Its Limits

Where labs go quiet, institutional journalism with named sourcing chains fills part of the gap. Its 2025-2026 track record is genuinely mixed, and worth separating rather than averaging. The Wall Street Journal’s reconstruction of OpenAI’s Orion/GPT-4.5 struggles — at least two large training attempts spanning several months each, a roughly $0.5B compute-cost estimate for a six-month run, Sutskever quoted as “the data is not growing because we have but one internet” — has held up as the canonical account, cross-corroborated later by OpenAI’s own retrospective video (WSJ reporting via Fortune, Feb 25, 2025). Reuters’ October 2024 scoop on OpenAI’s “Tigris”-codenamed custom-silicon effort with Broadcom, led by former Google TPU-program head Richard Ho, has also held up — but needs a correction most secondary coverage misses. The chip that actually shipped in June 2026, “Jalapeño,” is explicitly inference-only. The training-chip effort has produced no shipped product, so any claim that OpenAI has “its own training chip” in production conflates two separate programs (Reuters, Oct 29, 2024; OpenAI’s own announcement, June 24, 2026).

SemiAnalysis occupies a tier of its own within this layer, and deserves an honest accounting on two fronts. First, its track record: it repeatedly pre-empted official disclosures, anticipating Fairwater and MRC content that Microsoft and OpenAI later confirmed. That assessment is our own, formed by cross-referencing its Fairwater/MRC reporting against Microsoft’s November 2025 and OpenAI’s May 2026 official posts — not a claim SemiAnalysis makes about itself, and not a timing-audited study. Second, its accessibility: several of its most consequential pieces here — “Llama Behemoth [4] Failure Post-Mortem,” “xAI’s Colossus 2 - First Gigawatt Datacenter In The World,” “Meta Superintelligence – Leadership, Compute, Talent, and Data” — are referenced by title elsewhere but sit behind a paywall we could not clear. Their existence is confirmed; their content is not, and every claim that leans on one of them says so rather than treating “SemiAnalysis says” as uniformly load-bearing regardless of whether the underlying text was read.

One channel is structurally different from everything else in this chapter: compelled courtroom testimony. OpenAI President Greg Brockman testified under oath in the Musk v. OpenAI trial that OpenAI “plans to spend $50 billion on computing power in 2026,” up from costs that were “roughly $30 million in 2017” and had reached “tens of billions of dollars” by the time of testimony (Bloomberg, May 5, 2026, from the trial record). Perjury risk changes the incentive structure in a way no PR blog post or “sources familiar with the matter” leak can, which gives this figure a fundamentally different evidentiary basis than any other compute number in this chapter. Litigation discovery is worth actively monitoring for the remainder of that trial, and for any parallel antitrust or IP litigation touching frontier labs.

The layer’s structural weakness: even its most reconciled-looking numbers frequently are not reconciled at all. The Anthropic-Google TPU relationship alone carries at least three non-overlapping public figures across ten months — the October 23, 2025 official framing (“up to 1 million TPUs,” “well over a gigawatt,” “tens of billions of dollars”); an April 7, 2026 official follow-on with Broadcom (“3.5 GW starting 2027,” alongside a disclosed $30B quarterly run-rate revenue and a $380B valuation off a $30B Series G); and Bloomberg’s May 18, 2026 cumulative framing (“up to $40 billion… 5 gigawatts… over five years… up to one million Ironwood chips”), a bigger number than any single Anthropic or Google release states on its own. These are plausibly snapshots of a moving, tranche-by-tranche negotiation rather than genuine contradictions. But no single source supplies the full ledger, and stitching one together would outrun what the primary record supports — so the figures stay separate, flagged as snapshots. xAI’s Colossus 2 financing shows the same pattern from another angle: roughly $18-20B of GB200/GB300 hardware, structured as ~$7.5B equity plus $12-12.5B of GPU-collateralized debt through a special-purpose vehicle that buys the chips and leases them back to xAI, with lenders reportedly pushing for three-year repayment terms — a credit-market skepticism signal absent from any headline “$X billion raised” figure (WSJ and The Information, July 2025 onward).

Podcasts and Talks as Primary Sources

A genuinely distinct evidence tier, underused by most secondary coverage, is the long-form podcast interview — Dwarkesh, Latent Space, Sequoia’s Training Data, No Priors, Lex Fridman — where lab principals speak at length, unscripted, often with a full transcript the show publishes directly. We treat those transcripts as primary sources when directly fetched. When a quote is recovered only via a third-party transcript aggregator such as podscripts.co or singjupost.com, it is paraphrase, and treated as such wherever the distinction matters.

What this tier buys is candor written disclosure rarely matches. Jeff Dean and Noam Shazeer confirmed on Dwarkesh, in terms no Google blog post has used, that cross-datacenter synchronous training is already routine production practice at Google, gated purely by a bandwidth-versus-step-time budget rather than any new algorithm: “as long as the gradient accumulation and communication of the parameters across metro areas happens fast enough relative to the step time, you’re golden,” with Shazeer adding the near-identical formulation, “as long as you can sync all of the parameters of the model across the different data centers and then accumulate all the gradients, in the time it takes to do one step, you’re pretty good” (Dwarkesh Podcast, Feb 12, 2025). On the same show roughly a year later, Jensen Huang dismissed TPU adoption in blunter terms than any Nvidia earnings call: “Anthropic is a unique instance, not a trend. Without Anthropic, why would there be any TPU growth at all? It’s 100% Anthropic” (Dwarkesh Podcast, April 15, 2026). The widely circulated claim that Anthropic’s RL compute spend was, for a period, only about “$1 million” — deliberately so, “we aren’t in the compute limited regime for RL yet,” framed as a conscious strategy of buying algorithmic certainty before committing large compute — traces to Dario Amodei’s own framing as paraphrased on the show, and is routinely misattributed to Sholto Douglas, who appears in the underlying notes twice (at Google DeepMind in the earlier episode, at Anthropic in the later one) in a way those notes cannot cleanly separate (Dwarkesh Podcast, 2025; the fuller sourcing is in Ch2). Bob McGrew, OpenAI’s former Head of Research, told Sequoia’s Training Data that “the real lever for pre-training in 2025 is improving architectures” rather than further scale — and, more actionably for anyone scoping a 10T-parameter design, that a new architecture requires restarting pretraining “from the beginning.” The architecture choice is a one-shot commitment in a stronger sense than any subsequent data-curriculum or hyperparameter decision (Sequoia Training Data, June 17, 2025).

Dylan Patel’s appearances are the single richest podcast source here for hardware-supply-chain reasoning. On Dwarkesh in March 2026 he argued the long-run structural bottleneck sits not at power or chips but at ASML: “by 2028 or 2029, the bottleneck falls to the lowest rung on the supply chain.” His numbers: roughly 70 EUV tools shipped in 2025, 80 in 2026, capping near 100+/year by 2030 even under aggressive expansion — while a full gigawatt of Rubin-generation AI datacenter capacity needs only about 3.5 EUV tools (roughly $1.2B) against roughly $50B of total datacenter capex for that same gigawatt (Dwarkesh Podcast, March 13, 2026). On the same episode he put memory, not logic, as the acute near-term shortage: roughly 30% of Big Tech’s 2026 capex, consumer DRAM prices roughly tripling, smartphone volumes projected to fall from about 1.1B to 500-600M units/year as AI-datacenter DRAM demand cannibalizes consumer supply. And Michael Kagan, Nvidia’s CTO and Mellanox’s co-founder, gave the clearest plain-language explanation anywhere of why network jitter, not raw bandwidth, caps effective cluster size — directly relevant to Chapter 20’s networking math: “instead of being able to split your job to a thousand GPUs, you can split it only to ten GPUs, because you need to accommodate for the jitter.” He located the onset of near-certain component failure at “a few tens of thousands” of components, not the 100K+ threshold popularly cited (Sequoia Training Data, Oct 28, 2025).

A related but structurally different tier sits at technical conferences and community-authored venues — GPU MODE, Hot Chips, the Open Compute Project Summit, Meta’s @Scale series, individual engineer blogs — which produce measured ground truth independent of any lab’s PR apparatus. Academic microbenchmarking of Blackwell silicon reports 96.3% of theoretical peak throughput on FP8/BF16/FP4 tensor-core GEMM but only 80.7% on FP64 — with the caveat that the paper’s “theoretical peak” is a clock-derived ~4.0-PFLOPS baseline, not either B200 SKU’s spec-sheet number, so the percentage measures tensor-core efficiency at realistic tile shapes rather than distance from the datasheet (Jarmusch & Chandrasekaran, arXiv:2512.02189, Dec 2025). Community teardowns of B200 document a die-to-die interconnect tax invisible in any vendor slide deck — L2 cache bandwidth of 21 TB/s for same-partition access falling to 16.8 TB/s across the die boundary — which bears directly on expert- and tensor-parallel shard placement within a single logical GPU (Chips and Cheese, 2025). Meta disclosed at its own @Scale conference that its RoCE fabric scaled from 4,096 to 24,576 to 129,000 GPUs in under two years, a materially larger production figure than the 24,576-GPU number in its well-known SIGCOMM’24 paper (engineering.fb.com, Sept 2025). And one genuinely obscure primary source — an unpublished MIT MEng thesis by Laker Newhouse, working with Muon co-theorist Jeremy Bernstein — quantifies a “noise floor” implicit in Muon’s Newton-Schulz iteration: a maximum 485x singular-value inflation factor. The thesis shows that matching an unconstrained NanoGPT baseline’s 39.4% validation accuracy under a provable Lipschitz bound requires that bound to blow up to a figure the thesis itself states inconsistently — 10^274 in the abstract, 10^278 in the conclusion, almost certainly an internal erratum rather than two distinct results. At either exponent, it is vivid evidence of how vacuous formal robustness guarantees become at competitive model quality (Newhouse, MIT MEng thesis, May 2025, pp. 13, 57). None of this layer carries institutional accountability. But its incentives — recruiting, standard-setting, community credibility, academic priority — are orthogonal to a press release’s, and its numbers are measured rather than claimed. It underwrites much of Chapters 9, 16, and 17.

Epoch AI and the Economics of Estimation

Above both the journalism layer and the podcast layer sits a single organization functioning as the field’s closest approximation to a rigorous, methodologically transparent estimator: Epoch AI. Its frontier-training-run tracker assigns every entry a confidence label — Speculative, Likely, Confident — and discloses its methodology rather than black-boxing it. GPT-4.5’s headline 3.8e26 FLOP estimate, for instance, derives from “a median estimate of 187M H100-hours,” itself inferred from cluster-size reporting, combined with an assumed 20-40% MFU under the H100 FP8 spec (epoch.ai/data/notable_ai_models.csv, published Feb 27, 2025, pulled July 12, 2026). Strictly, that is an estimate built on an estimate built on a leak. But the chain of inference is visible and auditable in a way no journalism-sourced figure in this chapter is — which is why Epoch’s numbers get treated as their own tier rather than folded into either “official” or “hearsay.”

Two Epoch findings reshape how every compute claim downstream of here should be read. First, Epoch’s own analysis concludes GPT-5 used less pretraining compute than GPT-4.5 — 6.6e25 FLOP versus 3.8e26 FLOP, roughly a fifth to a sixth — breaking OpenAI’s historical pattern of roughly 100x pretraining-compute jumps between integer generations. Epoch attributes the break to a September 2024 reasoning/RL breakthrough that let post-training compute substitute for pretraining scale (epoch.ai/gradient-updates/why-gpt5-used-less-training-compute-than-gpt45-but-gpt6-probably-wont, ~Aug 2025). Epoch forecasts GPT-6 will eventually exceed GPT-4.5’s pretraining compute once post-training-scaling returns saturate. It hasn’t happened yet: GPT-5.6 shipped July 9, 2026, in three variants named Luna, Terra, and Sol (OpenAI, “GPT-5.6” and “Previewing GPT-5.6 Sol,” July 9, 2026), no OpenAI model has re-exceeded GPT-4.5’s February 2025 estimate in Epoch’s tracker, and trade coverage as of July 11, 2026 states “GPT-6 still has no announcement, no model card, and no date” — pushing the most defensible GPT-6 window to Q4 2026 at the earliest, possibly 2027 (per aggregated tracker sites — findskill.ai, felloai.com, lifearchitect.ai — weak sources, fitting for a claim about absence). Note the asymmetry: the shipped-model claim is OpenAI’s own words; the “no GPT-6 yet” claim rests on the absence of a disclosure, not a confirmed denial.

Second, and more consequential for sizing any single “training cost” figure quoted anywhere: Epoch estimates OpenAI’s single final, headline pretraining run in 2024 accounted for only about 9.6% of total R&D compute spend that year — roughly $500M of a ~$5B budget, the remaining ~90% consumed by ablations, failed runs, architecture search, and other experimentation. Chinese labs show a similar-order but higher ratio — MiniMax at 22.6% on a $141M base, Zhipu/Z.ai at 12.3% on a $216M base (epoch.ai/gradient-updates/r-and-d-vs-training-compute, fetched July 13, 2026). The estimate rests on three disclosed data points at markedly different scale, plus Epoch’s own assumptions (H800 peak FLOP/s of 9.89e14, pretraining MFU 0.15-0.35, RL/post-training MFU 0.05-0.20, rental pricing $1.50-3.00/hr) — a directional planning heuristic, not a precise multiplier. If it holds at frontier scale, every “headline training run cost” figure in circulation — including the widely repeated ~$0.5-1B GPT-4.5/GPT-5-class figures — understates the true compute budget required to reach that final run by close to an order of magnitude. Chapter 23’s cost modeling and Chapter 24’s gap-list costing both lean on this directly.

Epoch’s tracker also carries a structural staleness worth internalizing before treating it as current. Its GPU-cluster database, pulled directly (482 rows, every one Status=“Existing”), contains zero “Planned” rows despite the site’s own documentation claiming it tracks planned systems, and its most recent confirmed entry has a first-operational date of July 22, 2025 (pull of July 12, 2026). Colossus 2, Stargate Abilene’s GB200 buildout, Project Rainier, and any TPU 8t/Ironwood training pod are entirely absent from the structured, downloadable dataset — even though Epoch discusses several of them in separate narrative posts. As of the same pull, the tracker carries no confidence-rated FLOP estimate whatsoever for Gemini 3 Pro, any Claude Opus 4.x model, Claude Sonnet 4/4.5, or GPT-5.1 — every model that actually defines the frontier as of mid-2026. The single most-cited “rigorous” compute estimator in the field cannot currently see the current frontier. Its structured data is a floor on cluster scale running six to twelve months behind the press-release frontier; its blog-post commentary, which cites press releases directly rather than the CSV, is the more current layer.

That staleness produces the cleanest single illustration of the closed-frontier transparency inversion. The only “Confident”-rated large training-compute estimates anywhere in Epoch’s dataset belong to Chinese open-weight labs that publish real technical reports: DeepSeek-V3 at 3.3e24 FLOP (Confident), Kimi K2 at 2.976e24 FLOP (Confident), GLM-4.5/4.6/4.7 at 4.42e24 FLOP (Likely) (epoch.ai/data/notable_ai_models.csv, pulled July 12, 2026). The most rigorously estimated figures in the entire public record belong to the labs that publish, not the labs that dominate capability headlines — a quantifiable inversion Chapters 3 and 4 take up directly.

A complementary cross-check comes from Epoch analyst Josh You’s estimated operational compute capacity per lab — distinct from any single training run — which shows the same asymmetry from a different angle: OpenAI roughly 1.7M H100-equivalents (up from 400K in 2024, 100K in 2023, roughly 4x/year growth); Anthropic 1M+ H100e (roughly 70% of OpenAI’s, catching up fast); xAI 600-700K H100e across Colossus 1+2; Google DeepMind 1-2M H100e (likely under half of Google’s total ~4M, the rest serving Search and Cloud); Meta MSL under 1.7M H100e, split with non-frontier recommender and ads workloads (epoch.ai/gradient-updates/frontier-labs-dont-use-most-ai-compute, ~2026). Combined, OpenAI, Anthropic, and xAI represent only 20-30% of global operational AI compute, and the top five labs together control under half of the world total. A majority of the world’s AI compute sits outside the frontier-pretraining labs entirely — in serving, enterprise, and non-LLM workloads.

Reading the two tables together, the pattern is stark: evidence quality is inversely correlated with current relevance. The best-evidenced numbers in the dataset — DeepSeek-V3’s 3.3e24 FLOP, Llama 4’s 390 TFLOPs/GPU — are six to eighteen months old, belong to non-frontier-scale runs, or both. The models that actually define “frontier” as of July 2026 — Gemini 3 Pro, Claude Opus 4.5/4.7, GPT-5.5/5.6 — have no FLOP estimate anywhere in the public record, rigorous or otherwise. This is not a research gap that harder digging closes. It is a structural feature of 2026 disclosure discipline, and a sharp change from 2023-2024, when GPT-4’s compute and even Gemini 1.0 Ultra’s got semi-official or leaked estimates within months of release.

What Is Knowable, What Is Estimable, and What Is Genuinely Dark

Three tiers, stated plainly here because every later chapter uses them implicitly.

Knowable, with real confidence: physical infrastructure. Chip counts, network topologies, power and cooling figures, and cluster-buildout timelines are disclosed abundantly and specifically by every major lab and hyperscaler, because this category of disclosure serves competitive signaling and investor confidence without revealing the actual secret sauce — data mixture, architecture, hyperparameters. This is the category Chapters 16 through 21 draw on most heavily, and it carries the fewest live epistemic disputes of any evidence class in the chapters ahead.

Estimable, with wide and explicitly labeled uncertainty: aggregate training-run compute, via triangulation of disclosed or leaked cluster size, run duration, and an assumed MFU range. Epoch’s tracker is the field’s best tool here, and its own confidence labels — never higher than “Likely” for any closed US lab’s current flagship — are the honest ceiling on what this category supports. These estimates appear throughout, always with Epoch’s confidence label attached, never presented as harder than the underlying methodology permits.

Genuinely dark, as of July 2026: exact token counts, data mixtures, architecture specifics (layer count, hidden dimension, expert count, routing design), full hyperparameter schedules, the ablation and experimentation compute spend that Epoch’s analysis suggests runs roughly nine times the headline training-run figure — and, the single most consequential unknown for anyone planning a 10T/200B-active run, whether a genuinely new, giant pretraining run exceeding GPT-4.5-class compute is in flight right now at any US lab. No source in the evidence base confirms or denies that for OpenAI post-“Spud”/GPT-5.5, for Anthropic, or for Google. It is precisely the question anyone planning a run like this would most want answered, and precisely the question the entire 2026 evidence base cannot answer.

Implications for the 10T/200B Run

Trust the hardware and topology chapters (16-21) with high confidence: that evidence class is abundant, cross-corroborated, and current, and it is where a project’s actual engineering plan should anchor. Treat every FLOP figure elsewhere as bracketed by the confidence label attached to it, and remember that the best-evidenced numbers are systematically the stalest. Budget total compute — not just the headline training-run figure — using something closer to Epoch’s roughly 10x ablation-tax ratio than the press-quoted training cost alone, while remembering that ratio is itself extrapolated from only three labs’ disclosed figures and Epoch’s own stated MFU and pricing assumptions, not a physical law. When benchmarking architecture and data-curriculum decisions (Chapters 5-7 and 12-14) against “what the frontier labs actually did,” expect the open literature to be dominated by Chinese labs precisely because they publish — DeepSeek, Kimi, GLM, and their peers are, in the narrow but load-bearing sense of “documented enough to actually replicate,” the only frontier-adjacent labs supplying gold-tier evidence at all. And hold this chapter’s negative result as a standing caveat on everything that follows: nobody outside five or six buildings on Earth currently knows whether the next GPT-4.5-scale jump in pretraining compute is already running, and nothing in the public record can resolve that for you. Chapters 3 and 4 take up the publish/don’t-publish inversion directly; Chapter 2 takes up the lab-by-lab disclosure record in full.
