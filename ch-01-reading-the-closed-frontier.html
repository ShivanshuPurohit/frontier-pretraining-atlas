A book about how to train a 10-trillion-parameter model has to start by admitting that the labs who have actually done something like it will not tell you how. What follows instead is a patchwork: engineering blogs about racks and fabrics, a handful of technical reports that stopped coming after one generation, sworn courtroom testimony, unscripted podcast asides, community microbenchmarks of silicon the vendor never fully specified, and an analyst shop that rates its own confidence on a three-point scale because nothing sturdier exists. None of these routes are equally trustworthy, and conflating them is the single most common failure mode in press coverage of frontier AI. This chapter sets the tagging convention this book uses for every claim about the closed labs, walks the 2026 evidence base tier by tier, and closes with a triangulated picture of what is actually knowable about frontier-run scale as of this writing — which, as it turns out, is considerably less than the discourse around it implies.

The Provenance Discipline

Every citation in this book carries a tag identifying the claim’s evidentiary route, carried through unaltered from the underlying research rather than upgraded or downgraded by editorial judgment at write-time. The tags in circulation, and what each implies about how hard the reader should lean on it:

This is not pedantry; it is the single most common failure mode this book’s sourcing has to guard against, and the field supplies a clean, dated worked example. In November 2025 an outlet called MarketNewsData asserted flatly that “Gemini 3 was trained entirely on Google’s own TPUs, with no Nvidia GPUs involved,” specifically crediting the Ironwood (TPU v7) generation — sourced only to unnamed “multiple industry sources,” naming no individual and linking no primary document [RUMOR] MarketNewsData, “What’s Actually New About Gemini 3 on TPUs”, Nov 20, 2025. Within months, IntuitionLabs, WioNews, Ynetnews, and BusinessWorld had recycled the same framing without adding new reporting — a single unsourced blog post fanning out into what reads, by mid-2026, like multi-outlet consensus. The more careful outlet working the same beat, SemiAnalysis, corroborates only the narrower and more defensible claim — that Gemini 3, “like all earlier versions of Gemini, was trained entirely on TPUs” — while explicitly declining to confirm the Ironwood-specific detail or supply any chip count or duration for the actual run [CREDIBLE-HEARSAY] SemiAnalysis, “Google TPUv7: The 900lb Gorilla In the Room,” fetched July 13, 2026. The defensible claim (“all-TPU, no Nvidia fallback”) and the widely-repeated claim (“trained exclusively on Ironwood”) are not the same claim, and only tracing the second one back to its unnamed-sources origin reveals the gap. This is the pattern this book is written to catch on every page, not just this one.

A second discipline sits alongside tagging: convergence as an explicit upgrade mechanism, used deliberately rather than silently. Where several independent secondary write-ups of an unfetchable primary source substantially agree on specifics, this book treats the convergent core as higher-confidence paraphrase and flags anything appearing in only one account as lower-confidence — the method needed, for instance, to reconstruct OpenAI’s April 2025 “Pre-Training GPT-4.5” retrospective video, which could not be directly transcribed, from three independent secondary accounts (a Medium deep-dive, a Followin.io writeup, an OpenTools summary) that substantially agree [OFFICIAL-VIDEO, secondhand transcription] “Pre-Training GPT-4.5,” OpenAI, April 11, 2025. The run’s jump past 100,000 GPUs, the multi-cluster requirement it forced, the torch.sum bug, and the “we’re no longer compute constrained” framing all appear across at least two of the three accounts and are treated as solid; the specific detail that the bug was found when the run was “roughly 40% complete” appears in only one account and is flagged accordingly rather than folded silently into the narrative.

The Source Landscape: What Closed Labs Actually Say

The organizing fact for reading 2026 frontier-lab disclosure — developed in full lab-by-lab detail in Chapter 2 — is that it splits into two categories with almost no overlap. Labs are extraordinarily voluble about physical infrastructure and almost completely silent about what any specific flagship model actually consumed to train.

On infrastructure, the 2025-2026 disclosure record is the richest the field has ever produced. OpenAI, together with AMD, Broadcom, Intel, Microsoft, and Nvidia, published its Multipath Reliable Connection (MRC) networking protocol as an Open Compute Project contribution in May 2026, disclosing a two-tier fabric supporting “131,072 GPUs at full bisection bandwidth,” NIC-side (not switch-side) routing state, and concrete production-resilience evidence — training jobs surviving simultaneous optical-transceiver failures on four links, core switches rebooted mid-training without job interruption [OFFICIAL-BLOG] OpenAI, “MRC supercomputer networking”, May 2026. Microsoft’s November 2025 “Azure AI superfactory” post disclosed that Fairwater Wisconsin and Fairwater Atlanta operate as one logical training fabric over a dedicated “AI WAN” built on 120,000+ new fiber miles in a single year, with Azure CTO Mark Russinovich on record that “the amount of infrastructure required now to train these models is not just one datacenter, not two, but multiples of that… nobody’s really run into that problem yet because they haven’t gotten to the scale we’re at now” [OFFICIAL-BLOG] Microsoft, Nov 12, 2025. Google’s April 2026 Cloud Next disclosures for its Virgo Network and TPU 8t/8i went further still, disclosing 134,000 TPU 8t chips in a single non-blocking fabric per site at 47 Pb/s bisectional bandwidth and a claim that JAX+Pathways can orchestrate “more than 1 million TPU chips in a single training cluster” [OFFICIAL-BLOG] Google Cloud, April 22, 2026. Anthropic discloses compute partnerships on a cadence of roughly every two to four months — “nearly half a million” Trainium2 chips for Project Rainier (Oct 2025), up to one million TPUs from Google (Oct 23, 2025), a Google-Broadcom follow-on adding 3.5 GW starting 2027 (April 7, 2026) — each a real, named, dollar-figured announcement [OFFICIAL-BLOG] Amazon/AWS and Anthropic/Google, Oct 2025-Apr 2026.

None of that candor extends to the models the infrastructure actually trains. Anthropic’s system cards describe pretraining data sources (web crawl, licensed data, self-generated synthetic data, opted-in user data) but disclose zero GPU/TPU/Trainium counts, FLOPs, or token counts for any specific model — compute scale lives entirely in partnership press releases, never in the model cards [OFFICIAL, system cards via secondary summarization]. Google’s Gemini 1.5 technical report remains, as of mid-2026, the single most quantified infrastructure disclosure the field has received from a closed lab — SuperPod topology, a goodput figure (“increased from 85 to 97 percent” on the largest-scale job), an explicit SDC-rate expectation (“every week or two”) [PAPER] Gemini Team, Google, arXiv:2403.05530, March 2024 — and no Gemini release since has matched it. Meta’s Llama 3 paper is the field’s other landmark disclosure, with a full infrastructure chapter (16,384-of-24,576 H100s, 38-43% BF16 MFU, 419 unexpected interruptions across a 54-day window) [PAPER] arXiv:2407.21783, July 2024 — but Llama 4 Behemoth, the ~2T-total/288B-active successor, gave the field exactly one hard number (“390 TFLOPs/GPU” on 32K GPUs, FP8) before being quietly shelved, never released, and apparently never resumed at that scale by Meta Superintelligence Labs [OFFICIAL-BLOG] Meta AI, April 2025; shelving per [CREDIBLE-HEARSAY, SemiAnalysis] aggregated reporting, 2026. One technical-report-grade disclosure per lab, then infrastructure-only disclosure for every subsequent generation, is close to a universal pattern across the labs Chapter 2 surveys in full.

The Insider-Journalism Layer: Track Record and Its Limits

Where labs go quiet, institutional journalism with named sourcing chains fills part of the gap, and its 2025-2026 track record is genuinely mixed in ways worth separating rather than averaging together. The Wall Street Journal’s reconstruction of OpenAI’s Orion/GPT-4.5 struggles — at least two large training attempts spanning several months each, a roughly $0.5B compute-cost estimate for a six-month run, Sutskever quoted as “the data is not growing because we have but one internet” — has held up as the canonical account, cross-corroborated by OpenAI’s own later retrospective video [REPORTED-BY:WSJ via Fortune] Feb 25, 2025. Reuters’ October 2024 scoop on OpenAI’s “Tigris”-codenamed custom-silicon effort with Broadcom, led by former Google TPU-program head Richard Ho, has also held up, but needs a correction most secondary coverage misses: the chip that actually shipped in June 2026, “Jalapeño,” is explicitly inference-only, and the training-chip effort has not surfaced a shipped product as of this writing — any claim that OpenAI has “its own training chip” in production conflates two separate programs [REPORTED-BY:Reuters] Oct 29, 2024; [OFFICIAL-BLOG] OpenAI, June 24, 2026.

SemiAnalysis occupies a distinct tier within this layer. This book’s underlying research credits it with repeatedly pre-empting official disclosures — its Fairwater and MRC reporting anticipated content Microsoft and OpenAI later confirmed officially — but that assessment is this book’s own editorial judgment about SemiAnalysis’s track record, formed in the course of annotating a Dylan Patel Dwarkesh appearance as a source, not a claim Patel or SemiAnalysis has made on the record about itself, and it was not independently timing-audited against publication dates [ANALYSIS, this book’s research corpus, cross-referencing SemiAnalysis’s Fairwater/MRC reporting against Microsoft’s Nov 2025 and OpenAI’s May 2026 official disclosures]. But several of SemiAnalysis’s most consequential pieces for this book’s purposes — “Llama Behemoth [4] Failure Post-Mortem,” “xAI’s Colossus 2 - First Gigawatt Datacenter In The World,” “Meta Superintelligence – Leadership, Compute, Talent, and Data” — are referenced by title elsewhere but sit behind a paywall this research could not clear. Their existence is confirmed; their content is not [CREDIBLE-HEARSAY, existence/title only confirmed]. This book flags that distinction every time it applies rather than treating “SemiAnalysis says” as uniformly load-bearing regardless of whether the underlying text was actually read.

A structurally different and, in one specific instance, uniquely reliable channel opened in 2026: compelled courtroom testimony. OpenAI President Greg Brockman testified under oath in the Musk v. OpenAI trial, reported May 5, 2026, that OpenAI “plans to spend $50 billion on computing power in 2026,” up from costs that were “roughly $30 million in 2017” and had reached “tens of billions of dollars” by the time of testimony [REPORTED-BY:Bloomberg, sourced to sworn courtroom testimony] May 5, 2026. This figure has a fundamentally different evidentiary basis than any other compute number this chapter cites — perjury risk changes the incentive structure in a way no PR blog post or “sources familiar with the matter” leak can — and litigation discovery is worth actively monitoring for the remainder of that trial and any parallel antitrust or IP litigation touching frontier labs.

The layer’s structural weakness is that even its most reconciled-looking numbers frequently are not reconciled at all. The Anthropic-Google TPU relationship alone carries at least three non-overlapping public figures across ten months: the October 23, 2025 official framing (“up to 1 million TPUs,” “well over a gigawatt,” “tens of billions of dollars”); an April 7, 2026 official follow-on with Broadcom (“3.5 GW starting 2027,” alongside a disclosed $30B quarterly run-rate revenue and a $380B valuation off a $30B Series G); and Bloomberg’s May 18, 2026 cumulative framing (“up to $40 billion… 5 gigawatts… over five years… up to one million Ironwood chips”) — a bigger number than any single Anthropic or Google release states on its own [OFFICIAL-BLOG + REPORTED-BY:TechCrunch/Bloomberg], 2025-2026. These are plausibly different snapshots of a moving, tranche-by-tranche negotiation rather than genuine contradictions, but no single source supplies the full ledger, and this book does not attempt to manufacture one where the primary record doesn’t support it. xAI’s Colossus 2 financing shows the same pattern from a different angle: roughly $18-20B of GB200/GB300 hardware, structured as ~$7.5B equity plus $12-12.5B of GPU-collateralized debt through a special-purpose vehicle that buys the chips and leases them back to xAI, with lenders reportedly pushing for three-year repayment terms — a credit-market skepticism signal about xAI’s balance sheet absent from any headline “$X billion raised” figure [REPORTED-BY:WSJ/The Information] July 2025 onward.

Podcasts and Talks as Primary Sources

A genuinely distinct evidence tier, underused by most secondary coverage of this field, is the long-form podcast interview — Dwarkesh, Latent Space, Sequoia’s Training Data, No Priors, Lex Fridman — where lab principals speak at length, unscripted, often with a full transcript the show publishes directly. This book treats these transcripts as primary sources when directly fetched, and as [SPOKEN-CLAIM] paraphrase, with corresponding caveats, when recovered only via a third-party transcript aggregator such as podscripts.co or singjupost.com.

The value of this tier is candor written disclosure rarely matches. Jeff Dean and Noam Shazeer confirmed on Dwarkesh, in terms no Google blog post has used, that cross-datacenter synchronous training is already routine production practice at Google, gated purely by a bandwidth-versus-step-time budget rather than any new algorithm: “as long as the gradient accumulation and communication of the parameters across metro areas happens fast enough relative to the step time, you’re golden,” with Shazeer adding the near-identical formulation, “as long as you can sync all of the parameters of the model across the different data centers and then accumulate all the gradients, in the time it takes to do one step, you’re pretty good” [SPOKEN-CLAIM] Dwarkesh Podcast, Feb 12, 2025. On the same show roughly a year later, Jensen Huang dismissed TPU adoption in blunter terms than any Nvidia earnings call: “Anthropic is a unique instance, not a trend. Without Anthropic, why would there be any TPU growth at all? It’s 100% Anthropic” [SPOKEN-CLAIM] Dwarkesh Podcast, April 15, 2026. Sholto Douglas — at Google DeepMind on RL scaling in the earlier episode, at Anthropic in the later one, and the notes underlying this book could not cleanly separate which claim belongs to which date — disclosed that Anthropic’s RL compute spend was still only “$1 million” and deliberately so: “we aren’t in the compute limited regime for RL yet,” framed as a conscious strategy of buying algorithmic certainty before committing large compute [SPOKEN-CLAIM] Dwarkesh Podcast, 2025. Bob McGrew, OpenAI’s former Head of Research, told Sequoia’s Training Data that “the real lever for pre-training in 2025 is improving architectures” rather than further scale, and — a specific, actionable claim for anyone scoping a 10T-parameter design — that a new architecture requires restarting pretraining “from the beginning,” meaning the architecture choice is a one-shot commitment in a stronger sense than any subsequent data-curriculum or hyperparameter decision [SPOKEN-CLAIM] Sequoia Training Data, June 17, 2025.

Dylan Patel’s appearances are the single richest podcast source this book draws on for hardware-supply-chain reasoning specifically. On Dwarkesh in March 2026 he argued the long-run structural bottleneck sits not at power or chips but at ASML: “by 2028 or 2029, the bottleneck falls to the lowest rung on the supply chain,” citing roughly 70 EUV tools shipped in 2025, 80 in 2026, capping near 100+/year by 2030 even under aggressive expansion — while a full gigawatt of Rubin-generation AI datacenter capacity needs only about 3.5 EUV tools (roughly $1.2B) against roughly $50B of total datacenter capex for that same gigawatt [SPOKEN-CLAIM] Dylan Patel, Dwarkesh Podcast, March 13, 2026. On the same episode he put memory, not logic, as the acute near-term shortage — roughly 30% of Big Tech’s 2026 capex, consumer DRAM prices roughly tripling, smartphone volumes projected to fall from about 1.1B to 500-600M units/year as AI-datacenter DRAM demand cannibalizes consumer supply. Michael Kagan, Nvidia’s CTO and Mellanox’s co-founder, gave what this book treats as the clearest plain-language explanation anywhere of why network jitter, not raw bandwidth, caps effective cluster size — directly relevant to Chapter 18’s networking math: “instead of being able to split your job to a thousand GPUs, you can split it only to ten GPUs, because you need to accommodate for the jitter,” and located the onset of near-certain component failure at “a few tens of thousands” of components, not the 100K+ threshold popularly cited [SPOKEN-CLAIM] Michael Kagan, Sequoia Training Data, Oct 28, 2025.

A related but structurally different tier sits at technical conferences and community-authored venues — GPU MODE, Hot Chips, the Open Compute Project Summit, Meta’s @Scale conference series, individual engineer blogs — which produce measured ground truth independent of any lab’s PR apparatus. Academic microbenchmarking of Blackwell silicon reports 96.3% of theoretical peak throughput on FP8/BF16/FP4 tensor-core GEMM but only 80.7% on FP64 [BENCHMARK] Jarmusch & Chandrasekaran, arXiv:2512.02189, Dec 2025. Community teardowns of B200 document a die-to-die interconnect tax invisible in any vendor slide deck — L2 cache bandwidth of 21 TB/s for same-partition access falling to 16.8 TB/s across the die boundary — directly relevant to expert- and tensor-parallel shard placement within a single logical GPU [BLOG] Chips and Cheese, 2025. Meta disclosed at its own @Scale conference that its RoCE fabric scaled from 4,096 to 24,576 to 129,000 GPUs in under two years, a materially larger production figure than the 24,576-GPU number in its well-known SIGCOMM’24 paper [OFFICIAL-BLOG] engineering.fb.com, Sept 2025. And a genuinely obscure primary source — an unpublished MIT MEng thesis by Laker Newhouse, working with Muon co-theorist Jeremy Bernstein — quantifies a “noise floor” implicit in Muon’s Newton-Schulz iteration (a maximum 485x singular-value inflation factor) and shows that matching an unconstrained NanoGPT baseline’s 39.4% validation accuracy under a provable Lipschitz bound requires that bound to blow up to a figure the thesis itself states inconsistently — 10^274 in the abstract, 10^278 in the conclusion, almost certainly an internal erratum rather than two distinct results — vivid evidence, at either exponent, of how vacuous formal robustness guarantees become at competitive model quality [THESIS] Laker Newhouse, MIT MEng thesis, May 2025, pp. 13, 57. None of this layer carries institutional accountability, but its incentives — recruiting, standard-setting, community credibility, academic priority — are orthogonal to a press release’s, and its numbers are measured rather than claimed. It underwrites much of Chapters 7, 14, and 15 specifically.

Epoch AI and the Economics of Estimation

Sitting above both the journalism layer and the podcast layer is a single organization functioning as the field’s closest approximation to a rigorous, methodologically transparent estimator: Epoch AI. Its frontier-training-run tracker assigns every entry a confidence label — Speculative, Likely, Confident — and discloses its methodology rather than black-boxing it: GPT-4.5’s headline 3.8e26 FLOP estimate, for instance, derives from “a median estimate of 187M H100-hours,” itself inferred from cluster-size reporting, combined with an assumed 20-40% MFU under the H100 FP8 spec [EPOCH-ESTIMATE] epoch.ai/data/notable_ai_models.csv, published Feb 27, 2025, pulled July 12, 2026. This is, strictly, an estimate built on an estimate built on a leak — but the chain of inference is visible and auditable in a way no journalism-sourced figure in this chapter is, which is precisely why this book treats Epoch’s numbers as a distinct tier rather than folding them into either “official” or “hearsay.”

Two Epoch findings reshape how the rest of this book’s compute claims should be read. First, Epoch’s own analysis concludes GPT-5 used less pretraining compute than GPT-4.5 — 6.6e25 FLOP versus 3.8e26 FLOP, roughly a fifth to a sixth — breaking OpenAI’s historical pattern of roughly 100x pretraining-compute jumps between integer generations, attributed to a September 2024 reasoning/RL breakthrough that let post-training compute substitute for pretraining scale [EPOCH-ESTIMATE/ANALYSIS] epoch.ai/gradient-updates/why-gpt5-used-less-training-compute-than-gpt45-but-gpt6-probably-wont, ~Aug 2025. Epoch forecasts GPT-6 will eventually exceed GPT-4.5’s pretraining compute once post-training-scaling returns saturate — but as of this writing (GPT-5.6 shipped July 9, 2026, in three variants named Luna, Terra, and Sol [OFFICIAL-BLOG] OpenAI, “GPT-5.6” and “Previewing GPT-5.6 Sol,” July 9, 2026) no OpenAI model has yet re-exceeded GPT-4.5’s February 2025 estimate in Epoch’s own tracker, and trade coverage as of July 11, 2026 states “GPT-6 still has no announcement, no model card, and no date,” pushing the most defensible GPT-6 window to Q4 2026 at the earliest, possibly 2027 [CREDIBLE-HEARSAY, aggregated tracker sites — findskill.ai, felloai.com, lifearchitect.ai]. The shipped-model claim and the “no GPT-6 yet” claim are not equally solid: the former is OpenAI’s own words, the latter rests on the absence of a disclosure rather than a confirmed denial.

Second, and more consequential for sizing any single “training cost” figure quoted elsewhere in this book: Epoch estimates OpenAI’s single final, headline pretraining run in 2024 accounted for only about 9.6% of total R&D compute spend that year — roughly $500M of a ~$5B budget, the remaining ~90% consumed by ablations, failed runs, architecture search, and other experimentation. Chinese labs show a similar-order but higher ratio — MiniMax at 22.6% on a $141M base, Zhipu/Z.ai at 12.3% on a $216M base [EPOCH-ESTIMATE/ANALYSIS] epoch.ai/gradient-updates/r-and-d-vs-training-compute, fetched July 13, 2026. This rests on three disclosed data points at markedly different scale, and on Epoch’s own assumptions (H800 peak FLOP/s of 9.89e14, pretraining MFU 0.15-0.35, RL/post-training MFU 0.05-0.20, rental pricing $1.50-3.00/hr) — a directional planning heuristic, not a precise multiplier. If it holds at frontier scale generally, every “headline training run cost” figure quoted anywhere in this book — including the widely repeated ~$0.5-1B GPT-4.5/GPT-5-class figures — likely understates the true compute budget required to reach that final run by close to an order of magnitude, a point Chapter 21’s cost modeling and Chapter 22’s gap-list costing both lean on directly.

Epoch’s tracker also carries a structural staleness worth internalizing before treating it as current. Its GPU-cluster database, pulled directly for this research (482 rows, every one Status=“Existing”), contains zero “Planned” rows despite the site’s own documentation claiming it tracks planned systems, and its most recent confirmed entry has a first-operational date of July 22, 2025 [EPOCH-ESTIMATE, dataset structure], pulled July 12, 2026 — meaning Colossus 2, Stargate Abilene’s GB200 buildout, Project Rainier, and any TPU 8t/Ironwood training pod are entirely absent from the structured, downloadable dataset even though Epoch discusses several of them in separate narrative posts. As of the same pull, the tracker carries no confidence-rated FLOP estimate whatsoever for Gemini 3 Pro, any Claude Opus 4.x model, Claude Sonnet 4/4.5, or GPT-5.1 — every model that actually defines the frontier as of mid-2026 [EPOCH-ESTIMATE, absence noted]. The single most-cited “rigorous” compute estimator in the field cannot currently see the current frontier; its structured data is a floor on cluster scale roughly six to twelve months behind the press-release frontier, and its blog-post commentary — which cites press releases directly rather than the CSV — is the more current layer.

That staleness produces this book’s cleanest single illustration of the closed-frontier transparency inversion. The only “Confident”-rated large training-compute estimates anywhere in Epoch’s dataset belong to Chinese open-weight labs that publish real technical reports: DeepSeek-V3 at 3.3e24 FLOP (Confident), Kimi K2 at 2.976e24 FLOP (Confident), GLM-4.5/4.6/4.7 at 4.42e24 FLOP (Likely) [EPOCH-ESTIMATE] epoch.ai/data/notable_ai_models.csv, pulled July 12, 2026. The most rigorously estimated figures in the entire public record belong to labs that publish, not to the labs that dominate capability headlines — a quantifiable inversion this book returns to directly in Chapters 3 and 4.

A complementary cross-check comes from Epoch analyst Josh You’s estimated operational compute capacity per lab — distinct from any single training run — which shows a different but consistent picture of the same asymmetry: OpenAI roughly 1.7M H100-equivalents (up from 400K in 2024, 100K in 2023, roughly 4x/year growth); Anthropic 1M+ H100e (roughly 70% of OpenAI’s, catching up fast); xAI 600-700K H100e across Colossus 1+2; Google DeepMind 1-2M H100e (likely under half of Google’s total ~4M, the rest serving Search and Cloud); Meta MSL under 1.7M H100e, split with non-frontier recommender and ads workloads [EPOCH-ESTIMATE, Josh You] epoch.ai/gradient-updates/frontier-labs-dont-use-most-ai-compute, ~2026. Combined, OpenAI, Anthropic, and xAI represent only 20-30% of global operational AI compute, and the top five labs together control under half of the world total — a majority of the world’s AI compute sits outside the frontier-pretraining labs entirely, in serving, enterprise, and non-LLM workloads [EPOCH-ESTIMATE, Josh You] same source.

Reading the two tables together, the pattern is stark: evidence quality is inversely correlated with current relevance. The best-evidenced numbers in the dataset — DeepSeek-V3’s 3.3e24 FLOP, Llama 4’s 390 TFLOPs/GPU — are six to eighteen months old, belong to non-frontier-scale runs, or both. The models that actually define “frontier” as of July 2026 — Gemini 3 Pro, Claude Opus 4.5/4.7, GPT-5.5/5.6 — have no FLOP estimate anywhere in the public record, rigorous or otherwise. This is not a gap this book’s research failed to close; it is a structural feature of 2026 disclosure discipline, a sharp change from 2023-2024, when GPT-4’s compute and even Gemini 1.0 Ultra’s got semi-official or leaked estimates within months of release.

What Is Knowable, What Is Estimable, and What Is Genuinely Dark

Three tiers, worth stating plainly before the rest of this book proceeds to use them implicitly in every chapter.

Knowable, with real confidence: physical infrastructure. Chip counts, network topologies, power and cooling figures, and cluster-buildout timelines are disclosed abundantly and specifically by every major lab and hyperscaler, because this category of disclosure serves competitive signaling and investor confidence without revealing the actual secret sauce — data mixture, architecture, hyperparameters. This is the category Chapters 14 through 19 draw on most heavily, and it carries the fewest live epistemic disputes anywhere in this book.

Estimable, with wide and explicitly labeled uncertainty: aggregate training-run compute, via triangulation of disclosed or leaked cluster size, run duration, and an assumed MFU range. Epoch’s tracker is the field’s best tool here, and its own confidence labels — never higher than “Likely” for any closed US lab’s current flagship — are the honest ceiling on what this category supports. This book uses these estimates throughout, always tagged, never presented as harder than the underlying methodology permits.

Genuinely dark, as of this writing: exact token counts, data mixtures, architecture specifics (layer count, hidden dimension, expert count, routing design), full hyperparameter schedules, the ablation and experimentation compute spend that Epoch’s own analysis suggests runs roughly nine times the headline training-run figure, and — the single most consequential unknown for anyone planning a 10T/200B-active run — whether a genuinely new, giant pretraining run exceeding GPT-4.5-class compute is in flight right now at any US lab. No source in this book’s evidence base confirms or denies that for OpenAI post-“Spud”/GPT-5.5, for Anthropic, or for Google. This is precisely the question a reader planning the run this book is written for would most want answered, and precisely the question the entire 2026 evidence base cannot answer.

Implications for the 10T/200B Run

A planner reading this book for infrastructure and systems guidance should trust the hardware and topology chapters (14-19) with high confidence — that evidence class is abundant, cross-corroborated, and current, and it is where a project’s actual engineering plan should anchor. A planner reading for “what scale is competitive right now” should treat every FLOP figure as bracketed by the confidence label attached to it, remember that the best-evidenced numbers are systematically the stalest, and budget total compute — not just the headline training-run figure — using something closer to Epoch’s roughly 10x ablation-tax ratio than the press-quoted training cost alone, while remembering that ratio is itself extrapolated from only three labs’ disclosed figures and Epoch’s own stated MFU and pricing assumptions, not a physical law. A planner benchmarking architecture and data-curriculum decisions specifically (Chapters 5, 6, 12) against “what the frontier labs actually did” should expect the open literature to be dominated by Chinese labs precisely because they publish — DeepSeek, Kimi, GLM, and their peers are, in the narrow but load-bearing sense of “documented enough to actually replicate,” the only frontier-adjacent labs supplying gold-tier evidence at all. And every reader should hold the negative result of this chapter as a standing caveat on everything that follows: nobody outside five or six buildings on Earth currently knows whether the next GPT-4.5-scale jump in pretraining compute is already running, and this book, like every source it draws on, cannot resolve that for you. Chapters 3 and 4 take up the publish/don’t-publish inversion directly; Chapter 2 takes up the lab-by-lab disclosure record in full.
