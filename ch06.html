Chapter 06 · The framework decision is the run

Framework shoot-out: Megatron-Core vs NeMo vs MaxText/JAX vs TorchTitan vs DeepSpeed at frontier scale

The training framework is the single most consequential, hardest-to-reverse choice in a 10T-total / 200B-active MoE program. It fixes which parallelism mappings you can express, which precision recipes are production-validated, how the all-to-all overlaps — and, at 100,000 GB200 GPUs where mean-time-between-failure is measured in minutes, whether the run survives at all.

~30 min read5 figuresgrounded in Megatron-Core, MaxText src/maxtext/layers/moe.py

The framework decision dictates which parallelism mappings you can express, which precision recipes are production-validated, how the all-to-all is overlapped, and — at 100,000 GB200 GPUs where mean-time-between-failure is measured in minutes, not hours — whether your run survives at all. This chapter is an opinionated, evidence-weighted comparison of the five contenders that matter: Megatron-Core, NeMo / Megatron-Bridge, MaxText/JAX, TorchTitan, and the legacy DeepSpeed stack. The verdict is given up front and defended below: for a first frontier run on NVIDIA Blackwell, Megatron-Core (driven by Megatron-Bridge) is the lowest-risk EP engine today, MaxText/JAX is the strongest contender if your organization already has TPU-grade XLA muscle and wants quantization-as-config, TorchTitan is the best de-risking and rapid-iteration vehicle, and DeepSpeed is a legacy fallback you should not build a new 10T run on.

Whichever stack you pick, no framework has a published >2K-GPU MoE MFU curve — at 100k you are past every documented envelope, and the binding constraint is fault tolerance and silent-data-corruption resilience, not kernel throughput.

01A note on the denominator: dense FP8 peak, not sparse marketing

Before any MFU claim, fix the denominator. NVIDIA's headline GB200 NVL72 figures — 1,440 PFLOP/s FP4, 720 FP8, 360 BF16 per 72-GPU rack — are with-sparsity (2:4) numbers; the GB200 NVL72 product page footnotes them explicitly ("Dense is one-half sparse spec shown") (nvidia.com/.../gb200-nvl72). For dense training FLOPs the meaningful peaks are 360 PFLOP/s FP8 and 180 PFLOP/s BF16 per rack, i.e. roughly 4.5 PFLOP/s dense FP8 and 2.25 PFLOP/s dense BF16 per B200 GPU. A 100,000-GPU fleet is therefore ~0.45 EFLOP/s dense FP8 peak, not the ~1.0 EFLOP/s you get only by counting sparsity.

Every "TFLOPS/GPU" number quoted by Megatron, NeMo, MaxText and TorchTitan below is a dense-FLOP-equivalent achieved throughput; to convert to honest MFU you divide by 4,500 (FP8) or 2,250 (BF16). So Megatron-Core's 1,048 TFLOP/s/GPU for DeepSeek-V3-685B on GB200 (arxiv.org/abs/2603.07685) is ~23% of dense FP8 peak — and that is the best published Blackwell MoE number. The realistic large-MoE FP8 MFU band for 2025–2026 is ~35–40% for friendlier routing and ~18–35% for heavy fine-grained routing; nobody is near 50% on a 200B-active model, and any vendor pitch implying otherwise is quoting sparse peak or a dense Llama-style transformer.

The one number to plan against

Plan your token budget against ~20–35% FP8 MFU, not 50%. Convert every quoted TFLOP/s/GPU to MFU by dividing by 4,500 (FP8) or 2,250 (BF16). Anything implying 50% on a 200B-active MoE is quoting sparse peak or a dense model.

compute MFU = TFLOP/s/GPU ÷ dense-peak ; dense-peak = 4500 (FP8) · 2250 (BF16) GB200 dense FP8 = ½ × 720 PF sparse ÷ 72 GPU ≈ 4.5 PFLOP/s/GPU. Megatron's 1048 TF/s ÷ 4500 ≈ 23%.

02Megatron-Core: the EP-maturity leader

Megatron-Core is the de-facto reference implementation for frontier MoE, and the March 2026 NVIDIA report "Scalable Training of Mixture-of-Experts Models with Megatron Core" (arXiv 2603.07685, 45 authors, 88 pages) is the densest primary source in the field (arxiv.org/abs/2603.07685). It frames the problem as three coupled "walls" — memory, communication, computation — and argues MoE forces co-design because relieving one wall shifts pressure to another. Parallel Folding: decoupling attention from experts

Parallel Folding is the architectural centerpiece, and it is essentially mandatory for fine-grained MoE. The classical Megatron constraint was world_size = TP×CP×PP×DP with EP ⊆ DP, forcing attention and expert layers to share a parallelism mapping. Parallel Folding (precursor arXiv 2504.14960) decouples them via a ProcessGroupCollection tracking two independent hierarchies: attention uses (tp, cp, dp, pp); MoE uses (ep, etp, expt_dp, pp) (arxiv.org/html/2603.07685v1). This resolves the dense-sparse mismatch directly: dense attention — especially MLA with its large up-projection — benefits from high TP, while fine-grained experts are fragmented by high TP and prefer ETP=1 with large EP.

The load-bearing data point lives in the folding precursor: on fine-grained Mixtral-8x22B-G8T8 (128 H100), folding lifts MFU from 17.1% to 28.8% (+68%), versus only 46.3%→49.3% for standard Mixtral (arxiv.org/pdf/2504.14960). The fine-grained regime — exactly the 10T/200B target with hundreds of small experts — is where folding matters most. FP8 stacks on top: Mixtral-8x22B reaches 631.7 TFLOPS (FP8+folding) vs 458.3 BF16, a 1.30× lift.

ETP=1, high EP for fine-grained experts

Parallel Folding lets attention run TP-heavy (MLA up-proj loves it) while experts run ETP=1 with large EP. On fine-grained Mixtral-8x22B this is a +68% MFU swing (17.1%→28.8%) — the single biggest architectural lever for the 10T/200B target.

EP placement and the dispatcher zoo

The EP-placement philosophy is the biggest single throughput lever, and it is topology-driven. The DeepSeek-V3 case study lands on EP=8, ETP=1, TP=4, PP=2, VPP=2, CP=1, micro-batch=1 on a GB200 NVL72, deliberately keeping EP entirely inside the 72-GPU NVLink domain so all-to-all never crosses the scale-out IB fabric (arxiv.org/html/2603.07685). The motivation is stark: unoptimized cross-node A2A can consume up to 60% of step time for DeepSeek-V3-style multi-node experts. A2A volume scales as T·K·h·(EP−1)/EP, so confining EP to one NVLink island (130 TB/s aggregate on NVL72) is the difference between a healthy run and a comm-bound one.

comms A2A volume ∝ T · K · h · (EP − 1) / EP Approaches T·K·h as EP grows. Confining EP inside one 130 TB/s NVL72 island is what keeps the (EP−1)/EP factor riding NVLink instead of the IB fabric.

Megatron-Core ships three dispatcher backends: AllGather (memory-heavy, tiny-EP only), AllToAll (NCCL P2P, the workhorse), and Flex — a unified backend wrapping DeepEP (DeepSeek's NVSHMEM+IBGDA kernels, for cross-node fine-grained) and HybridEP (NVIDIA's own TMA+IBGDA kernels tuned for NVL72-class topologies). For GB200 NVL72, NVIDIA explicitly recommends HybridEP over DeepEP (--moe-flex-dispatcher-backend hybridep), as it is integrated into Megatron-Core's fused all-to-all module (docs.nvidia.com/.../performance-guide). A practical wrinkle from the perf guide: use 64 of the 72 GPUs to avoid NVLink/network-domain partitioning complexity, and TP4+PP2 can beat TP8 when both fit memory.

Dropless, precision, and the three walls

Token-dropless (dMoE) is the default and the recommended frontier path — no capacity factor, no dropped tokens, implemented via token permutation + all-to-all so each expert sees a contiguous GEMM. The hard part is CUDA Graphs: dropless routing produces dynamic shapes, so full-iteration graph capture requires the newer sync-free kernels, MoE ECHO, and Paged Stashing machinery — recent and less battle-tested than the rest of the stack. Load balancing offers aux_loss, seq_aux_loss, global_aux_loss, Sinkhorn, Z-loss, and the DeepSeek-style auxiliary-loss-free per-expert bias (--moe-router-enable-expert-bias --moe-router-bias-update-rate 1e-3).

Precision is a first-class lever on all three walls. Via Transformer Engine + TEGroupedMLP: per-tensor FP8 (generic), blockwise FP8 (the recommended Hopper production recipe, loss "negligibly different" from BF16), MXFP8 (Blackwell microscaling), and NVFP4 (Blackwell 4-bit) (github.com/NVIDIA/Megatron-LM/.../moe/README.md). NVIDIA's standalone NVFP4 result is the most concrete ceiling: 1.59× over BF16 on GB200 NVL72 (1,850 vs 1,165 TFLOP/s/GPU), MMLU 52.83% vs 52.73% BF16 on Llama-3-8B/1T tokens — but with two sharp caveats: the final four transformer layers must stay BF16, and the recipe is sensitive (AdamW ε=1e-8, LR 6e-4→6e-6, GBS=768) (developer.nvidia.com/.../nvfp4). MoE-specific FP8 plumbing includes grouped quantization fused with Grouped GEMM and padding/unpadding fusion (--moe-router-padding-for-fp8) to satisfy static-shape alignment.

The memory wall is attacked with dual-level fine-grained recomputation (selectively recompute MLA up-proj, layernorm, moe_act rather than whole layers), fine-grained activation offloading (per-layer host offload in forward, prefetch in backward, VPP-aware), and a precision-aware distributed optimizer (ZeRO-1-style, BF16 master weights/moments, ~50% optimizer-memory cut). The communication wall, beyond dispatchers, adds --overlap-moe-expert-parallel-comm for batch-level EP-A2A/compute overlap, DP reduce-scatter overlapped with backward, and 1F1B EP-A2A overlap on the roadmap.

Forward-tracking is Megatron's quiet superpower

The 2026 Q2 roadmap (Issue #4815) already targets DeepSeek-V4 (mHC, sqrt-softplus / Hash MoE scoring, CSA+HCA hybrid attention, Muon distributed optimizer with FP8 primary weights, MTP), Qwen3.5 (Gated Delta Rule kernels), HybridEP for GB200, DeepEP v2, and the ECHO/Paged-Stashing CUDA-graph trick (github.com/NVIDIA/Megatron-LM/issues/4815). It tracks frontier architectures fastest of any open stack.

Where Megatron is worst at 100k

Four real risks. (1) Published benchmarks top out at "thousands of GPUs" / 1,024-node strong scaling — there is no published 100k-GB200 MFU curve, so frontier-scale claims are extrapolation, and the existing curves already shed ~8–15 MFU points going 128→1,024 GPUs. (2) DP-scaling of A2A and PP bubble: at 100k you need very large DP, and gradient all-reduce/reduce-scatter plus PP warmup/cooldown bubbles dominate. (3) Resiliency is bolted on, not native — NVRx fault tolerance, in-process restart, straggler detection, and async/local checkpointing live in the Megatron-Bridge/NeMo layer and require Slurm + ft_launcher, PyTorch ≥2.5.1, NCCL ≥2.26.2, torch_dist checkpoints (docs.nvidia.com/.../resiliency). (4) Silent data corruption: emerging work (LLM-PRISM, arXiv 2604.10390) embeds RTL-level GPU fault injection into Megatron-LM and shows SDC silently perturbs gradients — there is no first-class SDC detection in the core training loop (arxiv.org/html/2604.10390v1).

The real 100k bottleneck lives outside the compute kernels

Net: the compute/communication kernels are frontier-ready; the fault-tolerance and SDC-resilience envelope is the real 100k-GPU bottleneck, and it lives outside Megatron-Core proper — in the Megatron-Bridge/NeMo layer. Which is exactly why you drive Megatron-Core through Bridge, not raw.

03NeMo / Megatron-Bridge: orchestration, recipes, and a migration in flight

The most important fact for a 2026 planner is that the "NeMo 2.0" abstraction you may be anchored to is mid-migration. NeMo 2.0 was a code-first Pythonic rewrite that wrapped Megatron-Core as the engine but kept PyTorch Lightning as orchestration (Trainer, MegatronStrategy, callbacks) with config scattered across Lightning args, callbacks, and strategy params. As of 2026, NVIDIA has refactored this into Megatron-Bridge (NVIDIA-NeMo/Megatron-Bridge, latest v0.5.0, June 22 2026), a PyTorch-native training loop that drops Lightning entirely, consolidates everything into a single ConfigContainer, and pins Megatron-Core as a git submodule tracking main+dev via scripts/switch_mcore.sh, with bidirectional HuggingFace↔Megatron checkpoint conversion (github.com/NVIDIA-NeMo/Megatron-Bridge).

Critically, NVIDIA's own production MoE pretraining now runs on Megatron-Bridge: the Nemotron Nano 3 recipe (hybrid Mamba-2/attention/MoE, 128 routed + 1 shared expert, top-6, ~3.5B active / ~31.6B total, 25T tokens, aux-loss-free balancing) states "Pretraining is implemented using Megatron-Bridge" (docs.nvidia.com/nemotron/.../nano3/pretrain).

The correct mental model for a fresh 10T/200B run starting in 2026: Megatron-Core = engine, Megatron-Bridge = training loop/recipes, NeMo-Run = launcher/orchestrator, with "NeMo 2.0" a transitional waypoint you should not build new large runs on. The Bridge layer adds what raw Megatron-Core lacks: (1) preset model providers and recipes (deepseek_v3_pretrain_config(), DeepSeek V2/V3/V4, Qwen3-MoE, Qwen3.5/3.6-VL, Gemma-4-VL 26B-A4B, OLMoE, Ling 2.0 MoE); (2) mixed-precision recipe presets (bf16_with_fp8_current_scaling_mixed, bf16_with_mxfp8_mixed, bf16_with_nvfp4_mixed); (3) checkpoint/HF bridging; (4) nvidia-resiliency-ext and AutoConfigurator integration; (5) NeMo-Run as the cluster launcher. The MoE math — token dispatch, grouped GEMM, parallel folding, FP8 — all lives in Megatron-Core, so MFU is fundamentally a Megatron-Core property; Bridge is ergonomics on top. The reference DeepSeek-V3 recipe uses TP2, PP16, EP64, ETP1, SP on, GBS 4096, seq 4096, MTP 1 layer (loss scale 0.1), selective recompute on a 1,024-GPU/128-node reference (docs.nvidia.com/.../deepseek_v3).

The resiliency stack: why a 100k run survives

The resiliency stack is the reason a 100k run survives, and it is the strongest in the open ecosystem. nvidia-resiliency-ext (NVRx) exposes five components:

  1. straggler detection (relative + per-GPU scores; does not stop by default, needs stop_if_detected=True);
  2. fault tolerance (section-based monitoring, in-job restart within the existing Slurm allocation, requires ft_launcher not torchrun);
  3. in-process restart (inprocess.Wrapper) — restarts within the same process via a while True: initialize→health_check→run→except→abort→rank_assignment loop, with a documented latency budget of destroy_process_group ~0.5s + rank reassignment ~0.5s + TCPStore barrier ~0.5s @ 16k ranks, total 1.5–3+ seconds vs minutes for a relaunch, built on NCCL 2.27's ncclCommShrink (nvidia.github.io/.../inprocess/usage_guide, developer.nvidia.com/.../nccl-2-27);
  4. async checkpointing (torch_dist only, 5–15× overhead reduction vs synchronous);
  5. local checkpointing (node-local SSD/RAM with configurable cross-node replication).

Maturity warnings you must internalize. In NeMo 25.09, DeepSeek subchannel/blockwise FP8 pretraining was broken; the FP8-current-scaling workaround left MTP loss non-converging (docs.nvidia.com/.../knownissues). A 100k fleet spans multiple domains — plan the outer loop demonstrated @ 3,072 GPUs

A 100k-GB200 fleet almost certainly spans multiple NVLink/IB domains and possibly multiple datacenters. NVIDIA's Hierarchical AllReduce work (DP/PP across sites, ReduceScatter→cross-DC AllReduce→AllGather, ~1/N inter-DC bandwidth, 96% scaling efficiency and MFU 49% vs 51% single-site at 21ms RTT, projected to 500k+ GPUs) shows the path — but is demonstrated at only 3,072 GPUs (developer.nvidia.com/.../turbocharge-llm-training).

NeMo-Run rounds out the picture with Slurm (Pyxis)/Skypilot/DGXCloud/Lepton executors, torchrun and ft launchers, and the AutoConfigurator grid search over TP/PP/CP/EP/MBS/activation-checkpointing-layers (docs.nvidia.com/.../autoconfigurator).

04MaxText / JAX: can XLA:GPU compete on GB200?

MaxText (AI-Hypercomputer/maxtext) is a pure-Python/JAX reference trainer that leans almost entirely on XLA ("largely optimization-free"), and it is the user's actual codebase for this program — so its specifics deserve precise treatment. As of the Feb 2026 src-layout restructure it ships Flax-NNX models, Orbax checkpointing, Optax/Tunix optimizers, Grain data loading, and a GSPMD logical-axis surface in src/maxtext/configs/base.yml that exposes mesh_axes = ['diloco','data','stage','fsdp','fsdp_transpose','context','context_autoregressive','tensor','tensor_transpose','tensor_sequence','expert','autoregressive'], each independently mappable to ICI vs DCN via ici_*_parallelism/dcn_*_parallelism (github.com/.../configs/base.yml). Recommended defaults are ici_fsdp_parallelism=-1 (auto) and dcn_data_parallelism=-1; dcn_tensor_parallelism and dcn_sequence_parallelism are flagged "never recommended." Sharding is GSPMD-by-annotation (logical axis rules + jax.sharding), with shard_map reserved for kernels and hand-written collectives.

The MoE path is the center of gravity and is unusually rich. In the user's actual codebase (MaxText), src/maxtext/layers/moe.py implements RoutedMoE with a get_topk() that dispatches routing variants covering the full frontier lineage: DeepSeek-V3 sigmoid + learnable-bias (aux-loss-free balancing via routed_bias_update_rate), DeepSeek-V4 HASH routing (a tid2eid lookup table, Tid2EidVar, first_num_hash_layers), Llama4 sigmoid gate, and Gemma sigmoid. Expert parallelism is realized through permute()/unpermute()/local_permute()/get_all_to_all_params() implementing the EP all-to-all, plus an optional use_ring_of_experts scheme built on jax.lax.ragged_all_to_all with custom Pallas ragged-sort kernels (a2a_ragged_sort, ring_ragged_sort, ring_ragged_unsort) and a ragged_buffer_factor knob trading token-drop vs memory (capacity_factor = -1.0 selects the dropless worst-case buffer). The grouped-matmul sparse_matmul() has three backends — Tokamax ragged_dot (use_tokamax_gmm), Megablox GMM, and jax.lax.ragged_dot — with explicit tiling knobs (wi_tile_*/wo_tile_*). DeepSeek-V3 is a first-class config, not a fork.

src/maxtext/layers/moe.pyRoutedMoE.get_topk → sparse_matmulrouting + EP all-to-all + grouped GEMMillustrative
class RoutedMoE(nnx.Module):
  def get_topk(self, gate_logits, tid):                        # [1]
    if self.config.is_hash_routing:                            # DeepSeek-V4 first layers
      return self.tid2eid[tid], jnp.ones_like(tid, jnp.float32)
    # DeepSeek-V3: sigmoid affinity + learnable per-expert bias (selection only)
    sel = jax.nn.sigmoid(gate_logits) + self.routed_bias       # bias is NOT a grad param
    return jax.lax.top_k(sel, self.config.num_experts_per_tok)

  def sparse_matmul(self, x, w, top_k_indices):
    x_sorted, sort_idx, gs = permute(x, top_k_indices)         # [2]
    p   = get_all_to_all_params(gs, self.shard_id, self.ep)    # offsets/sizes, not one-hot
    xd  = jax.lax.ragged_all_to_all(x_sorted, self.buffer, *p) # [3] EP dispatch
    xl  = local_permute(xd, ...)                               # regroup by local expert
    yl  = self.gmm(xl, w, local_group_sizes)                   # [4] 3-backend grouped GEMM
    yd  = jax.lax.ragged_all_to_all(yl, self.buffer, *p_rev)   # EP combine
    return unpermute(yd, sort_idx, combine_weights)            # un-biased gating weight

1Full frontier lineage in one method. Hash routing (V4), sigmoid+bias (V3), Llama4/Gemma gates — selected by config, no fork.

2Permute, not one-hot. permute argsorts by flattened expert id; get_all_to_all_params builds offset/size metadata — no dense BSEC mask.

3Ragged all-to-all. ragged_all_to_all moves only valid per-expert runs into a sized buffer (the ragged_buffer_factor knob bounds it).

4Three backends. gmm() picks Tokamax Mosaic ragged_dot, Megablox GMM (quantized), or XLA-native jax.lax.ragged_dot with wi_tile_* hints.

Attention is equally complete: attentions.py/attention_op.py/attention_mla.py provide MHA/GQA and a full MLA class (q_lora_rank, kv_lora_rank, qk_nope_head_dim, qk_rope_head_dim, v_head_dim), with kernel dispatch hardware-branched — TPU splash (Pallas) vs GPU cudnn_flash_te (Transformer Engine cuDNN fused attention). The model configs in configs/models/ are exactly the frontier set: deepseek3-671b.yml (256 experts top-8, 1 shared, 61 layers, 3 dense-first, MLA q_lora 1536/kv_lora 512, YaRN rope_factor 40, 163840 ctx, routed_scaling_factor 2.5), deepseek4-284b.yml (hash-routing layers), llama4-17b-128e.yml, and qwen3-480b-a35b.yml. Precision spans AQT int8/intmp, fp8/nanoo_fp8, and the newer Qwix path (use_qwix_quantization, with N:M sparsity), with AQT being deprecated in favor of Qwix mid-flight.

The optimizer/loop in optimizers.py and trainers/pre_train/train.py is notably mature for resilience: AdamW and Muon (optax.contrib._muon.muon), a skip_step_on_spikes() wrapper (rolling-mean ± scaling_factor·std, Bessel-corrected, preserving opt state), z-loss, MoE LB-loss aggregation, post-optimizer MoE bias updates, host-offload of params, gradient accumulation, and clipping. Checkpointing uses Orbax with EmergencyCheckpointManager / EmergencyReplicatorCheckpointManager (async/in-memory) and OCDBT — a genuinely strong in-framework resilience primitive.

The Blackwell evidence is real but thin

The strongest GPU source is NVIDIA's "Train Models Faster with JAX and MaxText Using NVFP4 on NVIDIA Blackwell": on GB200/GB300, NVFP4 vs FP8 yields 1.44× (405B/GB200) and 1.73× (405B/GB300), with Llama-3.1-405B hitting 2,241 TFLOP/s/GPU (GB200, NVFP4) and 3,633 (GB300, NVFP4) (developer.nvidia.com/.../train-models-faster-with-jax-and-maxtext-nvfp4). That 2,241 figure on GB200 is higher than Megatron's 1,048 — but it is a 405B dense model, not a 200B-active MoE, and the config is pure FSDP=128 (zero TP/PP/EP) at seq 8192. It proves XLA:GPU + GSPMD can hold a 405B-dense run on data/FSDP sharding inside one NVL72 domain; it does not prove the expert all-to-all scales.

GPU FP8/NVFP4 is delivered via Transformer Engine (quantization=te_nvfp4/te_nvfp4_no_rht), applied to MLP GEMMs only, attention higher-precision, +0.026-nat loss gap over 10k steps — so the GPU training precision path is TE-owned, while Qwix/AQT is the TPU-native path. The second decisive source is NVIDIA's Feb 2026 "Accelerating Long-Context Model Training in JAX and XLA": on a GB200 NVL72 running Llama-3-8B, an NVSHMEM-backed CollectivePermute beats NCCL by +36.3% at 256K seq (CP=16/TP=2, 32 GPUs: 366.94→500.22 TFLOP/s), selected by a new XLA pass, CollectiveBackendAssigner, that picks NCCL vs NVSHMEM per-collective and "always uses NVSHMEM for CollectivePermute" (requires JAX 0.6.2+) (developer.nvidia.com/.../accelerating-long-context-model-training-in-jax-and-xla). This shows XLA:GPU collective infra now reaches into on-stream NVSHMEM P2P — but the published win is CP-attention, not expert all-to-all.

The honest JAX-on-GPU risk verdict

Strengths. The math works (2,241–3,633 TFLOP/s/GPU on 405B with NVFP4 is competitive Blackwell utilization), the DeepSeek-V3 architecture is already GPU-and-TPU-tested, sharding/quantization/remat are config edits not kernel rewrites, and MaxText even reimplements DeepSeek's FP8-comms trick in pure JAX — deepseek_batchsplit_fp8.py's quantized_psum_scatter does a BF16 reduce-scatter forward and a quantized FP8 all-gather backward via custom VJP (github.com/.../deepseek_batchsplit_fp8.py).

Risks specific to 100k GB200.

  • (a) published GPU benchmarks top out at 128 GPUs (405B FSDP) and 64 GPUs (8B context-parallel) — there is no public MaxText-on-GPU result even at 1k GPUs, so multi-NVL72 / cross-DCN GSPMD scaling for a 200B-active MoE is externally unvalidated.
  • (b) Pathways — the single-controller, elastic, fault-tolerant runtime that makes 9,216-chip TPU Ironwood runs survivable — is documented almost exclusively for TPU; Google Cloud's GTC 2026 GB200/GB300 (A4X) story leans on Vertex AI + NVIDIA's NeMo/TensorRT-LLM/Dynamo, with no Pathways/MaxText-on-GPU mention. On a 100k-GPU NVIDIA cluster you would likely run multi-controller JAX (IFRT) and own your restart/elasticity layer — though MaxText's in-framework Emergency checkpointing and skip_step_on_spikes soften this.
  • (c) GPU FP8/NVFP4 is TE-dependent, so you inherit TE's coverage and gaps (GPU flash rejects attention sinks, QK-Clip / record_max_logits, and the decode stage per the code's validate_gpu_flash_attention).
  • (d) The MoE expert all-to-all on GPU — the hardest collective for a 200B-active model — has no published NVSHMEM/XLA result; only CP-attention does.
  • (e) Tokamax GPU kernels are self-described "heavily under development," H100-and-newer only.

The XLA-flag asymmetry tells the maturity story bluntly: TPU has dozens of mature overlap flags (xla_tpu_enable_async_collective_fusion*, SparseCore offload, even an xla_tpu_enable_sdc_checker) versus a handful of GPU flags plus the emerging NVSHMEM path.

Prefer MaxText on Ironwood; treat MaxText-on-GB200 as co-engineering

Net: JAX+XLA can match Megatron on per-GPU Blackwell throughput and is architecturally ahead on quantization ergonomics, but on a 100k-GB200 cluster the unproven pieces are large-scale GPU GSPMD, GPU expert all-to-all, and the resilience/orchestration layer that on TPU is Pathways. For this program — where MaxText is the incumbent codebase — the pragmatic stance is to run MaxText on TPU (Ironwood) where Pathways exists, and treat MaxText-on-GB200 as a co-engineering effort with NVIDIA's JAX-Toolbox team rather than a turnkey path.

05TorchTitan: the composable, PyTorch-native challenger

TorchTitan (arXiv 2410.06511, ICLR 2025) is a thin, model-readable trainer built entirely on first-party PyTorch distributed primitives: DTensor (Shard/Replicate/Partial placements over a DeviceMesh), FSDP2 (per-parameter fully_shard, DTensor-native), TP/SP/async-TP, torch.distributed.pipelining, Context Parallel, DCP checkpointing, Flight Recorder, and torch.compile (arxiv.org/abs/2410.06511). The thesis is composability: every technique is a DTensor transformation over a named mesh, so 1D→4D parallelism is a config change. The original H100 numbers (Llama-3.1 8B +65.08% at 128 GPUs; +12.59% adding 2D TP at 70B/256; +30% adding 3D PP at 405B/512) were dense and tested only to 512 GPUs — the central caveat for a 100k reader.

MoE/EP is now first-class and mirrors Megatron's folding. The mesh axes (parallel_dims.py) are dp_replicate, dp_shard, cp, tp, pp, ep plus a separate ETP degree. Routed expert FFNs live on a sparse sub-mesh (EP × expert-FSDP), distinct from the dense attention/shared-expert sub-mesh (FSDP × TP) — exactly the Parallel Folding decoupling. The MOE_SHARDING.md contract is precise: routed expert weights are Shard(0) on EP, MoE input redistributes Shard(1)→Shard(1), output is Partial→Shard(1) reduced once at the MoE boundary, with a LocalMapConfig converting DTensor→local so the grouped-GEMM runs on plain local tensors (github.com/.../MOE_SHARDING.md). Three EP all-to-all backends: a naive PyTorch all-to-all (degrades badly — ~50% of step time at EP=32), DeepEP (NVSHMEM+IBGDA, where dispatch-backward is combine), and MinimalAsyncEP (SymmetricMemory-native all-to-all-v via symm_mem.empty()/rendezvous(), enabling microbatch overlap without the DeepEP dependency).

The load-bearing Blackwell number is the PyTorch blog "Enabling up to 41% faster pre-training… DeepSeek-V3 on B200": DSV3-671B, 256× B200, seq 8192, EP=32, ETP=1, TP=2, PP=2, full AC — BF16+naive-EP = 651 tok/s; BF16+DeepEP = 859 (+32%); MXFP8-grouped-GEMM+DeepEP = 918 (+41%) (pytorch.org/blog/enabling-up-to-41-faster-pre-training). The decisive lesson: the DeepEP win (+32%) dominates the MXFP8 win (+9% incremental) — comms, not precision, is the lever.

AMD-side, TorchTitan+Primus-Turbo hit 96% scaling efficiency 128→1,024 GPUs on DSV3-671B with DeepEP capping EP comm at ~18% of runtime (vs ~50% naive) (pytorch.org/blog/efficient-moe-pre-training-at-scale-with-torchtitan) — proof the stack is genuinely vendor-portable, a non-obvious de-risking option for a GB200 buyer. The largest published dense run is Llama3-70B on 1,856 B200 GPUs (HSDP2, CP=2) with MXFP8 holding 1.22–1.28× over BF16 and only ~5% efficiency loss 4→188 nodes (pytorch.org/blog/accelerating-2k-scale-pre-training) — still ~18× below target.

Precision is delegated to torchao: Float8 tensorwise/rowwise with FSDP2 fp8 all-gather, MXFP8 (native torch._scaled_grouped_mm, ~28% over BF16 dense, ~1.2–1.8× on MoE grouped GEMMs), and an NVFP4 path that is QAT/inference-mature but not yet a validated end-to-end pretraining recipe as of mid-2026 — the precision frontier to watch, not to bet the cluster on. The comms substrate is SymmetricMemory (NVLS multimem_all_reduce/all_gather, NVSwitch v3+), on which async-TP is built (up to 29% fwd / 8% e2e on Llama3-8B) — but async-TP and the SymmetricMemory all-to-all are intra-node/NVSwitch-bound, and SGLang's testing found pure-SymmetricMemory all-to-all does not beat NCCL for large payloads after correctness fixes (github.com/sgl-project/sglang/issues/22583), which is why DeepEP remains the production multi-node EP path. On GB200 NVL72 the entire 72-GPU rack is one NVLink domain, which structurally expands the regime where SymmetricMemory/multimem and async-TP win — design EP inside an NVL72 domain.

Comms is the lever — ship DeepEP before chasing precision

The +41% TorchTitan result is +32% DeepEP, +9% MXFP8. On a GB200 buy, get the EP all-to-all onto DeepEP/HybridEP first; precision is the smaller, second-order win. And keep EP inside the NVL72 domain so SymmetricMemory/async-TP/multimem actually pay off.

Fault tolerance is the gating concern, and torchft is the credible PyTorch-native answer. torchft (meta-pytorch) gives checkpoint-free recovery: a global Lighthouse quorum server + per-replica-group managers run quorum-as-transaction (commit-or-rollback per step), and recovering HSDP replica groups pull weights from healthy peers via async P2P, not disk. The demo survived 2,000+ synthetic failures (one per 15s) on 300 L40S GPUs with 82.3% step efficiency at 60s-MTBF, and DiLoCo/Streaming-DiLoCo semi-sync gave 4,000 vs 1,200 tok/s on Ethernet (pytorch.org/blog/fault-tolerant-llama-training). But the demo is 1B params on 300 GPUs — three orders below target — and the FT path runs across replica groups (HSDP outer), adding a semi-sync outer loop you must validate against MoE convergence. The SPARe paper (arXiv 2603.00357) addresses the exact 100k+-GPU regime (arxiv.org/pdf/2603.00357).

TorchTitan's unproven edges at frontier scale

Bottom line: production-real for MoE to ~1K GPUs on both NVIDIA and AMD, the most composable and readable option, and where DTensor/SymmetricMemory/torchao innovations land first — but EP throughput parity with Megatron's GB200 TFLOPS, any published run beyond ~2K GPUs, and torchft at >10K-GPU MoE scale are all unproven.

06DeepSpeed: the legacy fallback

DeepSpeed pioneered ZeRO (stages 1–3), ZeRO-Offload/Infinity, and an early MoE path, and remains widely deployed. For a new 10T/200B run on 100k GB200, it is the wrong primary choice and warrants only a short verdict. Its ZeRO sharding is conceptually subsumed by Megatron-Core's precision-aware distributed optimizer and TorchTitan's FSDP2 + HSDP, both of which compose ZeRO-style sharding with the 5D parallelism and folded-EP that DeepSpeed's MoE never matched. DeepSpeed's MoE lacks the production-grade dropless dispatcher zoo (DeepEP/HybridEP), the fine-grained recompute/offload taxonomy, native MXFP8/NVFP4 grouped-GEMM training, and — decisively — the Blackwell-tuned NVL72 EP placement and modern resiliency (NVRx in-process restart, torchft quorum) that the other stacks now ship. There is no recent DeepSpeed GB200 MoE TFLOPS number remotely competitive with Megatron's 1,048 or TorchTitan's 918 tok/s DSV3 result.

Do not architect a frontier run on DeepSpeed

Keep it only as a fallback if a dependency forces it; do not architect a frontier run on it. Its ZeRO sharding is subsumed by Megatron's distributed optimizer and TorchTitan's FSDP2+HSDP, and it never matched the folded-EP, dropless dispatcher zoo, or Blackwell resiliency the other stacks ship.

Recommendation for the 10T/200B/100k-GB200 target. Run the production training on Megatron-Core driven by Megatron-Bridge, with HybridEP confined to NVL72 domains, ETP=1 with high EP for the fine-grained experts, TP-heavy folded attention for MLA, blockwise FP8 at bring-up migrating to MXFP8/NVFP4 (final four layers BF16), and the NVRx full resiliency stack (in-process restart + local/async checkpointing) as the non-negotiable substrate. This is the lowest-risk EP engine with the only credible Blackwell MoE throughput numbers and the most complete open resilience story.

Use TorchTitan as the de-risking and rapid-iteration vehicle — prototype recipes there (torchao precision, DTensor readability, fast 1→1K-GPU iteration) and run a head-to-head 1K-GB200 bake-off against Megatron using DSV3-685B as the reference, gating the 100k decision on EP-comm scaling inside NVL72 and torchft hardening. MaxText/JAX is the credible alternative only if your org has TPU-grade XLA expertise: it is architecturally ahead on quantization-as-config and already implements the DeepSeek-V4 lineage (hash routing, MLA, FP8 comms, DSA) in the user's actual codebase — but on a 100k GPU cluster you inherit unproven large-scale GSPMD, an unbenchmarked GPU expert all-to-all, and the absence of Pathways, so prefer it on Ironwood TPU and treat MaxText-on-GB200 as co-engineering. DeepSpeed is out for a new build.

The risks you are actually buying

Whichever you pick, no framework has a published >2K-GPU MoE MFU curve — Megatron caps at "thousands," TorchTitan at ~1,024–1,856, MaxText at 128 on GPU. At 100k you are past every documented envelope, the binding constraint is fault tolerance and silent-data-corruption resilience, not kernel throughput, and a multi-domain/multi-DC fleet will force Hierarchical-AllReduce-style outer loops nobody has demonstrated past ~3K GPUs. Budget for co-engineering with your silicon vendor, validate FP8/FP4 convergence on a 100B-token slice before committing the full token budget, and treat the ~20–35% dense-FP8 MFU band — not 50% — as your planning number.
